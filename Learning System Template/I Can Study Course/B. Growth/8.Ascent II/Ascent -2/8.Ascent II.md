**Bear hunter system – shoot**

**Shoot**

The Shoot step is the second part of the Bear Hunter System. It is an upgraded version of the green light step of TLS that you are used to. **At this point, you can completely replace your TLS with using the Aim and Shoot steps.**

Aim vs. Shoot

During Aim, we prime our brain to find meaningful relationships by asking importance-based questions.

During Shoot, we satiate that curiosity to clarify relationships, add detail, explore further relationships, make judgments on chunks and relationships, and focus on constructing our map.

Note that not every question has to be answered perfectly. Sometimes, you will ask questions that are so complex or advanced that it is not realistic to be able to answer them. The learning occurs when we attempt to answer it, which helps us to reinforce our relationships. 

**Finding Shoot easy?**

**Good! If you’ve aimed properly, shooting is easy.** 

Shooting becomes difficult when old bad habits or lower-order, linear learning interfere. Lower-order habits can cause us to have impaired order control. Using the non-linear note-taking system of Shoot will help to anchor your thoughts and keep them focused.

**Effective note-taking**

**Evidence-based note-taking**

The science around note-taking is complicated. Here are some of the key principles of note-taking that we know from reviewing the latest research.

Key principles of note-taking

The method we use to take notes matters less than how we are thinking as we take notes.

Good note-taking can help our brain think by reducing unhelpful and extraneous cognitive load.

Bad note-taking can make us avoid thinking, which reduces the effectiveness of learning.

Wordier notes are usually less processed and therefore less effective. Typing can exacerbate this as it is usually easier to create high volumes of notes compared to freehand writing.

**Effective note-taking: visual**

**V for Visual**

The first letter, V, stands for** Visual. **Here, you reduce the amount of words and use more visuals – pictures, symbols, doodles, shorthand and diagrams.

Using visuals greatly helps with **retention** as it incorporates multiple modes of learning. Visuals act as landmarks in your memory. It also makes your notes fun and personal! 

**Key principle**

**As with all note-taking, what you produce is less important than the processes you used to produce it.**

Even though other people probably won’t understand your doodles, you will have a deep and meaningful connection to them because you went through the cognitive process of imagining and creating them.

Lesson correction: The hemispheric myth

The video above may contain some premature assumptions that do not reflect the most recent research. Please read below.

The idea that the brain has different hemispheres with fixed specific functions is now under contention.

**What we know:**

Different parts of the brain have different functions, as demonstrated through a large volume of brain imaging studies.

Hemispheres seem to have no relationship to personality.

People do not seem to have one hemisphere that is dominant at a cognitive level for information processing, though dominance for sensory and motor function is well established.

Different areas of the brain seem to be discretely activated for different functions, and a general preference for one hemisphere over the other has been demonstrated. However, recent findings also show other parts of the brain being activated for certain forms of information that are not consistent with the existing theory.

Teaching to include multimodal learning is supported by evidence. However, there is insufficient evidence to suggest that this benefit is due to the differential usage of hemispheres rather than cortices.

Based on the current level of research, the argument made in this video that the dominant hemisphere is responsible for more rational thinking while the non-dominant hemisphere is responsible for abstract thinking may be contended as there is insufficient evidence to make this conclusion. While motor and sensory dominance has been consistently demonstrated, this is not necessarily the same as information-processing dominance or limited to strict hemispheres.

While this is theoretically valid to explore, it may not practically matter if hemispheric dominance is true or not since incorporating multimodal learning has been shown to have a benefit, regardless of why.

**Effective note-taking – processed, relational, freehand**

**Processed**

The next letter in the acronym, P, stands for **Processed**.

You must process the information somehow – we want to process it as a **problem** by **asking questions**. Again, visuals will also help you to process information.

**Relational**

“Re” stands for **Relational**. Do not focus on remembering individual pieces of information; always relate it to other information. If you read linearly, your brain does not process the information as effectively, and any information that goes in will quickly go out. That is why retention dramatically increases when you focus on strengthening the **relationships **between pieces of information. We do this by using mind maps.

**Freehand**

“F” stands for **Freehand**. Typing is too single-minded and doesn’t encourage much thinking. Freehand writing forces us to process the information more. It also allows us to represent things more visually, whereas typed documents don’t represent relationships well.

App and device recommendations for digital note-taking

A good note-taking app has the following features:

Facilitates the correct cognitive processes for learning based on modern research (most important)

Has good organizational features for multiple notes (less important)

To facilitate the correct cognitive processes, a note-taking app should have an infinite canvas and allow for convenient, fast freehand drawing with a digital stylus. Since most note-taking apps were designed based on **what would sell best** rather than **actual learning research**, many of them are restrictive to efficient learning.

*We have independently reviewed and tested all of these apps under a variety of circumstances.*

**The following apps are well-known but inefficient:**

**Evernote**: Has leading levels of note organization but a horrible and linear note-taking experience, which is extremely restrictive.

**Notion**: Has high levels of note organization and lots of note-taking options, but all of the options are linear. For this reason, Notion is extremely limited and not recommended for efficient or effective note-taking.

**The following apps are well-known and reasonable:**

**Notability**: Has decent note organization and a good writing experience, but is severely limited by its lack of infinite canvas.

**OneNote**: Has good note organization and a somewhat infinite canvas, but the canvas is limited by its low level of zoom-out, which eventually causes notes to become very difficult to read. The writing experience is also average, with no palm detection or pressure sensitivity (two small but meaningful features that make your note-taking more immersive).

**The following apps are recommended:**

**Freeform for iOS: **Comes bundled as part of iOS and is therefore "free" (if you purchase an iPad). Works well but lacks some of the aesthetic functionalities that Concepts has. Recommended app for those on iOS and on a budget.

**Concepts**: Was originally an app for design and architecture. Concepts is overloaded with features you will never use, but it has an excellent writing experience with full infinite canvas and acceptable note-organization features.

**Squid**: One of the best (and most underrated) note-taking apps on the market. Excellent, intuitive, simple writing experience and good note organization alongside infinite canvas. Not overloaded with features like Concepts.

**Leonardo x OneNote or Evernote for Windows**: Windows does not have **any** good note-taking apps, but Leonardo is the best alternative. It is a fully-featured drawing app with infinite canvas, but it has no inbuilt organization. You simply save project files and can export drawings as images or PDFs. Its lack of organization can be overcome by exporting these files and importing them into something like Evernote or Onenote.

**Explain Everything**: An app created for online teachers and instructors. We use this app to record all of our tablet videos. It is fully featured with great organization features but is more expensive.

**i****i****i. Effective note-taking – reflective**

**Reflective**

“R” stands for **Reflective**. You need to take time to reflect on your notes and check that they are not in the same order as the resource (e.g. textbook). We are used to working in order from start to finish, but this is not how our brain learns. To avoid falling into bad habits, reflect to see if your notes are in an order that makes sense to your brain (not linear).

**Efficient**

“E” stands for **Efficient** and is a check for understanding. You have to ask yourself if your note-taking is allowing you to learn a lot within the amount of study time.

Are you forgetting more than 30% of the information you are writing in your notes? If you are, there is something wrong with how you are doing the previous points of VPReFRE.

**Key principle**

Effective note-taking should **help our brain ****learn,**** **not help it avoid learning.

This final check is to make sure you are **retaining** the information and can **use **and **apply** it. If you can’t, something in your note-taking process needs to be changed.

**Beginner’s ****mindmap**** guide**

Art of mapping

By now, you've probably realised that creating mind maps is a surprisingly technical skill.

Below, we'll summarise some of the biggest steps as a general refresher of the process. Then we will explore some additional examples and dissections.

Creating a good mindmap: An overview

Identify the biggest chunks. These will be hypothetical at first. There are usually 3 to 7 for a single topic.

Arrange the chunks and most important contained concepts so that the relationship and flow between them is expressed.

Identify sub-concepts and sub-chunks and add them to the map. Always think about how each new chunk or concept fits into the big picture.

Add more details until the topic is complete. Information that is highly specific and not related to other parts on the map can be added to memorisation techniques such as flashcards.

General tips

When arranging your first chunks, leave big spaces between them so there is room for sub-chunks and other concepts.

Make the main flow of relationships between the largest chunks visually very clear. This is the "backbone" of the topic.

Remember to relate each new item to the bigger picture as you go.

Constantly refine and revise your structure. Do not overly commit to your first chunk structure. Everything is just a hypothesis!

Missing something?

If you spot a part of the process that you were neglected or skipping, attempt a mindmap following the guidelines above before moving on.

**Mindmap**** dissection**

**Learning happens in the brain ****🧠**

Simply completing a mindmap does not make your learning effective. If misused, mind maps can be just as bad as other types of notes. What's important is to use the right cognitive process so that mindmapping helps your brain think.

You know you are mindmapping correctly when you...

Find it easier to think of new relationships and chunks by seeing what you have already written down

Are able to reflect on how each individual piece connects to the big picture

Find that it feels messy and confusing at first, but over time as the mindmap becomes more organised and accurate, your knowledge also feels more organised and accurate

Continuously refine and update your mindmap (which indicates that you are constantly thinking of the big picture, holding yourself to a high standard, and keeping structures hypothetical)

Constantly engage in higher-order thinking processes (analysing by finding similarities and differences and evaluating by prioritising and making value judgments)

**Chunking by importance**

Evaluating importance

By now, you realise that there are many ways to chunk. The critical part of learning that drives efficiency is to engage in *evaluate* where the value of those chunks is judged and critiqued. This is where chunking by importance comes into play.

Example

Consider a topic around cellular biology. Read the below sample excerpt on how the topic is presented and think about how you might chunk this topic.

Sample textbook excerpt

**Title: Types and Arrangements of Epithelial Cells**

**Cell Types**

**Squamous Cells**

Description: Flat, scale-like cells.

Function: Efficient diffusion and protection in stratified squamous epithelia (e.g., skin, oral cavity).

**Cuboidal Cells**

Description: Cube-shaped cells.

Function: Secretion and absorption in simple cuboidal epithelia (e.g., kidney tubules, gland ducts).

**Columnar Cells**

Description: Tall and slender cells.

Function: Nutrient absorption and secretion in simple columnar epithelia (e.g., digestive tract).

**Cell Arrangements**

**Stratified Epithelium**

Description: Multiple layers of cells.

Function: Provides strength and protection, e.g., stratified squamous epithelium in the skin.

**Simple Epithelium**

Description: Single layer of cells.

Function: Facilitates diffusion, secretion, and absorption. Found in simple cuboidal and simple columnar epithelia.

**Pseudostratified Epithelium**

Description: Appears stratified but isn't.

Function: Moves mucus and particles in the respiratory tract for protection (e.g., pseudostratified columnar epithelium).

**Important Epithelial Cell Linings**

**Stratified Squamous Epithelium**

Function: Provides protection against abrasion and pathogens (e.g., skin, oral cavity).

**Simple Cuboidal Epithelium**

Function: Regulates urine composition through secretion and absorption (e.g., renal tubules).

**Simple Columnar Epithelium**

Function: Facilitates nutrient absorption and secretion of digestive enzymes (e.g., digestive tract).

**Pseudostratified Columnar Epithelium**

Function: Moves mucus and particles to protect the respiratory system (e.g., respiratory tract).

You might decide that rather than trying to memorise the functions of each important cell lining, we can chunk them into types vs. arrangements, since the important linings are simply examples of different combinations of types and arrangements.

By chunking it differently, we emphasise different relationships. This decision is made based on what makes the topic more memorable, more intuitive, and less overwhelming. For most learners, simpler means better and the first attempt at creating a chunk structure is almost *never* the simplest it can be.

**Key principle**

Often, the best chunk structure comes from seeing the topic from a different perspective to how it was originally taught. There must be an active effort and willingness to consider other ways of chunking. Almost always, finding a perspective of chunking that you have not found before will feel difficult or even impossible. Try anyway.

**As a general rule, ****always assume**** that there is a missing perspective or simpler way. Make it your job to find it. Yes, it is difficult. Yes, you must still find it!**

Those who push themselves to this standard will unlock a rare level of knowledge mastery and learning efficiency that is seen only in the top learners of the world.

In this instance, the function came first; however, chunking by function is not always best. For example, in Chemistry, “function” is not the reason why some functional groups or reactions are important. It could instead be due to them having similar reaction mechanisms or reactivity. In English, importance could be assigned to the author’s perspective or elements of their background and worldview.

**This is why the question is "Why is it important?" and not “What are their shared functions?”**

**The standard for importance**

Many learners struggle to find the most meaningful and important chunks for a topic. In many cases, the question of "why is it important" was not explored fully and to a sufficient standard.

**We can think about this as finding immediate reasons vs. root reasons.**

**Immediate vs. root reasons**

A root cause is the fundamental reason something happens. Similarly, a *root reason* could be considered the fundamental reason for something to be important. This can be illustrated by a story from the famous marketer, Seth Godin, who explains why people are motivated to buy things. 

**Godin asked why people buy drill bits (the metal things on the end of drills to make holes).**

The immediate answer could be that they want to make a hole in the wall. But this is not the *root reason*. 

People don't want a hole in the wall. They want the screw that will go inside the hole. But this is still not the root. They don't just want the screw; they want the picture that will hang on it. And they don't just want the picture; they want the feeling they will get from looking at the picture on the wall.

In this case, the *root reason* is that people buy drill bits to feel good when they see a picture on the wall.

**Root reasons in learning**

**Similarly, in learning, the ****root reason**** something is important is sometimes not immediately obvious.**

For example, when studying about *genetic diversity *(the variety of genes and genetic characteristics within a population or a species), learners may conclude that genetic diversity is important because it leads to variation.

However, if we ask, "why is variation important?", we can arrive at a root reason that variation is essential for the adaptability, resilience, and long-term survival of species and ecosystems.

Therefore, instead of creating a chunk called "variation", we may create one called "survival" instead. Notice how the second chunk is much broader and can likely encompass many more sub-concepts or sub-chunks. It will probably also be easier to create relationships to and from.

**The "So what?" approach**

Some people use the "So what?" approach where every time we find a reason for importance, we then ask "So what?" until the question no longer makes sense to ask. At this point, we have presumably found the *root* *reason*.

**Layers of learning**

**Four layers of learning**

“Layers of learning” focuses on implementing a structure when learning information. 

Key principles

Always start from the logic layer and work out towards the later layers.

The logic and concepts layers remain hypothetical and can be refined and adjusted at any point of the learning process.

Most information resources are not divided into optimal layers. Usually, detail comes far too early.

Order control allows us to rearrange the order in which our brain consumes and organizes information; layers of learning tell us how to rearrange it.

**Alert**

Layers of learning is one of our program's core pillar concepts.

**Overview of each layer**

Expand the accordions below to read an overview and description for each layer.

Logic

The first and innermost layer is **logic**. You start with the “backbone” of information, finding the big-picture purpose of what you are learning. This backbone is made up of the largest concepts, chunks, and most important relationships.

We build these relationships by asking, “Why is it important?” and “How does it relate?” and seeing how everything logically connects together. 

**People often skip this step entirely – this is like rushing to build a house and neglecting the foundation.**

Concepts

The second layer is the **concepts**. These are the main details and ideas that make up the bulk of subject-specific understanding.

The concept layer builds upon the first layer by taking all of those big ideas and asking, “How does it happen?” and “What is it?”. 

Students who ignore the first layer will often struggle with learning the concepts

Details (important)

The third layer is the **important details**. 

Details are specific information that often needs to be rote-learned and memorized. However, some details will be **important** because they help you understand the concepts, while others may just be **arbitrary**. Important details allow abstract concepts to be understood more concretely. 

You can identify the important details by asking, “Does knowing this detail help me understand the concept it is attached to?” If the answer is yes, it’s important. If the answer is no, it’s arbitrary!

Details (arbitrary)

The fourth and outermost layer is the **arbitrary details**. These are disconnected pieces of information that may seem random or unnecessary but are still in the curriculum or exam. 

We only learn these details because we need them for assessments. They must be rote-learned, at least until one day when we have learned far more, and they naturally make sense.

When we look at the total volume of information we need to study, only about 10% is conceptual, and about 20% is important details. This means the remaining 70% is arbitrary details. So, if you just study from beginning to end, you will not be able to retain much of the information because most of it is arbitrary and unconnected! You will find yourself trying to remember a lot of information that doesn’t have any significance or meaning.

Conversely, if you start with deep, foundational information, this will **dramatically** increase your overall studying speed because by increasing the relevance of information, it turns arbitrary details into relevant details!

**Key principle**

**Layers are not static.** How relevant a detail is depends on whether we have the structure to give it relevance. This means that errors in building an importance-based logic and concepts layer often mean we have more seemingly irrelevant details. The trick to reducing layer 4 (arbitrary details) is to improve layers 1 and 2.

*Even if a tree has 10,000 leaves, it may only have 5 or 6 main branches. Spending time on the branches allows the leaves to arise more naturally.*

**As a general guide, it is usually best to overinvest in developing clear layers 1 and 2, as this makes a dramatic difference to the time it takes to cover layers 3 and 4, where the majority of time can sometimes be wasted.**

**Layering and cognitive load**

Learning in layers is one of the most powerful principles you can integrate into your learning system. It makes large volumes of content less overwhelming and more relevant, improves retention, and creates deeper mastery. One of the reasons for this is that learning in layers optimises your cognitive load.

**Read our in-depth article **** on how layering and "scaffolding" improve learning.**

**The value of theory**

Understanding how layering improves learning at a theoretical level helps you self-diagnose and adapt your learning if it isn't as effective as you want it to be. This helps you to coach yourself when you face new challenges, which is an essential part of self-regulated learning!

**Barriers for shooting**

**Beware of barriers**

Be careful of the following barriers to shooting. These are mistakes that students have made in the past, which prevented them from successfully taking on these techniques.

**Which barriers resonate with you?**

Aim-Shoot takes too much time!

The time spent building your mind map will create more connections in your brain and ultimately save you more time. The real waste of time is returning to traditional forms of learning, where you memorise pages of text with no escape** (deathly spiral warning!)**. Also, building mind maps becomes much faster as you become more proficient at the technique, which makes learning even quicker over time.

We recommend reviewing our article  on how to accurately measure your learning efficiency to see if it really does take too long or if it just *seems* that way.

Aim-Shoot is too difficult!

Learners can become apprehensive of this technique because they cannot gain certainty about their knowledge for a long time.

However, the certainty that comes from traditional methods is actually an illusion of learning that does not reflect the knowledge you build mentally. This results in inconsistent performance and results, which lead to frustration, demotivation, and performance anxiety.

Remember that making and finding mistakes is a critical part of learning and improving. Students who do not find it difficult cause alarm bells to ring because it usually means they are not applying the technique to the correct standard!

What if I miss something?

You will miss more things by **not **using a mind map and following the layers. Mind maps structure your learning to match how your brain learns, which is ultimately the most optimal way to learn. You also don’t have to worry about missing details as you jump around and use order control because using the full Bear Hunter System with SIR will prevent any gaps.

Baking Without Ingredients

Prechunking can be helpful by giving us a hypothetical chunk structure to start our evaluation process. However, it is crucial that the chunk structure is considered hypothetical and is actively challenged and refined during the inquiry process. Our pre-chunked structures will often not be the best structures to use, especially if they are based on common patterns of chunking that are repeated many times across similar topics. This problem was alluded to in the *chunking for medicine* sublesson in Ascent 1’s *Aim examples* lesson.

Because framing bias can be incredibly powerful, learners who struggle to break free from prechunked structures are advised to avoid prechunking altogether and form chunks only after deliberate inquiry.

**Learning notes**

A Cosmic Trio

When relational networks are formed with a *cracked glass* appearance, the relationships can be hard to remember due to their complexity. Often we see chunks connected to other chunks through their sub-concepts, rather than directly from chunk to chunk. Avoid this mistake by creating clear backbones between major chunks before committing to detailed information prematurely.

Cracked glass can also be a sign of *segmental mapping* whereby learners focus on fully developing just one chunk or concept to full depth before moving onto the next one. This is problematic as it does not consider how each chunk or concept is related to the big picture. Segmental mapping diminishes the quality of the chunk structure, adds potentially massive time inefficiency as the brain does not have big-picture relevance for the chunks it is trying to organise, and increases the likelihood of many other problems, including *spiderwebbing*.

**Learning notes**

Interchunkecular Forces

Importance checklisting is similar to *simple question and answer* in that they both obstruct the benefit of the inquiry process. With importance checklists, the importance-based questions (while high quality) are not explored beyond a single reason for importance. As a result, only one relationship is formed. Avoid this by finding multiple reasons for importance and evaluating how multiple concepts are similar in different ways. These similarities allow us to form chunks and different kinds of similarities help us identify alternative chunks.

In many cases, all it takes is to rearrange a few concepts into an alternative chunk to be able to see an entirely new perspective to the topic and a potentially drastically different chunk structure.
