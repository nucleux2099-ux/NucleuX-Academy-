# Chunking by importance
## Evaluating importance

By now, you realise that there are many ways to chunk. The critical part of learning that drives efficiency is to engage in _evaluate_ where the value of those chunks is judged and critiqued. This is where chunking by importance comes into play.

### Example

Consider a topic around cellular biology. Read the below sample excerpt on how the topic is presented and think about how you might chunk this topic.
#### Sample textbook excerpt

**Title: Types and Arrangements of Epithelial Cells**

**Cell Types**

1. **Squamous Cells**
    - Description: Flat, scale-like cells.
    - Function: Efficient diffusion and protection in stratified squamous epithelia (e.g., skin, oral cavity).
2. **Cuboidal Cells**
    - Description: Cube-shaped cells.
    - Function: Secretion and absorption in simple cuboidal epithelia (e.g., kidney tubules, gland ducts).
3. **Columnar Cells**
    - Description: Tall and slender cells.
    - Function: Nutrient absorption and secretion in simple columnar epithelia (e.g., digestive tract).

**Cell Arrangements**

1. **Stratified Epithelium**
    - Description: Multiple layers of cells.
    - Function: Provides strength and protection, e.g., stratified squamous epithelium in the skin.
2. **Simple Epithelium**
    - Description: Single layer of cells.
    - Function: Facilitates diffusion, secretion, and absorption. Found in simple cuboidal and simple columnar epithelia.
3. **Pseudostratified Epithelium**
    - Description: Appears stratified but isn't.
    - Function: Moves mucus and particles in the respiratory tract for protection (e.g., pseudostratified columnar epithelium).

**Important Epithelial Cell Linings**

1. **Stratified Squamous Epithelium**
    - Function: Provides protection against abrasion and pathogens (e.g., skin, oral cavity).
2. **Simple Cuboidal Epithelium**
    - Function: Regulates urine composition through secretion and absorption (e.g., renal tubules).
3. **Simple Columnar Epithelium**
    - Function: Facilitates nutrient absorption and secretion of digestive enzymes (e.g., digestive tract).
4. **Pseudostratified Columnar Epithelium**
    - Function: Moves mucus and particles to protect the respiratory system (e.g., respiratory tract).


You might decide that rather than trying to memorise the functions of each important cell lining, we can chunk them into types vs. arrangements, since the important linings are simply examples of different combinations of types and arrangements.

For most learners, simpler means better and the first attempt at creating a chunk structure is almost _never_ the simplest it can be.

### Key principle

Often, the best chunk structure comes from seeing the topic from a different perspective to how it was originally taught. There must be an active effort and willingness to consider other ways of chunking. Almost always, finding a perspective of chunking that you have not found before will feel difficult or even impossible. Try anyway.

**As a general rule,** _**always assume**_ **that there is a missing perspective or simpler way. Make it your job to find it. Yes, it is difficult. Yes, you must still find it!**

Those who push themselves to this standard will unlock a rare level of knowledge mastery and learning efficiency that is seen only in the top learners of the world.

In this instance, the function came first; however, chunking by function is not always best. For example, in Chemistry, “function” is not the reason why some functional groups or reactions are important. It could instead be due to them having similar reaction mechanisms or reactivity. In English, importance could be assigned to the author’s perspective or elements of their background and worldview.

**This is why the question is "Why is it important?" and not “What are their shared functions?”**

## The standard for importance

Many learners struggle to find the most meaningful and important chunks for a topic. In many cases, the question of "why is it important" was not explored fully and to a sufficient standard.

**We can think about this as finding immediate reasons vs. root reasons.**

### Immediate vs. root reasons

A root cause is the fundamental reason something happens. Similarly, a _root reason_ could be considered the fundamental reason for something to be important. This can be illustrated by a story from the famous marketer, Seth Godin, who explains why people are motivated to buy things. 

**Godin asked why people buy drill bits (the metal things on the end of drills to make holes).**

![[Image 41.jpg|Extreme closeup photo of a drillbit]]

The immediate answer could be that they want to make a hole in the wall. But this is not the _root reason_. 

People don't want a hole in the wall. They want the screw that will go inside the hole. But this is still not the root. They don't just want the screw; they want the picture that will hang on it. And they don't just want the picture; they want the feeling they will get from looking at the picture on the wall.

In this case, the _root reason_ is that people buy drill bits to feel good when they see a picture on the wall.

### Root reasons in learning

**Similarly, in learning, the** _**root reason**_ **something is important is sometimes not immediately obvious.**

For example, when studying about _genetic diversity_ (the variety of genes and genetic characteristics within a population or a species), learners may conclude that genetic diversity is important because it leads to variation.

![[Image 42.jpg|An artistic photo of a double helix DNA strand made of plants with flowers growing off it.]]

However, if we ask, "why is variation important?", we can arrive at a root reason that variation is essential for the adaptability, resilience, and long-term survival of species and ecosystems.

Therefore, instead of creating a chunk called "variation", we may create one called "survival" instead. Notice how the second chunk is much broader and can likely encompass many more sub-concepts or sub-chunks. It will probably also be easier to create relationships to and from.

#### The "So what?" approach

Some people use the "So what?" approach where every time we find a reason for importance, we then ask "So what?" until the question no longer makes sense to ask. At this point, we have presumably found the _root_ _reason_.
