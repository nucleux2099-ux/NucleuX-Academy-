# Learning notes

The purpose of the logic layer is to create a _backbone_ of information. This is composed of a network of the most important chunks containing the most important concepts and relationships between chunks.

To identify these chunks, we must analyse (find similarities and differences) and evaluate (make judgments about the value and importance of these similarities and differences) to find chunks that are relevant to the big picture. Simply identifying similarities and possible chunks is not the same as evaluating the quality of these chunks _compared to the alternative_ chunk structures.

# Learning notes

The chances that our initial ideas at the chunk structure are correct are very low. In most cases, our initial hypothetical chunk structures serve just to help us think about and process the information. Once we have been able to offload some of our cognitive load into an initial structure, we can then refine it further as we learn more in depth and in detail.

The likelihood that we will need to then change the structure again down when we are in the details layers are generally low (but not impossible) if we have continually tried to update our overall structure in the early layers.

# Learning notes

Learning in layers gives a clearer structure and outcome for targeting our order control and Aim-Shoot cycle. By following our good inquiry process, we can discern which chunks are the most important, allowing us to separate the information into logic, concepts, and important details layers. Naturally, the remainder becomes the arbitrary details layer.

It is crucial that we do not judge that information is an arbitrary detail and commit to rote-memorisation before doing the proper inquiry process. Remember, the layers are not static. The amount of information that seems arbitrary and irrelevant depends directly on the structures you can create in the logic and concepts layers.
