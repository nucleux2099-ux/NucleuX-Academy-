# Effective note-taking -  Visual
## V for Visual

The first letter, V, stands for **Visual.** Here, you reduce the amount of words and use more visuals – pictures, symbols, doodles, shorthand and diagrams.

Using visuals greatly helps with **retention** as it incorporates multiple modes of learning. Visuals act as landmarks in your memory. It also makes your notes fun and personal! 

### Key principle

**As with all note-taking, what you produce is less important than the processes you used to produce it.**

Even though other people probably won’t understand your doodles, you will have a deep and meaningful connection to them because you went through the cognitive process of imagining and creating them.

#### Lesson correction: The hemispheric myth

The video above may contain some premature assumptions that do not reflect the most recent research. Please read below.

The idea that the brain has different hemispheres with fixed specific functions is now under contention.

### What we know:

1. Different parts of the brain have different functions, as demonstrated through a large volume of brain imaging studies.
2. Hemispheres seem to have no relationship to personality.
3. People do not seem to have one hemisphere that is dominant at a cognitive level for information processing, though dominance for sensory and motor function is well established.
4. Different areas of the brain seem to be discretely activated for different functions, and a general preference for one hemisphere over the other has been demonstrated. However, recent findings also show other parts of the brain being activated for certain forms of information that are not consistent with the existing theory.
5. Teaching to include multimodal learning is supported by evidence. However, there is insufficient evidence to suggest that this benefit is due to the differential usage of hemispheres rather than cortices.

Based on the current level of research, the argument made in this video that the dominant hemisphere is responsible for more rational thinking while the non-dominant hemisphere is responsible for abstract thinking may be contended as there is insufficient evidence to make this conclusion. While motor and sensory dominance has been consistently demonstrated, this is not necessarily the same as information-processing dominance or limited to strict hemispheres.

While this is theoretically valid to explore, it may not practically matter if hemispheric dominance is true or not since incorporating multimodal learning has been shown to have a benefit, regardless of why.
