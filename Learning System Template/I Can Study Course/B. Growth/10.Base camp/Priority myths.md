# Priority myths
## Myths

### Myth 1: Working faster makes you more productive.

Your brain doesn’t work this way. You are not a factory – working faster will not necessarily produce more output. Instead, when you try to perform _faster_, the quality of your work decreases, and errors increase.

Of course, making errors due to poor understanding or retention is an unavoidable part of the learning process. However, we should still aim to reduce _unnecessary or unproductive_ errors to avoid becoming inefficient.

### Myth 2: Trying harder makes you more productive.

Often, people try so hard that they don’t have the mental space, time or perspective to see if their actions are correct. They become so consumed with trying hard that they don’t realise their efforts are not taking them towards their goals.

A great example is studying. Most students are so consumed with studying harder that they don’t even realise how bad their technique is. Even if they realise, they do not have time to learn to work smarter! Furthermore, activities that seem unproductive may improve productivity, like relaxing to maintain mental health.

#### Key principle

Hard work can prevent smart work. Working smarter requires time and mental freedom. If a standard of performance is only possible with high levels of effort, the only option is to take a short-term loss.
