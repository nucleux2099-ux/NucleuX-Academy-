# Neuroticism -  Management – chain analysis
## Chain analysis

_Chain analysis_ involves finding the causes of your responses and emotions. Clinical psychologists use this technique to help with neuroticism, and it is a versatile life-skill to gain mastery of. There are many ways to perform a chain analysis. One simple and effective version that psychologists may employ is outlined below.
### 1. Target behaviour

**What is the specific behaviour that you want to change?**

It could be a bad habit like procrastinating, over-eating, spending too much time on social media, binge-watching Netflix, or even emotional behaviours you use to cope, such as getting angry at someone and yelling at them.

### 2. Prompting event

**What was the event that sparked the chain leading to the target behaviour?**

Be as specific as possible.

### 3. Vulnerability factors

**What made you vulnerable to the prompting event?**

This could be your state of mind, mood, preceding events, or even your habits that caused the event to affect you more than it might have for others or if it had happened on another day.

### 4. Links

**What happened between the prompting event and the target behaviour? These are the links in the chain.**

- What happened? How did you behave? How did you react?
- How did you feel at each point? How did your feelings evolve throughout the links?
- What thoughts did you have after the prompting event and before the target behaviour?

Be as specific as possible.

### 5. Consequences

**What were the short-term and long-term consequences of your behaviour?**

### 6. Solutions

**What potential solutions can you use to change your behaviour in the future?**

- **Exposure:** How can you limit your exposure to the prompting event or similar events? If this isn’t feasible, how can you limit your exposure to vulnerability factors?
- **Skills:** What skills can you develop or use to mitigate your vulnerability factors, reduce your exposure, modify and influence the events, behaviours, emotions and thoughts, or manage the consequences?
- **Cognitive modifications*:** How can you change your mindset or perspective about an event so that it has less of an impact on you emotionally and mentally? How will you remind yourself about this next time a similar trigger arises?
- **Contingency management:** We can’t always get things perfect. If all the previous steps fail, what can we do to reduce the short-term and long-term consequences? How can we help ourselves be more successful at reducing the previous steps in the future? Perhaps we need stronger reminders or a more specific or clearer structure and system.

## *About cognitive modifications

Even if you don’t have the skills to manage and deal with problems directly, you can drastically reduce the influence and impact it has on you by changing how you perceive it. 

**For example**, anxiety often becomes worse when you are aware of and actively focusing on not being anxious. This is an anxiety spiral and can lead to panic attacks. A cognitive modification such as acknowledging your anxiety, appreciating your brain’s reaction to try and protect you from danger, but reassuring it that there is no real danger to be afraid of, is a successful technique for managing anxiety spirals. 

Cognitive modifications can be simple changes in mindset or perspective that you try to remind yourself of in the future. Or they can be more specific frameworks and techniques you learn from trustworthy sources.
