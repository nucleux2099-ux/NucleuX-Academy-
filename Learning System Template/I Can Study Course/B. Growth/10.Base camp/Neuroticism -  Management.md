# Neuroticism -  Management
## When push comes to shove

From the lessons so far, we have learned steps to prevent neuroticism from overwhelming us.

These steps may initially feel unfamiliar or artificial, but they give you more freedom and time as they release you from the shackles of inefficiency and lack of control. It just takes a bit of effort to free yourself!

_Managing_ neuroticism is about training our brains to not fixate on some thoughts and emotions. This helps us become more focused, less distracted, and more emotionally stable.

![[Image 54.jpg|A graph showing how our attention fluctuates and our recovery depends on our ability to "let it go" and recenter our focus]]

When we struggle to let go of thoughts and feelings, it consumes our attention for a long time.

We can achieve this using _focus training_.

### Focus training

Emotional self-regulation and remaining focused is less about _forcing_ our brain to focus and much more about _preventing_ our brains from being hijacked by thoughts and emotions. Focus training helps improve your brain's ability to let go of thoughts and feelings and return to a stable baseline.

![[Image 55.jpg|Focus training helps us stay centered more easily]]

Focus training helps us to "let go" and remain more stable.

**In this way, focus can be considered a cognitive skill.**

Training focus is conceptually simple:

1. Put ourselves in a situation where our attention drifts (usually when there are no distractions - this is also why our mind wanders before we go to sleep).
2. Practice letting go and bringing our attention back.

We can use the steps below to train ourselves.

#### Warning

If you have past trauma, focus training (which is a form of _mindfulness meditation_) can resurface traumatic memories. If you are aware of having past trauma, we recommend engaging in this activity with the guidance of a therapist or psychologist. Most trained therapists will be experienced in guiding clients on this technique.

#### Focus training instructions

1. Sit somewhere comfortable away from distractions. Close your eyes.
2. Take slow, deep breaths in and out.
3. Focus on the sensation of each breath in and out. Be attentive to the air flow, how your body moves, and any other sensation associated with breathing.
4. After a few seconds, you should start having other thoughts and your mind will wander. Each time your mind wanders, acknowledge the thought and let go of it without pursuing or fixating on it. Bring your attention gently back to your breathing.
5. Repeat this process for 15 to 20 minutes (it's hard!)

You train your focus and self-regulation ability every time you let go and bring your attention back. It's normal for your mind to wander every three or four seconds. It can be frustrating at first, but remember that the point is to experience this and practice letting go and settling back into your breathing.

Over time, you will find this easier, and the time between wandering thoughts will grow longer. Even after years of practice, having no more than 10 seconds of "mental silence" is normal. However, this represents a tripling of focus ability!

#### Tip

Do not deliberately try to _stop_ having distracting thoughts. Let those thoughts do what they will, like clouds floating in the sky or leaves floating down a stream. Observe them and let them pass without holding onto them, reaching out for them, or giving them your attention.

### Therapeutic dose

The "dose" of focus training to make significant improvements is generally considered 20 minutes daily for 30 days. In our experience, effects are seen with just 10 minutes daily. Consistency is key. 

**We recommend anchoring this in your routine as a new habit to form.**

#### Research on focus training/meditation

Read about [**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)**](https://positivepsychology.com/act-acceptance-and-commitment-therapy) for [youth](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15374416.2015.1110822) and some of the [metaphors](https://belmontwellness.com/wp-content/uploads/Effective-Use-of-Metaphors-in-the-ACT-Theory.pdf) it uses, including the yellow jeep. This is a form of cognitive behavioural therapy used by all clinical psychologists.

Read about [**mindfulness meditation**](https://www.mindful.org/meditation/mindfulness-getting-started/) and the related articles on this website to gain some basic knowledge about this highly effective practice.

There is so much research on mindfulness meditation and ACT that you may benefit from searching [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/) for those terms. There is a lot of very interesting research on many different types of people and problems.
