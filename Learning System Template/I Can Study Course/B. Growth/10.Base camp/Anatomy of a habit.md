# Anatomy of a habit
## Cue and response

### Optimising cue and response

1. Identify the cues that trigger the response (i.e., the good or bad habit).
2. Get rid of bad cues that cause you to respond undesirably.
3. Adopt and integrate good cues that facilitate you to respond desirably.

**For example**, a bad cue might be **seeing a social media icon** when you open your browser or turn on your phone. Remove this cue by deleting the icon and browsing in incognito mode when you need to focus. Another bad cue might be **seeing your phone**, which you can remove by putting it at the bottom of your drawer or giving it to someone else.

A good cue for studying might be **seeing your books open** on the right page, which you can arrange the night before. A good cue for exercise might be **seeing your running shoes**, which you can arrange by hanging them off your doorknob.

## Conditioning a response

Another thing that affects our behaviour is **conditioning**. This occurs when an unrelated external stimulus can evoke the intended response.

**For example**, you can condition yourself to study (the response) by doing something that makes you happy immediately afterwards (the stimulus). Once your brain has conditioned itself, it will start to enjoy studying even when you don’t do the reward activity afterwards.
