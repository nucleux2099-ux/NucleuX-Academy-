# Neuroticism -  Prevention
## Principles of prevention

Trigger avoidance can be an effective way to reduce overwhelming neuroticism. To prevent the triggers of neuroticism, there are three aspects we need to address: awareness, attention and analysis. 

### Awareness

In our busy lives, we often ignore our thoughts and ‘force through’ them by immersing ourselves in more work, responsibilities, and expectations. If these thoughts are not processed healthily, everyday life becomes stressful, with the outcomes of every action feeling like it could be potentially life-ending. This is a psychological phenomenon known as _catastrophising_.

#### Step 1: Identifying distortions

Cognitive distortions are mental biases and skewed thoughts that increase anxiety and make us feel bad about ourselves. They are common in highly neurotic individuals and may have acted as a cautionary mechanism for humans in our ancient past. Awareness involves identifying distortions and how they make us feel. Techniques like Kolb's naturally improves emotional self-awareness and root cause analysis.

### Attention, analysis and acceptance

Attention involves diving deeper into those emotions and causes as we feel them. Analysis involves objectively dissecting the emotional process, including cues, behaviours, and patterns. This gives us more control over the emotions and their effects on us, rather than letting them affect us without restraint.

We often think that emotions are outside our control or influence. But by being attentive to the paths and ‘life cycles’ of our emotions, we can think about them differently and even influence how they affect us.

#### Example

If we pay attention to where our anxiety comes from, we may be able to express gratitude for it since it means our brain is trying to protect us from danger. As a result, our anxiety can impact us less negatively, breaking the habit-forming cycles that make anxiety dangerous.

### Avoidance behaviours

While avoiding triggers can be effective, there are many situations where trigger avoidance is impossible. When we develop habits of avoiding discomfort and emotional triggers, we can enter into something called _avoidance behaviours_, which can make anxiety worse.

With avoidance behaviours, we become consumed with avoiding triggers, leading to a restricted and hypervigilant lifestyle in constant fear of triggers. The right balance is to avoid triggers that lead to emotional overwhelm but learn to manage our exposure to unavoidable triggers for a free and healthy lifestyle.
