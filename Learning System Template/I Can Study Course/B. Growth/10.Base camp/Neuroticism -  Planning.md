# Neuroticism -  Planning
## Proper planning

Welcome back to marginal gains!

You should break down your final goal (i.e., your desired outcome) into smaller, performance-based goals. This will help you focus on the _process_ rather than just the results. Remember that outcomes also depend on factors outside your control.

### Break down your goals

Use the following strategies to help reduce overwhelm

1. Use reverse goal-setting to find smaller, performance goals
2. Focus on the relationship between outcome, skills, attributes, and mindsets
3. Create plans on how you will address the limitations in skills/processes, attributes, or mindsets
4. Expect difficulty - it's much easier to persist through difficulty when we expect and accept it

## About reverse goal setting

Reverse goal setting is any instance where the desired outcome is dissected into its determinants and then traced back to our current position. The example in this video is one way we can apply reverse goal setting. A slightly different method incorporating force-field analysis may also be appropriate for especially complex goals.

Learn about an alternative method with force-field analysis on the YouTube video [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bq1x659j8mA).

 

### How to perform reverse goal setting

1. Identify the desired end outcome
2. Define the skills, attributes, and mindsets necessary to achieve this with maximal certainty
3. (Optional) Rate the level we need to be at each out of 10
4. Rate our current level for each of these out of 10
5. Create a plan to improve the skills and attributes necessary within the desired deadline (see additional notes below)

## Creating a plan

We can create plans with a _top-down_ vs. _bottom-up_ approach. Expand the accordions below to learn about each approach.

### Top-down

Top-down means we start at the end and work backwards. The example given in the video uses a top-down approach. This approach is best for when:

1. It is easier to deconstruct our goals from the end result (often the case with complex goals that involve several components with a variety of timings)
2. It is difficult to assess our current skill and attribute level, or we have not begun to develop them

We can assess the feasibility of the plan by seeing if our _true_ current state is aligned with the _supposed_ current state we arrive at by tracing back from the desired goal towards the present day. If (by working backwards), we find that we currently need to be in a better state than we are, the plan is not feasible and must be reconsidered.

### Bottom-up

Bottom-up means we start at the current state and work forwards. The example in the YouTube video uses a bottom-up approach. This approach is best for when:

1. Our current skills and attribute level is clearer
2. It is unclear what is needed towards the later stages of achieving the goal
3. There is a more direct relationship between skills and attributes and the goal

We assess the feasibility of the plan by seeing if we can reach the goal within the deadline based on the step-wise progression of skill and attribute development. If (based on our anticipated pace of development) we are unable to reach the desired level of skill to reach our goals in the time available, the plan is not feasible and must be reconsidered.

**Bottom-up approaches are still reverse goal setting if we identify the required skills and attributes based on analysing our desired goal.**
## Use both

In many cases, we may want to use both a top-down _and_ bottom-up analysis to assess the viability of our plan and see if we have missed anything.
