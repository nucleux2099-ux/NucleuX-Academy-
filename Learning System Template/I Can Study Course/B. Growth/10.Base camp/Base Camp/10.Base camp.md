**Hipshot: BHS variation**

**Building on mastery**

Now that we have close to unconscious competence of the traditional Bear Hunter System with Aim-Shoot cycling, we will build upon it with an advanced variation we call **Hipshot**.

**Hipshot**

The Hipshot variation will switch quickly between Aim and Shoot. It is essentially a faster method of Aim-Shoot cycling that exploits the high cognitive load tolerance you have trained and your ability to form relational networks rapidly.

**Instructions**

Collect keywords as normal

Perform the Aim step mentally to identify a hypothetical logic layer.

Immediately Shoot on your mental model of the logic layer to validate or correct it.

As you gain clarity about the validity and intuitiveness of the logic layer, commit it to a GRINDE map.

Repeat this process from step 2 until you work through each of your layers.

Perform the Skin step once you are satisfied with your initial map and feel ready for refinement.

**Correct usage of Hipshot allows you to save time by:**

Drastically reducing the time for Aim

Reducing the number of iterations before arriving at a map to Skin

While Hipshot increases speed and efficiency, it is vital that quality and accuracy are not impacted. Hipshot is only possible with close to unconscious competence with the traditional Aim-Shoot cycle. If you attempt Hipshot and struggle, please return to the traditional BHS.

**Note**

Like Aim-Shoot cycling itself, Hipshot often occurs as a natural byproduct of unconscious competence developing with Aim and Shoot individually.

**BHS implementation variations**

Standard BHS implementation

This standard implementation uses all the normal BHS techniques daily. It is a good starter schedule to get used to replacing normal study with BHS. 

**Characteristic features of the standard BHS implementation:**

Aim always happens during prestudy and covers at least one week's worth of material.

Shoot happens before (if viable), during, and after main learning events

Skin always happens after the main learning event. 

**Time efficiency:** 5/10 

**Difficulty:** Low

**Instructions:**

Perform Aim step for one week’s worth of material at the end of the previous week (e.g. on Sunday)

Do a shoot for only the most basic and important concepts/relationships if you have time after the Aim step. If you don’t have time, do this in the morning of, or at any time before learning it in your *main learning event*.

Continue the Aim-Shoot cycle during the *main learning event*, finishing your Shoot step.

If you have time, do the Skin step at the end of the day or, alternatively, at the end of the week for the entire week’s worth. 

You can also do a Skin for each day’s material and continually add to the GRINDEmap with each new day’s content, then re-Skin the entire week again. Both methods will be effective. What’s more important is that you do what you have time for and can do consistently. Some busier weeks may require you to alternate.

Accelerated BHS implementation

This accelerated implementation is an intermediate schedule to get used to working independently from the slower pace and restrictions of classes and lectures.

**Characteristic features of the accelerated BHS implementation:**

The Aim always covers an entire topic at a time.

You are working at a different pace and order than your peers.

Aim and Shoot always occur before main learning events.

Skin occurs at the end of the week for an entire topic at once.

**Time efficiency:** 7/10 

**Difficulty:** Moderate

**Instructions:**

Spend one weekend Aiming and Shooting for around 60-70% of the content for the entire next topic you will study for a single subject. This gives you a significant lead and reduces the pressure of trying to "keep up".

Every weekend, do the same process for a different subject, allowing you to stay weeks ahead of the formal curriculum pace for most subjects at all times.

During the week, complete the Shoot for the remaining 30-40% of content before, during and after main learning events, much like how you might in the standard BHS. The goal is to finish both Aim and Shoot for the entire topic for a subject within one week of the formal timing.

Perform Skin at the end of the week.

Attending classes or lectures after you have already self-studied the material is useful as a recheck, re-skin and easy multi-order retrieval session. 

**Tips:**

Use materials or resources at your disposal for self-study, including Google.

Don’t worry about covering 100% of the curriculum – you have plenty of chances to pick up things you missed as you will be so far ahead.

Continue a weekly + monthly interleaved retrieval (appropriate to knowledge type and order) to find gaps, maintain retention, and ensure comprehensive mastery

Continue lower-order retrieval methods for layer 4 details throughout. This should not take much time if you have effective encoding methods.

You should still have plenty of time available each week to focus on other subjects as needed for tests, assignments or unexpected difficulties.

Ultra-accelerated BHS implementation

This ultra-accelerated implementation is for students adept at using the standard and accelerated BHS with a high mastery over the core technique. It creates total academic independence from curriculum pacing, creating the most free time and efficiency.

**Characteristic features of the ultra-accelerated BHS implementation:**

Aim, Shoot and Skin all happen before main learning events using self-study material

The time during curriculum weeks is used for interleaved retrieval and getting further ahead or more skills development.

Entire topics are studied in a single session as the norm.

**Time efficiency:** 10/10 

**Difficulty:** High

**Instructions:**

The instructions are brief because it relies on simply cramming through as much as possible, as fast as possible, without sacrificing the quality of learning or retention. The objective is to be so far ahead of formal curriculum pacing that most pressures and challenges are eradicated.

This combination is essentially impossible for most learners who have not had substantial cognitive training.

On weekends, breaks or other times without classes or lectures, Aim, Shoot and Skin for the entire subject's topic. 

Continue to do this for your subjects every weekend, aiming to be at first a few weeks ahead for one subject, then a few weeks ahead for every subject by the next week, then several weeks ahead for all subjects by the week after. 

Continue with spaced interleaved retrieval and prioritise gap-finding and perfecting your knowledge mastery for the remaining time.

**Tips:**

Because your speed of self-study should be much higher than the curriculum pace, you should be able to complete the entire next topic for a subject in approximately one day or even less.

Don’t worry about getting 100% coverage. Instead, aim for 90% coverage to 90% retention. You have plenty of time to cover the gaps later. 

Attending classes or lectures after you have already self-studied the material is useful as a recheck and easy multi-order retrieval session.

**3. ****Fixed mindset vs growth mindset**

**Nurturing growth**

Those with a fixed mindset see their ability level and intelligence as fixed and unchangeable. To overcome a fixed mindset, we must:

**Take action** - one of the easiest ways to change our thoughts, feelings, and beliefs, is through action. it is a misconception that we have to *feel* like taking action first. We can take action despite not feeling motivated to.

**Reflect effectively** - use Kolb's to turn mistakes into learning.

**Focus on the chain performance **(see below)

**Chain of performance**

One useful model to help with your marginal gains is to identify what determines a desirable outcome. Simply wanting an outcome does not create it. By analysing the chain of performance, we can understand which aspects hold us back the most. Our efforts can become more targeted and effective.

**What determines outcomes?**

Outcomes and performance (which come from...)

Skills and processes (which come from...)

Attributes and behaviours (which come from...)

Mindsets, beliefs, and identities.

Examples of skills and processes

Encoding and retrieval skills

Time management and prioritisation skills

Self-reflection and problem-solving skills

Focus and attention management skills

Emotional self-regulation skills

You can see how deficiencies in any of these skills could cause significant problems with outcomes and performance.

Examples of attributes and behaviours

Open-mindedness

Willingness to experiment

Willingness to face discomfort

Diligence and commitment

Patience

Humility

Self-compassion

When these attributes (or the behaviours naturally associated with these attributes) are absent, it can cause significant challenges to developing skills. For example, imagine trying to develop the skills in this program without patience or open-mindedness!

Examples of mindsets, beliefs, and identities

Fear of failure

Beliefs about intelligence and abilities

Beliefs about talent and capabilities

Mindsets, beliefs, and identities influence our attributes and behaviours.

**Neuroticism: prevention**

**Principles of prevention**

Trigger avoidance can be an effective way to reduce overwhelming neuroticism. To prevent the triggers of neuroticism, there are three aspects we need to address: awareness, attention and analysis. 

**Awareness**

In our busy lives, we often ignore our thoughts and ‘force through’ them by immersing ourselves in more work, responsibilities, and expectations. If these thoughts are not processed healthily, everyday life becomes stressful, with the outcomes of every action feeling like it could be potentially life-ending. This is a psychological phenomenon known as *catastrophising*.

Step 1: Identifying distortions

Cognitive distortions are mental biases and skewed thoughts that increase anxiety and make us feel bad about ourselves. They are common in highly neurotic individuals and may have acted as a cautionary mechanism for humans in our ancient past. Awareness involves identifying distortions and how they make us feel. Techniques like Kolb's naturally improves emotional self-awareness and root cause analysis.

**Attention, analysis and acceptance**

Attention involves diving deeper into those emotions and causes as we feel them. Analysis involves objectively dissecting the emotional process, including cues, behaviours, and patterns. This gives us more control over the emotions and their effects on us, rather than letting them affect us without restraint.

We often think that emotions are outside our control or influence. But by being attentive to the paths and ‘life cycles’ of our emotions, we can think about them differently and even influence how they affect us.

**Example**

If we pay attention to where our anxiety comes from, we may be able to express gratitude for it since it means our brain is trying to protect us from danger. As a result, our anxiety can impact us less negatively, breaking the habit-forming cycles that make anxiety dangerous.

**Avoidance behaviours**

While avoiding triggers can be effective, there are many situations where trigger avoidance is impossible. When we develop habits of avoiding discomfort and emotional triggers, we can enter into something called *avoidance behaviours*, which can make anxiety worse.

With avoidance behaviours, we become consumed with avoiding triggers, leading to a restricted and hypervigilant lifestyle in constant fear of triggers. The right balance is to avoid triggers that lead to emotional overwhelm but learn to manage our exposure to unavoidable triggers for a free and healthy lifestyle.

**Neuroticism: planning**

**Proper planning**

Welcome back to marginal gains!

You should break down your final goal (i.e., your desired outcome) into smaller, performance-based goals. This will help you focus on the* process* rather than just the results. Remember that outcomes also depend on factors outside your control.

**Break down your goals**

Use the following strategies to help reduce overwhelm

Use reverse goal-setting to find smaller, performance goals

Focus on the relationship between outcome, skills, attributes, and mindsets

Create plans on how you will address the limitations in skills/processes, attributes, or mindsets

Expect difficulty - it's much easier to persist through difficulty when we expect and accept it

**About reverse goal setting**

Reverse goal setting is any instance where the desired outcome is dissected into its determinants and then traced back to our current position. The example in this video is one way we can apply reverse goal setting. A slightly different method incorporating force-field analysis may also be appropriate for especially complex goals.

Learn about an alternative method with force-field analysis on the YouTube video .

**How to perform reverse goal setting**

Identify the desired end outcome

Define the skills, attributes, and mindsets necessary to achieve this with maximal certainty

(Optional) Rate the level we need to be at each out of 10

Rate our current level for each of these out of 10

Create a plan to improve the skills and attributes necessary within the desired deadline (see additional notes below)

**Creating a plan**

We can create plans with a *top-down* vs. *bottom-up* approach. Expand the accordions below to learn about each approach.

Top-down

Top-down means we start at the end and work backwards. The example given in the video uses a top-down approach. This approach is best for when:

It is easier to deconstruct our goals from the end result (often the case with complex goals that involve several components with a variety of timings)

It is difficult to assess our current skill and attribute level, or we have not begun to develop them

We can assess the feasibility of the plan by seeing if our *true* current state is aligned with the *supposed* current state we arrive at by tracing back from the desired goal towards the present day. If (by working backwards), we find that we currently need to be in a better state than we are, the plan is not feasible and must be reconsidered.

Bottom-up

Bottom-up means we start at the current state and work forwards. The example in the YouTube video uses a bottom-up approach. This approach is best for when:

Our current skills and attribute level is clearer

It is unclear what is needed towards the later stages of achieving the goal

There is a more direct relationship between skills and attributes and the goal

We assess the feasibility of the plan by seeing if we can reach the goal within the deadline based on the step-wise progression of skill and attribute development. If (based on our anticipated pace of development) we are unable to reach the desired level of skill to reach our goals in the time available, the plan is not feasible and must be reconsidered.

**Bottom-up approaches are still reverse goal setting if we identify the required skills and attributes based on analysing our desired goal.**

**Use both**

In many cases, we may want to use both a top-down *and* bottom-up analysis to assess the viability of our plan and see if we have missed anything.

**Neuroticism: measurement**

**Measuring the process**

When planning the process, you need to include metrics. When will you see development, and how much? 

With metrics, you’ll know you are doing things correctly because the numbers will show if you are achieving the right processes, learning the right skills and keeping a productive mindset.

**Feedback Analysis Loop**

The **Feedback Analysis Loop (FAL)** compares your expectations with what happened in reality. 

If your expectations *match* reality, you know there’s no problem with your processes. 

If your expectations *don’t match* reality, you need to adjust your processes as they are unlikely to help you learn the skills and reach your goals.

The FAL is another framework we can apply to Kolb’s experiential learning cycle. Using FALs gives us concrete, manageable information, making reflecting and finding abstractions easier.

**Note**

FALs can be modified for any need (e.g., studying, sports, personal development). You can add extra steps or criteria depending on what you want to measure.

The only components that need to stay are:

expectations,

reality, and 

a comparison between the two. 

If your FAL has these three elements, you can modify it however suits you!

**Neuroticism: management**

**When push comes to shove**

From the lessons so far, we have learned steps to prevent neuroticism from overwhelming us.

These steps may initially feel unfamiliar or artificial, but they give you more freedom and time as they release you from the shackles of inefficiency and lack of control. It just takes a bit of effort to free yourself!

*Managing*** **neuroticism is about training our brains to not fixate on some thoughts and emotions. This helps us become more focused, less distracted, and more emotionally stable.

When we struggle to let go of thoughts and feelings, it consumes our attention for a long time.

We can achieve this using *focus training*.

**Focus training**

Emotional self-regulation and remaining focused is less about *forcing *our brain to focus and much more about *preventing* our brains from being hijacked by thoughts and emotions. Focus training helps improve your brain's ability to let go of thoughts and feelings and return to a stable baseline.

Focus training helps us to "let go" and remain more stable.

**In this way, focus can be considered a cognitive skill.**

Training focus is conceptually simple:

Put ourselves in a situation where our attention drifts (usually when there are no distractions - this is also why our mind wanders before we go to sleep).

Practice letting go and bringing our attention back.

We can use the steps below to train ourselves.

**Warning**

If you have past trauma, focus training (which is a form of *mindfulness meditation*) can resurface traumatic memories. If you are aware of having past trauma, we recommend engaging in this activity with the guidance of a therapist or psychologist. Most trained therapists will be experienced in guiding clients on this technique.

**Focus training instructions**

Sit somewhere comfortable away from distractions. Close your eyes.

Take slow, deep breaths in and out.

Focus on the sensation of each breath in and out. Be attentive to the air flow, how your body moves, and any other sensation associated with breathing.

After a few seconds, you should start having other thoughts and your mind will wander. Each time your mind wanders, acknowledge the thought and let go of it without pursuing or fixating on it. Bring your attention gently back to your breathing.

Repeat this process for 15 to 20 minutes (it's hard!)

You train your focus and self-regulation ability every time you let go and bring your attention back. It's normal for your mind to wander every three or four seconds. It can be frustrating at first, but remember that the point is to experience this and practice letting go and settling back into your breathing.

Over time, you will find this easier, and the time between wandering thoughts will grow longer. Even after years of practice, having no more than 10 seconds of "mental silence" is normal. However, this represents a tripling of focus ability!

**Tip**

Do not deliberately try to *stop* having distracting thoughts. Let those thoughts do what they will, like clouds floating in the sky or leaves floating down a stream. Observe them and let them pass without holding onto them, reaching out for them, or giving them your attention.

**Therapeutic dose**

The "dose" of focus training to make significant improvements is generally considered 20 minutes daily for 30 days. In our experience, effects are seen with just 10 minutes daily. Consistency is key. 

**We recommend anchoring this in your routine as a new habit to form.**

**V. ****Neuroticism: management – chain analysis**

**Chain analysis**

*Chain analysis* involves finding the causes of your responses and emotions. Clinical psychologists use this technique to help with neuroticism, and it is a versatile life-skill to gain mastery of. There are many ways to perform a chain analysis. One simple and effective version that psychologists may employ is outlined below.

1. Target behaviour

**What is the specific behaviour that you want to change?**

It could be a bad habit like procrastinating, over-eating, spending too much time on social media, binge-watching Netflix, or even emotional behaviours you use to cope, such as getting angry at someone and yelling at them

2. Prompting event

**What was the event that sparked the chain leading to the target behaviour?**

Be as specific as possible.

3. Vulnerability factors

What made you vulnerable to the prompting event?

This could be your state of mind, mood, preceding events, or even your habits that caused the event to affect you more than it might have for others or if it had happened on another day.

4. Links

**What happened between the prompting event and the target behaviour? These are the links in the chain.**

What happened? How did you behave? How did you react?

How did you feel at each point? How did your feelings evolve throughout the links?

What thoughts did you have after the prompting event and before the target behaviour?

Be as specific as possible.

5. Consequences

**What were the short-term and long-term consequences of your behaviour?**

6. Solutions

**What potential solutions can you use to change your behaviour in the future?**

**Exposure:** How can you limit your exposure to the prompting event or similar events? If this isn’t feasible, how can you limit your exposure to vulnerability factors?

**Skills:** What skills can you develop or use to mitigate your vulnerability factors, reduce your exposure, modify and influence the events, behaviours, emotions and thoughts, or manage the consequences?

**Cognitive modifications*: **How can you change your mindset or perspective about an event so that it has less of an impact on you emotionally and mentally? How will you remind yourself about this next time a similar trigger arises?

**Contingency management: **We can’t always get things perfect. If all the previous steps fail, what can we do to reduce the short-term and long-term consequences? How can we help ourselves be more successful at reducing the previous steps in the future? Perhaps we need stronger reminders or a more specific or clearer structure and system.

***About cognitive modifications**

Even if you don’t have the skills to manage and deal with problems directly, you can drastically reduce the influence and impact it has on you by changing how you perceive it. 

**For example**, anxiety often becomes worse when you are aware of and actively focusing on not being anxious. This is an anxiety spiral and can lead to panic attacks. A cognitive modification such as acknowledging your anxiety, appreciating your brain’s reaction to try and protect you from danger, but reassuring it that there is no real danger to be afraid of, is a successful technique for managing anxiety spirals. 

Cognitive modifications can be simple changes in mindset or perspective that you try to remind yourself of in the future. Or they can be more specific frameworks and techniques you learn from trustworthy sources.

**Anatomy of a habit**

**Cue and response**

**Optimising cue and response**

Identify the cues that trigger the response (i.e., the good or bad habit).

Get rid of bad cues that cause you to respond undesirably.

Adopt and integrate good cues that facilitate you to respond desirably.

**For example**, a bad cue might be **seeing a social media icon** when you open your browser or turn on your phone. Remove this cue by deleting the icon and browsing in incognito mode when you need to focus. Another bad cue might be **seeing your phone**, which you can remove by putting it at the bottom of your drawer or giving it to someone else.

A good cue for studying might be **seeing your books open** on the right page, which you can arrange the night before. A good cue for exercise might be **seeing your running shoes**, which you can arrange by hanging them off your doorknob.

**PEER ****peer**** system – prep**

**P for Prep**

We use the anatomy of a habit to create a robust system for conquering neuroticism: the **PEER-Peer system**. This involves planting *good* cues that help you study and removing *bad* cues that make you procrastinate. Make sure the bad cues are removed to the point that you would be too lazy to procrastinate!

**If at first you don't succeed...**

According to *nudge theory *and the broader field of behavioural science, changing your environmental cues is one of the most powerful influencers of behaviour. If your first cue optimisation is unsuccessful at creating the desired behaviour, optimise and optimise again!

**It is rare for cue optimisation to be ineffective when repeatedly optimised.**

Nudge theory: Environment is more important than willpower

Lots of research shows that the environment influences our behaviour more than willpower. This is called **nudge theory** – a branch of behavioural analysis science that explores how modifying our environment nudges us to engage in good or bad default behaviours**.**

Successful, productive people use , while people who constantly burn out or rely on motivation only use willpower.

Read  by James Clear, the author of Atomic Habits, on how we can optimise our environment for decision-making.

**PEER p****eer**** system – easy, exit, reward and peer**

**Easy**

We make things ‘easy’ by establishing **minimum viable goals (MVG)**, which we’ve discussed before.

**Tip**

Students often make their MVGs too difficult! If procrastination is a problem, even standing up or sitting at your desk can be considered a good MVG.

**Exit**

‘Exit’ means that at each MVG, you tell your brain you can quit anytime. This keeps your brain focused on the MVGs so that the final goal won’t scare you away.

**Reward**

Set a reward for whenever you attain a goal. Make sure your reward requires a minimum amount of work; try to make this time as long as possible. The closer you can get to your final goal before rewarding yourself, the better. It’s up to you to decide what is ‘good enough’ for a reward.

**Tip**

Do not let the reward tempt you prematurely. Consider physically locking away the reward, perhaps with the help of others. Often, people rely on willpower alone to stay away from their reward. We can see how this is prone to failure.

**Peer**

**Peer** is the last step. It creates another level of accountability using someone else (e.g., family, friends, or new friends from the iCanStudy community). *We covered this when discussing the burnt ships strategy.*

Once the behaviours have become habits, this step will no longer be necessary. The best thing about this system is that you will need to use it less and less as the behaviours all become fully automatic.

Summary checklist

Prep

Identify cues that trigger unwanted behaviours such as procrastination. Aim to identify at least three or more.

Remove and lock away these cues however possible. Consider using site-blocking apps like SiteBlock. We want these “bad cues” to become INVISIBLE in our daily lives.

Identify cues that trigger desirable behaviours. Aim to identify at least three or more. 

Plant these cues so that they are **unavoidable** in your daily life. We want these cues to almost intrude into our vision or path. Like a boulder rolling downhill to roll to the bottom inevitably, our environment should be crafted so that we *inevitably* roll towards our desired behaviour or action.

Easy

Write your final desired goal (for example, studying for 3 hours). 

Write down all the steps required to get there. Make the first few steps as easy as possible to make it ridiculously easy to transition into your behaviour.

Conversely, we want to make our undesirable behaviour close to impossible. **Try to write down the steps required to start your undesirable behaviour to see how easy it might be!**

**For example**, procrastinating on social media might be as simple as taking out your phone and opening the app or tapping the button if you’re already on your phone. We can see how much more willpower it takes to do one type of behaviour (beneficial or productive) than another (harmful or time-wasting). This is partly because companies invest millions to make it as easy for us to get distracted as possible! They have crafted environments for us so we must take back control.

Exit

Remind yourself that you can quit after every MVG. The only decision you need to make is whether you do the next MVG or quit.

Reward

Identify two or three actions, behaviours, or activities you enjoy. Choose things that you can access and do readily. Consider how addictive your reward will be and if that affects your next scheduled task or event. 

For example, if you have no other focus blocks planned after your reward break, it doesn't matter so much. Remember that a sleep and wind-down routine is also a scheduled task! 

Make this reward inaccessible without doing the desired behaviour. 

For example, blocking Netflix outside of specific hours of the day or using an accountability partner (e.g. a parent) to show that you’ve completed something before getting them to unlock apps or websites.

You can use incognito mode to stop getting personalised recommendations and subscriptions on YouTube that make it too easy for you to get side-tracked. 

You can also block specific words from appearing in the URL instead of blocking entire websites. For example, if you need YouTube for studying, use incognito to prevent easy access to your recommended content and subscriptions. 

This forces you to search for videos manually. When you search for something on Youtube, the search term appears in the website URL; therefore, if you block those search words in your site blocker, your site-blocking app will allow you to search for “good” content while blocking the site every time the distracting search term pops up in the URL!

**You can also enable these in the parental control options in most router settings.**

Peer

Find one or two accountability partners. Find a partner in the iCanStudy community if you need people on the same wavelength as you!

Establish and agree to consequences. 

Ensure the consequences cause you to feel at least a moderate sense of dread and fear. If the consequence isn’t significant, your laziness or procrastination will win.

Write down your agreed consequence to make it tangible and real. This symbolises that you are taking it seriously.

**Tips**

Find the balance. If the consequence is too extreme, you will likely lie to avoid the punishment. Find a consequence that is significant and undesirable yet not so extreme that you would avoid it at all costs. For example, giving someone $50 might be appropriate, while giving someone $500 is too extreme. **Priority myths**

**Myths**

Myth 1: Working faster makes you more productive.

Your brain doesn’t work this way. You are not a factory – working faster will not necessarily produce more output. Instead, when you try to perform *faster*, the quality of your work decreases, and errors increase.

Of course, making errors due to poor understanding or retention is an unavoidable part of the learning process. However, we should still aim to reduce *unnecessary or unproductive* errors to avoid becoming inefficient.

Myth 2: Trying harder makes you more productive.

Often, people try so hard that they don’t have the mental space, time or perspective to see if their actions are correct. They become so consumed with trying hard that they don’t realise their efforts are not taking them towards their goals.

A great example is studying. Most students are so consumed with studying harder that they don’t even realise how bad their technique is. Even if they realise, they do not have time to learn to work smarter! Furthermore, activities that seem unproductive may improve productivity, like relaxing to maintain mental health.

**Key principle**

Hard work can prevent smart work. Working smarter requires time and mental freedom. If a standard of performance is only possible with high levels of effort, the only option is to take a short-term loss.

**Using Priority 0+1**

**Instructions**

Look at your goals and identify your top priority. You can use reverse goal setting principles to identify this.

Block out time to accomplish your top priority.

Fill the rest of your timetable according to your lower priorities.

In the case of unforeseen events, shuffle lower-priority items to protect the highest priority tasks.

We recommend prioritising and scheduling your tasks **the day before**. On the day, keep a list of tasks that pop up and throw them into the Eisenhower matrix to prioritise and schedule for the next day.

Key principles for prioritisation

Decide if tasks are important or not based on the consequence of failure; not an intuitive feeling of importance which is often based on urgency.

Tasks with a low consequence of failure (that are acceptable and manageable) are not important, even if it "feels" important.

Avoid scheduling urgent tasks first as this can lead to urgency trapping where time is constantly spent on urgent tasks and your future self is never more equipped to handle them or remove workload at the root cause.

**Priority 0**

Priority 0 refers to your **values and goals**. We recommend stepping back and reassessing these **once every 6 months**. Regularly reassessing your Priority 0 is important because it ensures that your Priority 1 tasks are aligned with your goals. It doesn’t take long and will save you a lot of time by keeping you on the right track.

**Common questions**

What are some examples of urgency trapping?

A common example of urgency trapping among students is filling all their time with urgent homework and never having time to work on study skills. If academic challenges increase faster than your skills, the gap between skills and challenges widens. This means students falling into this trap can eventually fail to reach academic goals as by the time it's felt as urgent, it is already too late.

This doesn’t mean you must schedule urgent tasks later in the day. It simply means you should schedule important and non-urgent tasks into your day *first* and fit in the urgent tasks around them.

**For example,** you could schedule 20 minutes every evening for meditation, which is important but non-urgent. You can then fill the afternoon with other tasks. This way, your urgent tasks will not preclude your important task (meditation).

How do you identify Priority 0?

Priority 0 comes to core values and long-term vision. Values are your fundamental beliefs that dictate how you behave and act. Long-term visions are open and abstract ideas of the type of person we want to be and the type of life we want to live.

What is the difference between a long-term vision and a long-term goal?

Your long-term vision is *not* a SMART goal. Setting goals that are too long-term can create tunnel vision. Over time, our personality and priorities are guaranteed to change. While some things remain with you for your entire life, it’s naïve to believe that we know enough about ourselves now to confidently think that it will not change for the next 10 years. 

This is especially true before the age of 30 in modern society when you are transitioning into adulthood and experiencing many things for the first time. These new experiences are some of the most transformative moments that shape who you will be for the rest of your life. Instead of setting “goals” that are very specific, we can instead set visions. 

Visions are a more open and abstract idea of the type of person we want to be and the type of life we want to live. This ensures that we have a broad checklist that we can use to calibrate our medium-term goals, making sure we are always heading in the right direction but not being so narrow that we close off other opportunities.

**For example:**

A long-term vision might be to have financial freedom, working at an intellectually stimulating job but with enough free time to explore multiple hobbies and interests. We want to be the type of person who is patient, calm, confident and capable of adapting and overcoming challenges enjoyably and easily. 

This means that medium-term goals that do not take us closer to this long-term vision are unlikely to be good decisions. For example, getting tutoring to help us get better grades does not give us the skills to overcome challenges independently, nor does it increase our time management to give us the skills to have enough free time for hobbies and interests.

How often does Priority 0 change?

**Always evaluate your long-term vision open-mindedly. It can be tempting and comfortable to believe that our long-term vision never changes, but this is unlikely. **

We are a living, dynamic organism that constantly takes in information about the world and ourselves. We are always learning more about our thoughts, emotions, values, and experiences, shaping our personality and priorities. It is normal for your long-term vision to change, often in unexpected and sometimes radically different ways. Instead of going into this exercise assuming that your thoughts are unchanged, deliberately look for reasons, thoughts or emotions that indicate that what you want long-term *has* changed. 

This helps us overcome the uncontrollable biases that can blind us from seeing our thoughts objectively. You can read more about cognitive biases . You can evaluate your own cognitive biases .

**We recommend reviewing your Priority 0 every 6 months.**

How often does Priority 1 change?

This priority 1 step should be repeated once every day or week, depending on how variable your schedule is. Busier people with more things going on in their lives will need to do this daily.

How can I be confident to delete tasks?

If the task is truly important, it will return to your list. Unless you have ample free time and a willingness to spend it on these tasks instead of relaxation, sleep, or more non-urgent, important tasks, it is not advisable to spend time on them.

**7.****OFF-rest timing**

OFF-rest timing

**Limitations of the Pomodoro technique**

The Pomodoro technique is widely known and adopted but has disadvantages. After all, it did originate from a literal tomato cooking timer, not research!

The origin of the Pomodoro technique!

Unoptimised focus time

The first disadvantage is that your brain’s ability to focus will **vary depending on the situation**. If you’re focusing well (often called being in *flow*), you don’t want to cut off precious focus time prematurely, nor do you want to extend unfocused time if you can’t stay focused for the full 25 minutes.

Unoptimised rest time

The second disadvantage is that it doesn’t explain how to use the “rest” time. Having only five minutes to rest may be insufficient.

**OFF-rest timing**

Many people do not find the traditional Pomodoro technique sustainable and effective in the long term. As a result, most experienced and productive people do not use it at all. Instead, it is better to personalise the Pomodoro technique and optimise it for yourself. One variant that is commonly recommended instead is the Flowmodoro technique, which more closely resembles our guidelines and variation.

Ultimately, the important thing is that your method works for you. In our experience, OFF-rest timing is the most flexible and adaptable variant we have encountered, with compatibility in diverse settings.

OFF-rest timing is short for **Optimised-For-Focus rest timing**. It aims to keep your efficiency and focus high for as many cycles** **as possible throughout the day.

**Using OFF-Rest timing**

**Use OFF-rest timing for extended work periods where you will have multiple cycles of work and rest.**

The type of rest you take depends on the circumstances, but you still want to be as productive as possible during your breaks. 

**Analogy**

Imagine moving a heavy boulder. The hardest part is getting it rolling in the first place. It takes very little energy and effort to keep it moving if you keep pushing it a little bit whenever it slows down. But if you wait until the boulder has completely stopped before pushing again, you must spend much more energy to get it back in motion.

**Determining the type of break**

If you are relatively fresh, take a topical or miscellaneous break.

If you are low on energy, take an active relaxation break (we suggest doing focus training or mindful meditation).

Regardless, use a timer to measure how long you stay focused, and ensure that your rest time is one-third of your focused time.

**Not all rest is equal**

You should rest** ***as soon as* your brain is starting to become dull. This way, you can refocus and feel restored much more quickly. If we wait until we are noticeably tired before resting, refocusing and restoring our energy will take much longer, making us less productive overall.
