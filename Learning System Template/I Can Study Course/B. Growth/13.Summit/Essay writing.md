# Essay writing
## Components of writing an essay

Writing an essay (or any high-level writing, such as a literature review) requires you to construct a clear and logical flow of ideas. One of the most common problems with learners at all levels is that ideas are not communicated in an order and flow that is easy to understand. New perspectives and concepts are introduced randomly, conclusions are not well-justified, points lack examples and are unclear, or the overall progression of ideas is hard to follow without deep concentration.

Fundamental principles of good writing

- Language is appropriate for the expected standard, but simple and concise.
- Arguments and perspectives are represented logically.
- Ideas develop in a way that is easy to follow.

### Tip

Most people spend far too little time planning their structure and flow. This leads to wasted time trying to figure out the flow while writing. Plan the flow first, then elaborate on each point to develop your writing. It's the same as creating a clear logic layer before entering the more detailed layers. 

**Place more emphasis on crafting the logical flow so it is as simple and understandable as possible. This is one of the biggest separators of good writing vs. mediocre writing.**

Methods such as BHS can help you map your ideas and identify the most important concepts and chunks. This can help you to structure your writing and linearise it similarly to performing WPW.

The remaining work concerns gaining procedural skills and practising writing with good technique. Most people struggle with both the declarative flow and the procedural skills. This is why good writing skills are rare.

## Structuring essays: ReCOVer System

The ReCOVer system is one method for helping to structure your essay writing during assessments and examinations.
### Recall

After applying the BHS, we should have a deep and heavily connected knowledge of the topic. In this step, we will recall all the relevant points concerning our essay question stem.

Craft a flow of main points and chunks, precisely as you would a logic and concepts layer, but use the question stem or essay prompt as your guideline for what kind of chunk structure is most relevant.

_During exams, you should spend up to 10 minutes on this step._

**Difficulties with this step likely indicate a lack of higher-order learning of the topic during preparation.**

### Chunk

Once we have our draft flow, remove any weaker or less relevant points for supporting the main argument. Consider whether the flow of ideas is indeed the most easily understandable. This step is akin to Skinning your map.

We want to end up with a clear logic layer with:

1. A one-directional flow and progression of ideas (this helps to linearise it in the essay)
2. Supporting concepts and arguments that clearly support each chunk of the logic layer
3. A total of three to four main chunks (to keep the argument concise for most essays - this may change depending on your level of writing)

_In an exam, spend up to 5 minutes at this step._

### Order

In this step, we will clearly linearise our structure.

Arrange your biggest chunks and major sub-chunks (if any) into a linear order. An example is given below:

- Introduction
    - Main point 1
    - Main point 2
    - Main point 3
- Main point 1
    - Supporting concept 1
    - Supporting concept 2
    - Bridging concept 3
- Main point 2
    - Supporting concept 1
    - Supporting concept 2
- Main point 3
    - etc.

This is the order in which you will write your essay, starting with an introduction that cleanly covers the main ideas and how you will flow between them. Each chunk is unpacked with supporting evidence and statements.

**The table below gives an indication of how the number of chunks and complexity of topics translates into essay length.**

|**Level**|**Chunks**|**Sub-Chunks**|**Points Per Chunk**|**Total Discussion Points**|**Essay Length**|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|High school|3 to 5|0 or 1|2 to 4|12 to 20|Approx. 2000 words|
|Early university|5 to 7|1 or 2|3 to 5|20 to 40|Approx. 3000 words|
|Late university|5 to 7|1 to 3|4 to 7|40 to 70|Approx. 10,000 words|
|Academic publication or thesis|5 to 10|2 to 4|5 to 10|50 to 100|Approx. 30,000 to 100,000 words+|

If you feel like your organisation of chunks is messy, it is unlikely that your essay will be good. Furthermore, you are likely to waste significant time during the writing process trying to figure out how to structure your points and references mid-sentence.

**If even the plan looks bad, it’s unlikely that the execution will be better. If it is, then it would undoubtedly be slower and less accurate.**

_In an exam, spend up to 5 minutes at this step._

### Verbalise

The final step is to expand your skeleton outline into sentences and paragraphs. Depending on your technical writing skill and mastery of the language, this should be the easiest step since you already know what you want to say and how all the supporting statements and evidence fit into the bigger picture.

If you have many points, you should create bullet points in the same order and then turn the bullet points into sentences. You will be unlikely to have time to do this in an exam, but exam essays tend to be much shorter with fewer points, so it is also less likely that you will need this extra step.

_In an exam, your remaining time is spent at this step, excluding time for review._

**If you struggle with this step, you may have a lower level of language mastery (which requires more practice and interleaving to reach the desired level of language fluency).**




|**Problem felt**|**Likely cause(s)**|
|---|---|
|Lacking evidence to support points or feeling the need to waffle due to inadequate meaningful statements.|Recall step inadequate or learned too superficially initially.|
|Unsure how to use points you’ve recalled to support major themes or conclusions.|Inadequate chunking or lack of relationship-based learning initially.|
|Unsure how to navigate from point to point, or feeling like you don’t know where you are taking the essay direction.|Inadequate ordering of chunks or sub-chunks. Inadequate ordering can also happen due to inadequate chunking.|
|Difficulty with making the sentences flow together in a pleasing and natural way while **not** having any of the above issues.|Inadequate language mastery.|
