# Multipass system
## About the multipass system

The multipass system uses the Swiss-cheese model that was introduced to you earlier. The different passes are like the different layers in the Swiss-cheese model and enable you to cover almost all the material in just a few days.

### What is the relationship between the BHS and multipass?

BHS is a foundational system of learning that gives you the core skills and steps for learning any new information. It gives you the methods of thinking and processing information to encode knowledge efficiently.

The multipass system is a strategy for implementing your BHS in different ways. It gives additional guidance on _when_ to learn and a high-level direction for your _order control_.

Each learning session still uses the steps from BHS. Multipass dictates what you will learn in each session and how to divide the topic into manageable and efficient learning blocks.

This is especially useful for large, dense topics that will inevitably take multiple 'passes' to learn. Multipass gives a clear framework for how this topic can be divided into individual passes. Each pass involves the utilisation of the BHS methods.

_There are other systems for utilising the core skills you learned from the BHS in the supplementary learning modules available from your toolkit._


## Performing multipass

There are two ways we can perform the multipass system.

### Multipass methods

1. Inquiry-led: Order control is dictated by what feels intuitively relevant based on our inquiry process and aim-shoot cycling.
2. Objectives-led: Order control is dictated by learning objectives.


||Inquiry-led|Objectives-led|
|---|---|---|
|**Benefits**|Feels natural and engaging. An extension of the conventional BHS, divided into multiple sessions, each covering a variable number of layers of learning.|Learning is always directly relevant to most assessable material. Time spent has a high likelihood of translating to performance results.|
|**Disadvantages**|Can be less strategic when under significant time pressures.|Sacrifices cognitively optimal encoding for performance strategy, which can reduce retention and ability to respond to the most higher-order or unanticipated 'curveball' questions.|
|**Use cases**|- Rapid learning for high volumes under more flexible time restrictions.<br>- When the material to be learned needs to be fundamentally strong for future learning and application.|- Imminent time pressures requiring high volumes of learning with minimal study time.<br>- Topics with limited long-term relevance.|

Note that both approaches focus on the logic and concepts layers of learning first. This is a key difference compared to conventional cramming which starts with detail layers with fluctuating relevance. As a result, there is much more time required to be performance-ready.

## The strategic benefit of multipass

Regardless of the approach, using the multipass system helps prepare you for assessments better as it emphasises developing the logic and concepts layers first. As most questions are derived from logic and concepts, it is much easier to logically extrapolate and answer a wide range of questions.

### Interleaving and lower-order performance

If there is time, adding some interleaving strategy (preferably a HVRP method such as WPW) is also recommended. You may also need to simultaneously supplement with a lower-order memorisation strategy like flashcards or Mmol.
