# Sleep and diet
## Diet and sleep

Diet and (especially) sleep are two of the most critical factors for your cognitive performance (and health).

### Sleep

You must protect your sleep! We have covered how important this is in the past. Sleep deprivation is always a losing battle when we need peak cognitive performance.

### Diet

Diet is about what to eat and when to eat. The food you eat is not going to turn you into a genius, but [research](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn2421) shows that it helps your brain operate with better ‘mental fitness’. Interestingly, _when_ you eat can have a bigger impact on assessment performance. 

This is because immediately following a large meal, we typically become drowsy (especially if you are also sleep deprived)*. This is called **postprandial somnolence** and is the “rest and digest” response, which is opposite to the “fight and flight” response. You will feel less energetic and more drowsy after big meals. Therefore, avoid eating large meals within two hours before an assessment. 

#### *Research note

More recent research (Bazar et al., 2004) into post-prandial somnolence indicates that it is not a redistribution of blood flow, but rather a change in neurohormonal concentrations that create the effect. The blood-flow hypothesis is still taught in many medical schools, as is the neurohormonal hypothesis. This latter hypothesis was **not** mentioned in the video.

Bazar, Kimberly A., A. Joon Yun, and Patrick Y. Lee. “Debunking a myth: neurohormonal and vagal modulation of sleep centers, not redistribution of blood flow, may account for postprandial somnolence.” Medical hypotheses 63.5 (2004): 778-782.
