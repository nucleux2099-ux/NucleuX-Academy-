**How to aim**

**Overview**

**Aiming** is about pointing your brain in the right direction. By asking the right questions, we encourage our brain to think in specific patterns and form specific relationships. 

Two questions that consistently create high-quality networks for any subject are:

**Why is this important?**

**How is this related to ___?**

**Purpose of this step**

To make new information relevant and immediately form networks

To activate higher-order learning as early as possible

To create groups (chunks) and relationships that are robust, versatile and suit our retrieval needs

**Common questions**

How does Aim fit with prestudying?

**Prestudy**** and Aim often overlap. However, they are not the same thing.**

The purpose of prestudying is to prime our brain to create relationships before an intensive main learning event. We explore the purpose of prestudying in this article .

The Bear Hunter System is a system of learning, whereas prestudy is a chronological step in the learning journey. The Aim step should be one of (if not the very first) step in the learning journey.

If this happens *before* an intensive main learning event, this can be considered prestudy. If there is no prestudy, Aim should still be the first step of the learning journey. As it is very difficult to manage this kind of thinking in an intensive lecture or class, Aim may have to occur after the main learning event during a self-study session instead. This is not optimal.

However, the amount you can cover in prestudy is a decision. You could do a very brief prestudy of just 5 or 10 minutes, in which time a full Aim may not be possible. In this case, you would do a very superficial Aim to develop only the largest chunks and major relationships.

Alternatively, it's possible to do a much more comprehensive prestudy. In this case, we could do a full Aim and even add more detail. Once you learn the Shoot step which comes after Aim, this could also be done during prestudy.

**In summary, ****prestudy**** is about ****when**** you start learning. The Bear Hunter System is ****how**** you learn.**

Once I learn Aim, should I stop doing the TLS red light?

Yes, the Aim completely replaces TLS red light. While red light creates some initial connections and relationships, Aim is a more focused and strategic use of questions to create only those relationships that are likely high-yield.

What is the difference between "Why is it important" and "How does it relate to ____"?

These two questions may seem conceptually identical. However, we have arrived at these two key questions after years of trial and observation.

**Why is it important?**

This question focuses our attention on the big picture. It creates a focus, purpose, and alignment between the ideas. It helps us push through to the *Evaluate* level of thinking where we make judgments and prioritisations of networks. Asking this question critically helps our chunks become more memorable and intuitive.

**How is it related to [another concept]?**

This question explores additional relationships that may not be obvious. When we explore why something is important, it is difficult to escape our existing biases and preconceptions about the information. Two concepts may initially seem unrelated, and only when forced to explore their relationship do we find new perspectives and potential chunks. Sometimes, this leads us to re-evaluate our entire chunk structure and reasons for importance.

**Aim – question quality**

**Types of questions**

‘What’ questions are usually too simple and unconnected to produce high retention. ‘**Why**’ and ‘**How**’ questions connect the information to other pieces of information and therefore are much more meaningful.

Questions that are more **emotional** or **personal** and that **spark curiosity** also connect much more. Since the objective is to enter a problem-solving mindset, do not ask questions about problems to which you already know the answer. If you are genuinely curious about the topic, you will be able to generate more knowledge.

**Note**

Asking the right questions may be challenging in the beginning. However, by generating more curiosity, you will also find that you start to enjoy studying more, making it much easier to learn and retain information.

**Inquiry clean-up**

In this lesson we will...

Stop asking "what" questions

Develop deeper and more refined chunk structures

Learn to actively manage cognitive load

**Quality, not quantity**

In the previous stages, we focused on asking a wide range of questions to learn how to invoke curiosity and see how the questions we ask influence the way we process information.

In Ascent I, we will start reducing the number of questions and focusing our efforts on just a few high-yield questions that produce consistent and predictable patterns of connections. By asking questions centred around evaluating and judging the *importance* of information, our chunks also reflect these judgments.

Therefore, we call this method **importance-based chunking**, which you will see and hear in our community and Live Clinics often.

**Alert**

Importance-based chunking is considered a core pillar of our program. It is a method of thinking that will influence every form of information processing from here onwards.

**Instructions for importance-based chunking**

Ask the question, "Why is this keyword or concept important?"

Look for the possible answers (there will usually be multiple reasons for something to be important).

Move on to the next keyword (in whatever order feels relevant to you) and repeat this process.

With each keyword, you will start seeing similarities in why some things are important. These become potential chunks.

Make a judgment on which relationships are important enough to keep and which similarities are important enough to choose to form chunks.

Continue to build your knowledge by constantly repeating this process for each keyword until you have covered all of the concepts.

While you are performing this, aim to process and consolidate each new keyword as you go. You want to use chunking and mapping to reduce cognitive load before introducing new ideas.

**How we manage cognitive load**

We strategically utilise our mental effort (increase cognitive load) to engage higher-order learning, and offload with chunking and non-linear note-taking to reduce cognitive load and prevent overload.

Although we are preventing overload, the optimum range of cognitive load can still feel uncomfortable for those with primarily lower-order learning habits.

**Inter-chunk relationships**

Once we have importance-based chunks, we can then ask how these chunks (or sub-chunks) are related to and affect each other. This gives us **inter-chunk relationships**. When our inter-chunk relationships are clean, this creates a simple, clean **backbone** to our learning, making the learning process much faster.

The ability to do importance-based chunking and create clean inter-chunk relationships is one of the main differences between beginner and intermediate learners!

At this stage, we also want to completely remove “what” questions (i.e. fact or recall-based questions). These questions only lead to lower-order learning, whereas questions that **force us to compare concepts against each other** (e.g. why X is important, how it relates to Y) also produce higher-order learning.

One amazing thing about our brain is that when we do higher-order learning, it will automatically complete lower-order learning, too. This means that “why” and “how it relates” questions can completely replace “what” questions. It might seem impossible or counterintuitive, but just give it a go!

**Importance gives our brain focus**

Understanding why something is important gives us an idea of how the information is **structured**. In other words, we know **how** to think about the subject, which is incredibly important and a defining feature of an intermediate learner.

When our brain doesn’t know **how** to think about the subject, it’s trying to solve lots of problems at the same time:

What does this mean?

Why do I need to know it?

How do I use this?

Is this relevant?

Do I need to memorise all the details?

How do I solve problems with it?

How does it fit in with everything else?

Since a lot is happening in your brain, you become less focused. This can cause you to spend more time and energy without learning much. 

When information is not well-organised, **retention is low**.

**The magic of structure**

The convenient thing about learning **how** to think about something is that these general logical frameworks and patterns repeat.

**A repeating pattern**

As a young child, you might have thrown something on the floor. In doing so, you learned about a framework called “cause and effect”. You naturally interleaved this by throwing lots of things and hearing many noises.

Thus, the framework of evaluating how important something is through the lens of "cause and effect" is something that most people can understand easily as an adult.

Repeating frameworks and structures can be found in subject domains as well. You will discover many of them when asking why a concept is important. As you build experience, you will learn what questions and structures to ask to produce the most logical chunks.

Here are some common types of chunks you will find in different subjects. This list can help guide your thinking when considering why concepts from different subjects might be important. 

**Please note that this is not a comprehensive list. If you rely solely on this list to create importance-based chunks, you will encounter problems later. Instead, always start by asking yourself, “Why is it important?”.**

**Common patterns of importance**

Biology

In biology, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Function

Structure vs. function

Scale (microscopic to macroscopic)

A group of causes leading to a group of effects

**Example:** Instead of chunking organelles based on their location, try chunking them based on their function.

Chemistry

In chemistry, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Mechanisms of reaction

Real-world applications

Electronegativity

Common reagents or products

**Example:** Instead of writing an unordered list, try chunking reactions based on the mechanisms that cause them.

Physics

In physics, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Real-world applications

Conceptually important precursors to other concepts

**Example:** Instead of following the textbook, try chunking concepts and equations based on common applications.

Maths

In maths, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Real-world applications

Conceptually important precursors to other concepts

**Example:** Instead of following the textbook, try chunking groups of equations that produce a certain effect on a graph.

English

In English, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

The author/director/artist’s background, motivations or values

The story’s significance

**Example:** Instead of writing an unordered list, try chunking themes and techniques based on which of the author’s values they derive from.

History

In history, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Major cultural, political, religious, philosophical or social movements at the time

Cause and effect

Predominant emotions or motivations of key players

Subjects like history may have the most diverse range of possible importance-based chunking structures. Since history tracks a massive range of human events and activities, there is also a diverse range of reasons why a historical event may be important.

**Example:** Instead of chunking events based on geographical location, try chunking them based on their motivating forces.

Law/Legal Studies

In law, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Underlying social principles of justice

Pragmatic considerations and compensatory mechanisms

Landmark cases that exposed gaps in the system

Social, political or economic principles that must be protected

**Example:** Instead of listing cases and bills in isolation, try chunking them based on the underlying legal principles they relate to.

Languages

In languages, “importance” could mean chunking based on:

Common usage

Situational usage

How they affect messaging or communication

Languages are the hardest to chunk by importance because languages are not inherently logical. In this case, importance would relate to how you intend to use the language.

**Example:** Instead of writing an unordered list, try chunking words and phrases based on their emotions or use cases.

**Barriers to aiming**

**Common objections**

Using the BHS takes too much time.

This objection is understandable. The process of encoding the first time can take considerably longer when first learning the skill. Although this becomes faster and more efficient as your skill develops, the short-term consequence can still seem unacceptable.

However, when we start measuring the efficiency of our learning holistically, we find that although we can lose some time in the initial encoding, we more than make up for this even in the early stages of skill competence.

Read our article on how to accurately measure your learning efficiency .

It's too hard! It takes too much effort and it's too tiring.

Effective learning always takes effort. Unfortunately, many learners have been conditioned to believe otherwise. In reality, avoiding difficulty and discomfort during learning is only viable for easy content at moderately low volumes.

With practice, you will learn to associate higher cognitive loads with effective learning. Instead of desirable difficulty only feeling difficult, you will start to feel that it is also desirable!

In other words, as your skill improves, the cognitive load does not reduce, but you become better at managing it and will no longer be bothered by it. Rather, many learners start to become bothered with passive learning because they realise how ineffective it is!

The questions I am thinking of aren't the same as how I am assessed.

When encoding information, we should always prioritise what our brain finds relevant. This is the shortest path to expertise. Once we have developed expertise, we can practise retrieving it in the way we need to for assessments.

**This is much more efficient than limiting our encoding ability (and therefore forgetting more) by trying to match it artificially to assessments in the initial encoding phases.**

*An analogy for this is in constructing a network of roads in a city. Once the roads are constructed, you can use them in whatever way you want to get to wherever you need to!*

You may also be concerned that your questions and learning sit outside the scope of an assessment. However, it is more efficient to go slightly outside the scope of a curriculum to improve your depth and retention of information instead of staying within the scope but suffering from reduced retention or depth.

You can also use your own judgment alongside learning objectives (if available) to prevent you from wandering too far from the topic’s scope.

**Prechunking**

Prechunking is when we create some chunks *before* engaging in inquiry.

In many cases, this is not advisable as it can bias the chunks we form. However, this is not problematic if we make sure our chunk structures are only hypotheses during the entire chunking process.

*Problems arise when the **prechunks** do not serve as a springboard for thinking, but rather are assumed to be correct and learners are resistant or hesitant to modify it.*

General advice about prechunking

Prechunking is sometimes acceptable and saves time, but only if the chunks remain hypotheses and continue to be modified.

If there is any difficulty with avoiding chunking in simply the same ways information is presented (i.e. engaging order control), avoid prechunking.

If there are any issues with spiderwebbing or the Aim step feels ineffective, avoid prechunking.

In the demonstrations (both in the sublessons below as well as in Live Clinics) you will notice that sometimes prechunking is used and sometimes it is not.

**What matters is that chunks are formed independently and challenged with good inquiry.**

Deep dive: anatomy

*Feel free to skip this lesson if you are not learning anatomy.*

**Overview**

Many chunk structures can be used for learning anatomy. The goal is to reduce memorisation by creating chunk structures that offload the **most difficult aspects**. Avoid creating chunk structures that are so obvious they contribute nothing to your learning.

The following are potential chunk structures you can explore. In most cases, you will need a combination of these approaches, even within the same “region” of anatomy.

Anatomical division (e.g. anterior vs. posterior)

Functional division (e.g. flexion vs. contraction)

Embryological rationale (can be highly effective if you keep it simple)

Clinical application

A chunk such as “clinical application*”* can also be divided into multiple types of applications. Consider the differences between these clinical applications:

Surgical approaches and techniques

Forming differential diagnoses

Urgency of management

Mechanism of injury/disease

Management complications

If you have never considered clinical applications this way, this is a high-yield area for you to look into. Not only will this help with your immediate retention, but it is also a method of thinking you are expected to be competent in during your clinical years. 

**Unfortunately, many medical schools do not prioritise this.**

**Gaining fluency**

When chunking for anatomy, **fluency** is about identifying multiple possible chunk structures, then evaluating which approach feels most intuitive and reduces rote memorisation as much as possible.

**Important**

It is important to have these chunk structures in mind **as soon as you begin studying**. It would be inefficient to learn the information in isolation first and arrive at the structures later, since you are not giving the information relevance while you study it.

Framing bias can also make it difficult to consider alternative chunk structures. Even though information is usually retrieved in **multiple ways** during clinical practice, many students can only think of **one way** to chunk the information. This is a red flag indicating that the learner is heavily influenced by framing bias and how they “believe” the information needs to be structured.

Significant interleaving is essential in all learning, but especially anatomy. Make sure to test on alternative chunk structures and lower-order fact recall through spaced interleaved retrieval.

**Chunking for medicine: Framing bias**

Beyond medicine

Although this is a specific example of learning medicine, we recommend this sublesson for all learners. This sublesson discusses a common mistake called "framing bias", elaborated on below. Medical studies have been used to explain this concept because it is one of the strongest and most common ways that we see framing bias influence learning.

Framing bias

Framing bias is the cognitive bias that says that our conclusions and decisions are influenced by how the options are presented to us.

Framing bias in learning

In learning, framing bias commonly affects the chunks we form by making them similar to how information is chunked for us by teachers, resources, lectures, etc.

These chunks are often not the most cognitively optimal for our brain, which is why order control and independent chunking through the correct process of inquiry is crucial.

Find the way

Many learners conclude that there is no other way to chunk information because the way it is given to them seems logical and intuitive. However, there are often multiple ways to logically and intuitively chunk information while being cognitively more advantageous. Our job as learners is to find those ways, not accept that "there isn't any other way".

**Distractions**

Cognitive Switching Penalty

Modern research tells us that when we multitask, the quality of our focus is reduced, and we can only switch between two or three things rapidly. This is known as cognitive switching penalty.

This means that if we multitask while learning, the quality of our learning will decrease, and we will take longer to learn things because we are wasting time needing to refocus.

Key principle

Highly efficient people do not multitask. Instead, they concentrate on one task at a time to avoid the cognitive switching penalty. This is fundamental to developing deep focus.

**Maintaining focus**

**Increasing focus**

Here are four easy techniques to increase your focus. Regardless of your experience level, we recommend ticking these off as a first step.

**Techniques**

Intention Setting: Set specific, achievable goals you want to accomplish by the end of your study block. Make sure you physically write them down.

Ritualisation: Create a set routine to prepare yourself to focus. This routine will help your brain to transition into high-focus mode automatically.

Zone: Set up a study area that has zero distractions. Making sure you have no distractions while studying is extremely important for building focus.

Cheat Sheet: If anything distracts you while studying, write it down on a cheat sheet and remove that distraction from your study zone.

**Learning notes**

Be Like Water

Waterfalling is an issue we commonly see in learners who are strongly influenced by *framing bias*. It indicates a pattern of mapping where the connections are “top-down” with very little connectivity between ideas that exist on the same “layer” of the map hierarchy. Waterfalling can be avoided by frequently thinking about how concepts relate to each other, even after they have been added into chunks. It’s also important to consider how chunks are related to other chunks.

It can be tempting to simply confirm that the only relationships are those we have already committed and thought about. However, high-level learners take the time to consider the relationships they have *not* yet thought about, as these could influence their chunk structure and, therefore, the quality of their memory and understanding.

**Learning notes**

Antisocial Chunks

Lonely chunks refers to when chunks are created but contain no content. It is essentially a storage box with nothing inside it. This commonly occurs when topics are prechunked based on framing bias and not corrected by the inquiry process. If chunks are formed correctly based on identifying similarities between ideas and then looking for ways to group them, there should never be a chunk that is empty. This is because the chunk is defined by the potential contents in the first place.

Lonely chunks are acceptable when hypothesizing potential groups. However, this should be corrected while studying more as we realise this chunk is unnecessary. Keeping lonely chunks is detrimental to memory as it adds additional elements to our map and increases our cognitive load unnecessarily.
