## Learning notes

The question “why is _______ important?” is one of the most high-yield questions you can ask. It forces us to not only examine a fact or concept in relation to other facts and concepts, but we must also make a judgment on how important that relationship is.

This facilitates higher-order learning and thinking which improves retention and depth of understanding.

## Learning notes

When this question is answered by finding only a single relationship, the usefulness of the question diminishes significantly.

This is because the purpose of asking this question is to find multiple relationships and force ourselves to judge the importance of each relationship. The need to rationalise and justify our decision is what forces higher-order learning and the development of a robust knowledge network.

When learners answer a question with a single, simple answer, we call this problem “simple question and answer”. It is a common way that some learners avoid the discomfort of deeper processing by subtly modifying their inquiry-based learning technique.

## Learning notes

Asking about examples is usually not a sufficient replacement for asking more broadly about why something is important. Examples can be lower-order, which does not necessarily improve the relationships we form between other concepts.

Examples also may not help us to chunk information in the best way because it does not sufficiently help us find various similarities between concepts that could be used for grouping.

### Question 3.2

Your friend creates a series of Aim questions and wants your feedback. On the topic of metacognition, they have written:

- What are examples of metacognitive artifacts?
- What is social metacognition?
- What are examples of social metacognition?

You advise them to make more questions around why each concept is important. However, they reply that their final question which asks about examples of social metacognition already provide the reasons why it is important.

## Answer

Which of the following questions could also be used to build relationships other than “why is _________ important?”. Select all that apply.

How do metacognitive artifacts occur?

**Does social metacognition affect metacognitive artifacts?**

**What is the purpose of learning and knowing about social metacognition?**

**How are metacognitive artifacts different from social metacognition?**

---

## Learning notes

Asking questions that directly probe on relationships, interactions, dependencies and influences of one concept on another concept are effective ways of building relationships. This is weaker than a “why” question because the relationship that is built is only between two (or maybe three) concepts, whereas “why is it important?” questions tend to make us think about multiple concepts simultaneously, as a big picture.

“What is the purpose” questions are similar to “why is it important” questions that have been reworded.

Asking questions like “how does xyz occur?” is a question that asks about how a process occurs. This is the same as “what is the process?” which does not make us think about the information in context with other concepts. This can easily just lead to us memorising the process.
