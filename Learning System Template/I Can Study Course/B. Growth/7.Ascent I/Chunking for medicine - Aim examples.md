# Chunking for medicine - Aim examples
## Beyond medicine

Although this is a specific example of learning medicine, we recommend this sublesson for **all learners**. This sublesson discusses a common mistake called "framing bias", elaborated on below. Medical studies have been used to explain this concept because it is one of the strongest and most common ways that we see framing bias influence learning.

### Framing bias

Framing bias is the cognitive bias that says that our conclusions and decisions are influenced by how the options are presented to us.

## Framing bias in learning

In learning, framing bias commonly affects the chunks we form by making them similar to how information is chunked for us by teachers, resources, lectures, etc.

These chunks are often not the most cognitively optimal for our brain, which is why order control and independent chunking through the correct process of inquiry is crucial.

### Find the way

Many learners conclude that there is no other way to chunk information because the way it is given to them seems logical and intuitive. However, there are often multiple ways to logically and intuitively chunk information _while_ being cognitively more advantageous. Our job as learners is to _find_ those ways, not accept that "there isn't any other way".

## Iteration 2

The text in pink represents a whole new chunk that was added which we discovered we missed by asking more lateral relationship questions.

In the next iteration above, we have introduced more lateral connectivity. However, it is disorganised. If we leave it here, we would have the problem of _spiderwebbing_.

By evaluating the connections, we can see that the chunk "Types" near the bottom has a high density of connections. If the concepts related to "Types" were chunked together, there would be no need for so many lines.

**We must explore other chunk structures to try and resolve this.** _This process creates significant cognitive load and can take time._

## Iteration 3

![[Image 34.jpg|A very organised mindmap on retinal detachment which has been rechunked.]]

The same relationships have been expressed here as before.

In this final iteration, we have re-chunked the entire topic. The problems of _waterfalling_ and _spiderwebbing_ have been resolved using a different chunk structure. It also represents this topic in a more unique way, which makes it more memorable. 

While it looks much cleaner and simpler, it (amazingly) retains the relationships identified in the second iteration. Moreover, the relationships are much easier to remember because they are reflected in the core organisational structure of the topic now.
