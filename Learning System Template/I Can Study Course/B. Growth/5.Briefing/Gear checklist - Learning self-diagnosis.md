# Gear checklist - Learning self-diagnosis
## Are you ready?

Let’s run through our current system and make sure we’re happy with our cognitive “equipment” before moving on. After all, only a fool would try to climb a mountain with a ripped harness and broken clips!

### Gear checklist

By the end of this lesson, we would expect that you have met each of the outcomes listed below. Expand each item to read through the outcomes. **If there are any outcomes you have not yet reached, we recommend practising those before progressing.**
#### General competence

**Deep processing**

1. You are gaining experience with finding similarities and differences between information, grouping them, and gaining a big-picture understanding. 
2. You have familiarity with the discomfort and confusion involved in trying to find the right groups and express them with non-linear note-taking. Your tolerance for this discomfort is slowly starting to improve.

**Self-regulation**

1. You are developing an awareness of how different cognitive processes and methods of learning affect your memory, learning experience, and quality of knowledge.
2. You are beginning to understand how difficulties in learning can be desirable.
3. You have some experience with modifying your learning system to achieve different results and effects.

**Retrieval**

1. You are using SIR and developing competence with at least three interleaving methods
2. You are developing greater awareness for how different types of knowledge and different orders of learning require different types of thinking and practice.

**Mindset**

1. You have experience with experimenting with different learning methods and learning from mistakes.
2. You engage with Kolb's regularly.

**Self-management**

1. You are able to influence your behaviours and productivity using evidence-based strategies for affecting focus, attention, and motivation.
2. You regularly manage your tasks and time actively.

#### Prestudy

1. You do prestudy regularly before intensive learning events
2. Prestudy focuses on the big picture rather than small details
3. Main ideas and concepts are grouped into high-level categories (chunks)
4. Prestudy does not take too long (usually less than 30 minutes)
5. When possible, prestudy is done across multiple classes/lectures to cover the full topic in one go, which preserves the relationships within the topic

#### Notes and Processing

1. Notes always involve processing of information instead of passive, linear note-taking.
2. Processed information is made simpler and more concise.
3. You try to express information with non-linear notes representing relationships through arrows and spatial arrangement.
4. You avoid using full sentences and too many bullet points when writing notes.
5. You are used to not having to write everything and are able to tolerate the uncertainty and insecurity that can come from writing less (but thinking more).
6. You are developing competence with delaying your note-taking.
7. Questions of “Why is this important?”, “How can I use this?” and “How can I apply it?” are asked during the processing step for groups of information in order to help simplify it or connect ideas together more meaningfully.
8. You focus on developing a big-picture understanding of the topic before attempting to memorise details or understand concepts in great depth.
9. You are able to group concepts together based on shared similarities more comfortably.

#### Revision/retrieval

1. You have a dedicated spaced retrieval schedule that _generally_ follows a spacing of one day, one week, one month.
2. You use full, uncued recall during retrieval sessions.
3. Retrieval involves multiple orders of learning (as required by your learning needs)
4. Retrieval methods are appropriate for the topic's knowledge type (declarative vs procedural)

#### Learning system regulation

1. Your learning methods are regularly evaluated for effectiveness to make sure you are using methods that are fit-for-purpose and logically aligned.
2. You use lower-order memorisation techniques for small details (if necessary)
3. You actively experiment with new methods and use Kolb's to turn them into marginal gains at least twice per week.
4. You use your skills at creating learning flows to assess your system.

#### Focus management

1. Distractions are removed from the environment before work sessions.
2. Distraction cheat sheets are used during work sessions to track unchecked distractions and remove them next time.
3. You take frequent breaks while working to keep your energy levels high _(as a rule of thumb, once you break out of flow and feel that it takes more effort to stay focused, it's break time)_.
4. Breaks are timed to prevent them from taking longer than expected.
5. You are gaining a higher level of self-awareness about what things improve or reduce your focus and distractibility.
6. You are developing experience with modifying your environment and observing how it impacts your motivation, productivity, and behaviours.

#### Time and task management

1. You regularly prioritise your tasks in a way that avoid urgency trapping
2. You schedule your time realistically using under scheduling
3. You preserve your sleep duration and quality
4. Sleep is never compromised within 48 hours of an exam or test


### Have you attended a Live Clinic yet?

By now, you're ready to attend a Live Clinic (if you haven't already).

Live clinics are live, interactive coaching workshops that help you build the skills for self-diagnosis and problem-solving. - Develop skills faster
- Make fewer mistakes and waste less time
- Require less guidance as they can help themselves

    Perform your first skills audit
    
    **As your learning system grows, so too does the importance of being able to modify and adjust it.**
    
    A skills audit is a core intermediate-level learning strategy that ensures your learning system is adaptable. To perform a skills audit:
    
    1. Assess your effort and methods:
        1. Am I putting in reasonable effort (at least 60-70% of my max)? _If **no**, increase effort to around 70%. If **yes**, answer the next question._
        2. Are the methods I'm using giving me the types of results I want? _If **yes**, there are no probably no major issues. If **no**, your methods can be optimised. Move onto the next step._
    2. Create a learning flow map as you learned in an _earlier_ _task_.
    3. Review each step of your learning flow to assess whether it follows the principles of efficient learning that you know so far.
    4. Make adjustments and use Kolb's to gain familiarity with your adjusted system.
    
    As you learn about more principles, your audit will become deeper and more fine-tuned.
    
    To assist you in your first skills audit, you may wish to review the [**Fundamentals 2 checkpoint lesson**](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning/pr-academic/ph-high-yield/st-fundamentals-2/ls-fundamentals-checkpoint/pg-fundamentals-checkpoint-video) and the [**Gear checklist**](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning/pr-academic/ph-growth/st-briefing/ls-learning-self-diagnosis/sl-gear-checklist/slp-gear-checklist) items for an overview of the most important principles.
    
    _In the future, we recommend completing a skills audit every time you feel that your learning system is not achieving the results you want._
