# Common traps - Briefing
## The danger of traps

"Traps" refer to behaviours and tendencies that cause _massive damage and harm_. While there are not many single techniques that are so powerful that they completely transform your learning by themselves, traps can be so harmful that even _one_ of them can make all of your hard work and "right methods" useless!

![[Image 12.jpg|Man in a pitfall unable to get out despite a rope and a ladder being there.]]

One trap can cause disproportionate harm.

### Alert

**This lesson is considered high-yield.** Many learners struggle with improving due to these three issues. We recommend that you pay careful attention to catch yourself.

## The three most common traps

These common traps are behaviours and patterns that cause many learners to waste energy with minimal benefit.

The traps are...

1. Deathly spirals
2. Bandwagon fallacy
3. Misfitting techniques

### Explanations
#### Deathly spirals

This happens when you use a technique that helps in the short term but harms you long-term. The most dangerous part is the deeper you fall, the less time and opportunity you have to escape! The best approach is to **avoid** these entirely. I’ll warn you about possible spirals throughout this program.

These can create something we call **learning debt**. This is where your future self must do extra work to pay for inefficiencies in your current methods. Because your future self will probably be even busier than you are now, this creates a high chance of being increasingly overwhelmed as time passes.

![[Image 13.jpg|Deathly spiral illustrated to show that the benefits of a short term solution are outweighed by the negative side effects.]]

The negatives of investing in a short-term solution far outweigh the positives.

A common deathly spiral is not having enough time to work on learning skills because there is too much time spent on learning using ineffective methods. Using ineffective methods helps short-term because (although ineffective) it addresses your immediate need to "cover content". However, long-term, having inadequate skills to tackle the volume or complexity of learning will force a no-win scenario where the only options are to fail or give up.

#### Bandwagon fallacy

This happens when you use a technique just because other people do. If a technique is _common_, that doesn’t mean it’s _good._

This can be insidious because common methods are often the ones many successful people use. **However, amazing athletes don't necessarily make amazing coaches.** Successful people often do not know what contributed to their success. In addition, people who _failed_ with the same methods are often much less vocal and public about their failures.

![[Image 14.jpg|An infographic showing that 8/9 people used a method and only 1 was successful. Yet this means 100% of success used the method.]]

Copying success can be dangerous because we often don't know who was unsuccessful with the same methods.

**To understand if a method is effective or not, we must know:**

1. Who used the method and was **successful**?
2. Who used the method and was **unsuccessful**?

#### Misfitting techniques

This happens when you use techniques incorrectly or in the wrong situation. The right technique often makes short work out of the problem. If you need substantial effort with a method and it isn’t giving you results, it might not be the right solution. We will also make these situations clear throughout the program for the techniques we teach.

![[Image 15.jpg|Bar chart showing one method with small effort needed for same results as another method.]]

If the only way to achieve results is with high effort, our methods need to be evaluated.

In the diagram above, you can see that method 1 requires much less effort than method 2 to achieve the same result. **This means that method 1 is a superior approach to achieving the desired result.** 

When we increase our efforts using method 1, we can also achieve even greater results. On the other hand, when we increase our efforts using method 2, we may be unable to improve by much as the method itself limits us.


## Flashcard overreliance: The perfect storm

Flashcards (especially using apps) have taken the world by storm. They have a cult-like following in some circles. Flashcards are useful for creating easier spaced retrieval schedules and utilising micro-learning (a form of learning where sessions are only a few minutes long).

**However, overreliance on flashcards (due to bandwagon fallacy) can directly create a misfitting technique and a deathly spiral!**

### How flashcards can become deadly

1. A system is created that increases dependence on more flashcards
2. It creates learning debt by relying on your future self to do more repetition to cover the material
3. It struggles to create knowledge networks, which results in poor memory and superficial understanding
4. This increases the need for more repetition and other methods to fill gaps in depth or complexity of knowledge
5. There is less time available to use other methods of learning (or even learn about other methods)
6. Every day, more cards accumulate, which increases the level of sacrifice needed to escape the spiral
