## Question 1

Jeremiah writes this in the “experience” section of their Kolb’s:

I received 75% on my last maths test which is much less than I had expected.

# Answer

Which of the following statements is true?

This is an example of using an outcome as an experience, which is what we want.

**This is an example of using an outcome as an experience, which is likely to lead to problems.**

Regardless of the contents of the experience, what is written is too brief for it to be valuable.

The biggest problem is that this experience is not an experiment from a previous cycle.

---

# Learning notes

Jeremiah has fallen into the mistake of using an outcome as an experience. Because outcomes are the result of multiple processes, Kolb’s should be focused on the processes that led to the outcome. Otherwise, it is difficult to identify what we can do differently and improve. Outcome as experience can often lead to frustration as people can spend lots of time reflecting, but not have any meaningful insights.

The experience section of Kolb’s should always be brief and concise. Furthermore, not every experience needs to be the experiment of a previous cycle. This is only the case when trying to stack improvements for solving the same problem. New experiences can also be reflected on. If experiments are designed for this new experience, it should feed into the next cycle until sufficiently resolved.

I tried non-linear note-taking during lectures and found it overwhelming and challenging.

Then I jotted down keywords the lecturer said or that I saw on the slides.

During this experience, I felt very overwhelmed and stressed. It was difficult for me to keep up with the content and I struggled to get everything down.

## Abstraction

# Answer

Zhao spent almost 2 hours completing this Kolb's and struggled with the reflection section. Which of the following statements is true?

This is normal in the early stages of learning a new skill. Zhao needs to continue practising.

This is abnormal in the early stages of learning a new skill. Zhao needs to seek more feedback and help with his reflection before doing more cycles.

**Zhao should limit how long he spends on Kolb's to around 30 minutes per day, even if he isn't able to complete it.**

Kolb's is a technique that naturally takes a long time. There are major concerns.

---

# Learning notes

If Zhao takes 2 hours to complete a Kolb’s, it will be difficult for him to keep this up frequently. It’s stated that he struggled with the reflection. This indicates that perhaps his self-awareness and/or reflective technique is limited him. As a result, the following stages (abstraction and experiment) will also be limited by the quality of his reflection.

Zhao should stick to around 30 minutes. This means he may not even get to the abstraction or experiment stage, but over time, his self-awareness and reflection will become better and easier. As his efficiency increases, he will naturally be able to complete the remaining stages within the same amount of time.

With good self-awareness and reflective ability, most people should be able to do a full Kolb’s within 30 minutes (or less). It may take longer for very complex issues.

# Learning notes

Zhao’s reflection is far too superficial and brief. Notice how there is very little reflection other than the sentiment of “it felt overwhelming”. We do not get any information about when it felt overwhelming or the metacognitive processes he used to regulate it. It is clear to see the difference in detail comparing Zhao’s with the exemplars given in the lesson. By reading Zhao’s reflection, it is not clear where the root cause of the problem is. All we can simply conclude is that he finds some part (or parts) of the process overwhelming.

As a result, it is very challenging for Zhao to find trends or habits that lead to his problem. There is just not enough information about his actual behaviours. Instead of analysing the reflection and realising it is too brief and superficial, Zhao has tried to think of why the problem is occurring based on his own ideas. Therefore, his abstraction is also theoretical and not well-supported by his reflection. He is making assumptions about the root cause. In this example, he completely ignores changes he could make in his cognitive processes and prestudy routines that would help solve the problem.

Zhao’s experiment does make sense based on how he perceives the problem and it is specific and actionable. However, it will fundamentally not help him because the root cause was incorrectly analysed. Thankfully, as long as Zhao cycles this into his next Kolb’s, he will realise that it also has limitations and he should eventually start to realise his mistakes.

## Question 4

Lilly is determined to improve her learning skills. After completing the High-Yield phase, she has identified 5 techniques that are new to her that she wants to integrate into her learning and self-management system.

Every day, she sets herself the task of attempting to use all of these techniques as accurately as possible. She makes a conscious effort to follow the guidelines and push through difficulties.

After a month of diligent effort, she finds that she is not able to improve as quickly as she expected. Her consistency is poor and each experiment simply reveals more mistakes. She feels that she understands how to use each technique with enough clarity, but there are nuances when she applies them in practice that make them more challenging. It seems like developing conscious competence is impossibly slow!

# Answer

Which of the following statements are probably true for Lilly's situation? Select all that apply.

**Conscious competence does indeed take a long time. If she is finding mistakes, this indicates that she is making marginal gains.**

Lilly is limited by the quality of her reflection or abstraction. For most High-Yield phase techniques, a month should be enough time to gain conscious competence.

**Lilly has experiment overload.**

Lilly needs to review the lessons again as there are probably theoretical gaps in her understanding of the technique.

---

# Learning notes

As Lilly has enough clarity to execute the techniques, her level of theoretical understanding is good enough to start practising and evaluating with Kolb’s. The fact that she is finding mistakes is a sign of marginal gains at the conscious incompetence level. However, she is probably developing skills very slowly because she is attempting to use 5 new techniques simultaneously! This is considered experiment overload. 

While a month may be enough time to gain competence for High-Yield phase techniques, experiment overload will slow down her pace of development significantly. There is insufficient information to conclude that she is limited by her reflection or abstraction (although this could also be the case).
