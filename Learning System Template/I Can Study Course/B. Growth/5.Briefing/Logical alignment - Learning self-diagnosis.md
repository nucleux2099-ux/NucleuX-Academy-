# Logical alignment - Learning self-diagnosis
## The essence of self-regulated learning

We must be able to control our studying to overcome unpredictable obstacles safely. Sam struggled because his learning methods were ineffective, and he could not make the necessary adjustments. 

In this lesson, we’ll go through three major components that form a learning triad: _cognition_, _notes_ and _revision_. For you to reach your learning goals, these three components must logically _align_.

![[Image 19.jpg|Great memory and depth, high consistency, and active load management only occur when all 3 are aligned. Venn Diagram.]]

All three must be aligned to have a problem-free learning system.

**Refer to these major components whenever you want to do a rapid assessment of your learning methods.**

### Analogy

It’s like playing an instrument. You need the right know-how (cognition), a good technique so you can practise and improve (notes) and a good ear to pick up on mistakes (revision). If you were missing even one of these, you would struggle to become an expert musician!

## The learning triad

### Cognition

Aligned cognition means that the thinking processes involved in learning must be effective. This involves:

1. Having active learning that uses energy and effort the right way
2. Forming connections and relationships by comparing and contrasting
3. Making sense of information by simplifying and grouping it
4. Connecting new information with existing information

Sam was very passive. He was unaware of what his brain was doing when trying to learn. This meant he didn’t know what to improve to become more efficient.

**Awareness of how we are thinking is called metacognition.** High metacognition is one of the strongest predictors of learning success.

### Notes

Aligned note-taking means it helps _cognition_. Information overload is prevented by offloading information into notes. However, note-taking is not so excessive and passive that we use it as a way to stop thinking.

**Think of note-taking like solving a jigsaw puzzle:**

1. You pick up pieces from the box and put them together to form a big picture.
2. You can look at the picture you're putting together and compare it to new pieces you pick up. 
3. It's much easier than trying to connect all the pieces together in your mind (**overload**). 

**On the other hand, passive note-taking is like if you were to:**

1. Mindlessly place every piece you pick up without thinking about where it fits
2. As you continue, the picture you form becomes messier.
3. There are fewer pieces left in the box (making us feel good), but what you've placed is just as messy, so now you must go through that again to sort it. What a waste of time!

Sam would write very long and linear notes, which is passive and essentially just photocopying text from the teacher or textbook. This leads to very low retention and understanding.

### Revision/retrieval

Aligned retrieval means:

1. We have spacing
2. We are using interleaving purposefully for the right type and order of knowledge
3. The methods and frequency of retrieval are suited to our needs

To revise, Sam would repeatedly go through past papers only. He didn’t even know how many papers he needed to do; he fell to the bandwagon fallacy and copied his friend’s way of revising, which was incorrect.
