**Common questions**

When do I use Kolb's?

You can use Kolb’s to develop any new skill. For this program, use Kolb’s whenever you learn anything new in the course – this could be after every sub-lesson, every lesson, or every 2 to 3 lessons. Whether it’s a technique, a mindset shift, or a tip, you should gain some experience applying it before moving on.

How frequently do I need to do Kolb's?

If you wait too long before doing a Kolb's on an experience, you will forget about important details within that experience. Ideally, each Kolb's should be done when the experience is still fresh in your mind.

In addition, waiting longer can also introduce other variables, making analysis more complicated. For example, if you do one Kolb's at the end of the week for how studying was that entire week, there are many additional variables to consider since each session of studying is actually a separate experience.

For most people, doing a Kolb's after every experience is not viable. For this reason, we recommend doing it at the end of every day (if possible), or at least twice per week.

How much experience do I need before moving on to next lessons?

We don’t want to rush our progress and sacrifice building good foundations. However, after a certain point, you will be ready to move on even though you haven’t fully mastered the current skill. This is because we grow at optimal speed when we are always slightly expanding our comfort zone. 

*Read ** for an in-depth explanation of how to know when you should progress.*

What do I use to practise new learning methods?

You can practise on anything!

For learning skills, you can use material you have already learned or will learn in the future. If the material is new, you get the core benefit of increasing your skills, plus the secondary benefit of studying ahead! You can also practise on irrelevant material like Wikipedia articles or random topics you are interested in.

For other skills like time management, try to apply them to your life in general (e.g. scheduling your week, setting up nightly routines). The exact approach will depend on which technique you’re practising.

How do I practise changes in mindsets?

Sometimes, the outcomes from mindsets are not obvious or quantifiable. However, they follow a similar progress to the Hierarchy of Competence. New mindsets require conscious effort to adopt, whereas mindsets that have turned into habits can be activated immediately and reflexively.

For example, imagine that someone spills tea on your shirt. If you have fully trained yourself to take unexpected events in your stride, you may be briefly surprised or inconvenienced for a few seconds, but then you will move on, accept it, and plan how to adapt. On the other hand, if you have only recently started cultivating this mindset, you may be annoyed for 5 or 10 minutes while actively trying to regulate your emotions and work towards a solution.

To guide your experiential learning cycle for adopting new mindsets, think about the following:

What is the problem this mindset addresses?

How often would I encounter this kind of problem?

If you often encounter the problem (i.e. at least once every few days), you will naturally experience plenty of situations where you can practise the new mindset. Stress, focus and procrastination are examples of problems you may encounter frequently.

If you don’t often encounter the problem (i.e. less than once every few days), think about how you can deliberately put yourself in situations where you will encounter the problem. That way, you can practise the mindset and gain competency earlier instead of waiting weeks or months to face a relevant situation.

Because mindsets cover many problems, your exact experiential learning cycle and plan might look quite different each time. As long as you follow these guidelines, your plan should be acceptable:

Experience a challenge or problem that induces a specific type of mindset.

Reflect on what your mindset was like during the experience.

Abstract on the approach you took to activate or maintain the right mindset. Why did (or didn’t) it go well? How could you do it better next time?

Experiment with this.

**What are marginal gains?**

**Marginal gains are the perspective of viewing progress as a series of small (sometimes almost unnoticeable) improvements.**

**This perspective has several benefits for self-development and is widely respected as a framework for planning improvements and progress in business, manufacturing, IT, personal development, and most other industries.**

**Why are marginal gains important?**

**Psychological: marginal gains create more positive feedback and reinforcement as gains in progress are more visible and acknowledged. This can improve intrinsic motivation.**

**Strategic: marginal gains help to plan a path with a high probability of success by identifying which actions and goals are most likely to take you closer to your ultimate goal while being nearly guaranteed to be attainable.**

**Return on investment: effort and time spent on achieving marginal gains have a high reward due to their extremely low failure rate, low risk, and high alignment with ultimate goals.**

**In addition, marginal gains have a compounding effect. Not only do marginal gains put you in an exponentially better position to achieve the next goal, but it also increases planning and metacognitive accuracy. **

**For example, achieving the marginal gain of learning more about your learning tendencies and habits not only puts you in a better position to prevent mistakes but also increases your self-****awareness about your learning process, which can lead to deeper and more accurate insights in future reflections.**

**On the other hand, attempting to achieve improvement with large strides has the following disadvantages:**

**High risk of failure: larger goals often involve more variables and a higher level of skill, control, and precision. As the margin for error is much smaller, the risk of wasted time and energy is much higher.**

**Low frequency of iteration: while failure is a normal part of learning and growth, achieving goals through fewer, larger strides increases the delay between attempts and feedback. Therefore, mistakes and areas for improvement are noticed less frequently, leading to slower growth. In real practice, because most people tend to view larger strides in the scale of weeks or months, while marginal gains should be planned on a daily or weekly basis, the difference in growth speed can be up to 20-fold. One person could improve a certain amount in one year, while another could achieve the same growth in just two or three weeks.**

**High risk of misalignment between actions and goals: even if large goals are achieved, they may be meaningless in the big picture. As the iteration frequency is slower and the number of variables is higher with larger strides, there is a high risk of achieving a goal without it practically contributing towards an ultimate, bigger-picture goal.**

**For example, someone could spend weeks calibrating and integrating an app into their workflow, but after changing their entire flow to use the software, they realise that it is incompatible with an extra step that they have recently learned is crucial for them to succeed. We commonly see this with learning where learners will maximise their use of a note-taking app, only to learn that the most efficient methods of note-taking are not fundamentally possible with the software.**

**What is Kolb's experiential cycle?**

**Kolb's experiential cycle is a framework for self-reflection that helps to find ways to improve based on past experiences. It allows you to learn from experiences very quickly if applied correctly.**

**Why is Kolb's cycle important?**

**Mistakes are inevitable when learning anything difficult or attempting to achieve any challenging goal. The ability to learn from mistakes and adjust processes and methods accordingly is a defining skill of successful people.**

**However, not all reflection is productive.**

**For example, reflecting on problems and feelings without processing it in a way that helps to make sense of it and change things is a characteristic behavioural pattern seen in mood and affective disorders such as anxiety and depression. This is called ruminating.**

**The way we teach Kolb's experiential cycle is designed to help turn reflections into useful and actionable steps while extracting maximum value from each experience. Thus, with each cycle, we learn more about the nature of the problem, its root causes, and approaches to solving the problem that have a high chance of success. it often takes multiple cycles to learn about the true nature of the problem, with more cycles to successfully implement a "fix".**

**We teach a specific series of questions that have been designed to reduce cognitive biases and common mistakes with critical reflection that have been observed over years of coaching experience.**

**Why are both marginal gains and Kolb's simultaneously important for practical self-improvement?**

**When there are problems or deficiencies in a certain process, Kolb's can help to quickly identify the root nature and eventually "fix" it with repeated cycles. However, in real-world practice, there are many potential problems that can be worked on at any given time.**

**Especially for beginners, there can be dozens of potential avenues for improvement. Not all of these directions are equally relevant to achieving the ultimate goal.**

**When paired with Kolb's, marginal gains is relevant for two primary purposes:**

**It helps with motivation and mindset as the progress made from Kolb's tends to be marginal in nature. For example, the first few cycles of Kolb's are often not likely to fix problems. Rather, it is more common to learn about problems more deeply.**

**It helps to prioritise which problems Kolb's should be performed on. It isn't viable to perform a Kolb's cycle on every problem, every day. Identifying which areas would offer the greatest overall benefit allows for a more efficient and productive allocation of reflection and experimentation time.**

Explanations

Deathly spirals

This happens when you use a technique that helps in the short term but harms you long-term. The most dangerous part is the deeper you fall, the less time and opportunity you have to escape! The best approach is to **avoid** these entirely. I’ll warn you about possible spirals throughout this program.

These can create something we call **learning debt**. This is where your future self must do extra work to pay for inefficiencies in your current methods. Because your future self will probably be even busier than you are now, this creates a high chance of being increasingly overwhelmed as time passes.

The negatives of investing in a short-term solution far outweigh the positives.

A common deathly spiral is not having enough time to work on learning skills because there is too much time spent on learning using ineffective methods. Using ineffective methods helps short-term because (although ineffective) it addresses your immediate need to "cover content". However, long-term, having inadequate skills to tackle the volume or complexity of learning will force a no-win scenario where the only options are to fail or give up.

Bandwagon fallacy

This happens when you use a technique just because other people do. If a technique is *common*, that doesn’t mean it’s *good.*

This can be insidious because common methods are often the ones many successful people use. **However, amazing athletes don't necessarily make amazing coaches. **Successful people often do not know what contributed to their success. In addition, people who *failed* with the same methods are often much less vocal and public about their failures.

Copying success can be dangerous because we often don't know who was unsuccessful with the same methods.

**To understand if a method is effective or not, we must know:**

Who used the method and was **successful**?

Who used the method and was **unsuccessful**?

This happens when you use techniques incorrectly or in the wrong situation. The right technique often makes short work out of the problem. If you need substantial effort with a method and it isn’t giving you results, it might not be the right solution. We will also make these situations clear throughout the program for the techniques we teach.

If the only way to achieve results is with high effort, our methods need to be evaluated.

A system is created that increases dependence on more flashcards

Every day, more cards accumulate, which increases the level of sacrifice needed to escape the spiral

**i.Prestudy**

**The power of ****prestudy**

Prestudy helps ensure you are on the right path at the beginning, which makes a significant difference throughout the rest of your learning. Fixing your prestudy can automatically fix more complicated issues down the track, saving a lot of time and effort.

**How does this work?**

Random information is often harder to remember than relevant information that is meaningful to you. This is because your brain recognises the importance of the information and is willing **and able** to store it more readily. This increases your retention and speed of recall. 

Good prestudying helps your brain think by giving new information a place to fit.

**Prestudy**** creates an outline for your brain to fill in later.**

**Principles of effective ****prestudy**

Whenever you do any prestudy, follow these principles to ensure you're getting the most out of it:

Group information based on how the information is related

Focus on the big picture - details can come later

Spend more time thinking about why something is important or relevant, instead of just trying to remember or understand it

Try to create analogies and connect the information to what you already know

Study the entire topic rather than individual classes or lectures so that you can see the full scope of relationships

Aim to be shallow and broad, rather than deep and narrow

**New terminology: Chunking**

The process of taking lots of information and putting it in groups is called **chunking**. We will refer to “chunks” (big groups), “sub-chunks” (groups within groups) and “chunking” (grouping) throughout the program.

**Focus**

**Elements of focus**

To focus is to concentrate deeply on a task and shut out distractions. It enables you to achieve more within a given time than someone who lacks focus. Here are some points to help you create and maintain focus:

**Sleep:** You must protect your sleep. In general, aim for at least 8 to 9 hours of sleep. Sleep has dramatic carry-over effects on focus, energy, mood, and  (a critical learning phenomenon).

**Remove distractions:** Before you start a focused work session, spend time removing all possible distractions. Remember to use your distraction cheat sheet to note down everything that distracts you, and make sure to remove all of those things at the beginning of your next study session.

**Breaks:** Have breaks more often, but keep them short (i.e. high frequency, low duration). This is because our body recovers faster when it is less exhausted. Frequent, shorter breaks allow for longer overall focus throughout the day – so it’s better to have a 20 to 30-minute study session and a 5-minute break than to study for 4 hours and have a 1-hour break. To keep the length of your break under control, use a timer set to 5, 10 or 15-minute intervals.

**How to use our methods in class**

If your teacher does not allow you to use our techniques in class or overwhelms you with homework, you can try these approaches:

**Discreetly use the technique anyway.** This is the easiest solution. Your teacher may force you to write linear or passive notes, but if they aren’t checking, you could get away with using our techniques anyway. If you can do your own work or study your own way in class without being detected by the teacher, do so.

**Do the minimum possible to satisfy your teacher.** If the above isn’t an option, spend as little time as possible on unweighted assignments or homework so you have more time for proper self-study. The “time-boxing” approach below may be useful here.

**Use class as a way to revise the material and find gaps.** When sitting in class, instead of just trying to absorb information, you can use class as a dynamic quizzing environment. Actively look for things you aren’t clear about, or ask deeper and more detailed questions you usually wouldn’t think about. This is a common strategy for university students who are doing effective prestudy.

**Do your homework during class.** Most of the time, homework is just about finishing questions. If you have already learned the material or find it easy, you can do the homework during class.

**Time-boxing**

Unlike time-**blocking**, which is a time management technique that involves scheduling blocks of time in your schedule to complete work, time-**boxing** is when you do not exceed the amount of time blocked out.

For example, if you schedule one hour to complete something, time-blocking allows room for flexibility. Time-boxing dictates that at the end of one hour, your time is up.

Time-boxing is a useful method for completing work that is either **very important** or **very unimportant (but compulsory)**.

**For very important tasks**

Many important tasks are not urgent. For example, improving your learning skills may be important but not urgent. These tasks can not usually be completed in a single 'session' anyway. Therefore, time-boxing is useful because it allows you to commit time to making progress on life-changing, important activities. 

**For very unimportant (but compulsory) tasks**

For tasks without any real consequence but are mandatory to complete, time-boxing gives you enough time to complete them to a sufficient standard without wasting time beyond its value.

The best example is homework that does not contribute to your final grade. You need to do these tasks for school, but they aren’t as important as doing well in actual exams or tests, so you shouldn’t spend too much time on them.

**How to time-box your homework**

Assign the amount of time you need for important tasks such as self-study or working through this course. These are truly important tasks and should not be compromised.

Schedule this time into your day or week.

Allocate some of your remaining time for unimportant tasks like unweighted homework. This may be less time than you are used to spending on those tasks.

Complete your unimportant tasks as quickly as possible within the allocated time.

The idea is not to get a perfect outcome but the best outcome in the time available (sometimes, we refer to this as the **best-for-time** outcome). This approach can also help to combat perfectionist tendencies.

**Principles related to time-boxing**

Parkinson's law

This suggests that an activity will contract or expand to fill the time available. Give yourself lots of time, and it will take lots of time. Give yourself very little time, and you will still get it done (within reasonable limits).

Pareto principle

Otherwise known as the 80:20 rule, this states that 80% of an effect resides in 20% of the work or input. For example, 80% of your learning occurs due to 20% of the information (this is a powerful phenomenon that we will heavily exploit later), 80% of your time is wasted on 20% of distractions, and 80% of your essay is written in 20% of the total time.

Eisenhower matrix

This prioritisation framework indicates how unimportant but urgent tasks might be handled.

**Alternatively, negotiate (maybe)**

If you feel your teacher is open to the idea, you may wish to openly discuss the techniques and methods you are trying to use and ask for permission. 

In cases where students have shown they have an excellent level of knowledge and are ahead of the class, some teachers have even issued a “hall pass” to students, allowing them to not come to class and instead self-study in the library or do their own revision during class time.

**Last resort**

If all else fails and you have to sit in class, learning nothing new, and are unable to self-study discreetly we can try to reduce our quantity of prestudy so class is more engaging.

In essence, we are deliberately increasing the level of confusion we would feel in class by having a less comprehensive and even shallower level of prestudy. The idea is that if you **must** attend class anyway, this is time that we would otherwise waste. By loading class time with more cognitive load instead of during prestudy, we make the class more challenging and, therefore, more engaging.

**We can then devote the self-study time we would have spent on deeper ****prestudy**** on interleaved retrieval instead.**

Alternatively, the other option is to stay ahead of the class and accept that class is boring. Perhaps you can practise some mindfulness meditation?

**Either way, remember that feeling bored in class because you're so ahead of the material and you already know so much is a great problem to have!**

**ii.Logical**** alignment**

Logical alignment

The essence of self-regulated learning

We must be able to control our studying to overcome unpredictable obstacles safely. Sam struggled because his learning methods were ineffective, and he could not make the necessary adjustments. 

In this lesson, we’ll go through three major components that form a learning triad: *cognition*, *notes* and *revision*. For you to reach your learning goals, these three components must logically *align*.

All three must be aligned to have a problem-free learning system.

Refer to these major components whenever you want to do a rapid assessment of your learning methods.

Analogy

It’s like playing an instrument. You need the right know-how (cognition), a good technique so you can practise and improve (notes) and a good ear to pick up on mistakes (revision). If you were missing even one of these, you would struggle to become an expert musician!

The learning triad

Cognition

Aligned cognition means that the thinking processes involved in learning must be effective. This involves:

Having active learning that uses energy and effort the right way

Forming connections and relationships by comparing and contrasting

Making sense of information by simplifying and grouping it

Connecting new information with existing information

Sam was very passive. He was unaware of what his brain was doing when trying to learn. This meant he didn’t know what to improve to become more efficient.

Awareness of how we are thinking is called metacognition. High metacognition is one of the strongest predictors of learning success.

Notes

Aligned note-taking means it helps *cognition*. Information overload is prevented by offloading information into notes. However, note-taking is not so excessive and passive that we use it as a way to stop thinking.

Think of note-taking like solving a jigsaw puzzle:

You pick up pieces from the box and put them together to form a big picture.

You can look at the picture you're putting together and compare it to new pieces you pick up. 

It's much easier than trying to connect all the pieces together in your mind (overload). 

On the other hand, passive note-taking is like if you were to:

Mindlessly place every piece you pick up without thinking about where it fits

As you continue, the picture you form becomes messier.

There are fewer pieces left in the box (making us feel good), but what you've placed is just as messy, so now you must go through that again to sort it. What a waste of time!

Sam would write very long and linear notes, which is passive and essentially just photocopying text from the teacher or textbook. This leads to very low retention and understanding.

Revision/retrieval

Aligned retrieval means:

We have spacing

We are using interleaving purposefully for the right type and order of knowledge

The methods and frequency of retrieval are suited to our needs

To revise, Sam would repeatedly go through past papers only. He didn’t even know how many papers he needed to do; he fell to the bandwagon fallacy and copied his friend’s way of revising, which was incorrect.

**iii.Confidence**

**The Dunning-Kruger effect**

Most people are confident in their studying skills, but studies consistently show that they are usually **overconfident**, especially in areas where they have a small amount of knowledge. The  graph shows a person’s confidence relative to their knowledge.

Note that there are only two positions where confidence would be high.

Mount Stupid

The first peak is **Mount Stupid**. This is where people gain a lot of confidence because their knowledge of a new topic has increased quickly. However, in absolute terms, they still know very little because their starting point was nothing! As a result, people at this stage are often confident despite having low levels of knowledge.

*Unfortunately, many learners are at this stage, as mainstream education teaches little about learning science.*

Valley of Despair

The first dip is known as the **Valley of Despair**. This is where people realise, after delving deeper into the topic, that there is actually a lot they don’t know. This is typically the stage that PhD candidates are at after studying the topic for 5+ years.

Slope of Enlightenment

The **slope of enlightenment** is the period where people gain knowledge. This period can last decades and eventually culminates at the **peak** (when they have mastered the topic).

**Confidence is a lie... or is it?**

One of the biggest challenges around overconfidence is that we are oblivious to our own ignorance.

If we are unaware of what we don't know, how can we ever know if we are overconfident... or just confident?

**One way is to objectively assess our confidence level and time invested into building expertise. Because there are only two points where we have high confidence, we can ask ourselves:**

Do I feel very confident?

Have I dedicated several years to intensively learning about this to become an expert?

If the answer to the first question is 'yes' and the second is 'no', then we can say we are probably overconfident. What's important to remember is that we still won't *feel* any less confident. But this doesn't have to dictate how we behave and think.

**The wise mind model**

In psychology, the Wise Mind model is a popular framework for understanding the "types" of minds humans engage in.

The wise mind is when emotions and logic are balanced.

**Operating with different minds:**

When we operate with the *emotional mind*, we are controlled by feelings. Too much emotional mind can cause us to ruminate and spiral. We can panic, become anxious, impulsive, or allow our feelings (such as confidence or motivation) to dictate what we do.

When we operate with the *rational mind*, we are controlled by logic and thinking. Too much rational mind can cause us to overanalyse and feel paralysed. It can also cause us to become unhealthily detached from our emotional needs or the needs of others.

**Often, the best balance is in between.** We can both be in tune with our feelings but let our behaviours and thoughts be guided by what we rationally tell ourselves through our logic and knowledge. This is the wise mind.

**Relating the wise mind to overconfidence**

We can use these self-assessment questions to identify if we are overconfident. By understanding that we are overconfident, even though we feel justified in our confidence, we can prevent ourselves from engaging in detrimental behaviours.

**The ability to use our rational mind to balance our emotional mind and prevent our behaviours and actions from being dictated by only how we feel is a critical skill for emotional and behavioural self-regulation.**

**Controversy around Dunning-Kruger**

Since around 2020, there has been increasing debate around the validity of the Dunning-Kruger effect. The following is some *optional *reading for those interested in diving deeper into the state of the research.

Against Dunning-Kruger

The arguments against Dunning-Kruger are a valid scientific inquiry, with the most robust positing that all humans are generally susceptible to bias and that bias does not disproportionately affect those with lower knowledge.

This is a contentious point. While we know that bias affects all humans, some research suggests that systems of proactively moderating biases are effective. There is also research around the illusion of competency and the illusion of fluency, which closely parallel the findings from the Dunning-Kruger effect.

In reality, we are likely oversimplifying the relationship between knowledge and confidence. There are plenty of overconfident people who are highly skilled, and there are plenty of unskilled and unknowledgeable beginners who are rightly humble.

**On the other hand**, many non-researchers are quick to criticise the Dunning-Kruger effect based on their limited knowledge. In reality, only one or two articles are inadequate to form an opinion about the scientific validity of something. Ironically, non-researchers are often on Mount Stupid regarding their view on Dunning-Kruger...

Against the graph

Some people contest the validity of the Dunning-Kruger effect because the graph is not from the original paper. However, proponents of this tend to ignore the empirical support for this graph that has subsequently been observed in a range of studies. We provide some key examples in the references below.

The state of the science

The current state of the science is not conclusive. Based on the current literature, we can say with reasonable confidence that:

Knowledge and skill level **do** seem to have an influence on confidence.

Unlike many other psychological processes, the Dunning-Kruger effect has some neurophysiological evidence that supports the use of different cognitive processes in over or under-estimators.

The extent and universality of this is **not** clearly defined, and it seems to vary depending on context.

Overconfidence seems to be more consistently found for skills than domain knowledge, which varies more significantly between studies.

The level of over or under-estimation seems to depend on the difficulty and **complexity** of the task (more complex tasks are more likely to have overconfidence), the **actual knowledge** and expertise of the person, and the person’s level of personal self-esteem and general self-confidence.

Peripheral, semi-consilient research on cognitive biases and perceptions of skill and expertise is broadly **supportive** of a Dunning Kruger pattern.

Knowing about the Dunning-Kruger effect can be **protective** for it.

The final point is incredibly important and is one of the most recent findings – i.e. that teaching someone about Dunning-Kruger can be enough to statistically eliminate it from some empirical studies.

Our experience

In our own experience, we have **strongly** found that new members who are overconfident have a massively higher proportion of technique error compared to students who are:

Intuitively overconfident, but moderate themselves and force humility and open-mindedness; or

Intuitively underconfident and doubt their abilities.

**By far**, the worst results received by our students are from overconfident beginners, who are usually overconfident because they have achieved prior academic success using their old techniques.

While the old techniques that worked well for them were usually quite valid, we’ve found that overconfidence can prevent them from changing the parts that didn’t work or are holding them back. In many cases, this unwillingness to change or modify techniques causes barriers to further improving their techniques in later stages, where many component skills must be mastered before we combine them into an advanced version.

**iv.Gear**** checklist**

**Are you ready?**

Let’s run through our current system and make sure we’re happy with our cognitive “equipment” before moving on. After all, only a fool would try to climb a mountain with a ripped harness and broken clips!

**Gear checklist**

By the end of this lesson, we would expect that you have met each of the outcomes listed below. Expand each item to read through the outcomes. **If there are any outcomes you have not yet reached, we recommend practising those before progressing.**

General competence

**Deep processing**

You are gaining experience with finding similarities and differences between information, grouping them, and gaining a big-picture understanding. 

You have familiarity with the discomfort and confusion involved in trying to find the right groups and express them with non-linear note-taking. Your tolerance for this discomfort is slowly starting to improve.

**Self-regulation**

You are developing an awareness of how different cognitive processes and methods of learning affect your memory, learning experience, and quality of knowledge.

You are beginning to understand how difficulties in learning can be desirable.

You have some experience with modifying your learning system to achieve different results and effects.

**Retrieval**

You are using SIR and developing competence with at least three interleaving methods

You are developing greater awareness for how different types of knowledge and different orders of learning require different types of thinking and practice.

**Mindset**

You have experience with experimenting with different learning methods and learning from mistakes.

You engage with Kolb's regularly.

**Self-management**

You are able to influence your behaviours and productivity using evidence-based strategies for affecting focus, attention, and motivation.

You regularly manage your tasks and time actively.

Prestudy

You do prestudy regularly before intensive learning events

Prestudy focuses on the big picture rather than small details

Main ideas and concepts are grouped into high-level categories (chunks)

Prestudy does not take too long (usually less than 30 minutes)

When possible, prestudy is done across multiple classes/lectures to cover the full topic in one go, which preserves the relationships within the topic

Notes and Processing

Notes always involve processing of information instead of passive, linear note-taking.

Processed information is made simpler and more concise.

You try to express information with non-linear notes representing relationships through arrows and spatial arrangement.

You avoid using full sentences and too many bullet points when writing notes.

You are used to not having to write everything and are able to tolerate the uncertainty and insecurity that can come from writing less (but thinking more).

You are developing competence with delaying your note-taking.

Questions of “Why is this important?”, “How can I use this?” and “How can I apply it?” are asked during the processing step for groups of information in order to help simplify it or connect ideas together more meaningfully.

You focus on developing a big-picture understanding of the topic before attempting to memorise details or understand concepts in great depth.

You are able to group concepts together based on shared similarities more comfortably.

Revision/retrieval

You have a dedicated spaced retrieval schedule that *generally* follows a spacing of one day, one week, one month.

You use full, uncued recall during retrieval sessions.

Retrieval involves multiple orders of learning (as required by your learning needs)

Retrieval methods are appropriate for the topic's knowledge type (declarative vs procedural)

Learning system regulation

Your learning methods are regularly evaluated for effectiveness to make sure you are using methods that are fit-for-purpose and logically aligned.

You use lower-order memorisation techniques for small details (if necessary)

You actively experiment with new methods and use Kolb's to turn them into marginal gains at least twice per week.

You use your skills at creating learning flows to assess your system.

Focus management

Distractions are removed from the environment before work sessions.

Distraction cheat sheets are used during work sessions to track unchecked distractions and remove them next time.

You take frequent breaks while working to keep your energy levels high *(as a rule of thumb, once you break out of flow and feel that it takes more effort to stay focused, it's break time)*.

Breaks are timed to prevent them from taking longer than expected.

You are gaining a higher level of self-awareness about what things improve or reduce your focus and distractibility.

You are developing experience with modifying your environment and observing how it impacts your motivation, productivity, and behaviours.

Time and task management

You regularly prioritise your tasks in a way that avoid urgency trapping

You schedule your time realistically using under scheduling

You preserve your sleep duration and quality

Sleep is never compromised within 48 hours of an exam or test 

**Have you attended a Live Clinic yet?**

By now, you're ready to attend a Live Clinic (if you haven't already).

Live clinics are live, interactive coaching workshops that help you build the skills for self-diagnosis and problem-solving. As you've probably realised, learning new skills always comes with uncertainty. Having the skills to find the root cause and make the right adjustments is a superpower for any learner. Learners who regularly attend Live Clinics tend to:

Develop skills faster

Make fewer mistakes and waste less time

Require less guidance as they can help themselves

**Keep your eye out to enrol in the next Live Clinic.** Announcements are sent to your email, posted on our Discord, and appear on your dashboard!

**Sorry, your friends can't come!**

Live Clinics are exclusively for members. Your friends will have to join the program too!

Our recommended tasks can't cover absolutely everything! Make sure you're adding custom tasks for your focus lists so you can keep translating new theory into practice. Remember, a skill is only useful if you can use it! **Spending time to practise and hone your mastery is always time well-spent.**

| Stage of Kolb’s | Most common mistakes |
| --- | --- |
| Experience | An outcome is used as the experience: Since you reflect on an outcome rather than a process, it is difficult to make productive adjustments. |
| Reflection | Brief reflection: If your reflection is too short, there is insufficient information to analyse in the abstraction step.
Superficial reflection: Although there is sufficient information, that information is not metacognitive enough to gain meaningful insights into the process. |
| Abstraction | Theoretical abstraction: Instead of analysing the information in the reflection, the abstraction is based on your own “ideas”, which could be influenced by biases.
Reflection as abstraction: Instead of analysing the information in the reflection, the abstraction is simply an extension of the reflection. |
| Experimentation | Experiment overload: This happens when there are too many experiments in one cycle.
Intention experimentation: This happens when experiments are about “putting in more effort” rather than something specific or actionable.
Random experimentation: This happens when experiments are not connected to the findings from the abstraction. |
| Cycling | Non-cyclical Kolb’s: This happens when experiments do not carry forward as the experience in the next Kolb’s. |
| Unconscious incompetence | We have no knowledge of a new skill and are unaware that we are bad at it. |
| --- | --- |
| Conscious incompetence | We perform and review the skill incorrectly; however, we are aware that we are not good at it. |
| Conscious competence | We can correctly analyse and apply the skill, but it requires active effort. |
| Unconscious competence | We can perform the skills intuitively with minimal thought and effort. |
