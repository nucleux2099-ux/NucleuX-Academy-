**Chunking**

**Recap**

By now, we have a clear understanding of chunking. In this stage, it’s time to deepen this knowledge and make sure we aren’t missing anything.

As a recap, chunking actually increases** **the amount of detail you can remember by making it easier for your brain to organise everything. This makes it easier to retain information, which is why we want to ‘chunk’ as much as possible!

**The 2-4 Rule**

The 2-4 Rule is an important intermediate-level process that promotes higher-order (*evaluate* level) learning while chunking.

**Instructions**

Follow these instructions to abide by the 2-4 Rule.

Always aim to have two branches coming off each node, and never have more than four.

When there are four or more branches coming out of an information node, you can form a new chunk.

When there are less than two concepts from a node, you can merge or re-chunk.

Notice how, by re-chunking, we have also added a new lateral relationship between the sub-chunks that would have previously created spiderwebbing.

Key principles

When there are more than four points from a single chunk, it should be sub-chunked.

Sub-chunking can reduce spiderwebbing and allow lateral relationships to be expressed more cleanly.

**Example: Less than two**

The following example shows a situation where less than two concepts come from a single chunk. In this situation, it clutters the space without adding organisational value. In this situation, we can try to *concatenate* (i.e. merge).

Notice that "British colonisation" in the middle only leads to one chunk, "Consequences in India".

When a chunk or concept links only from one chunk or concept to one other chunk or concept, we call this a **single-node chain**.

Key principles

Nodes with one in-bound and one out-bound connection are called single-node chains.

Single-node chains indicate that the chunk structure can usually be optimised as these nodes will have fewer relationships and connections.

Single-node chains may become weak points that are at risk of lower retention of mastery.

**In this situation, we must move British colonisation somewhere.**

After considering what makes the most sense, we might altogether remove British colonisation from the flow to create a flow of "Drivers" to "Consequences". This is logical, and since the entire topic is about British colonisation, we are unlikely to forget what the drivers or consequences are referring to. It is obvious enough that the drivers are "drivers for British colonisation", and consequences are "consequences of British colonisation".

There are no longer any single-node chains.

Notice we have re-chunked "economic gain" to become "drivers" so that the relationship to "consequences" is much more straightforward and intuitive. The chunk "economic gain" might be reintroduced later if we find multiple other drivers that could break the 2-4 Rule again (e.g. political or religious drivers). 

**An example of this scenario is given below to show the reintroduction of "economic gain" as a sub-chunk:**

Notice the three new sub-chunks under "Drivers". This would break the 2-4 Rule if not sub-chunked.

**By continually chunking, sub-chunking, and re-chunking to find the simplest structure possible, we free up cognitive and physical space in our notes to explore additional relationships.**

In this way, our understanding can become deeper, more nuanced, and intricately organised. You can see that some important and complex relationships between concepts have been represented above, despite looking organised and simple. 

**Each decision to chunk, re-chunk, re-arrange on the map, and sub-chunk is a higher-order decision involving the ****evaluate**** level of thinking.**

**Persistence is crucial**

Rechunking to abide by the 2-4 Rule can involve substantial cognitive effort and thinking. Remember, your role as a learner is to find the structure that works. Look for the solution and persist until you find it! If we give up because it is difficult, we will sacrifice the quality of our learning. As you continue this, it will become easier and faster.

**Example: Less than two (with a terminal node)**

Sometimes, we can end up with less than two concepts from a single chunk and no further connections beyond this. As there are no further connections, we could say that the single-node chain leads to a *terminal node*. Terminal nodes are like the leaves of a tree. 

**An example of this is given here and boxed in red:**

"Famine" has one in-bound and one out-bound connection. "Malnourishment" has no further connections, making it a terminal node.

**This is also a single-node chain.** 

Concatenating this can be easily achieved by adding "malnourishment" to "Famine" directly. This creates a clearer and less cluttered map.

Notice that "Poverty" and "working conditions" were moved further away to give more space to "Famine". A visualisation of rotting meat was added as a memory anchor to help remember malnourishment. In this example, we assume that malnourishment is a layer 3 detail and still needs to be in this mindmap.

When you chunk in this way, you are building highly organised groups of concepts.

These concepts will also be represented more simply, as good chunking improves the spatial arrangement of your mind map. With more chunks, you will have fewer isolated points, which means fewer arrows and a less complicated mind map. The beauty of this is that you aren’t sacrificing your connections and relationships because the chunks are still connected!

**GRINDE maps**

**We're on the GRINDE**

GRINDE maps are the final and best version of your mind map. GRINDE is an acronym that we use to refine our mind map during the ‘skin’ step.

*You may notice similarities with **VPReFRE**; this is because GRINDE is a **mindmap**-specific extension of the same guidelines.*

**Grouped**

This is where we chunk the information on the mind map, as outlined in the previous lesson. 

**Reflective**

Here, you reflect on the order control, just like with VPReFRE. Pay attention to the order of the relationships, logic, concepts, and details to determine whether it reflects the way your brain understands the topic.

Your mind map should fulfil the layers of learning; if it doesn’t, take this opportunity to sort the information into clear layers (**backbone/logic → concepts → important details**).

**Tip**

This additional effort to make the layers clear is a characteristic indication of an advanced level learner.

**Interconnected**

Make sure your arrows are interconnected and not all pointing in a single direction or going purely outwards. There should be connections between chunks and concepts within the same layer as well as between layers (where relevant and important).

Good chunking creates nice, simple branching. Good interconnectedness extends this into a web of concepts that clearly influence each other. There is a clear sense of purpose to the information.

**Wheel and spokes**

The *wheel and spokes* problem is when there may be good chunking, but all of the relationships seem to radiate out from a central hub. This indicates a lack of *lateral* connectivity between chunks and concepts.

**Non-verbal**

Use doodles, visuals, and as few words as possible. An image will help you retain much more information than lines of words. You should also explore shorthand and contractions that you can use to form your own personal abbreviation system!

**Directional**

Have a flow of information and ideas in your mind map. This way, your mind map will be more logical because it shows the influence of concepts on other concepts. You always need to relate information to the big picture, as this will give you a sense of direction in your learning.

Whether or not your mind map is **directional** will heavily depend on how you stick to the Aim step, your problem-solving mentality, and how importance-based your first layer of logic is.

**Emphasised**

Make sure the layers are easy to see. You can do this by using different sizes, colours, or fonts.

Although this may seem insignificant, it helps you to pick out the main logic layer, concepts and sub-concepts much faster. This speeds up your revision time and facilitates your brain’s ability to create relationships. Cognitively, it improves learning because it requires you to be highly explicit about which concepts and relationships are most important. *Think of it like building mental highways and motorways.*

**Intuitive chunking**

Key principles

Chunks are “intuitive” when they leverage prior knowledge. The more you rely on prior knowledge, the more logical it feels, and the less memorisation is required.

When you compare chunk structures and decide which one makes the most sense to you, you are engaging in higher-order learning.

Rather than looking for a single “right answer,” you want to find the answer that best fits your knowledge schema.

Instead of just deciding on a chunk structure and not evaluating alternatives, look for opportunities to reverse-chunk so your chunking is truly importance-based.

When performed correctly, importance-based chunking creates intuitive chunks. By checking whether our chunks are intuitive, we can see whether or not we’ve performed importance-based chunking.

**Learning notes**

Blooming Chunk Structures

Reverse chunking is when we rechunk something based on a repeated element in layer 2 or 3. For example, a specific feature, characteristic, or fact may repeat itself across multiple different chunks in the map. When this happens, we can often explore an alternative chunk structure where this repeated feature, characteristic, or fact becomes the name of the chunk. By doing this, we can rearrange the concepts as sub-chunks or details that come from the new chunk. Many times, this creates a simpler and more intuitive flow of information. The perspective may be unique and more original while maintaining accuracy.

Reverse chunking is not always appropriate. For example, sometimes we end up with repeated elements no matter how we chunk. In this case, we must make a decision about which chunk structure is most appropriate. What’s important is that we evaluate the options instead of assuming one chunk structure is the best before properly exploring alternatives.

Another mistake we see is *Single node chains*, which are when there is a chunk or “node” of information on the map with only one inbound relationship and one outbound relationship. These nodes are weak points because of the low level of connectivity in the network. Single nodes can often be merged with the node before or after it, re-chunked, or simply removed.

A Collision Of Theories

Unshaped is a common Ascent 2 and 3 mistake that is easily fixed. It refers to a map that lacks a clearly defined shape. The overall “shape” of each topic is unique. This is because the relationships and chunk structures are unique. Unshaped occurs when the spatial arrangement is not emphasised. By being more deliberate and intentional with our spatial arrangement, we force a higher level of evaluation by being more explicit with how we want to express relationships and main flows. It also creates a visually more distinctive map which serves as an additional memory cue.

The type of map in this example is a common and normal iteration after Shooting. However, during Skin, we should try to create a clearer shape that expresses the backbone and main relationships more explicitly.

**Learning notes**

Read Between The Chunks

We see a mild case of *Islands* and *Cracked glass* in this example. Cracked glass, as we covered previously, is when chunks are connected via their sub-concepts instead of directly from chunk to chunk. These relationships are often less memorable and superficial. Often, it indicates a lack of clear evaluation between how chunks are related to each other, or the evaluation was done late in the learning process.

*Islands* are another manifestation of inadequate evaluation of how chunks relate to each other. Islands refers to a larger map that has been created from multiple maps superficially connected together. We often see that there are discrete and relatively isolated patches of dense and more heavily connected networks, and then just a couple of long arrows connecting it to another much denser network. This indicates that each “mini-map” was considered in isolation, without frequent consolidation into the big picture. It can occur if the prestudy was too detailed and narrow or with *segmental mapping*.

Both *Islands *and *Cracked glass* create the illusion of big-picture connectivity. However, the strength and significance of the relationships that connect chunks together are likely to be weak and easily forgettable.

**Learning notes**

Ascent 3 Stopper

Wheel and spokes is when the whole map radiates out from a single, central theme, like spokes on a bicycle wheel. This is often the topic title. In more advanced variations, the learner may repeatedly use a “wheel” or “cycle” pattern of chunk structure for all of their maps. Both of these are indications of inadequate independent chunking. Usually, the name of the topic does not need to be on the map at all. 

By placing a central theme, it creates the illusion that everything is connected, by this takes away from exploring the relationship between the key concepts and forming a distinctive backbone. When the main ideas branch from a central theme, it often only represents the idea that “these are the main concepts for this topic”, which should be expressed through a backbone instead. Wheel and spokes sometimes also occurs alongside *waterfalling*.

Wheel and spokes mistakes tend to create very similar-looking maps for all topics, with a relatively low level of mastery of the core concepts. Learners are not able to retrieve the knowledge in complex and nuanced ways because the core ideas are not deeply connected.

There is also insufficient chunking with five chunks radiating from the central theme. The visuals also need keyword anchors. This is an example of someone who likely rushed the program as these issues should never occur at an Ascent 3 level.

**Learning notes**

Shape Of You

This map is both* detail stripped* and *unshaped*. Detail stripped refers to a map where the more detailed layers are not expressed at all. It leads to a very simple and “barren” map. While this may be appropriate for the early phases of learning a new topic or for testing hypothetical chunk structures, leaving maps without sufficient detail is detrimental as it avoids processing the more detailed information that we will need to retrieve. It also makes it harder to see additional relationships between detailed concepts and facts, which may further refine and challenge our chunk structure.

Learners may avoid adding extra detail and keywords because they feel that they may be overwhelmed. This can be avoided by having a clearer backbone and logic layer, as well as more step-by-step chunking for each new “layer” of keywords and detail (i.e. inquiry, identifying similarities, proposing chunks, evaluating which chunks are most intuitive, and hypothesising chunk structures).
