# Unlearning
## Out with the old 

We have helped many people learn to learn more efficiently and gain more time in their lives. However, there are also many who struggle to improve due to issues with [**unlearning**](https://hbr.org/2016/11/why-the-problem-with-learning-is-unlearning).

### The ego of unlearning

**Unlearning** is the ability to replace old habits, skills, ideas and practices with alternatives that are more effective. This requires us to detach from our ego and acknowledge that our practices are sometimes wrong.

## Unlearning is crucial for success

[Studies have shown](https://edtechdigest.com/2017/07/13/what-is-unlearning/) that successful people have a strong ability to unlearn.

To adopt a new system or habit, you must take your old habits and unlearn them. If you retain the bad habits from your old learning system, this jeopardises the effectiveness of your new system. Be open to adopting new practices and exercise your unlearning skills.

![[Image 31.jpg|A diagram showing that open mindedness allows for integration of new ideals resulting in growth and transfomation.]]

Sometimes, the change we are looking for is not what we expect it to be.

Open-mindedness is a door that allows us to grow and transform, even if the change in methods and habits we need is **not** what we expect we need.

## Accelerate unlearning

### Tips for ensuring effective unlearning

1. Observe how you react when new methods seem to contradict your expectations. Do you remain open or close off to the idea?
2. Spot your hesitancies. Are there methods you feel reluctant to try because it goes against your existing system or habits? This could become problematic.
3. Avoid 'tug of war' practice. This is when you revert to old habits or alter methods to be 'easier' when new methods become difficult or unfamiliar. This prevents our brain from properly retraining itself. Tug of war practice can be subtle!
4. Avoid 'batman' style practice. This is when new methods are practised separately without replacing existing methods. Old habits by day, new methods by night! Like 'tug of war', this prevents retraining of habits.
