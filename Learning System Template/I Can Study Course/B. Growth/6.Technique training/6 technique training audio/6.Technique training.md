**Cave theory**

Cave brain in a modern world

Our brain is the same as it has been since caveman times, but modern learning is very different. The way we tend to learn now is deeply influenced by the formal education system and the industrial revolution.

As a result, the way many people learn is disconnected from how our brains want to and prefer learning. Our *cave theory* tells us we need to learn how the brain likes to learn, which makes learning more effective, engaging, and even enjoyable!

**Memory**

**What is a "good memory"?**

Information (or memory) is stored in your brain as a network. Your brain determines whether information is worth retaining (*remember Cave theory*) based on how connected this information is to other information.

**If it's part of a bigger network**, it judges it to be probably more important and, therefore, worth keeping. 

**If information is isolated and not very connected**, it is probably less relevant. Your efficient brain will cleanly prune it!

Simple information that is isolated (or only some simple connections) is forgotten easily.

**Survive and thrive**

**Survive-and-Thrive: It's all about the network**

Information is given value, meaning, and strength of memory based on it's relationship to other pieces of information. For example, if a fact is memorised by itself - it is not related to anything else. If a concept is understood, we might think we will remember it, but if the concept is not related to any other concepts, we will still forget it.

Groups and relationships create structure and organisation. The resulting network produces better memory.

Examples of methods that improve memory and depth

**All of the methods below are examples of techniques that help form networks of knowledge, which improve memory and depth:**

Creating analogies that are accurate and comprehensive

Relating new information to knowledge you already have

Simplifying new terminology by giving them simpler names you find more intuitive

Applying information and solving a problem immediately

Comparing new information with other new information to see how they are related to each other

Grouping new information into chunks based on shared similarities

Drawing arrows between chunks based on how the chunks affect each other

Positioning chunks on your notes in different places depending on the other concepts they might need to be connected to

## Types of relationships

Think about the cognitive processes involved in each of the methods above.

Notice how methods 1, 2, and 3 create connections between new information and existing knowledge (we call these analogous relationships), whereas methods 5 to 7 create connections between new information (we call these in-context relationships). Method 4 could be both analogous or in-context, depending on the type of problem we are trying to solve.

Relationships built with other new information (in-context) vs. with existing knowledge (analogous)

Therefore, we can say that there are two types of networks we can form with new information: analogous and in-context. When we learn, we should always try to make both types of connections, if possible.

There are also ways to help prime our brains to create relationships (both analogous and in-context). For example, doing some problem-solving or practice questions before learning a topic can help prime us about potential relationships.  that build big-picture understanding also give us more points of relevance to not only build analogous relationships but also make it easier to perceive and hypothesise in-context relationships.

Why does the difference in types of relationships matter?

Although we separate them into analogous vs. in-context in this lesson, many methods can help with both (directly or indirectly). The goal is not to be absolutely certain about which relationships are in-context vs. analogous but rather to remind ourselves to always look for both types of connections when possible. We may struggle to relate information to prior knowledge but find it easier to make in-context relationships, or vice-versa.

If you cannot make *any* relationships, it likely means what you are learning is too detailed for your current level of knowledge. Instead, we need to build a stronger foundation of surface-level knowledge with prestudy. We will introduce more techniques related to this concept in the later stages.

Our brain adapts to solve problems and learns things along the way. Good memory and depth of understanding are symptoms of your brain creating good knowledge structures. It does not come from simply **intending** to remember or understand something!

System 1 vs. system 2 thinking

The brain recognises if information is worth processing or not. It always looks for an energy-efficient shortcut. This split in the way our brain thinks has been noted in lots of research, including  by Daniel Kahneman, who won the Nobel Prize based on his work. He identified two systems of thinking, one fast and reactive (based on pattern recognition) and one slow and deliberate.

The hot-cold empathy gap

Differences in our brain's processing ability can also be seen in hot vs. cold decision-making. This phenomenon is where we make different decisions when under pressure or stimulated (hot) than when we are calm and calculating (cold).

**For example,** while you are “cold”, you decide to study for 2 hours after school. However, after school, you are tempted to watch YouTube or go out with your friends, and you make a different decision in the heat of the moment.

**5****.****Inquiry-based learning**

**The power of questions**

When we learn by creating problems and then answering them, it changes what information we consume and how we think about things. This process of seeking an answer and being curious, followed by satiating that curiosity, is called .

Inquiry and questions help your brain focus on what is relevant.

When we learn by solving problems, it also gives us more information to ask *even more* questions. This ongoing cycle results in a deeper, more applicable understanding and a higher retention rate. 

People often struggle to apply the knowledge they’ve learned. The real issue is that people usually do not try to apply information immediately after they've learned it.

*That’s like running down a road because someone told you to, only to realise an hour later that you don’t know where you’re going or why you’re running!*

**Key principle**

Studying only produces learning when the information you consume has a purpose. Problems can help create purpose.

Conventional learning is unfocused and causes you to learn too much information at once, making it harder to process. This causes you to forget the information quickly, so you will waste a lot of time going back and relearning it. In addition, your brain doesn’t like the unstructured nature of conventional learning, so you will feel the information is tedious and unenjoyable.

*It’s like throwing a bunch of items into a room, then realising you don’t know where anything is, so taking it all out and organising it again. The better approach would have been to organise it first!*

**🚦**** The traffic light system**

To start implementing inquiry-based learning, you can train your brain using the traffic light system (TLS). TLS is divided into **red light** vs. **green light** phases of learning.

**Red light **forces us to think of questions and directs our focus. It helps break bad habits of passive learning or just reading and consuming information without a clear big-picture purpose.

**Green light** is when we satiate our curiosity by answering those questions. This is when we read, listen, and search through content to find relationships, write our notes, and think about how everything is connected.

🔴 Red light

When you first start studying, create a list of keywords and concepts. It doesn't have to be comprehensive but try to cover the main ideas of the topic.

**Questions to ask during red light**

Once you have a list of keywords, ask yourself the following questions...

Which keywords feel easier or more relevant to me immediately? (Start with these keywords)

What am I curious about?

Why do I need to know this?

What problems does it help me solve or how can I apply this?

As you ask yourself these questions, you may think of other questions to ask, especially relating to what you are curious about. Make a note of these questions for the green light step coming next.

**By the end of red light, you should have a list of questions to answer.**

🟢 Green light

Go and answer those questions to learn the topic in an organic way. Don’t** **read everything and try to memorise all the information. Instead, focus on naturally answering the questions you have asked.

**Tips for green light**

While answering your questions, follow these principles...

Think of multiple answers for each question. The point is not to find one perfect answer but to use the question as a springboard to find multiple relationships.

As you explore each keyword, you will find many relationships between keywords. Try to express these in your notes. It will be messy at first!

After every 2 or 3 keywords, look at your growing map and try to simplify it and make it cleaner. Make judgments on what is important and worth keeping.

Continually revise your overall structure. Your map won't be "concrete" until much later. Everything you map now is just to help you think and organise your thoughts.

Green light finishes once you have answered all your questions from red light. Go back to *red light* and continue this cycle until you feel like you have learned most of the topic. 

**Some information may still be so detailed and specific that you struggle to answer the related questions. These can go on your flashcards or other lower-order rote-memorisation method for now.**

**Keep your note-taking non-linear!**

During TLS, you'll find that you discover lots of new relationships! Make sure to use good non-linear note-taking to help your brain think about and explore all these different relationships. Trying to figure it all out in your head will be overload!

**If you're still using the collecting and processing technique, now is a good time to start moving towards fully non-linear "****mindmap****" style note-taking.**

**Common questions**

Which keywords should I start with?

You can start from any keyword that you feel is an important idea. However, with correct inquiry, you should be able to start from any keyword and identify which words are truly the most important. 

The goal of inquiry is to compare and contrast ideas and see how concepts influence and are related. We then group words based on this information. Remember not to let your groups be dictated by how it was presented to you or your preconceived (potentially suboptimal) ideas about how the information should be organised. 

**To be an effective learner, we must arrive at our own critically examined conclusions about how to organise the information.**

Controversy and problems with inquiry-based learning (optional reading)

**Problems**

Researchers have recently contested the effectiveness and validity of inquiry-based learning. Their concerns are valid. Inquiry-based learning suffers from the following:

Lack of agreed definitions on what constitutes inquiry-based learning

No clear guidelines on how it can be implemented

Significant dependency on the facilitator

Significant variation in effectiveness observed in empirical research

Questionable underlying theory

Questionable underlying science

Furthermore, almost all of the research on inquiry-based learning is aimed at educators and curriculum makers rather than individual learners. It is currently not considered a method that individuals apply for themselves in self-regulated learning practice.

Learning researchers in the cognitivist school of thought (compared to traditionally constructivist perspectives on learning) argue that there is no validity to inquiry-based learning due to the above problems.

**Our perspective**

Inquiry-based learning in the current research space is indeed highly limited. In some cases, the way it is implemented is either useless or harmful. The direction of research in the mainstream research space is also not highly promising. Many researchers seem to be ignoring the criticisms and justifying the validity based on the same flawed arguments.

However, the underlying theory of inquiry-based learning has significant overlap with currently more empirically supported cognitivist theories within cognitive load theory. Our approach is to extract the useful aspects of inquiry-based learning and frame it from a self-regulated learner's perspective. Unlike any other approach to inquiry-based learning that we have seen, we teach it from a cognitivist perspective.

As a result, we have seen this approach being able to produce consistent, theoretically aligned effects in learners. The version of inquiry-based learning we teach is substantially modified and, therefore, technically not considered inquiry-based learning within mainstream research.

**6.****Order control**

**Taking control**

One of the key differences between inquiry-based learning and conventional learning is the order in which information is gathered. 

Because the types of relationships we build depend heavily on our prior knowledge and how we can make sense of information, the "best" order to learn a topic will differ for each person. 

Methods such as the *Traffic Light System* help us to be more deliberate about the order in which we learn new information.

**What does the right order mean?**

Many learners initially feel uncertain about learning in a different order from how the content is presented to them. Remember, resources are not created directly for your brain and set of knowledge. Experts try to take their knowledge network and present it in a way that they assume will make sense. 

**It is always the job of the learner to take this information and construct their own knowledge network. **

Expertise has no "order". It is non-linear. However, many learners prioritise the linear order of teaching.

**The ego of unlearning**

**Unlearning** is the ability to replace old habits, skills, ideas and practices with alternatives that are more effective. This requires us to detach from our ego and acknowledge that our practices are sometimes wrong.

**Unlearning is crucial for success**

 that successful people have a strong ability to unlearn.

To adopt a new system or habit, you must take your old habits and unlearn them. If you retain the bad habits from your old learning system, this jeopardises the effectiveness of your new system. Be open to adopting new practices and exercise your unlearning skills.

Sometimes, the change we are looking for is not what we expect it to be.

Open-mindedness is a door that allows us to grow and transform, even if the change in methods and habits we need is **not** what we expect we need.

**Accelerate unlearning**

**Tips for ensuring effective unlearning**

Observe how you react when new methods seem to contradict your expectations. Do you remain open or close off to the idea?

Spot your hesitancies. Are there methods you feel reluctant to try because it goes against your existing system or habits? This could become problematic.

Avoid 'tug of war' practice. This is when you revert to old habits or alter methods to be 'easier' when new methods become difficult or unfamiliar. This prevents our brain from properly retraining itself. Tug of war practice can be subtle!

Avoid 'batman' style practice. This is when new methods are practised separately without replacing existing methods. Old habits by day, new methods by night! Like 'tug of war', this prevents retraining of habits.

**ii.****Scheduling**** better – prioritising**

**Tasks over time**

Prioritising maximises the value of your time. Just 15 minutes spent prioritising can save you days. This is why task prioritisation is more important than time management.

**Not all time is spent equal.**

Time spent on something that gets you to your goals is more important than time spent on something less meaningful.

No matter how great your schedule is, it won’t help you reach your goals if it is not *aligned* with those goals. To achieve your goals as efficiently as possible, you must prioritise the *content* of your schedule.

When we have full schedules, but the tasks are not well-prioritised, we do not improve our position or progress towards our goal effectively.

Don't confuse being busy with being productive. Productive usage of time means that your time is spent on things that get you closer to your goals (whatever those goals are).

When most of our tasks are prioritised, we make much faster progress towards our goal without being any busier!

**A simple priority check**

At the end of every day, identify the one or two highest priority items that put the future you in the best position. Remember that urgent doesn't always mean important!

**Once you have identified these, block them into your schedule. All of your other tasks should be centred around these blocks.**

**Helpful questions for prioritising well**

Ask yourself these questions to identify high priority items to block out in your schedule

What are the one or two most important things for me to complete?

What (if done) would provide me the best value or put me in the best position in the future?

What skill, attribute, or resource do I need that would be life-changing (even if it isn't urgent right now)?

**iii. ****Scheduling tips**

Scheduling guidelines

This lesson will show you how to schedule according to your priorities. You can go through this like a checklist.

Set your priorities first, then set your schedule.

You should transition from setting weekly priorities to setting daily priorities. Every night, make sure to set your schedule for the next day.

Guidelines

Schedule your top two priority items first.

Use either a diary or digital schedule. For most people, digital apps are more efficient and manageable, but if you have a low volume of tasks, pen-and-paper may be sufficient.

Be very specific in your scheduling entries. To avoid procrastination, break down your study time into 30-minute blocks, and make sure to leave breaks and gaps.

Overestimate the time required to complete each task by 25 to 50%.

Ensure 15 minutes of buffer between each task.

Have at least one hour of unscheduled time per day as emergency.

Schedule time for reflecting and setting priorities at the end of every day.

**9.****Loss aversion**

"Good enough"

After seeing all of the skills and strategies in this course, you may feel it’s too difficult or time-consuming to implement and practise them.

Often, learners have expressed that their current methods are “good enough”. However, many people have commented to us in our community, through emails, reviews, and in-person, that they regretted this months or years later.

The small pond

People often compare themselves with those immediately around them. They feel satisfied as long as they outperform the people they can see. However, this is misleading if you are having to compete against others outside of your immediate visibility.

Ask yourself: Am I comparing myself to others, or am I comparing myself to my standards?

Key principle

Always seek to set realistic and appropriate standards and use them for comparison. Comparing yourself to others to understand your performance is risky, unreliable, and unhealthy.

Short-term pain, long-term gain

What is “good enough” now may not be good enough for the future. People have a misconception that if things become more difficult, they can solve it by just trying harder. However, this often fails because everyone else also tries harder!

Ask yourself: Am I preparing myself for future challenges?

Key principle

Always seek to use and familiarise yourself with methods and approaches that will help you both in the short and long term. If your current method is heavily effort and motivation-dependent, this leaves very little room for error or improvement.

Loss aversion bias

Loss aversion is a phenomenon whereby people always prefer not to lose something they already have, even if it means forgoing an opportunity to gain something else. Because of loss aversion, you may tend to think your current process is “good enough” and hesitate to make changes.

Ask yourself: Am I investing in gaining skills and assets that will benefit me for life, or am I avoiding the opportunity to gain tomorrow out of fear of losing today?

Key principle

Become aware of when you make decisions out of fear and avoidance. This can indicate that your decisions are being made with too much emotional mind and not enough rational mind!

Further reading on loss aversion

Loss aversion is a phenomenon that has been shown repeatedly in multiple replicated . There is also some newer  that suggests that the effects of loss aversion can be subtle and unpredictable.

Risk management

Part of overcoming the “good enough” mentality is systematically sorting through our “risks”. Sometimes, the risks we *perceive* in a decision or action may not be the full scope of the risks.

**Learning notes**

99.99% Of Beginners Makes This Mistake

Studying over learning is when people do not understand how to correctly measure their learning efficiency. Traditionally, learners measure their learning efficiency based on simply content covered over a period of time. However, this is problematic because it drastically oversimplifies learning to the point where measuring efficiency in this way becomes harmful. The consequences of *studying over learning* are demotivation and frustration as the marginal gains of efficiency gain are not seen. We strongly recommend that you review our article on how to measure learning efficiency .

The other mistake seen in this case is *brief reflection*. This is simply when there is not much information given in the reflection part of Kolb’s. This may occur due to inadequate self-awareness (which must be developed over time) or a lack of effort (which can be solved immediately). A common sign of brief reflection is when only single sentence answers or a couple bullet points are given as the answer to each prompting question of Kolb’s reflection.

**Learning notes**

A One Way Traffic Light

Simple question and answer is a common mistake for those first engaging in self-directed inquiry-based learning. The purpose of questioning is to help direct the brain to identify relationships and similarities. These similarities are the foundation for creating groups and chunks. The relationships also help to create a flow of interconnected chunks and concepts. In simple question and answer, learners do not explore multiple possible answers and instead just answer each question as directly as possible, as if it were an exam. This bypasses the discovery of relationships, which makes chunking and relational learning very difficult.

The other mistake visible is *lower-order inquiry*. This is when the questions only have one possible answer to begin with. These questions are often fact-based and start with “what”. These questions are not useful for forming networks and enhancing memory or depth of understanding. Often, lower-order questions can make learners feel overwhelmed while also being ineffective for learning. For more information, we recommend you review the lessons on the Traffic Light System for guidelines on how to ask higher-order questions.

Beginner Prestudying - Room For Room

The *no backbone* mistake is a common mistake that can often be fixed easily and offers a large benefit for memory and understanding. No backbone refers to the lack of a clearly identified set of main ideas or chunks *and* the lack of a clear flow between these main ideas or chunks. The result can be a map that has relationships (sometimes many more than in the example case here), but it is unclear how it connects together or to gain a sense of the big picture. It can also occur when there is too much focus on the details at an early stage of the learning. Make sure to keep an eye on the big picture and constantly look for the clearest and simplest way to express these ideas.
