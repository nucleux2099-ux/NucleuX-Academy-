## Question 2

# Answer

Regarding what you have learned about the nature of memory, which of the following methods do not build networks of knowledge? Select all that apply.

Creating an analogy for a new concept.

**Reading and re-writing information multiple times.**

**Creating and reviewing flashcards on simple facts and concepts.**

Mapping how concepts relate to other concepts.

Creating simplified groups of information based on their similarities.

---

# Learning notes

Reading and re-writing, as well as isolated fact recall with flashcards are both examples of common methods that do not create knowledge networks. This results in lower overall retention and increased workload to try and reach the desired level of performance.

However, flashcards still have a positive effect on retention through spaced retrieval. For some types of information, this can be a valid method to add to a toolkit. It is unlikely to be sufficient if used as the primary method of learning.


## Question 3

# Answer

Regarding what you have learned about the nature of memory, which of the following methods help to improve retention but not necessarily the ability to use information for complex applications? Select the most correct answer.

Creating an analogy for a new concept.

Reading and re-writing information multiple times.

**Creating and reviewing flashcards on simple facts and concepts.**

Mapping how concepts relate to other concepts.

Creating simplified groups of information based on their similarities.

---

# Learning notes

Flashcards can improve memory through spaced retrieval. However, they do not build networks if used on isolated facts and concepts. 

Creating an analogy for a single new concept arguably has minimal impact on building knowledge that is helpful for complex applications. Creating analogies for multiple concepts and how they relate to each other would be much more helpful. However, flashcards are still the most correct answer in that they generate even less networks than analogies of single concepts.
