# Survive and thrive
## Survive-and-Thrive: It's all about the network

Information is given value, meaning, and strength of memory based on it's relationship to other pieces of information. For example, if a fact is memorised by itself - it is not related to anything else. If a concept is understood, we might think we will remember it, but if the concept is not related to any other concepts, we will still forget it.

![[Image 28.jpg|Connected and grouped information is retained]]

Groups and relationships create structure and organisation. The resulting network produces better memory.

### Examples of methods that improve memory and depth

**All of the methods below are examples of techniques that help form networks of knowledge, which improve memory and depth:**

1. Creating analogies that are accurate and comprehensive
2. Relating new information to knowledge you already have
3. Simplifying new terminology by giving them simpler names you find more intuitive
4. Applying information and solving a problem immediately
5. Comparing new information with other new information to see how they are related to each other
6. Grouping new information into chunks based on shared similarities
7. Drawing arrows between chunks based on how the chunks affect each other
8. Positioning chunks on your notes in different places depending on the other concepts they might need to be connected to

## Types of relationships

Think about the **cognitive processes** involved in each of the methods above.

Notice how methods 1, 2, and 3 create connections between new information and existing knowledge (we call these _analogous relationships_), whereas methods 5 to 7 create connections between new information (_we call these in-context relationships_). Method 4 could be both analogous or in-context, depending on the type of problem we are trying to solve.

![[Image 29.jpg|Relationships built with other new information (in-context) vs. with existing knowledge (analogous)]]

Relationships built with other new information (in-context) vs. with existing knowledge (analogous)

**Therefore, we can say that there are two types of networks we can form with new information: analogous and in-context.** When we learn, we should always try to make both types of connections, if possible.

There are also ways to help prime our brains to create relationships (both analogous and in-context). For example, doing some problem-solving or practice questions _before_ learning a topic can help prime us about potential relationships. [Correct prestudy techniques](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-support/prestudy-guide) that build big-picture understanding also give us more points of relevance to not only build analogous relationships but also make it easier to perceive and hypothesise in-context relationships.

### Examples of analogous relationships

- Jill learns about the physiology of muscle contraction. She tries to make sense of it by creating an analogy of a person climbing up a ladder.
- Geoffrey learns about how a dam generates electricity using water currents. He makes sense of this by relating the mechanisms to his understanding of how wind turbines work that he learned in the previous semester.
- Sarah is learning about the principles of economics. She forms an analogy by equating supply and demand to a seesaw, where the equilibrium point is the balance achieved when both sides are equally weighted.
- Robert is exploring the principles of genetics in his biology class. He draws upon his prior knowledge of Mendelian inheritance from a previous semester to understand how genes are passed from one generation to the next in complex genetic traits.
- Emily is learning about international trade and globalization in her economics course. She connects this to her previous studies in history, understanding that trade routes and the exchange of goods have shaped the course of civilizations throughout history.
- Samuel is diving into the study of 20th-century literature. He relates the themes and social contexts of the literature he is currently reading to his earlier coursework in sociology, recognizing how societal changes influenced the literary works of that era.
- Ethan is exploring the concept of chemical reactions and catalysts. He relates this to a cooking analogy, likening reactants to ingredients in a recipe and catalysts to a chef's secret ingredient that speeds up the cooking process.
- Alex is studying the structure of an atom in chemistry. To simplify it, he creates an analogy where the nucleus is the "core" of an atom, and electrons are like planets orbiting around it, similar to our solar system.

### Examples of in-context relationships

- Sarah is studying environmental science and has just learned about the water cycle. She immediately applies this knowledge to analyze the recent weather patterns in her region, forming an in-context relationship between the theory of the water cycle and its real-world application.
- In a history course, Michael is exploring the causes and consequences of World War I. He compares and contrasts these with the events and outcomes of World War II, identifying in-context relationships between the two major historical events.
- In a biology lecture, Finn is studying cell division and the cell cycle. He makes theoretical connections to the concept of mitosis and meiosis, understanding how these processes govern cell replication and genetic diversity.
- In an economics seminar, Joanna is delving into supply and demand dynamics. She observes the theoretical link between supply and demand curves and the concept of market equilibrium, where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied.
- In a music theory class, Jihee is learning about chord progressions and harmonic structures. She discerns the theoretical connection between diatonic chords and the concept of tonal harmony, understanding how certain chords naturally complement each other within a musical key.
- In a chemistry lab, Emily is experimenting with chemical reactions and observing how different factors affect reaction rates. She draws connections between her observations and the chemical principles she learned in lectures, creating in-context relationships between theory and practical experimentation.
- John is taking a finance course and has just studied the concept of compound interest. He immediately applies this knowledge when evaluating different investment options, forming in-context relationships between the financial theory he's learned and its practical implications for personal finance decisions.

### Why does the difference in types of relationships matter?

Although we separate them into analogous vs. in-context in this lesson, many methods can help with both (directly or indirectly). The goal is not to be absolutely certain about which relationships are in-context vs. analogous but rather to remind ourselves to always look for both types of connections when possible. We may struggle to relate information to prior knowledge but find it easier to make in-context relationships, or vice-versa.

If you cannot make _any_ relationships, it likely means what you are learning is too detailed for your current level of knowledge. Instead, we need to build a stronger foundation of surface-level knowledge with prestudy. We will introduce more techniques related to this concept in the later stages.
# Memory and understanding are symptoms

Our brain adapts to solve problems and learns things along the way. Good memory and depth of understanding are symptoms of your brain creating good knowledge structures. It does not come from simply **intending** to remember or understand something!

In the classical view of Bloom's taxonomy, memorising and understanding are considered the bottom levels. We commonly refer to this as lower-order learning. However, Bloom's has also been criticised for representing knowledge and learning in a hierarchy. We know now that this is not entirely accurate.

![[03 Spaces/Learning System/attachments/Image 20.png|A classical view on Bloom's revised taxonomy (2001)]]

The classical view on Bloom's revised taxonomy (2001)

In reality, memory and understanding occur when we engage in effective learning strategies. It is not a linear order we must follow, starting from the bottom and working our way up to the top. In authentic learning, we constantly go back and forth between levels. Dr Justin Sung created his own framework to explain this.

# Related additional reading (optional)
## System 1 vs. system 2 thinking

The brain recognises if information is worth processing or not. It always looks for an energy-efficient shortcut. This split in the way our brain thinks has been noted in lots of research, including [Thinking Fast and Slow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thinking,_Fast_and_Slow) by Daniel Kahneman, who won the Nobel Prize based on his work. He identified two systems of thinking, one fast and reactive (based on pattern recognition) and one slow and deliberate.
## The hot-cold empathy gap

Differences in our brain's processing ability can also be seen in hot vs. cold decision-making. This phenomenon is where we make different decisions when under pressure or stimulated (hot) than when we are calm and calculating (cold).

**For example,** while you are “cold”, you decide to study for 2 hours after school. However, after school, you are tempted to watch YouTube or go out with your friends, and you make a different decision in the heat of the moment.

This difference in how we think causes so many problems that there is even something called the [hot-cold empathy gap](https://www.behavioraleconomics.com/resources/mini-encyclopedia-of-be/hot-cold-empathy-gap/), which talks about the problems that arise from the bad decisions we make when we are hot.
