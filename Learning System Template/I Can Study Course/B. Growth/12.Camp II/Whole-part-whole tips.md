# Whole-part-whole tips
Tips to execute WPW correctly

1. Use reverse explanations.
2. Always look for big-picture relevance.
3. Don’t get locked into the way you taught it or learned it the first time. The ideal WPW flow may not be the same as your GRINDE map layers.
4. Teach based on your memory/recall.
5. Doodle a mind map as you are teaching.
6. Speak out loud to slow yourself down. Don’t rush.
7. Give yourself at least 3 days after finishing your GRINDE map.
8. Use full recall to find gaps, then revise them to perfect your recall.
9. If you struggle at first, keep trying. This means you are finding gaps.

## General teaching flow

WPW teaching should follow two stages.
### Stage 1: Layman's terms

_Layman’s terms_ refer to common terminology that most people can understand, even without prior knowledge of the topic.

**There are four parts to this stage.** You should cover them in order, using only layman’s terms (no terminology):

1. Explain what the topic is generally about (big picture).
2. Explain why this is important to know.
3. Explain how the topic can be divided into major components.
4. Explain how the components relate to each other.

Once you have covered these four parts, move on to Stage 2.

This stage tests our understanding of **concepts and relationships** at the first and second layers of learning. This is good for protecting yourself against conceptual questions, difficult short-answer questions and curveball questions in exams.

_By the end of this stage, the “listener” should have a solid conceptual understanding of the overall topic, why it is important, what its major components are, and how those components relate to each other._

### Stage 2: Technical explanation

This stage involves introducing terminology. 

**There are two parts to this stage:**

1. Add technical terminology to the components you have explained. (This is known as **labelling**.)
2. Using the terminology, explain each component in full detail. (This is known as **technical elaboration**.)

Ineffective teaching introduces the terminology first and teaches the definition and explanation later. By forcing yourself to explain the topic without relying on the terminology, you challenge your understanding of the concepts and relationships.

During the technical explanation, you must recall details and specific information. It protects us against detail-oriented questions, definitions and pure-recall questions.

_By the end of this stage, they should know the technical names of concepts and components and be able to make sense of the details._
