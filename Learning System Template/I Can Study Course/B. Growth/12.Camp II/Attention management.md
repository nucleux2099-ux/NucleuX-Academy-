# Attention management
## About attention management

Attention Management is a system for optimising flow. Rather than prioritising the time of day, it prioritises maintaining flow states for as long as possible, particularly by bridging flow states between tasks.

Attention management can allow for extremely high levels of productivity that can appear superhuman to others. When used alongside refined prioritisation systems, it may be possible to increase your meaningful goal-oriented output by 10x compared to the normal person.

Key principles for attention management

- Train yourself to have a keen level of self-awareness at where your attention is and if it aligns with your intentions. You must self-regulate your actions to prevent yourself from straying into unintended tasks and activities.
- Maintain flow at all times. Do not worry about when you finish or start a task. Simply operate in deep flow, trusting that this produces the fastest possible output at high quality.
- Remove decisional delays throughout the day. Every decisional delay is a flow-breaker. You want to be as close as possible to a robot following a set of pre-programmed instructions.
- Optimise your environments ahead of time to remove the need for environmental optimisation during the day.
- Utilise work-rest timing with active relaxation and recovery via breathing techniques for as many rests as possible.

### Note

You will be forced to break out of flow and attention management when there are scheduled events, such as meetings, appointments, classes, etc.

This is unavoidable, so you may wish to plan small reprioritisation sessions intermittently throughout the day, before and after these mandatory disruptions and events.

## Common questions

### How do we measure the success of our attention management?

In conventional time management, we measure productivity by our ability to achieve planned tasks within a set time period. However, this has its own limitations. What if you are at 60% efficiency and focus? What if you waste 20 minutes procrastinating? What if you get distracted so much that you only get 45 minutes of real work done? 

With attention management, we measure success based on our ability to achieve a planned task (e.g. "study the 3rd unit of biology to an 80% level of depth”) and how long we were able to maintain flow. It prioritises the achievement of the task over the time it took, understanding that our performance during flow states is likely near our maximum pace anyway.

### When is attention management not appropriate?

Attention management prioritises the achievement of the task over the time it took, understanding that our performance during flow states is likely near our maximum pace anyway.

This approach is not always the best. For example, when time-boxing tasks, what is more important is that we limit the time we spend on a task rather than the magnitude of output. This is one reason why attention management is an advanced skill that requires significant experience with conventional time and task management principles.

### How do I schedule for a day with attention management?

With conventional time management, we may set a task like "study biology" for two hours. If the task takes longer, we may micromanage our time and reschedule other items or use pre-scheduled buffer time. If the task ends earlier, we risk introducing decisional delays in planning what to do with the unexpected extra time.

**Of course, when there is not much to do with a relaxed workload, this is no problem. We can simply rest and take time off! However, these small delays can be stressful when under heavy workloads.**

With attention management, we create more generic time blocks such as "study". Once we achieve the goal for quantity and depth of learning we wish to achieve, we simply move to the next priority item for studying. If there is no next priority item for studying, we can directly move on to the next task in our plan.

Often, we can use these small pockets to handle "non-urgent and important" tasks or "urgent but not important" tasks - both of which do not require task-level completion, but simply some level of time dedication. Microlearning might also be implemented.

### What about the power of deadlines and Parkinson's law?

Parkinson’s Law says that any activity or task fills the time given to it. If an essay is due in two weeks, it takes two weeks. If it’s due in two days, it takes two days. The difference in quality is insignificant. This is why so many of us leave assignments to the last minute – we know we can do it, and the result will be much the same!

_Read about_ [_Parkinson’s Law on Wikipedia_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parkinson%27s_law)_._

**Even though we are not keeping to time-based deadlines with attention management, Parkinson’s Law can still be applied to our advantage.**

To create a Parkinson's Law effect, set an estimated time to complete your task and a timer. Your goal is to finish it in that time. If you finish early, move to the next task. If you finish late, then Parkinson’s Law wasn’t enough to boost your output anyway.

Note that timing every focus session can also create unnecessary stress and pressure which has a psychological toll. It can be useful when you want to push yourself, but it is not recommended as a regular tactic.

### Are there any long-term benefits of attention management compared to conventional time management?

There are some significant benefits to using attention management on a regular basis:

1. You develop a keener self-awareness of your attention vs. your intentions. This helps to notice procrastination, distractions, and drifting focus earlier, which allows for faster self-correction.
2. It becomes easier to refocus and centre once distracted because you are constantly practising to bring your attention in alignment with intentions (almost like extended focus training).
3. It becomes easier to enter flow states of deep focus and concentration that use energy and cognitive resources very efficiently.
