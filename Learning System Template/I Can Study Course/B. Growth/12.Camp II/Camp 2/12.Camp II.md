**Preparing for revision**

We have covered the four layers of learning and the tools to learn each layer properly (including GRINDE maps, flashcards, and the modified method of loci).

The only thing left is a strong** revision strategy** to fill any gaps.

Before revising, you should quickly sweep through your course material to see if you’ve missed anything you need to learn. You can also use the modified method of loci to improve your mind map by replacing lists of information with drawings.

**Revision pitfalls**

**The nature of revision**

**Revision** involves looking at the information you have learned and then finding and filling any gaps. A robust revision strategy will catch any small details you missed when learning the information, meaning you don’t need to worry too much about missing things!

Many students only revise by doing past papers or re-reading notes. Often, their strategies are passive and do not engage the appropriate levels of challenge that we introduced in the *interleaving table*.

Key principles for a safe revision strategy

Interleave with different methods at different orders and types of knowledge.

Use retrieval methods that are strategic for finding your gaps and filling them.

Always utilise recall over recognition.

Maximise the benefit from past papers using more active and elaborative methods, instead of passively solving and checking answers.

**Alert**

If you are unsure about which methods of retrieval are suitable for finding gaps or would help maximise the benefit from practice questions, we **strongly** recommend reviewing the previous recommended tasks around interleaved retrieval and the interleaving table.

**At this point in the program, all of this should be a recap.**

**What defines a knowledge gap?**

A knowledge gap is any missing information or relationship that is important and relevant. You can identify gaps by assessing and testing yourself. There are different kinds of gaps, explained below.

You struggle to identify the correct approach and strategy to solving a problem

This indicates a declarative knowledge gap on how concepts can be related to each other and their purpose/influences. This kind of gap is common among engineering, coding, physics, and maths where there is insufficient attention given to the conceptual network and excessive focus is on procedural repetition and practice.

You struggle to recall information

This indicates a broad retrieval gap that should be filled with deeper relational encoding or a lower-order memorisation method, depending on the type of information and the layer it belongs to. If a high volume of information is forgotten, the problem is almost always due to a suboptimal chunk structure. Evaluate your maps for *waterfalling* and *spiderwebbing* and ensure the chunks are *intuitive*. Beware of *simple question and answer*, which can undermine the inquiry process.

You struggle with consistently solving problems due to errors

This indicates a procedural knowledge gap caused by a lack of practice and execution-style interleaving. In some cases, the errors are due to conceptual/declarative knowledge misunderstandings.

You struggle to apply knowledge in different contexts or more nuanced settings

This indicates a higher-order knowledge gap caused by missing relationships.

Key principles for finding gaps

Look for gaps - don't avoid your weaknesses

Test at multiple orders

Do not refer to answer sheets until you have created your own answer sheet through self-study first. Premature answer sheet checking often creates illusions of knowledge and is a common mistake.

Remember that struggling to answer a question indicates a gap. Even if you get the question right, you might just have gotten lucky! Difficulty matters more than correctness.

**How do you measure learning efficiency?**

**Skill development**

**The problem with efficiency**

Most people think about learning efficiency as how much content you can cover in a learning session. For example, an efficient learner can cover 5 lectures in 2 hours.

**However, this is very problematic for multiple reasons:**

It does not consider the retention of knowledge

Extra time is spent relearning information that is forgotten

It does not consider the quality of learning that occurred or the person's ability to perform and use their knowledge in different ways

**Ultimately, thinking about efficiency as the "amount covered per session" is a useless metric.**

Efficient learners focus on learning to high quality. Inefficient learners focus on "doing more".

**The components of efficient learning**

What we want to know is the amount of time it takes for a learner to gain high-quality, usable knowledge and retain this information for their purposes. Efficient learning can be summarised as:

Achieving the necessary mastery of knowledge

Having sufficient retention of this knowledge at each level of required mastery

Being able to achieve the above in a short amount of time

**The learning efficiency equation**

*Learning efficiency = Total mastery and retention over Total time spent learning*

For example, we may say that an inefficient learner achieves 50% over 100 hours, while an efficient learner achieves 90% over 50 hours. We will explain what this means below.

*Total mastery = average of all partial masteries*

*Partial mastery = retention per mastery level / required retention per mastery level*

**How to calculate the values**

Identify the highest and lowest required knowledge mastery levels. Refer to our article on "How to measure knowledge mastery".

Assess your retention after one week (as a percentage) for each level. We use one week because our spacing intervals should allow for a one-week retrieval session. It can be extrapolated to the level of retention you may have for an assessment.

Identify the retention you require for each level for your assessment.

Calculate partial mastery by dividing your real retention after one week by the required retention.

Calculate total mastery by taking the average of your partial masteries.

Calculate the total time you spent on all learning and retrieval sessions for this subject (across however many weeks).

**Inefficient learners**

When we complete this calculation for inefficient learners, we often find that as hours increase, the total mastery percentage does not increase much. For example, if Jeremy is not a very efficient learner, his 30 hours may increase to 60 while his mastery only improves to 76%.

If the methods are limited, more hours of studying produces smaller and smaller benefits.

**By doubling his hours of studying, his results have only improved by 10%.**

This is because certain methods are inherently limited.

**Analogy**

It's like trying to build a house with only a hammer. At first, you can build more and faster by spending more time building and swinging your hammer. However, at some point, there are certain tasks that you cannot achieve with only a hammer. It doesn't matter how much time is spent or how hard we swing it.

This is why efficient learners must use methods specifically to achieve certain mastery levels.

Key considerations

Efficient learners can achieve a higher percentage in less time.

More time does not necessary increase the percentage of achieved mastery and retention. In most cases, the rate of forgetting becomes so high with large volumes of learning that learners never reach 100% retention, especially at higher levels of mastery.

The method of learning strongly dictates how easily higher levels of mastery can be attained. Some methods are frankly incompatible with achieving higher mastery, unless you have very high deep processing.

Learning efficiency depends on the required level of mastery and retention. Lower requirements are easier to meet in less time.

**Reconstruction**

**About reconstruction**

*Reconstruction* goes a step beyond recall. It involves not only recalling the information but also applying it to construct new and valid relationships.

This forces you to evaluate each piece of information critically (to see if your new relationships are accurate) while comparing and examining multiple chunks.

**Key principle**

Reconstruction works at the ‘Evaluate’ and ‘Create’ levels while also testing the lower orders.

In the next lesson, we will use a heavily reconstructive retrieval method.

**Which is better?**

Since reconstruction-based strategies tend to take longer, it is best to use a combination** **of reconstruction and recall during your revision. Some students may find 10% recall and 90% reconstruction efficient, while others may find 50% recall and 50% reconstruction more manageable. 

In the long term, most students find it efficient to do more reconstruction in the earlier phase of their learning. This is because it allows more rapid and comprehensive gap-finding. Recall can be used in later phases to develop more detailed retrieval performance and mastery.

**Whole-part-whole technique**

**About Whole-Part-Whole (WPW)**

The **WPW technique** involves looking at the topic as a whole, breaking it down into parts, and then returning to it as a whole.

It challenges your higher-order structures and clarity of prioritised relationships (ability to explain the whole clearly), the strength of relationships between layers (ability to move into parts and back into whole smoothly), and recall of detailed information (ability to explain each part accurately).

**Meta-checklist**

This table outlines how using this technique can feel and what these feelings mean at a cognitive level. Refer to this to ensure you understand the technique’s purpose and improve your metacognition. Connecting your emotional experience with the cognitive processes of learning can help you make fine-tuned adjustments and solve any issues.

**Whole-part-whole tips**

Tips to execute WPW correctly

Use reverse explanations.

Always look for big-picture relevance.

Don’t get locked into the way you taught it or learned it the first time. The ideal WPW flow may not be the same as your GRINDE map layers.

Teach based on your memory/recall.

Doodle a mind map as you are teaching.

Speak out loud to slow yourself down. Don’t rush.

Give yourself at least 3 days after finishing your GRINDE map.

Use full recall to find gaps, then revise them to perfect your recall.

If you struggle at first, keep trying. This means you are finding gaps.

**General teaching flow**

WPW teaching should follow two stages.

Stage 1: Layman's terms

*Layman’s terms* refer to common terminology that most people can understand, even without prior knowledge of the topic.

**There are four parts to this stage.** You should cover them in order, using only layman’s terms (no terminology):

Explain what the topic is generally about (big picture).

Explain why this is important to know.

Explain how the topic can be divided into major components.

Explain how the components relate to each other.

Once you have covered these four parts, move on to Stage 2.

This stage tests our understanding of **concepts and relationships** at the first and second layers of learning. This is good for protecting yourself against conceptual questions, difficult short-answer questions and curveball questions in exams.

*By the end of this stage, the “listener” should have a solid conceptual understanding of the overall topic, why it is important, what its major components are, and how those components relate to each other. *

Stage 2: Technical explanation

This stage involves introducing terminology. 

**There are two parts to this stage:**

Add technical terminology to the components you have explained. (This is known as **labelling**.)

Using the terminology, explain each component in full detail. (This is known as **technical elaboration**.)

Ineffective teaching introduces the terminology first and teaches the definition and explanation later. By forcing yourself to explain the topic without relying on the terminology, you challenge your understanding of the concepts and relationships.

During the technical explanation, you must recall details and specific information. It protects us against detail-oriented questions, definitions and pure-recall questions.

*By the end of this stage, they should know the technical names of concepts and components and be able to make sense of the details.*

Terminology is introduced, then explained, in that order. This ensures the student knows what it is called and what the definition or meaning is.

You identify gaps in your knowledge because you struggle to explain it simply, navigate from big picture to smaller details fluently, or recall the specific details within the parts. When this happens, you look at your notes or dive deeper into the topic to gain clarity and re-encode.

You maintain your fluency of teaching by referring to your notes and resources as you go so that your teaching flow is not disrupted (much like how you may teach a real class).

**Decisional delays**

**What are decisional delays?**

Decisional delays are the time it takes to decide what to do next. Cutting this time out may potentially save you hours a day. Decisional delays also interrupt your *flow state.*

Decisional delays are harmful because...

They are barriers to maintaining flow states. This has potentially game-changing impacts on overall productivity as each break in flow requires an average of 15 minutes to re-enter flow. This can easily contribute to over an hour of delays daily.

They use limited cognitive resources on planning that can make you unnecessarily tired and unfocused.

They provide minimal positive value. The extra processing time does not confer any meaningful advantage.

**Identifying decisional delays**

Follow these instructions to start becoming aware of how decisional delays affect you.

Use a time-tracking app and track how you spend your time every day for around one week.

Every time you break out of a deep work/flow state, end the task time tracking and restart it once you are re-entering a focused session.

Compare the time you tracked to the schedule you set.

Identify the amount of time that was spent between tasks that your schedule did not plan for and the number of flow breaks that occurred. Each flow break can create 10 to 15 minutes of cognitive disruption and "lost time".

Using these numbers, you can estimate how much time and disruption is caused by decisional delays.

Note that simply tracking your time and becoming aware of decisional delays is an important first step towards solving them.

**How to mitigate decisional delays**

Experiment with your schedule and task flow to batch planning and prioritisation at the beginning or end of each day to avoid the need for mid-day planning.

Block distractions from focus zones so new decisions do not enter your radar while you are working.

Create very clear and specific instructions for yourself during planning sessions so there is no doubt or need for decision-making when you are in execution mode.

**Attention management**

**About attention management**

Attention Management is a system for optimising flow. Rather than prioritising the time of day, it prioritises maintaining flow states for as long as possible, particularly by bridging flow states between tasks.

Attention management can allow for extremely high levels of productivity that can appear superhuman to others. When used alongside refined prioritisation systems, it may be possible to increase your meaningful goal-oriented output by 10x compared to the normal person.

Key principles for attention management

Train yourself to have a keen level of self-awareness at where your attention is and if it aligns with your intentions. You must self-regulate your actions to prevent yourself from straying into unintended tasks and activities.

Maintain flow at all times. Do not worry about when you finish or start a task. Simply operate in deep flow, trusting that this produces the fastest possible output at high quality.

Remove decisional delays throughout the day. Every decisional delay is a flow-breaker. You want to be as close as possible to a robot following a set of pre-programmed instructions.

Optimise your environments ahead of time to remove the need for environmental optimisation during the day.

Utilise work-rest timing with active relaxation and recovery via breathing techniques for as many rests as possible.

**Note**

You will be forced to break out of flow and attention management when there are scheduled events, such as meetings, appointments, classes, etc.

This is unavoidable, so you may wish to plan small reprioritisation sessions intermittently throughout the day, before and after these mandatory disruptions and events.

**Common questions**

How do we measure the success of our attention management?

In conventional time management, we measure productivity by our ability to achieve planned tasks within a set time period. However, this has its own limitations. What if you are at 60% efficiency and focus? What if you waste 20 minutes procrastinating? What if you get distracted so much that you only get 45 minutes of real work done? 

With attention management, we measure success based on our ability to achieve a planned task (e.g. "study the 3rd unit of biology to an 80% level of depth”) and how long we were able to maintain flow. It prioritises the achievement of the task over the time it took, understanding that our performance during flow states is likely near our maximum pace anyway.

When is attention management not appropriate?

Attention management prioritises the achievement of the task over the time it took, understanding that our performance during flow states is likely near our maximum pace anyway.

This approach is not always the best. For example, when time-boxing tasks, what is more important is that we limit the time we spend on a task rather than the magnitude of output. This is one reason why attention management is an advanced skill that requires significant experience with conventional time and task management principles.

How do I schedule for a day with attention management?

With conventional time management, we may set a task like "study biology" for two hours. If the task takes longer, we may micromanage our time and reschedule other items or use pre-scheduled buffer time. If the task ends earlier, we risk introducing decisional delays in planning what to do with the unexpected extra time.

**Of course, when there is not much to do with a relaxed workload, this is no problem. We can simply rest and take time off! However, these small delays can be stressful when under heavy workloads.**

With attention management, we create more generic time blocks such as "study". Once we achieve the goal for quantity and depth of learning we wish to achieve, we simply move to the next priority item for studying. If there is no next priority item for studying, we can directly move on to the next task in our plan.

Often, we can use these small pockets to handle "non-urgent and important" tasks or "urgent but not important" tasks - both of which do not require task-level completion, but simply some level of time dedication. Microlearning might also be implemented.

What about the power of deadlines and Parkinson's law?

Parkinson’s Law says that any activity or task fills the time given to it. If an essay is due in two weeks, it takes two weeks. If it’s due in two days, it takes two days. The difference in quality is insignificant. This is why so many of us leave assignments to the last minute – we know we can do it, and the result will be much the same!

**Even though we are not keeping to time-based deadlines with attention management, Parkinson’s Law can still be applied to our advantage.**

To create a Parkinson's Law effect, set an estimated time to complete your task and a timer. Your goal is to finish it in that time. If you finish early, move to the next task. If you finish late, then Parkinson’s Law wasn’t enough to boost your output anyway.

Note that timing every focus session can also create unnecessary stress and pressure which has a psychological toll. It can be useful when you want to push yourself, but it is not recommended as a regular tactic.

Are there any long-term benefits of attention management compared to conventional time management?

There are some significant benefits to using attention management on a regular basis:

You develop a keener self-awareness of your attention vs. your intentions. This helps to notice procrastination, distractions, and drifting focus earlier, which allows for faster self-correction.

It becomes easier to refocus and centre once distracted because you are constantly practising to bring your attention in alignment with intentions (almost like extended focus training).

It becomes easier to enter flow states of deep focus and concentration that use energy and cognitive resources very efficiently.
