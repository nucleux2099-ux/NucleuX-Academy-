# Revision pitfalls
## The nature of revision

**Revision** involves looking at the information you have learned and then finding and filling any gaps. A robust revision strategy will catch any small details you missed when learning the information, meaning you don’t need to worry too much about missing things!

Many students only revise by doing past papers or re-reading notes. Often, their strategies are passive and do not engage the appropriate levels of challenge that we introduced in the _interleaving table_.

Key principles for a safe revision strategy

1. Interleave with different methods at different orders and types of knowledge.
2. Use retrieval methods that are strategic for finding your gaps and filling them.
3. Always utilise recall over recognition.
4. Maximise the benefit from past papers using more active and elaborative methods, instead of passively solving and checking answers.

## What defines a knowledge gap?

A knowledge gap is any missing information or relationship that is important and relevant. You can identify gaps by assessing and testing yourself. There are different kinds of gaps, explained below.
### You struggle to identify the correct approach and strategy to solving a problem

This indicates a declarative knowledge gap on how concepts can be related to each other and their purpose/influences. This kind of gap is common among engineering, coding, physics, and maths where there is insufficient attention given to the conceptual network and excessive focus is on procedural repetition and practice.
### You struggle to recall information

This indicates a broad retrieval gap that should be filled with deeper relational encoding or a lower-order memorisation method, depending on the type of information and the layer it belongs to. If a high volume of information is forgotten, the problem is almost always due to a suboptimal chunk structure. Evaluate your maps for _waterfalling_ and _spiderwebbing_ and ensure the chunks are _intuitive_. Beware of _simple question and answer_, which can undermine the inquiry process.
### You struggle with consistently solving problems due to errors

This indicates a procedural knowledge gap caused by a lack of practice and execution-style interleaving. In some cases, the errors are due to conceptual/declarative knowledge misunderstandings.

### You struggle to apply knowledge in different contexts or more nuanced settings

This indicates a higher-order knowledge gap caused by missing relationships.




Key principles for finding gaps

- Look for gaps - don't avoid your weaknesses
- Test at multiple orders
- Do not refer to answer sheets until you have created your own answer sheet through self-study first. Premature answer sheet checking often creates illusions of knowledge and is a common mistake.
- Remember that struggling to answer a question indicates a gap. Even if you get the question right, you might just have gotten lucky! Difficulty matters more than correctness.
