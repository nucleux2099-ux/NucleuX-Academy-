**Flashcards**

Overview of flashcards

A flashcard is a card that has a question or prompt on one side and an answer on the other. If you’re using an app, your flashcards may have more than two "sides". The prompt on a flashcard can commonly be:

A question

Fill in the blanks

Image occlusion (i.e. fill in the blanks of an image)

Using flashcards as a learning strategy can be effective when executed correctly. Crucially, we want to avoid:

Having too many flashcards: This creates overwhelm and requires a high regular and repeating time investment. Only layer 3 and 4 should be on flashcards.

Testing on too many things in a single prompt: When we do this, it becomes difficult to decide whether we get a card "correct" or "incorrect" if there is a combination of correct and incorrect answers.

Flashcards vs. summary cards

Flashcards are different from summary cards. Flashcards test you on information, while summary cards are discrete cards that provide a summary of information. Unlike flashcards, summary cards do not produce a generative effect on learning.

Some people have hundreds or thousands of flashcards. This is inefficient and unsustainable. This can be reduced with proper layers of learning as more information becomes naturally relevant and deeply encoded.

Key benefits of flashcards

It allows spaced retrieval practice for lower-order information

It functions as a "second brain" reference source of information

It is easy to use

It enables microlearning

Microlearning

Flashcards are powerful because they allow you to engage in microlearning, or short bursts of studying (e.g., while taking the bus). Conceptual learning can’t usually be done in short bursts, making flashcards a great tool to fill in small gaps in your schedule.

Don’t use flashcards in long study sessions, as this doesn’t activate microlearning conditions and is thus less effective. Keep it to short bursts of 3 to 10 minutes per session.

Go Digital

Don’t use physical flashcards, which are harder to access for microlearning. Apps, like Anki, are much more accessible and effective, as they calculate the best time for you to review each flashcard based on spaced-repetition algorithms.

This level of flexibility, control and convenience is impossible with physical flashcards. The (extremely minor) benefit of writing flashcards by hand is insignificant compared to the benefits of going digital.

**Sublesson****: ****Active flashcard management**

**General flashcard guidelines**

Use digital flashcards (e.g., Anki)

Only use flashcards for layers three and four (the important and arbitrary details).

Reduce your number of flashcards as much as possible. You shouldn’t need more than 50 flashcards for an entire topic. If there are more than 50, there may be a mistake in your conceptual learning.

Each flashcard should have a targeted question and a discrete fact. Do not include more than two or three facts on a single flashcard.

Even if you don’t know the answer to a flashcard, give it your best guess before checking the answer. It’s not about getting it right; it’s about generating an answer and correcting yourself to activate generation and hypercorrection effects.

**Key principle**

The **generation** effect is critical for effective learning. Generating an answer produces more learning than simply checking an answer. For example, reading the answers for a practice exam is less effective than taking the time to write real answers.

The **hypercorrection** effect is a perfect synergy. Making a mistake and being corrected produces a higher level of learning than getting it right the first time. The more confidence you had in your incorrect answer, the higher your level of learning will be.

**One benefit of the Leitner system is that it prioritises weak areas. **

However, a disadvantage is that cards can accumulate quickly if you get many incorrect. If you have lots of flashcards (e.g., 200+), it is difficult to get them all correct. As a result, earlier stage boxes start “overflowing”, making it hard to stay on top of your flashcards. 

This is where our flashcard management guidelines come into play.

**Rule of 3 method for managing flashcards**

**Instructions**

Combine

Cut

Critique

Expand the accordions below to learn more about each rule.

Combine

**If you get a flashcard correct three times in a row:**

Merge them with another conceptually related card that you also got correct three times to create a higher-order question. 

For example, if one card asks about the definition and purpose of judicial law and another asks about the definition and purpose of parliamentary law, you could combine them to create a question like, **"What are the key differences between the definitions and purposes of judicial law compared with parliamentary law?"**. A further combination could be even more higher-order, such as, **"How important is judicial law compared to parliamentary law in heavily litigious industries?"**

This way, we reduce the number of flashcards in the deck while testing ourselves at higher orders. When combining cards, ensure that you include the lower-order information from each card as a prerequisite knowledge to answer the new higher-order prompt. You wouldn't want to create a card on two conceptually related pieces, but ask a completely different angle that does not build on your existing knowledge. In the examples above, the knowledge of definitions and purpose is still tested, just at a higher order.

Cut

**If you get a flashcard correct three times in a row (and there are no conceptually related cards to combine with):**

Remove this card from your deck. This rule is flexible: if you are worried, leave the flashcard in the deck and let the spacing algorithm naturally extend the delay between attempts. However, if you feel cluttered with cards, these cards should be the first to be removed.

At the very least, change the wording on the card to prevent yourself from falling into a *recognition trap* where you are unable to recall the information without this highly specific cue.

Critique

**If you get a flashcard incorrect three times (out of 5 attempts):**

Go back to the material and learn more deeply about it. Go outside the scope of the curriculum, consider its applications, and try to create a more robust conceptual understanding. You must give the information more relevance to prevent yourself from repeatedly forgetting it. 

These cards you get wrong consistently will take up more time because you never clear them from your deck. It is worthwhile to invest five to 10 minutes to consolidate more deeply instead of sticking with a lower-order approach, which has proved ineffective.

**Revision**

The purpose of revision is to **recall** the information in your study material.

However, there are different ways to revise. Some people only practise **recognising** the information. Other people rely on **cues** to help them recall it.

It is better to have **true recall**, where you can remember and apply the information even without cues.

*To help with your revision, we will go through techniques that build true recall.*

**Modified method of loci**

**About the modified method of loci**

The **modified method of loci** builds upon the original method of loci (more commonly known as the Memory Palace) to help you remember large strings of arbitrary information.

**Instructions**

Create a story or "scene" with different elements for each piece of information.

Make elements absurd. Mundane and boring elements are not memorable.

Keep elements spatially distinct. Don't have too many things happening in the same "location".

Make elements interdependent. Create relationships between elements to help with retention.

Draw out your "scene" in your mindmap canvas to serve as a memory anchor. This replaces the normal "visualisations" you have been using.

Your drawing doesn’t have to look nice; it just needs to consolidate the information in your brain. Even students with zero artistic ability can successfully use this technique.

Usage notes for this technique

As a lower-order memorisation method, it is not effective or efficient for information that has causal/functional/conceptual relationships with other pieces of information.

This method excels with lists of information (ordered or unordered).

It can be used for small lists of just a few items, or longer lists of 30+.

This method should not replace higher-order encoding approaches if a higher-order relational network is viable.
