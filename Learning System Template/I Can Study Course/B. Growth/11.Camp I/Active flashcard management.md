# Active flashcard management
## General flashcard guidelines

1. Use digital flashcards (e.g., Anki)
2. Only use flashcards for layers three and four (the important and arbitrary details).
3. Reduce your number of flashcards as much as possible. You shouldn’t need more than 50 flashcards for an entire topic. If there are more than 50, there may be a mistake in your conceptual learning.
4. Each flashcard should have a targeted question and a discrete fact. Do not include more than two or three facts on a single flashcard.
5. Even if you don’t know the answer to a flashcard, give it your best guess before checking the answer. It’s not about getting it right; it’s about generating an answer and correcting yourself to activate generation and hypercorrection effects.

## Key principle

The **generation** effect is critical for effective learning. Generating an answer produces more learning than simply checking an answer. For example, reading the answers for a practice exam is less effective than taking the time to write real answers.

The **hypercorrection** effect is a perfect synergy. Making a mistake and being corrected produces a higher level of learning than getting it right the first time. The more confidence you had in your incorrect answer, the higher your level of learning will be.

# Leitner system

The Leitner system is an implementation strategy of spaced repetition commonly adapted and utilised for flashcards and microlearning.

![a diagram of the Leitner system](https://learning.icanstudy.com/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fprod.icanstudy.com%2F%2Fsites%2Fdefault%2Ffiles%2Finline-images%2F1920px-Leitner_system_alternative.svg_.png&w=3840&q=75)

Source: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leitner_system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leitner_system)

As you can see from the diagram above, **incorrect flashcards move backwards** while **correct cards move forwards**.

If we track the movement of a single card, it might look like this:

1. Answered correctly
2. Moves to box 2, due for a repeat in 1 hour
3. Answered correctly again
4. Moves to box 3, due for a repeat in 4 hours
5. Answered correctly again
6. Moves to box 4, due for a repeat in 16 hours
7. Answered incorrectly
8. Moves back to box 3, due for a repeat in 4 hours
9. Answered incorrectly
10. Moves back to box 2, due for a repeat in 1 hour

**One benefit of the Leitner system is that it prioritises weak areas.** 

However, a disadvantage is that cards can accumulate quickly if you get many incorrect. If you have lots of flashcards (e.g., 200+), it is difficult to get them all correct. As a result, earlier stage boxes start “overflowing”, making it hard to stay on top of your flashcards. 

This is where our flashcard management guidelines come into play.

# Rule of 3 method for managing flashcards

## Instructions

1. Combine
2. Cut
3. Critique

Expand the accordions below to learn more about each rule.

### Combine

**If you get a flashcard correct three times in a row:**

Merge them with another conceptually related card that you also got correct three times to create a higher-order question. 

For example, if one card asks about the definition and purpose of judicial law and another asks about the definition and purpose of parliamentary law, you could combine them to create a question like, **"What are the key differences between the definitions and purposes of judicial law compared with parliamentary law?"**. A further combination could be even more higher-order, such as, **"How important is judicial law compared to parliamentary law in heavily litigious industries?"**

This way, we reduce the number of flashcards in the deck while testing ourselves at higher orders. When combining cards, ensure that you include the lower-order information from each card as a prerequisite knowledge to answer the new higher-order prompt. You wouldn't want to create a card on two conceptually related pieces, but ask a completely different angle that does not build on your existing knowledge. In the examples above, the knowledge of definitions and purpose is still tested, just at a higher order.

### Cut

**If you get a flashcard correct three times in a row (and there are no conceptually related cards to combine with):**

Remove this card from your deck. This rule is flexible: if you are worried, leave the flashcard in the deck and let the spacing algorithm naturally extend the delay between attempts. However, if you feel cluttered with cards, these cards should be the first to be removed.

At the very least, change the wording on the card to prevent yourself from falling into a _recognition trap_ where you are unable to recall the information without this highly specific cue.

### Critique

**If you get a flashcard incorrect three times (out of 5 attempts):**

Go back to the material and learn more deeply about it. Go outside the scope of the curriculum, consider its applications, and try to create a more robust conceptual understanding. You must give the information more relevance to prevent yourself from repeatedly forgetting it. 

These cards you get wrong consistently will take up more time because you never clear them from your deck. It is worthwhile to invest five to 10 minutes to consolidate more deeply instead of sticking with a lower-order approach, which has proved ineffective.
