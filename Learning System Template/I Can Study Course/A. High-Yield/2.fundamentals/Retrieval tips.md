# Retrieval tips
## What is retrieval?

![[03 Spaces/Medical Hub/🏥 Clinical Rotations/Clinical skills/Clinical cases/Attachments/Image 3.jpg|a cardboard box with a picture of a brain on the side. A hand is pulling out a stack of notes from inside the box.]]

**Retrieval** means taking information from your long-term memory and bringing it into your working memory to recall, solve problems and answer questions.

### Key principle

Retrieval practice is a well-evidenced and important part of learning. No learning system is complete without adequate retrieval practice.

## Types of retrieval practice

There are many different ways to do retrieval practice. We recommend utilising as many of these as possible. Time invested in these methods pays itself off in improvements to your retention.

## What is too much retrieval practice?

In modern research, we understand that over-reliance on retrieval practice without adequate encoding can be problematic. This usually occurs when encoding is done to a low quality, resulting in high rates of knowledge decay (forgetting) and an overwhelming amount of material to learn through tedious repetition.

### How to avoid over-relying on retrieval

1. Continuously work on improving your encoding skills (we will teach you these methods in the next stages).
2. Ensure you have multiple orders of interleaving (refer to the interleaving table in your learning resources).
3. Use lower-order rote-memorisation (direct recall with spaced repetition) as a last resort for information that does not integrate into your knowledge network.
4. Always try to connect new information to the big picture and think of why it is important. This helps to integrate it into your knowledge networks.
