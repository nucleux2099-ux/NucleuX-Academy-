# Notes and processing
## About this strategy

Collecting and processing is a **transitionary technique**, meaning we use it to transition between different strategies. In this case, we use collecting and processing to transition from heavily linear notes to processed notes. It helps learners spend more time thinking and analysing the information to break the habit of passive, mindless note-taking.

If you already feel comfortable with non-linear note-taking (e.g. mindmaps), you do not need to use this transitionary technique.

# Technique overview


Many people don’t learn new information properly. They write it down in their notes and assume they have learned it. But why don’t you absorb some information even after writing pages of notes? And why can you learn other things (e.g. details about your favourite TV show) without writing any notes?

**Clearly, our brain doesn’t learn information just because we wrote it down. This is where processing notes comes in.**

## Step 1: Collecting

While **collecting**, make the information as concise as possible. Only include keywords; remove words that are not needed, like “the” or “and”. You can use symbols like → or + to represent words or meanings. This step is very easy and does not activate any learning. It is simply a setting-up step for processing.
## Step 2: Processing

**Processing** involves thinking about the information to make sense of it and simplify it. Try to represent the information in the simplest way possible, bringing together as many concepts as possible.

Try moving from collecting to processing after every concept so that we are progressively building a more extensive **network**. As you continue to process, your "web" or "network" of knowledge should grow as more relationships, arrows, and groups form.

Don’t be afraid to oversimplify things! The details will always be on the left in the collection column. You want to focus on representing the main ideas as effectively as possible on the processing side.

# Important note

_Processing_ your notes is a core learning skill. Therefore, the note-taking skills and methods you learn in this step should carry over to all other note-taking you do later. In the future, when we teach you to create mindmaps (or more accurately, _chunkmaps_), we expect that they will still be _processed_.

# Grouping and creating relationships

**Grouping and creating relationships is one of the most important skills for improving your learning efficiency and deep processing.**

The thought processes involved in forming groups and creating relationships are crucial for high-quality encoding. Ask yourself:

- How can I group this information?
- Does it still make sense?
- What does it relate to?
- Why is this important?
- How does it fit into the big picture?

# Optimising our learning

The power of grouping comes from the activation of higher-order learning.

![Bloom's revised taxonomy showing higher-order vs. lower-order learning.](https://learning.icanstudy.com/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fprod.icanstudy.com%2F%2Fsites%2Fdefault%2Ffiles%2Finline-images%2FBloom%2527s%2520simplified%2520version.png&w=3840&q=75)

Bloom's revised taxonomy showing higher-order vs. lower-order learning.

Whenever we form groups and relationships, we want to ensure we **prioritise them.** This involves making a value judgment on how important one group or relationship is compared to others. 

# Key principle

Making a value judgment on the importance and relevance of information is ultimately subjective. Try your best to make an accurate judgment by reading and learning about the topic.

**The time and effort involved in making this judgment directly contributes to effective learning.** 

On Bloom's revised taxonomy, we are activating the _evaluate_ level of learning. This creates significant improvements in depth and retention of knowledge and is one of the most **crucial** steps to efficient learning.

You often won’t be sure which concepts are the most important or which way of grouping makes the most sense. **This is normal!** When this happens, revisit the material to gain more information and clarity. This **back-and-forth process** (where we think, question ourselves, seek answers, and consolidate) is precisely what higher-order learning feels like!

_It may differ greatly from how you usually learn if you have mostly lower-order habits._

# Prioritising to a high level

To help you prioritise relationships or groups, ask yourself these questions.

1. What are other ways that these concepts are similar to each other?
2. What wider purpose do these concepts have?
3. Why do I need to know this? Why is this relationship important?
4. What is the simplest way I can group these ideas together while keeping the relationships accurate?

**There is no right or wrong here.** The key is switching to a _problem-solving mindset._ This makes it easier to retain information compared to memorisation through repetition.

_This foundational skill of processing notes will help tremendously when transitioning to more advanced techniques later in the program._

# A note on the Cornell note-taking method

You may notice that this technique shares similarities with the well-known Cornell Note-Taking Method. The Cornell method is also a beginner-level technique; however, it is more limited and does not transition to more efficient note-taking. The Cornell method reflects a more outdated approach to note-taking. It is superior to the typical linear methods that most learners still use but is less effective than the methods you will learn in this program.
