# Study scheduling
## Study scheduling guidelines

Follow these scheduling guidelines to sustainably cover your core encoding and retrieval sessions needs.

1. A simple guideline for revision spacing is 1 day, 1 week and 1 month. If you have a similar spacing schedule that you have learned from our previous lessons, feel free to continue using that.
2. Schedule retrieval sessions in your calendar, and stick to that schedule.
3. Prestudy material before class. This will save you time later by reducing the amount of revision you need to do.
4. Schedule time for prestudy. Prioritise and protect this time, especially when you’re busy.
5. Snowball your learning of the topic. Learn superficially but broadly during prestudy, and eventually add more in-depth knowledge every time you study it.

# An easy schedule template

The following is a basic schedule that you can follow to start with. This schedule should be sufficient for most learners to get used to following the essential guidelines above.

**For more advanced schedules for spaced interleaved retrieval, read our article on spacing and scheduling** [**here**](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-resources/spacing)**.**

|Day and time|Activity|Focus while doing the activity|
|---|---|---|
|Sunday anytime|Prestudy the week’s material|Learn just the concepts and how they **relate** to each other. Don’t memorise any processes, steps or definitions. Make a very basic mindmap.|
|End of every weekday (after learning in class)|Revise the day’s material|Build a more detailed understanding of the topic. Create flashcards and elaborate on your mind map. Don’t spend time memorising definitions or highly detailed information yet. Focus on having a solid understanding of the concepts and how any processes work. Don’t make flashcards for conceptual information.|
|End of every week, e.g. Friday evening or Saturday|Revise the entire week’s material|Use an interleaved retrieval method for revision and return to any weaknesses and gaps. Start to memorise details. A good initial technique to use here is teaching and creating challenging questions.|
|During the week, at any available pocket of time|Go through flashcards.|Don’t worry about getting through all of them; just do whichever ones you can, given the available pockets of time.|
|End of the month on a weekend|Revise the entire month’s material.|This should be surprisingly easy as you have kept yourself from forgetting much. Use another interleaved retrieval method. By now, you should have memorised most of the details via the flashcards you have been consistently doing.|
|Two to three weeks before the test or exam|Do more retrieval practice.|Use another interleaved revision technique such as practice papers, challenge questions and peer quizzing. Brush up on any details you keep forgetting.|

If you have a lot of content to cover, you can add another revision session in the middle of the month (as a fortnightly review) or the middle of the week (as a 2-day review).

You can adjust the exact days in the example schedule to suit your needs. For example, instead of “Sunday anytime”, you can do your prestudy on Saturday or Monday morning. You can do two weeks’ worth of prestudy at a time instead of one week’s worth. In later lessons, we will develop your ability to prestudy an entire topic (4–6 weeks’ worth) in one go.

## Preview is better than review

Prestudying saves time on revision as you forget less. Therefore, as your prestudy technique improves, your revision time will decrease. Given the choice between doing good prestudy vs. reviewing previous material, always choose prestudy.

**You can always catch up on reviewing material later, but prestudying helps you have less to catch up on in the first place.**

# Common questions

## I'm struggling to follow these schedules. What should I do?

When we start to put more of these tasks in your schedule, it's common to identify weaknesses in your time and task management skills. In most cases, if you struggle to follow these schedules, it is because of:

1. issues with basic time management skills, or
2. issues with task prioritisation (such as prioritising retrieval sessions instead of good quality prestudy sessions)

Depending on where your issue is, this could be a cue for you to work on reviewing your self-management skills from the earlier modules. Just because this program starts with one lesson and moves in a linear order, don't let that stop you from going back and reviewing older lessons!

**Great learners know when to go back and forward based on closely monitoring their knowledge gaps.**

## How long do I need to practise using these schedules to know I have competence?

Give yourself a couple of weeks to use this new kind of schedule. You can also work on other techniques simultaneously. Scheduling does not have a long learning curve, so most students can set a schedule and start using it relatively quickly. 

However, it can take a little experimenting to make it suit your needs. Make sure you get real-life experience and reflect weekly on what isn’t working and how you can improve it.
