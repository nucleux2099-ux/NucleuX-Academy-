# Note-taking
## Note-taking guidelines

Follow these guidelines to make sure your note-taking helps your brain's ability to process information.

1. Always manipulate knowledge in a different way to how you consumed it.
2. Use non-verbal and non-linear note-taking to manipulate knowledge through visual and spatial parts of your brain.
3. Improve your note-taking with baby steps.
4. Limit your word-count. Look for big chunks of text in your notes that can be expressed through mindmaps, symbols, arrows, and doodles.

# Tips for visualisations

While using imagery can be a powerful memory cue, there are some important considerations to ensure you receive the maximum benefit.

## Avoid over-visualising

Over-visualisation refers to when learners turn too many words into visuals. Using the same "landmark" analogy, having too many images and visualisations reduces the benefit on our memory because there are too many landmarks. It's like having a different landmark at the end of every street! This can get overwhelming.

Images are best used to reduce big chunks of text or create memory anchors for core principles that are either conceptually important or at higher risk of forgetting.

## Keyword anchoring your images

Images can help create memory anchors for knowledge. These act as mini cues within your notes to help trigger your ability to recall information. Think of them like "landmarks" in your notes.

To make it easier to review your notes in the future, add a keyword under each drawing to make sure you remember what the drawing stands for. This way, we get the benefits of creating "landmarks" while also ensuring we don't forget what the drawings represented in the first place!

## Avoid hieroglyphics

Hieroglyphics refers to a method of visualising notes where the same images are used repeatedly instead of words. When this occurs, the imagery loses its memory benefit because it is too repetitive and common. Instead of being a "landmark", they become like streetlamps. Imagine trying to remember every streetlamp in a city!

In the following example, the following equations have been converted into images:

- Metal + acid = salt + hydrogen gas
- Metal + water = metal hydroxide + hydrogen gas

![Hieroglyphics in chemistry reactions](https://learning.icanstudy.com/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fprod.icanstudy.com%2F%2Fsites%2Fdefault%2Ffiles%2Finline-images%2FIMG_209CC244D6A7-1.jpeg&w=3840&q=75)

Metal reactions represented with hieroglyphics.

As you can see, images are used to replace a word directly, and they are used repeatedly. Therefore, this would be considered "hieroglyphics" and is not any more memorable than using words.

## Draw it yourself

While taking screenshots and diagrams others have made can be tempting, do so sparingly. A significant portion of the advantage of creating visualisations comes from the processing and thought required to think of imagery that is appropriate and memorable. By avoiding this work, we also avoid the beneficial effect it has on encoding.

Furthermore, diagrams and images from more "technical" sources are often too dense and detailed to be memorable.
# Images are just memory cues

In the next sublesson about **Notes and processing**, we will introduce other methods of thinking and note-taking involving creating groups and relationships. Creating groups and relationships is superior to simply creating an image. 

In the above example, with a chemical reaction, this information is better learned through good logic and a deeply processed understanding of the concepts. Relying on a superficial memory cue like this image is less efficient. _The example with chemistry was used in this lesson as it is a common example where learners often use hieroglyphics._ 

Visualisations and memory cues can **help to reinforce** the information if it has been processed and grouped already.

# Common questions

## I can't draw very well. What should I do?

Check out our article on [how to draw to learn](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-resources/drawing-to-learn)! It's much easier than you think. After all, we're drawing to learn, not sell our doodles to an art gallery.

## I'm not used to writing notes this way. What should I do?

If this is mostly new to you, we recommend you take it slow. Correct note-taking is an important part of studying. It's also a bottleneck for many students because linear note-taking is extremely limited. 

You may want to gradually change how you write your notes over the next 2 to 3 weeks. You can also practise other techniques in the meantime – especially the in-class techniques, which overlap heavily.

Over time, it will become easier and more natural for you to write non-linear notes!
