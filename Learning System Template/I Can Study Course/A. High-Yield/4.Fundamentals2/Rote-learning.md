# Rote-learning

## Rote-learning

# Rote learning guidelines

To do rote-learning and memorisation more efficiently, follow these guidelines.

1. Basic association allows you to make items more memorable by assigning highly memorable imagery to them. We can draw this imagery in flashcards or our notes.
2. Our brain is not good at memorising using words alone. Use visualisations and imagery too.
3. Make sure you use active recall instead of recognition.
4. Recall is more effective when combined with good spacing and interleaving.
5. To memorise details you might forget, use digital flashcards for spaced repetition via the Leitner system.
6. Try not to do flashcards in batches. Instead, do them throughout the day via microlearning.

# Remember

Rote-learning is only necessary for around 30% of university material or around 10 to 20% of high school material.

# Common questions
## How long do I need to practise these techniques before moving on?

Rote-memorisation techniques are often easy to master with very little practice. Try to use some of these methods in your learning for one or two study sessions. There is no need to delay moving on. It is more likely that you will need time to coordinate how these rote-memorisation techniques balance with your encoding and interleaved retrieval methods. This coordination can be worked on over time.
