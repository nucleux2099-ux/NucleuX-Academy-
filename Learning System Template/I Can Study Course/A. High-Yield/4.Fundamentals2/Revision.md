# Revision
## Improving your retrieval skills

We've already looked at how you can enhance your learning system with spaced, interleaved retrieval. However, as you can see from how extensive the [interleaving table](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-resources/interleaving-table) is, it can take a long time to find the right combination of methods and master them.

Instead of halting your progress while you learn these, this lesson introduces some high-yield techniques that can work well for many learners across most disciplines and levels of learning.

## Test, target, teach

### Test

When you test yourself, remember...

1. Apply your knowledge.
2. Create your own questions and use premade questions.
3. You can share these questions with your friends in a small study group.

### Why use self-made _**and**_ premade questions?

Creating your own challenge and exam questions can be more effective than using past papers and premade practice questions. Even if you don’t fully know the correct answer, you learn more from creating the question and attempting to answer it than you would from answering an existing question.

**Use premade questions to:**

1. Save time
2. Familiarise with the style of questioning in your actual assessments (but remember there's no guarantee and it could change)
3. Find gaps in your knowledge by testing yourself in ways you wouldn't have thought of

**Use self-made questions to:**

1. Strengthen your understanding of material you've already learned from a different angle
2. Find knowledge gaps in content you've already learned
3. Practise retrieval in all the ways (orders and types of knowledge) you need (it's easier to target weaknesses when you can control the type of retrieval challenge

### Protect your performance

An over-reliance on premade questions is one reason curveball questions throw students off so much. Creating your own questions is especially useful in subjects like maths and physics, which can often trip up students with real-world applications of formulae and equations that students don’t usually think of. 

**Stay ahead by anticipating curveballs before they are thrown! Good learners always use a combination of self-made and premade questions.** 

Note that there are significant overlaps between this method and the _advanced group study method_ taught in the [interleaving table](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-resources/interleaving-table). If you feel confident with the _advanced group study method_, you can use that instead.

### Target

When you target your retrieval practice, remember...

1. Look for areas you feel less confident in or have not used before.
2. Start with areas of weakness, even if it feels uncomfortable.

### Teach

When you teach content, remember...

1. Some methods of teaching are more effective than others.
2. Don’t just “explain” – teach! Change the order and approach when presenting the information so it makes sense for someone who hasn’t learned it before.

### Teaching challenge

An effective way to find your weaknesses is to explain why a concept is important **before** introducing any terminology. This means you need to find a way to explain it in lay terms. **For example,** imagine you are teaching the concept to a 10-year-old.

## When you find knowledge gaps

Using these methods, you can find knowledge gaps. Knowledge gaps are:

1. Any parts you struggle to recall
2. Any parts you get incorrect
3. Any parts you feel unconfident about

**Knowledge gaps are not only when you get things incorrect!**

Every time you find a knowledge gap, review your material and re-encode it using the other methods we have taught you. Whichever methods were used to learn it initially can be used again. However, try to connect the information more deeply or try to make sense of it from another perspective. This helps to improve your retention and depth.

## Common questions

### How much do I have to practise this before I know I have sufficient competence?

Try to do all three (test, target, and teach) at least once before moving on to the next set of lessons. You can continue to refine this skill over time, but the gaps in knowledge you find from using this technique will change how you interpret future learning. It will be beneficial to have this experience at least once or twice before moving on. 

**There are two signs that you should spend longer to practise before moving on:**

1. You struggle to apply these methods
2. You find it very easy but do not find any knowledge gaps

**If you struggle with these methods**, it is worthwhile to spend a couple more weeks practising them to reap the benefits. This prevents overload and maximises your learning efficiency.

**If you do not struggle but also do not find any knowledge gaps**, it most likely indicates that the technique was performed incorrectly. Unless you truly have pristine knowledge, you should be able to find gaps. Retrieval techniques are meant to find gaps. Not finding any means it was ineffective.

If the technique was easy **and** ineffective, it may indicate that they did not cover all the necessary orders of learning or types of knowledge testing. In this case, read our article on [measuring your learning efficiency](https://learning.icanstudy.com/learning-resources/learning-efficiency) to see if you are testing the appropriate levels.
