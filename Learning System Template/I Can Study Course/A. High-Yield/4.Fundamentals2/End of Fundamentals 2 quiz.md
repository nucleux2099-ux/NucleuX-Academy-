# Learning notes

## A-maze-ing

Over-visualisation is a common pattern we see when learners who are not proficient with higher-order learning and thinking focus too much on creating visuals instead of focusing on creating strong relational networks and groups. Visualisation of information can be fun and effective for serving as a memory cue. However, it’s important that we remember that too much of something can also be bad. If there are too many visuals, the map can become hard to follow and detract from creating a clear flow of information. Use visuals to create visual landmarks of the more important and/or forgettable parts of the map.

# Learning notes

## Beginner Level Prestudy - Basic Organisation

Insufficient chunking is one of the most common mistakes of a beginner. This often happens because after the most obvious groups have been created, there is no further exploration of how the information could be divided into further sub-groups. Remember that even within a group, information might be similar and related. Moreover, multiple groups can sometimes be combined into a larger parent group. The simpler and more discretely we can group and connect information, the better our retention and depth of expertise.

The other pattern that is worrying but not definite is _hieroglyphics_. This is because the visualisations that are used are discrete, isolated and directly replace words at a 1-to-1 ratio. The visuals are also not very abstract and are likely to be easily forgettable. A common additional problem that is not seen here is that learners can often then reuse the same images for whenever they encounter the same word, often on the same map. This is not obvious in this example.

# Learning notes

## Why We Process Notes

Hieroglyphics is the repeated use of simple images to directly replace words. This usually occurs because learners have misunderstood why visualisations can be effective. Using images can reduce word-count, force deeper processing of information, and create memory cues through the use of visual ‘landmarks’ in the notes. When images are used like hieroglyphics, it is not substantially more memorable than simply using words. Ultimately, our brain has to memorise the images and where they were to make sense of the knowledge, just like how it would have to with words. In the example, you can see the notes are ultimately still very linear and there are minimal relationships and networks between concepts.
