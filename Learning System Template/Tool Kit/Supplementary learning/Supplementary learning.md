**30-day challenges**

**Overview**

Turn yourself into a self-improvement sponge with a 30-day challenge. Challenges are a great way to learn about your strengths, weaknesses, and habits. In just 30 days, obtain experiences and insights that normally take people years to acquire! 

**Note:*** There is no retrieval challenge, as retrieval skills do not require prior experiences or insights, are relatively easy to implement, and are taught very early in the core program.*

Which challenge should I start with?

Read your diagnostic report to see which challenge is recommended for you. 

Are the challenges mandatory?

Every challenge is completely optional. Progression through the core program without the 30-day challenges is absolutely viable and reasonable if you do not have time.

Although all challenges are unlocked for you, you do not need to complete them immediately! They are intended to give you a headstart before you commence the core program. 

If you feel overwhelmed at any point in the core program or if you feel stuck improving a certain dimension, come back to these challenges to break through. Challenges can be completed multiple times throughout the duration of your program.

What if I don't have enough time?

The diagnostic report recommends prioritising certain dimensions. If you feel like those dimensions are very limiting, we recommend completing the 30-day challenge before the core program or progressing through the core program more slowly than you normally would.

If you feel that you are confident to improve your dimensions straight away, you can ignore the 30-day challenges and start with the core program only.

The challenges exist so people have an option to get an early start on skill development in a way that does not require much guidance and support, before their cohort commences.

Can I do multiple challenges at once?

Yes, you can complete multiple challenges simultaneously. However, we recommend starting on the dimension that is most limiting for you, based on the archetype calculated from your diagnostic assessment.

Can I do challenges later, while going through the core program?

Yes, you can complete the challenges anytime you feel like you are plateauing through the core program or that your ability to consolidate new techniques from the core program is slow. These situations may indicate that you lack the relevant experiences and insights to learn the core program skills, and you may benefit from exposure to these experiences through the challenges.

**If you feel overwhelmed going through the core program and challenges simultaneously**, we recommend focusing on only one. If you find the core program techniques relatively straightforward to implement and practice, prioritise moving through the core program until you face difficulties.

**If you find the core programs overwhelming and difficult to integrate into your system**, prioritise the 30-day challenges and move back to the core program when you have completed the relevant challenge.

**If you feel that you can do both simultaneously,** we recommend progressing through the core program at half the pace recommended in the stage overview descriptions.

How do the 30-day challenges help me?

*Solutions* are easier to appreciate when we have experienced the *problem*. Some learners struggle to learn new skills (especially if they are related to weaker dimensions) because they do not have the experiences, insights, and perspectives to make the skills relevant. 

For example, many learners struggle to improve their deep processing because they have never had experience thinking in the way deep processing requires. As a result, when they use a deep processing technique, they are unfamiliar with the challenges and struggle to overcome them.

Likewise, learners with low mindset scores can struggle with experimenting with new methods because they lack experience in failing fast *and* safely, causing them to be unnecessarily fearful of making mistakes.

**The 30-day challenges provide these key experiences that make learning any skill related to that dimension easier.**

**Week 1: Build your radar**

**Week 1**

The objective of week 1 is to gain more awareness of different *orders* of thinking and learning. We'll learn more about this throughout the core program, but here's a simplified version for now.

A simplified diagram representing higher-order vs. lower-order thinking

What does it mean?

The table below summarises some key differences between these orders of thinking.

Why higher-order thinking?

Higher-order thinking has a range of benefits.

Benefits of higher-order thinking

Improved memory and retention of new information (less repetition required).

Deeper and more nuanced understanding.

Ability to use knowledge more intuitively and in more complex ways.

More engaging and enjoyable!

However, higher-order thinking is often avoided because it involves more* mental effort. *On the other hand, lower-order thinking is easy (and often causes drowsiness).* *

**In this week's challenge, we will use these characteristics of mental effort to our advantage!**

**The challenge: Build your radar**

A major part of becoming better at deep processing is to learn to actively engage in higher-order thinking. We want to recognise when we are thinking at lower orders and "flick the switch" to higher orders.

To do this, we need to *build our radar*. This is about gaining *metacognitive self-awareness*, or in simpler terms, becoming aware of how we are thinking.

**Week 1 instructions**

Follow these instructions to build your radar.

Every time you sit down to study or learn something, keep a piece of paper next to you.

Whenever you notice you are thinking at lower orders, tally it on the paper¹.

Pause every 30 minutes and try to gain a clear big-picture understanding of everything you have studied in the session so far to avoid becoming too isolated and lower-order.

At the end of every session, reflect on how lower-order vs. higher-order thinking felt². Focus on the amount of mental effort it took, the level of engagement you felt, and how it felt for your memory and level of understanding.

**Notes**

¹ Refer to the table above to understand how lower-order thinking feels compared to higher-order thinking. Each type of thinking feels distinctly different. When you feel yourself thinking at lower orders, tally this.
² Focus on reflecting on your study session as a whole. Although in step 3, you pause every 30 minutes to retrospectively upgrade lower-order learning into higher orders, it is still not as effective as operating at higher orders in the first place. As a result, there will still be gaps in knowledge and weaknesses in retention. Over time, we will learn to improve this. However, in this challenge, we focus on developing a strong sense of self-awareness when we engage in different orders of thinking. Over the weeks, you should notice that a lower proportion of your time is spent in lower orders and that your overall quality of learning improves.

**Reflect, reflect, reflect!**

Don't skip the reflection at the end! Although it's tempting to skip this, reflection is crucial for converting experiences into learning through consolidation. Frequent reflection is a secret weapon that helps us accumulate meaningful learning from new experiences much faster than average.

**This challenge helps to train your deep processing by:**

Making you more self-aware of your thinking processes

Helping to catch yourself when you engage in less helpful types of thinking

Building experience at deliberately adjusting your cognitive processes

Over the next weeks, we will build on these experiences more.

Practice material

This challenge will require you to do some learning. If you do not have anything to learn right now, we recommend:

Getting a head start on future content if you can get your hands on some material to practise on

Picking a new topic you have wanted to learn about

Relearning something you feel weak in but have previously studied

Build your own curriculum on any topic you want using Google and Wikipedia articles!

*Remember, the primary purpose of these challenges is to build the skill. It doesn't matter if the content you are learning is not perfectly relevant!*

Before you start

Throughout the week, you will likely experience a few differences from your normal methods of learning. Below are some common observations and explanations to help you reflect and make sense of them.

Don't I need lower order thinking to understand information in the first place before doing higher-order thinking?

This is a common misconception. Refer to  for a deeper explanation of higher-order vs. lower-order learning and thinking and why "understanding" is not a type of thinking but rather an outcome or symptom.** ****Refer ****Article:****What**** is higher-order learning and thinking?**

It's too tiring

Higher-order thinking does involve more mental effort but it generates much more effective learning for the time spent. Over time, it will become easier to use higher-order thinking processes as they become new habits.

It takes too long

While higher-order thinking can take longer during a single session when you are not used to thinking in this way, it saves time overall by reducing the need for future repetition and study sessions to reach the level of retention and depth you need.

I'm rarely thinking at higher orders!

This is normal for those without deep processing habits. Instead of trying to haphazardly change years of habits in just one week, we first focus on building a strong awareness of when we are engaging in lower-order thinking. Gaining awareness of a problem is the critical first step to improvement.

**Week 2: Networking**

**Progression ****note**

To get the most value out of these challenges, we recommend following these challenges week by week and not rushing ahead. If you have not finished your first week yet, please complete this first.

**By now, you should have started to recognise:**

what lower-order vs. higher-order thinking feels like

how higher-order thinking affects retention and depth of understanding

In this second week, we will start exploring one of the key component skills of deep processing: *relational thinking*.

Relational thinking refers to the ability to create knowledge networks. Knowledge that exists in a network is more strongly retained and more deeply understood. Whether it's a tiny, technically detailed fact, or a broad concept, the more well-integrated we can place it in our network, the easier our learning becomes.

**Challenge: Networking**

**Week 2 instructions**

Follow these instructions to build networks.

After every 15 minutes, take 5 minutes look at what you have studied in the last 15 minutes and compare concepts, keywords, terminology, and facts to each other. Look for similarities, differences, trends, and relationships/influences.

After every hour, take 15 minutes to do the same process for everything you have studied within that entire topic thus far.

If there are any relationships that you are unsure of, feel free to spend one or two minutes to look it up. If you cannot arrive at a conclusion in this time, move on.

As you do this process, you may feel the desire to add more notes, or re-write your existing notes to represent some of these new insights and relationships. Spend this time to make the most important and relevant adjustments.

Add the end of each session, reflect on how connecting information to each other and relating it to the big picture feels and influences your retention and depth of understanding.

Common observations

It's overwhelming!

You may find a substantial number of new relationships to think about, which can feel overwhelming. Don't worry! This is normal and is a sign that you're thinking in the right way. Eventually, you will learn how to become less overwhelmed. For now, we focus on finding relationships and forcing ourselves to think of each piece of information in relation to other pieces of information.

Over time, we will learn how to use these relationships to help reduce content overwhelm. You may even discover strategies yourself that help to make it more manageable this week!

I'm struggling to express the relationships in my notes.

This is totally normal, and it means you're probably doing it right! We'll learn how to improve your note-taking to accommodate more relationships later. For now, just do your best and try to get more comfortable with thinking in relationships.

It's difficult to think of relationships.

This is a sign that you're not used to relational thinking. Relational thinking is a requirement for better deep processing, so do your best! Most people find it difficult when they try to think of relationships too generally. 

Give your brain a clear focus by explicitly looking for similarities, differences, trends, and relationships or influences. You can even keep a sticky note on your desk asking, "How are these similar?" to anchor yourself!

**Week 3: Pressure**

**By now, you should:**

be more comfortable with exploring relationships

have more experience with tolerating the discomfort and mental effort involved in exploring relationships

In this third week, we will add some pressure to help train deep processing habits by applying a higher standard to our thought processes.

**Challenge: Pressure**

In this challenge, we will start to prioritise and evaluate the significance of the relationships we find. This is a higher level of thinking than simply *identifying* relationships. It is also one strategy that helps to make it less overwhelming, similar to how a maze is easier to navigate when we actively try to map the correct path.

**Week 3 instructions**

Continue as you have for week 2.

Whenever you identify new relationships and connections, assign a value out of five for how significant this relationship is compared to other relationships. Significance should be determined by it's importance to the wider topic or big picture.

If you are uncertain about the significance, allow yourself three minutes to look it up, read further, or explore the topic in more depth to gain clarity about the significance. Try your best to assign the most accurate value you can within this time.

Continue to reflect at the end of every session. This time, additionally take note of how assigning explicit value on the importance of a relationship affects your retention and depth of understanding.

Although the instructions are simple, this week may be the most difficult challenge of the month!

You should notice that gaps in your knowledge become clearer and more obvious. Relationships that previously felt straightforward become more uncertain and challenged. You will likely have to read and learn more than before to gain clarity and make a value judgment.

Dealing with uncertainty

It is generally impossible to make a value judgment with 100% certainty. The time it takes to get this level of clarity may be unrealistic. The objective is not to create a completely certain judgment but to *try* to gain as much certainty before making a judgment as possible. 

**The value of uncertainty**

This extra step will drive your deep processing to a higher level, but it can also increase the uncertainty you feel while studying. This is because you are becoming mindful of more variables you previously ignored (which strengthens your network and helps deepen your understanding and memory).

**By now, you should:**

have had experience with the additional uncertainty and mental effort involved in assigning significance to relationships

be reasonably experienced with thinking relationally

have gained insights into how certain types of mental effort produce different effects on retention and depth of understanding

be familiar with exploring and learning more to gain clarity and more certainty while learning.

In this final week, we will combine all these experiences to start turning these processes into a more flexible and fluid habit of thinking.

**Challenge: Fluidity**

This week's challenge will take the individual processes of deep processing you've experimented with so far and apply them in a way that is more natural and authentic of a high deep processor.

**Week 4 instructions**

Every time you notice yourself entering into lower-order thinking, create networks by comparing ideas and looking for similarities, differences, trends, and relationships.

Try to limit the relationships you express in your notes to only those that feel 4 out of 5 importance or greater.

Any time it is not clear how what you're learning now relates to the big picture, try to create relationships between the existing networks you have and the wider topic. Aim to create a simple, overarching structure for the topic.

If you need to learn more to gain clarity, give yourself a 3-minute time limit to explore.

At the end of each study session, reflect on how easy or difficult it was for you to form relationships, prioritise them, and explore further.

Success criteria

You will have succeeded in this challenge if:

You are moderately comfortable with the uncertainty and mental effort involved in thinking of relationships and assigning significance (rather than avoiding it or only seeking simple relationships that are easier to think about)

You are able to relate new information to the big picture frequently

You can explore curiosities and gain clarity when needed to form networks

Looking back

How has this challenge changed your habits of thinking and learning? Do you feel more aware of how different types of thinking and processing influence memory and understanding? Can you recognise how different types of mental effort can be beneficial?

**Beyond 4 weeks**

If you still feel uncomfortable with this week's challenge and would like to deepen your newly forming habits, repeat this week's challenge as many times as you wish before commencing the core program (if you haven't already).

**Week 1: Mapping**

Self-regulation is about mastering your tools. To master them, you must first know what each tool is useful for and how to use it. In technical learning science language, we say that to be a proficient self-regulated learner, you need (at least):

Metacognition (thinking about thinking)

Accurate monitoring of cause and effect

Goal-aligned judgments based on what we have monitored to create productive adjustments

High-level overview of self-regulated learning.

In other words, we need to be aware of our thinking (metacognition) and how our thinking produces effects. We then need to think about this, make conclusions about the effectiveness of our methods, and propose adjustments that we hypothesise will help us produce the result more effectively.

**This first week's challenge will help us gain an understanding of how different methods produce different effects.**

**Challenge: mapping**

**Week 1 instructions**

Divide a piece of paper or document into 4 columns.

In the first column, write down all the methods of learning you tend to use.

In the second column, write down the effect you think this method has on memory and depth of understanding.

Throughout the week, continue to complete this document by adding any additional methods you think of in the first column.

After using a method, note down the real effect the method has in the third column. Based on more careful and intentional observation after using it, the real effect may be the same or different to what you thought.

In the final column, add notes on how easy or difficult the method feels to use and why it is more or less difficult than other methods.

**Uncertainty is normal**

If you've never thought about this before, it's normal to be unsure what effect a method has on memory and understanding. You will learn how to measure this accurately through the core program. For now, do your best to make a thoughtful estimate. Even if it isn't objectively the most accurate, there is value in becoming more in tune with your intuitive sense of effectiveness.

**We will build on this intuition in future weeks.**

**Throughout the week, you should start gaining a better sense of the relationships between:**

Methods

How methods feel to use

How effective you think they are

How effective they seem to be in reality

By developing this intuitive sense, we train our *metacognition*.

**In next week's challenge, we will use these insights to make more strategic decisions and adjustments.**

**Relics of the past**

Learners commonly notice that many of their techniques are used only out of habit. Rather than being a purposeful tool to achieve an outcome, they are merely residues of methods we used when we were younger.

**Week 1: Breaking your fall**

One of the first things to learn in many sports where there is a risk of falling, and injury is to fall safely. Whether it's  or , learning to fall is considered one of the most crucial beginner skills.

Unfortunately, we rarely learn to "fall safely" in learning and life.

When we fall safely, mistakes and failures do not impact us or leave any lasting consequences.

This week's challenge will teach you how to reduce the consequences of making mistakes. Over time, this will help to reduce the fear and anxiety of experimentation and failure.

**The challenge: Breaking your fall**

In this challenge, we will observe how the impact of errors can be reduced by:

Reducing the risk of errors by breaking down larger tasks into smaller steps

Reducing the magnitude of consequences by acting on these steps sooner

The risk of mistakes and failure is most significant when there is a high **chance** of failure and the consequence is **significant**. Often, this only occurs when we try to tackle something in one big attempt, or we have delayed the attempt until there is no time for recovery.

**Week 1 instructions**

Choose a skill or task that you know you are not very good at (high chance of making many errors).

Break down this task into smaller and smaller steps or components until you feel that the chance of error for each individual step is very low. If you can't break down the task in this way, choose a different task!

Experiment with doing more of these steps in a single go and observe how it affects your chance of making an error. At what point do errors occur? Is it earlier or later than you expected?

Reflect on the consequence of those errors. Are they smaller or greater than you expected?

If you felt this was easy, choose another skill where the risk of error feels more significant and repeat from step 2.

**Speed is your friend**

After we have broken down something into smaller steps, the consequences of failure can increase when we wait too long. For example, the consequences of how we learn tend to increase as we get closer to an exam. Or if we put off learning to learn for years, the stakes may be higher when we are eventually forced to deal with it!

**Once ****you**** complete step 2, move swiftly into step 3 for experimentation to observe your error rates.**

You may wish to repeat week 1 more than once until you feel comfortable with these steps.

**Common questions**

How do I reflect? How do I know if I have made an error with a technique or task?

An error can be defined in multiple ways:

**1. When the activity has not produced the desired result. **

For example, if you have attempted a mindmap, but it does not help improve your memory or depth of understanding, it has failed to achieve the primary outcome it was intended to achieve. Therefore we can say that some error was made. 

This kind of error is most useful to detect when we are just starting a new method or technique. We often don't know enough about it to identify more specific errors. Instead, we can start by broadly identifying that *something* isn't working. In later attempts, we can gradually begin to unravel what this *something* is.

**2. When we are unable to follow a set of procedures.** 

For example, a step in mindmapping may be to create arrows between concepts to show relationships. We may attempt this and find that our old habits prevented us from creating many arrows, and instead, we ended up writing relatively linear blocks of notes with only a few arrows. This indicates an error in following the procedure as instructed.

This kind of error is most useful when there are specific step-by-step instructions that may be difficult to follow. This often comes after the first type of error has been identified in an earlier attempt.

**3.** **When we get external feedback.**

Getting feedback from someone more experienced and knowledgeable is a valuable source of error identification. This kind of error is most useful when we are unsure what kind of error is being made and our self-feedback does not provide enough insight for another attempt or experiment. 

External feedback should not be relied on as a *replacement* for self-feedback, as the ability to give feedback on our own work is a crucial skill for self-regulated learning. Self-feedback should be the dominant form of spotting errors, with external feedback providing clarity and calibration.

Making mistakes meaningful

Mistakes are a valuable source of learning. However, the lesson isn't always clear! There are two main types of "lessons" mistakes can teach us. Mistakes can teach us about:

Barriers

Conditions

Expand the accordions below to understand each type.

Barriers

Barriers are anything that prevents us from being successful. This includes:

Not having enough energy

Inadequate planning

External distractions

Competing priorities

Inadequate resources

Insufficient knowledge and information

Existing habits and behaviours

As you can see, barriers can be (and often are) behavioural or internal rather than physical or external. People often try something, fail, and then say they can't do it because there isn't a physical or tangible change they can make. However, this doesn't mean it is fixed.

**For example,** if the barrier is insufficient knowledge, the way to overcome this is to learn more. If the barrier is an existing habit, the solution is to start challenging and changing this habit. If it is due to competing priorities, the solution may be to re-evaluate priorities, be stricter with time and task management, or schedule time away from competing priorities.

Having a solution-finding mindset is crucial. We don't just want to find problems. We want to find a solution. 

**Always believe a solution is possible. Remember, someone out there could find it. There's no reason that someone can't be you!**

Conditions

Doing something correctly and successfully often requires certain conditions to be met. When we first attempt something, we rarely know of all these conditions.

When the task is more complicated, there are usually more conditions that need to be first discovered and then met. This process takes time, discovery, experimentation, and observation.

If there are no barriers to your success, the reason you made a mistake may be that you simply did not know all of the variables to consider.

**For example,** many students believe hard work is all it takes to succeed academically. However, there are many other conditions to be met (such as deep processing and self-regulated learning).

**Gaining awareness of these conditions as quickly and as early as possible is crucial for growth and development.**

**Creating behaviour change**

**Progression ****note**

If you watch this before cohort commencement or early in the program, you will not have been introduced to Kolb's cycle yet. This is taught in *Briefing *(stage 1 of the Growth phase). For context, our modified Kolb's cycle is a strategic critical reflection method we teach to help you learn maximally from every attempt or growth experiment. Once you learn about Kolb's, you will easily be able to integrate it with this lesson.

Key principles

Behaviours are triggered by antecedents and reinforced or punished by consequences.

Setting-up events and vulnerability factors change how an antecedent affects behaviour.

Changing antecedents and vulnerability factors are much more likely to create a behaviour change than directly willing ourselves to change the behaviour directly.

Consequences can be used to facilitate habit change, but are less powerful than pre-behaviour interventions (in general).

Efforts and willpower should be directed in the V or A or the V-ABC framework.

Note that changing just a few antecedents may be insufficient. Instead of thinking binarily of whether a strategy to change behaviours is effective or not, think about the effectiveness of strategies as a spectrum. For example, you may need to change five antecedents and vulnerability factors to change a specific behaviour.

**Understanding sleep**

**Sleep is for the smart**

Sleep is an essential part of our natural human physiology. Failure to get adequate, deep, high-quality sleep can affect our:

Imagination

Creativity

Brain development

Concentration

Emotional regulation

Hormonal balance

Memory consolidation*

**Double impact**

Those who get inadequate sleep may both be unable to sleep at night as well as unable to stay awake during the day - a combination of insomnia *and* hypersomnolence.

*Sleep-dependent memory consolidation

This is a  phenomenon where our brain processes and organises information so that we can use it more effectively. In simple terms, this means that not getting enough sleep would cause part of what you studied to go to waste!

**Stages of sleep**

There are four stages of sleep, corresponding to the brain activity, that can be seen at each respective stage. The first three are NREM (non-REM), while the final is REM, standing for rapid eye movement.

NREM Stage 1 – Drowsy, low brain activity

NREM Stage 2 – Light sleep, lower brain activity

NREM Stage 3 – Deep sleep, lowest brain activity

REM Stage 4 – Deep sleep, higher brain activity

*Some consider a 5th stage of further REM sleep, which is argued to be an extension of stage 4.*

**Not all stages are equal**

Most of the beneficial aspects of sleep are only activated in stage 3 and stage 4. Therefore, light and frequently interrupted sleep is much less restorative and beneficial.

Stage hacking

For years, people have thought about how to get less sleep and unlock more hours per day. Unfortunately, studies have shown that this is not possible. Although it is possible to schedule your sleep so that you slip into deeper stages almost immediately, some of the benefits of sleep are removed when the sleep session is too short.

**The conclusion is that we need long, undisturbed, prolonged sleep of a high quality.**

**How much sleep do we need?**

To accumulate enough prolonged deep sleep, most people require 8 to 9 hours per day. This is much more than most people assume they need.

**Sleep hygiene and guidelines**

**SCRIBLED N Co**

Follow these guidelines to optimise your sleep. If you have chronically struggled with sleep, we recommend applying most or all of these guidelines first. You can always pull back with a less rigid routine later once your sleep is improved and your sleep debt has recovered.

**Guidelines**

Sound: Remove disruptive sounds, wear earplugs and/or play white noise.

Caffeine: Avoid caffeine (including caffeine in foods or tea) within six hours of sleep. For some, any coffee after noon can still be detrimental!

Routine: Do a wind-down routine 30 minutes before sleeping. Recommended to do meditation or focus training as instructed.

Incentive: Schedule events or activities the next morning to incentivise you to wake up early. Try to make them activities you look forward to or utilise an accountability buddy.

Bed space: Only sleep or light bed-time reading on or near your bed. If possible, do not study in your bedroom. If this is not viable, create a clear division between workspace and sleeping space.

Light: Use black-out curtains or remove any light sources as much as possible (including from your phone). Strong light in the morning can also improve circadian rhythms and give you a more natural sleep cycle.

Exercise: Engage in aerobic exercise during the day, preferably in the morning.

Devices: Turn off devices 30 minutes before sleep. Lead into your routine.

Napping: Avoid day-time napping if possible. Perform energising activities if you feel drowsy during the day (e.g. going for a walk or some breathing techniques like Wim Hof)

Consistency: Minimise social jet lag and sleep at similar hours on all days.

these steps may seem complicated at first, but they will become intuitive as you see how the system works and builds on itself over time.

Reviews common and important cases and conditions.

Engages in extremely high interleaving.

**Heck yes and heck no**

Key principles

If the decision is not heck yes or heck no, it probably isn’t too important what you decide.

This is only for simple decisions with low consequences and low complexity.

Using this framework helps train basic decisiveness on a daily basis.

Some aspects of this framework are referred to in much more complex decision-making frameworks (which we will cover later).

**Choice throttling**

Key principles

Our ability to decide is diminished by too many choices and variables

Choice throttling involves restricting our decisions and thinking to individual variables

*This concept is also related to a common scientific tool known as the **.*

**Instructions**

Identify the important, specific variables that create uncertainty in your decision.

Ask a closed (often binary yes/no) question for each of these variables.

Make your decision based on the answers to each of your binary questions.

Even complex decisions can be made much easier with this method.

Note that your ability to choice throttle depends on your self-awareness, insight, reflective ability and (depending on the context) your level of information about the decision and the consequences. The core program helps to give you tools to develop these (e.g. Kolb's, marginal gains, reverse goal setting, etc.). 

Furthermore, **simpler** decisions do not necessarily mean **faster**. For example, it may take months to properly answer yes or no to one question. This is often the case when determining which values matter most to us.

**Complex decision-making**

Key principles

The future is unknown and all decisions are uncertain; expecting certainty in complex decisions is unrealistic.

Good decisions are about managing probability and information (this will be explored further in later lessons).

Indecision is a decision and deciding based on comfort may be due to status quo bias, which may cause us harm.

Opportunity, uncertainty and information all change with time and are related to each other.

Holding off on a decision to gain more information is acceptable, but it is advisable to be aware of the consequences of the decision to hold off.

Information and risk is modifiable with our actions.

Gain information earlier in the process.

Complete the sublessons below to see how these principles modify our decision-making process in increasingly uncertain situations.

**Protecting your biggest downsides**

**Key questions**

What are the biggest downsides?

Are these significant?

Can I protect these? If so, how?

Significance can be absolute or relative. Absolute significance may be that it is not an acceptable loss at any level. Relative significance may be unacceptable only if the opportunity is not high enough.

**The importance of uncertainty**

It’s important to be comfortable with answering “I don’t know” and not to assume the answers to these questions, as they have a direct impact on your decision-making process. If you answer "I don't know", the next action should be to seek information to gain more certainty. The faster we can do this, the faster we can make a decision.

**Marginal gains**

In decision-making, gaining more information about the variables we are uncertain about is often the earliest and most productive marginal gains of making a good decision.

**What defines a good decision?**

Key principles

Due to the inherent uncertainty in decisions, judging the quality of a decision by the outcome is not likely to be accurate.

Decision-making processes should be the primary metric for the quality of a decision.

Outcomes can be used as information to be weighed against other information in the judgement of the decision-making process.

This concept of outcome uncertainty forms a fundamental principle of the next set of strategies.

**Positional decisions and expected value**

Key principles

Since outcomes are not certain, decisions can be made to further our position, which gives us more leverage and influence over future decisions.

Decisions with a positive expected value are more likely to produce the expected outcome over multiple repetitions.

Emotions are an important consideration of decision-making, but making only emotional decisions has a negative expected value.

Making position-based decisions is the norm in most industries and professions where decisions are uncertain and high-risk.

Note that expected value is also called +/- EV or just “expectancy” (e.g. *plus EV *vs. *minus EV*, or *positive expectancy* vs. *negative expectancy)*. Different fields use different terms, but they refer to the same concept.

**Perfectionism and overthinking**

Key principles

Perfectionism is usually a symptom of anxiety and insecurity.

The solution to overthinking is not underthinking, but rather more productive and targeted thinking.

We can use certain thinking systems and steps to force more perspective on our perfectionist thoughts.

About mental health and anxiety

Anxiety

Anxiety is a real and potentially serious condition. If you suffer from anxiety to the point where it impacts your life in a significant way, we recommend you see a medical professional such as your family doctor.

Types of interventions

**Intervention through medication** can help to kickstart a transformation, especially when certain emotions become ingrained, as your brain starts to adapt and mould to being better at feeling that emotion. Medication helps balance the playing field to give yourself a fighting chance without your neuroadaptive hormonal changes fighting against you.

**Psychologist interventions** act like brain coaches, much like how this course coaches you through learning. Trying to realise and train yourself to think in specific ways that may “cure” your mental health condition can take years or decades if done alone.

Furthermore, seeking help from untrained individuals can lead you in the wrong direction or not give you the support you need.

Your health record

Mental health diagnoses are confidential and do not impact almost any university program acceptance or career path outside of being a commercial pilot or some military positions. Even then, there is considerable leniency and humanism in the selection process.

**Curbing overthinking and anxious perfectionism**

The following framework can help to reduce overthinking and anxiety-driven perfectionism.

**Instructions**

Identify what emotions you are feeling. The most important part is to be specific.

Create a problem list of all the root causes for each feeling, whereby if these problems did not exist, you would not feel this way. The most important part here is to be both specific and comprehensive.

Breakdown the problems into a series of actionable tasks that can help you to overcome these problems*. Problems may be overcome by literally removing them, or changing our perspective or response to them.

Identify one task in your controllable list that you can do something about right now and act on it.

**You may conclude that some problems are outside of your control. It can be helpful to mark which problems are inside and outside your control to help direct focus and energy.*

**The power of action**

The more specific you can be and the smaller you can break down your problems, the more likely you will have something you can act on. The more you can act on them, the less overwhelmed you will tend to feel. Even the act of breaking down the problems in the first place can significantly reduce how overwhelming it feels.

**Important**

We do not address the *negative* aspects of a perfectionist identity in this lesson. Those with a perfectionist identity may overly push themselves towards high achievement and put themselves under unhealthy amounts of pressure. The ability to gain perfect outcomes can be tied to a value judgement on themselves and their worth. 

**If this resonates with you, we recommend discussing with a trained therapist who can help guide and coach you to dissociate self-worth from achievement.**

**Applying theory to reality**

Key principles

Due to the nature of wicked learning environments, it’s impractical to actually calculate EV’s precisely.

We can simplify the calculation with some generalisations which still allows a positive EV decision-making process.

To help simplify the variables of real-life decision-making we can apply the following steps.

**Instructions for simplifying decisions**

Remove only the smaller and insignificant variables.

Broadly categorise significance into four tiers.

Broadly categorise chance into four tiers.

Evaluate the EV of your decision based on this simplification.

**When changing decisions is best**

Key principles

Changing decisions is affected by several cognitive biases such as status quo bias, sunk cost bias and loss aversion bias.

Probability concentration shows that changing decisions can be advantageous.

Decisions made with greater information, at a later point in time, can be more appropriate than not changing.

Society typically values those who "stick with their decisions". However, this confuses resilience and persistence with narrow-mindedness. Resilience and grit are essential to overcome difficulties and challenges when the decision aligns with our goals, values, and priorities. On the other hand, changing decisions quickly is a sign of an adaptive and critical thinker. 

**Bestselling author and decision-making consultant Annie Duke says, "Quitting isn’t always a sign of defeat. Highly successful people often know exactly when to give up." **
