Unchained learners have high potential but are limited by their approach to self-improvement and a fixed mindset on growth. They are our most common archetype, making up over 40% of all our learners. Though they often have a strong drive and motivation to succeed, they can become burnt out because their methods waste too much effort. Unchained learners are restricted by their mindset and self-beliefs, but can experience the most dramatic improvements out of any archetype once these restrictions are removed. They often report feeling 'liberated' and 'able to tackle anything'.
## The fixed mindset "chain"

Research suggests several factors contribute to the development of a fixed mindset, as opposed to a growth mindset. Dweck (2006) highlights the core belief that intelligence and abilities are fixed and unchangeable. 

This belief can be shaped by early life experiences, such as receiving constant praise for natural talent rather than effort (Blackwell et al., 2007) or facing criticism that focuses on inherent flaws rather than specific actions (Cantor & Dweck, 1990). Additionally, exposure to societal messages emphasizing a limited pool of intelligence or talent can contribute to a fixed mindset (Burkland-Holm & Dweck, 2010). These experiences can lead individuals to prioritize appearing intelligent and avoid challenges that might expose limitations, ultimately hindering their growth and solidifying a fixed mindset (Dweck, 2006).

In many cases, grades and outcomes were heavily prioritised and the young learner may have associated self-worth and approval with achieving these outcomes. 

**References**

Blackwell, L. S., Dweck, C. S., & Dweck, Y. S. (2007). Praise its content, not just its effort: Is the message the medium? Journal of Educational Psychology, 99(4), 784-792.

## Unchained struggles
The greatest 'chain' is the fear of failure, which creates an avoidance of challenges and an unwillingness to experiment with new methods. As a result, this mindset fundamentally prevents any growth and improvement. They may also struggle with insecurity and emotional self-regulation, causing strain in relationships when their identity as 'a person who doesn't make mistakes' is challenged.

**We call the archetype 'Unchained' because that is the goal. You must focus your attention and efforts on removing the chains that prevent you from growth. Ensure that your decisions, actions and processes are aligned to achieving your goals, rather than escaping from a fear of failure.**
## Unchained strengths
Many people assume that those chained by a fixed mindset are simply disadvantaged. However, this is not true. In many cases, learners who have struggled with a fixed mindset for years have also developed a much higher level of other attributes to compensate. These attributes can include persistence, determination, methods of motivation management, and adaptability. Once the fixed mindset is addressed, these other strengths can shine.

Training skills and replacing old habits with new processes help to transform us into more complete and adaptable learners. Fortunately, our program is designed with guidelines and recommendations specifically for Unchained learners. 

**Some of our highest-performing students (and even coaches) started as Unchained!**
## Unchained pseudo-strengths
While Unchained learners have real strengths that help them succeed, some "pseudo-strengths" may appear to be strengths when they are actually detrimental. Recognising if you resonate with these is important, as they can become barriers to growth.

Common pseudo-strengths include:

- **Focus and persistence in specific areas:** Some argue that individuals with a fixed mindset, believing their skills are already honed, might focus intensely on specific areas they excel in. This focused effort can lead to deep expertise in those areas. However, this benefit comes with the drawback of **avoiding challenges and new learning opportunities** outside their comfort zone, potentially limiting overall growth and adaptability.
- **Fake confidence:** Fixed-minded individuals might appear confident in their abilities, especially in familiar situations. However, this confidence often stems from focusing on past achievements rather than a growth-oriented belief in their ability to learn and improve. This **misguided confidence** can be fragile and crumble when faced with setbacks or challenges that expose limitations. Learners can sometimes react over-defensively when their confidence is at risk. For example, it is common for some learners to blame others when they struggle as it challenges their identity as "an achiever".
