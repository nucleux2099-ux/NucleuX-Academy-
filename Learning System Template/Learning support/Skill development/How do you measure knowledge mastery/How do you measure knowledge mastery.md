# How do you measure knowledge mastery
**How do you measure knowledge mastery?**

**About knowledge mastery**

Learning is inherently complex and individually variable. Measuring how good of a "learner" you are can be challenging and intangible. The following metrics are useful guidelines so that you can create meaningful goals and track your progress.

**Mastery levels**

We find that a composite between Bloom's revised taxonomy and SOLO taxonomy (structure of observed learning outcomes) are useful measures for mastery of knowledge. We've outlined our framework below, inspired by Bloom's and SOLO taxonomies.

Higher levels of mastery mean your learning is deeper, which tends to correlate with being able to solve more complex problems and having a higher retention of knowledge.

Level 1 (lowest)

You can recall facts and isolated details only. Your learning tends to be heavily repetition and memorisation-based. You can recognise terminology and understand what it means.

**Common methods:**

Flashcards on facts and definitions.

Cover-copy-check.

Questions on isolated details

**Commonly tested by:**

Fact recall questions

Level 2

You can explain isolated concepts and have discussions about individual concepts. You can solve simple problems using this concept. You can engage in simple discussions about the concept but will struggle to discuss extended ideas or atypical applications of the concept.

**Common methods:**

Flashcards on explaining concepts or processes.

Questions on explaining how a process or concept works.

Teaching someone about a single concept.

Process maps and flow diagrams showing how concepts work.

**Commonly tested by:**

Questions to explain and describe processes and concepts

Level 3

You can see how concepts are related to each other. You analyse and compare concepts against each other. Relationships are between individual concepts but are not strongly connected to the big picture of the entire topic. 

You can have in-depth discussions about the topic. You may not be able to converse at an expert level, but you might know what questions to ask an expert.

**Common methods:**

Teaching about how two or more concepts are related to each other.

Comparing and contrasting multiple concepts.

Questions that test the relationship between two or more concepts.

Mindmaps that show how concepts are related to each other.

**Commonly tested by:**

Questions that ask you to compare and contrast multiple concepts against each other

Problems that require you to draw on knowledge from multiple concepts

Level 4 (Significant learning benefits)

You have a heavily connected appreciation of the relationships between multiple concepts. You can see how individual facts and details fit into the big picture. Often, your understanding feels "simpler" and more "intuitive", with very little information purely memorised through repetition. 

You should be able to have contextually nuanced discussions with an expert about the topic, navigating between concepts fluidly and quickly appreciating how changes to one concept can impact not only other concepts but the impact it has on the overall topic, process or function.

**The difference in impact between level 3 and level 4 is significant.**

**Common methods:**

Chunkmaps that show how concepts are categorised into groups, based on similarities, as well as the relationships between these chunks, with emphasis on the more important relationships compared to weaker and less important relationships.

Questions that require justification and discussion around the relative importance of concepts with respect to other concepts and relationships.

Whole-Part-Whole reteaching technique (as is taught in the course).

Discussion on how different contexts affect the importance of concepts and their relationships.

**Commonly tested by:**

Questions that require nuanced discussion of the influence of concepts on each other

Complex problems that draw on knowledge of multiple concepts and their influence on each other

Contextually abstract problems and questions that require the application of knowledge in diverse, multi-factorial scenarios

Level 5

You are creating new and novel information using your knowledge. You create hypotheses and conjectures.

**Common methods:**

Novel research and exploration of a thesis.

Discussion of ideas that are extensions of known principles and prior knowledge.

Hypothesising structures and connections using prior knowledge.

Exploring new information like an experiment to confirm or reject these hypotheses.

**Commonly tested by:**

High-level narrative, commentary, or research

Typically, there are no assessments at this level in formal education prior to Ph.D.

**Key principle**

Higher-order is integrated. Lower-order is isolated.

The defining feature of higher-order learning is a greater appreciation of how information fits into the big picture. This is because strong, simple and highly refined organisational schemas need to be created for your brain to navigate the topic quickly and fluidly (like highways for your mind). 

Better big-picture understanding leads to more meaningful information, which leads to greater retention and encoding. This reduces the need for frequent repetition.

Important considerations

Both lower-order and higher-order methods are needed in a comprehensive learning system.

Lower-order learning approaches only lead to lower-order outcomes.

Higher-order approaches lead to both lower-order and higher-order outcomes.

It is always better to start your learning with higher-order approaches, and use lower-order methods as a supplement or closer to assessments.

Higher-order learning methods make learning new content easier over time as you build expertise. Lower-order methods tends to make you more overwhelmed as new information stays isolated rather than consolidating into a network.
