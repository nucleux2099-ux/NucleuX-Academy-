# Whats the difference between the 30-Day Plan and Marginal gains
**What's the difference between the 30-Day Plan and Marginal gains?**

**Comparing the 30-Day Plan to Marginal Gains**

**The 30-Day Plan**

The 30-Day Plan is a **step-by-step process** for creating your personal success plan. It is taught in the early stages of the program.

This process maximises our chances of success by creating plans that consider a few crucial (often overlooked) elements. By proactively removing barriers and setting ourselves up for success, we can achieve our goals more easily and consistently.

**Marginal gains**

Marginal Gains is a **perspective and framework** for tracking and accelerating skill development. It allows for rapid, consistent, and motivating skill growth through compounding incremental (marginal) changes.

**What's the difference?**

Marginal gains provide a **guideline** on how to develop your skills. The 30-Day Plan gives you a **concrete plan** of what to do for the next 30 days to help you take action and make your desired marginal gains.

**Combining 30-Day Plans with Marginal gains**

To combine both frameworks effectively, you can use the Marginal Gains approach within the structure of a 30-Day Plan.

Start with the 30-day plan:

Identify a meaningful **medium-term goal** and break it down into most important short-term **performance-based goals.**

Explore other factors that can affect your behaviours and impede your performance (eg: limiting habits and environment)

Supplement this using Marginal Gains Tracking & Stacking:

Prioritize the 2-3 most important **skills, processes or attributes** that are important for achieving the medium-term and performance goals.

Aim to **track and stack** 1% of improvements to achieve steady attainment of these goals.

Make **pace adjustments** and continuous **marginal gains** in required areas.

Example

Zaheer aims to achieve an A* in his upcoming A-level examinations in 4-months

Starting with the 30-day plan:

He identifies the most **important processes** for the performance goals - scheduling, high-efficiency encoding, and interleaved retrieval practice.

Further, he realises that procrastination is a limiting habit in the attainment of his goals and he needs to devise a scripted action for it.

Supplementing this with Marginal Gains Tracking & Stacking:

Zaheer finds that the weakest link is ineffective interleaved retrieval practice and procrastination. He knows that improving this skill can significantly influence his success.

He decides to make a 1% improvement by practising relational teaching every alternate day as an interleaved retrieval practice.

To combat procrastination, he decides to practice the BEDSM framework consciously for a week.

He reflects on his attempts daily and makes tiny adjustments to his technique to fix new mistakes and opportunities for improvement he identifies.

| Aspect | Marginal Gains | 30-Day Plan |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary focus | Skill development (i.e. getting better at a skill). | Self-management (i.e. how to take action productively). |
| Unique concepts | Relies on the concept that skill development is faster, easier, and more motivating by compounding tiny gains, rather than aiming for big gains. | Relies on the concept that goals are more likely to be achieved when plans consider possible barriers in advance, especially around habits and tendencies. |
| Method | Involves tracking and stacking 1% gains through experimentation and reflection. | Involves clear goal-setting and evaluating our habits and behaviours to create a plan that has a high chance of success. |
| Usage | Should be used continuously and forever in any situation where skill development is desired. | Used when we need a concrete, safe plan to make rapid progress towards a goal. |
| Scope | Breaks down bigger skills into smaller, focused gains. | Breaks down bigger goals into a 30-day mini-goal and a clear plan. |
| End goal | Aims to build a high level of competence in the target skill | Aims to achieve a medium-term goal |
