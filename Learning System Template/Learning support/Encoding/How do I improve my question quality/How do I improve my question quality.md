# How do I improve my question quality
**How do I improve my question quality?**

Not all questions are created equal. Asking high-quality questions and invoking curiosity are fundamental skills for high-level inquiry-based learning (such as our Bear Hunter System).

**The most important questions to ask for any concept are:**

Why is it important?

How is it related to [another concept]?

These force you to connect the information at a higher level of knowledge mastery, reinforcing relationships and preventing information from being learned in isolated silos.

**The natural consequence is an increase (often significant) in retention.**

**The challenges of inquiry-based learning**

Inquiry-based learning is controversial in the learning science space. This is because there are few guidelines for best-practice and it depends on the quality of the facilitator. There is very little research on learners using inquiry-based learning for their own learning.

The most effective way to use inquiry-based learning (which we teach), is to remember the cognitive principles it must abide by.

Key principles

Asking questions must help you find relationships

Answering questions must force you to evaluate the importance of information and make value judgments

You must exert mental effort to make sense of the information

Information must always be connected to a big picture

Information must always form a network

The network should be logical and make as much sense to you as possible (i.e. just because the information is connected does not mean it was connected in a meaningful way)

You can use the above points as a checklist to see if your inquiry process adheres to all the points.

Note that anything that prevents you from finding multiple relationships or avoids the need for making a value judgment on the importance of relationships will reduce the quality of your learning. This is one of the most common reasons for ineffective chunking or reduced-quality encoding.

**Common issues**

Questions are answered with a simple, direct answer. The purpose of asking questions is to identify multiple relationships which you can then evaluate and make judgments on.

Questions are too "what" based. These questions are isolating and do not require students to look at relationships between other pieces of information.

Questions neglect to ask "why is it important?" - This is one of the most critical questions to ask in order to create chunks rapidly, as well as identify key relationships.

Questions are only asked for individual concepts, but not on chunks which have been identified.

**Common signs and symptoms of incorrect inquiry**

Information is retained less and recall is slower.

Curveball questions in exams are more difficult to answer as ideas have not been related to other ideas during the study process. Variations are therefore harder to synthesise and conceptualise, which curveballs test you on.

Mindmaps seem disorganised. This is because the backbone of concepts and logic are not formed strongly as either questions were not asked of the largest chunks.
