# What is the optimal function of motivation
**What is the optimal function of motivation?**

**Motivation vs. marginal gains**

In the early stages of the program, we learn about marginal gains and how it helps improve motivation. Later, we learn about motivation dependency.

How do these two concepts relate to each other? Why do we need to improve motivation when we should not be motivation-dependent? To answer these questions, we can explore the process of learning new skills and engaging in self-development.

**The process of self-development**

Identify desired outcomes

Identify necessary skills and processes to achieve the outcome

Unlearn old habits, remove barriers, and start training new habits

Learn the necessary skills

Continue forward with your set of new skills and habits

As you can see from the steps above, habit formation is not an immediate outcome. Forming habits takes time because:

We need habits of practice and self-reflection to develop other habits more intentionally and quickly

We have to spend time to unlearn old habits

We usually have to remove the barriers that prevent us from achieving 1. and 2.

After we have learned new habits, we no longer require motivation on a daily basis to perform the function that our habit fulfils. However, the early stages of learning a new skill require an upfront investment of time, diligence, and consistency. This is the period where demotivation can set in if we are unable to see the progress we are making.

Key principles

Motivation is useful as a resource when we need it to break through challenges and develop new habits

Motivation-dependency is undesirable when we use it to perform tasks and actions that could otherwise be driven by habit

Motivation should be used strategically to develop key habits

Marginal gains improves motivation in the skill development pathway until new habits have formed (this can take weeks or months)
