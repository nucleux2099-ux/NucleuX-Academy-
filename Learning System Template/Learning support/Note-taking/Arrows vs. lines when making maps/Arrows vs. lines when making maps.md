# Arrows vs. lines when making maps
**Arrows vs. lines when making maps**

**Note-taking**



**Why does it matter?**

Sometimes, we see that lines are used to represent relationships instead of arrows. Arrows require a deeper level of processing to be more explicit about the nature of a relationship, whereas lines can simply indicate the general awareness of a connection that has not been prioritised at higher levels.

This simple decision to use arrows forces us to consider the nature of a relationship more deeply, activating higher-order learning. Consider that an arrow going one way is different to an arrow going another way, which is different to a double-sided arrow, a curly-bracket arrow, or a deliberately non-arrow line.

**When should I use lines?**

Lines are still acceptable to indicate that the information is connected but without any directionality or cause/effect relationship. This is more common in the lower levels of detail, where information in a specific detailed chunk may be split into multiple component facts. The facts may simply exist within this lower-level chunk without having any directionality.
