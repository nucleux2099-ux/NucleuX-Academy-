# I have an exam soon. How should I prepare for it
**I have an exam soon. How should I prepare for it?**

**Formal ****educationUsing**** the ****iCanStudy**** program**



**Managing expectations**

Before we discuss this, it's important to note that the "best possible result" is not the same as the "best result".

*For example, we have many students entering our course just a few weeks before their exams, expecting that they can take a predicted mark of 60 or 70% and turn it into 95% in a matter of weeks. This is simply unrealistic.*

**Change takes time.**

**Prioritising your skills**

When coming up to an imminent test or exam, it's important that we gain mastery over our current techniques, filling in any immediate gaps without destabilising our existing system.

The worst thing we can do is learn many new techniques that take training to master, learn them incorrectly, and do even worse than if we had just slammed through a million flashcards instead.

This is why the course is divided into High-yield and Growth phases.

**If you're a new member**

Focus on the High-yield techniques. 

These are techniques that you can gain reasonable mastery of in under two weeks. They give immediate returns and are often enough to improve your results significantly. **Caution**

Learning the techniques in the Growth phase before mastering Fundamentals will likely result in **worse** performance than just focusing on Fundamentals. This is because almost all Growth phase techniques require weeks to gain competence.

**If you've been on the program for a while**

Stop progressing through the course and consolidate the techniques you have already learned. Use your existing set of techniques wherever appropriate to prepare for your exam

**Steps to consolidate your current system**

Complete a skills audit by checking against our milestone checklists at the end of High-yield phase, Technique Training, and Ascent 3.

Use Kolb's (taught in Briefing) to optimise weaknesses or deficiencies in your current system.

**Key principle**

When approaching an imminent assessment or performance challenge, the benefit of learning new techniques is usually not worth the instability it introduces to your system and the time it takes away from using effective foundational techniques.

The only exception is if you lack foundational techniques. In this case, the time it takes for mastery of a few core foundational skills is relatively low, while the benefit (even in the short-term) is high.
