# Hip Fractures

> 📚 **Sources:** Maheshwari Orthopedics 7th Ed, Campbell's Operative Orthopaedics 14th Ed, AAOS
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Hip fractures are fractures of the proximal femur, classified anatomically into **intracapsular** (femoral neck) and **extracapsular** (intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric) types. This distinction is crucial because the blood supply to the femoral head (via retinacular vessels) is at risk in intracapsular fractures.

**The Great Divider:** The joint capsule attachment determines prognosis
- **Intracapsular** → High risk of AVN, non-union → Often needs replacement
- **Extracapsular** → Good blood supply → Internal fixation works well

## Key Points

- **"Hip fracture"** typically refers to proximal femur fractures, not acetabular fractures
- Second most common fracture in elderly (after Colles)
- **Mortality**: 20-30% at 1 year in elderly (medical emergency!)
- **Osteoporosis** is the major predisposing factor
- **Female:Male ratio** = 3:1 (postmenopausal osteoporosis)
- Every hip fracture patient needs falls assessment and osteoporosis workup

### Intracapsular (Femoral Neck) Fractures

**Garden Classification** (most important for neck fractures)
| Grade | Description | Prognosis |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| I | Incomplete/impacted, valgus | Good - conservative/fixation |
| II | Complete, undisplaced | Moderate - internal fixation |
| III | Complete, partially displaced | Poor - high AVN risk |
| IV | Complete, fully displaced | Worst - replacement often needed |

*Garden I & II = Undisplaced (fixation)*
*Garden III & IV = Displaced (replacement in elderly)*

**Pauwels Classification** (angle of fracture line)
| Type | Angle | Shear Force | Prognosis |
|------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| I | <30° | Low | Good |
| II | 30-50° | Moderate | Moderate |
| III | >50° | High (vertical) | Poor |

### Extracapsular Fractures

**Boyd and Griffin Classification (Intertrochanteric)**
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| I | Stable, along intertrochanteric line |
| II | Comminuted, main fracture along IT line |
| III | Subtrochanteric extension |
| IV | Subtrochanteric with IT involvement |

**AO/OTA Classification** - Comprehensive, uses alphanumeric coding (31A1-3, 31B1-3)

## Clinical Features

### History
- Fall (usually trivial in elderly - low energy)
- Immediate inability to bear weight
- Pain in hip/groin
- High-energy trauma in young patients

### Classic Triad of Clinical Signs
1. **Shortening** of affected limb (1-2 cm in displaced fractures)
2. **External rotation** (due to unopposed pull of short external rotators)
3. **Abduction** (not always present)

### Examination
- Unable to do straight leg raise
- Tenderness over greater trochanter
- Pain on axial loading and rotation
- Assess neurovascular status (sciatic nerve)

### Beware: Impacted Fractures
- May be able to walk!
- Minimal deformity
- Pain only at extremes of movement
- Easy to miss - always X-ray elderly with hip pain after fall

## Imaging

### X-ray (AP Pelvis + Lateral Hip)
- **Shenton's line** disruption (arc from obturator foramen to femoral neck)
- Fracture line visible
- Displacement assessment
- Bone quality assessment (Singh index for osteoporosis)

### MRI
- **Gold standard for occult fractures**
- When X-ray negative but clinical suspicion high
- Shows bone marrow edema within hours

### CT Scan
- Pre-operative planning
- Complex fracture patterns
- Assess posterior wall integrity

### Singh Index (Trabecular Pattern)
| Grade | Trabeculae Present | Osteoporosis |
|-------|-------------------|--------------|
| 6 | All groups visible | Normal |
| 5 | Secondary tensile lost | - |
| 4 | Secondary tensile and secondary compressive reduced | Mild |
| 3 | Break in principal tensile | Moderate |
| 2 | Only principal compressive visible | Severe |
| 1 | Even principal compressive reduced | Very severe |

## Management

### Emergency Priorities
1. **Analgesia** - fascia iliaca block, IV morphine
2. **Fluid resuscitation** - hip fractures bleed (500-1000ml)
3. **DVT prophylaxis** - start immediately
4. **Medical optimization** - cardiac, respiratory assessment
5. **Surgery within 36-48 hours** - delays increase mortality

### Surgical Decision Making

#### Intracapsular (Femoral Neck) Fractures

**Young patients (<60 years) - Always try to save the head**
- Undisplaced → Cannulated screws (3 screws in inverted triangle)
- Displaced → Urgent reduction + internal fixation within 6 hours
- Failed fixation → THR

**Elderly patients (>60 years)**
| Factor | Fixation | Hemiarthroplasty | Total Hip Replacement |
|--------|----------|------------------|----------------------|
| Age | <60 yrs | 60-80 yrs | Active >80, <80 with arthritis |
| Fracture | Undisplaced | Displaced | Displaced |
| Activity | Very active | Low demand | High demand |
| Cognition | Any | Dementia OK | Good cognition |
| Arthritis | No | No | Pre-existing OA |

#### Extracapsular Fractures

**Intertrochanteric:**
- **Dynamic Hip Screw (DHS)** - stable patterns
- **Cephalomedullary nail (PFNA, Gamma nail)** - unstable/reverse oblique patterns

**Subtrochanteric:**
- **Cephalomedullary nail** - always (high stress area)
- Long nail needed

### Non-operative Management
- Only for non-ambulatory, severely demented patients
- Or those with prohibitive surgical risk
- Pain control, prevent complications
- High mortality

## Blood Supply of Femoral Head

**Critical for understanding AVN risk:**

1. **Medial femoral circumflex artery** (most important)
   - Gives retinacular vessels
   - Runs along posterior neck - damaged in displaced fractures

2. **Lateral femoral circumflex artery**
   - Minor contribution

3. **Artery of ligamentum teres**
   - From obturator artery
   - Minimal contribution in adults

**Intracapsular fracture disrupts retinacular vessels → AVN in 30% of displaced fractures**

## Complications

### Early
- DVT/PE (most dangerous early complication)
- Infection
- Pressure sores
- Urinary retention/infection
- Delirium
- Medical decompensation

### Late
| Complication | Intracapsular | Extracapsular |
|--------------|---------------|---------------|
| AVN | 30% (displaced) | Rare |
| Non-union | 30% | 2% |
| Malunion | Less common | Coxa vara |
| Implant failure | Cut-out | Cut-out |

### Implant Cut-out
- Screw/blade cuts through femoral head
- Risk factors: Poor placement, osteoporosis, unstable fracture
- **Tip-Apex Distance (TAD)** should be <25mm

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Garden classification** is for femoral neck fractures only
2. **Displaced femoral neck fracture in young** = Orthopedic emergency (reduce within 6 hours)
3. **Blood supply** to femoral head is via posterior retinacular vessels (medial femoral circumflex → superior and inferior retinacular arteries)
4. **Intracapsular fractures** - blood supply disrupted → AVN, non-union risk
5. **Extracapsular fractures** - good blood supply → heal well with fixation
6. **Garden III, IV in elderly** = Hemiarthroplasty (Austin Moore/bipolar) or THR
7. **DHS** = workhorse for stable intertrochanteric fractures
8. **Tip-Apex Distance <25mm** = good screw position, reduces cut-out risk
9. **Singh index** assesses osteoporosis severity on X-ray
10. **Mortality within 1 year** of hip fracture = 20-30% (treat as medical emergency!)
11. **Cemented hemiarthroplasty** gives better early mobilization in elderly
12. **Bisphosphonates** cause atypical subtrochanteric fractures (lateral cortex stress fractures)

| Fracture Type | <60 years | >60 years |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Neck - Undisplaced | Cannulated screws | Cannulated screws/Hemi |
