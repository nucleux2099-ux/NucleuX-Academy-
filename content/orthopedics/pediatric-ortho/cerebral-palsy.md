# Cerebral Palsy - Orthopedic Management

> 📚 **Sources:** Maheshwari Orthopedics 7th Ed, Campbell's Operative Orthopaedics, Miller's Review
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Cerebral Palsy (CP) is a **non-progressive disorder of movement and posture** caused by injury to the developing brain (before, during, or shortly after birth). While the brain lesion is static, the musculoskeletal manifestations are **progressive** due to abnormal muscle forces on growing bones.

**Orthopedic Role:** Manage the **secondary musculoskeletal problems** (contractures, deformities, hip displacement, scoliosis) to maximize function and prevent pain.

## Key Points

- **Incidence:** 2-3 per 1000 live births
- **Brain lesion is static**, but musculoskeletal effects are progressive
- **Spasticity** is the most common motor type (70-80%)
- **Hip displacement** is the most important orthopedic issue (30%)
- **GMFCS** (Gross Motor Function Classification System) determines prognosis and guides treatment
- **Multidisciplinary approach** essential (PT, OT, ortho, neuro, developmental pediatrics)

## Classification

### By Motor Type (Physiological)

| Type | Characteristics | % | Brain Area |
|------|-----------------|---|------------|
| **Spastic** | Increased tone, velocity-dependent | 70-80% | Pyramidal (motor cortex) |
| **Dyskinetic** | Involuntary movements | 10-15% | Basal ganglia |
| **Ataxic** | Poor balance, coordination | 5% | Cerebellum |
| **Mixed** | Combination | Variable | Multiple areas |

### By Distribution (Topographical)

| Type | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Hemiplegia** | One side (arm usually worse than leg) |
| **Diplegia** | Both legs (most common in premature) |
| **Quadriplegia** | All four limbs (arms often worse or equal) |
| **Monoplegia** | Single limb (rare) |
| **Triplegia** | Three limbs (rare) |

### GMFCS (Gross Motor Function Classification System)

**Most important classification for prognosis and treatment planning!**

| Level | Ability | Walking Aid |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **I** | Walks without restrictions | None |
| **II** | Walks with limitations | Minimal support |
| **III** | Walks with assistive device | Walker, crutches |
| **IV** | Self-mobility with limitations | Powered wheelchair |
| **V** | Transported in wheelchair | Full assistance needed |

**Prognosis by GMFCS:**
- Level I-III: Will walk (community or household)
- Level IV-V: Will likely need wheelchair for mobility
- **Hip displacement risk:** Level I: 0%, Level V: 90%

## Musculoskeletal Problems

### Spasticity Management

**Options (Least to Most Invasive):**
1. **Physical therapy, stretching, splinting** - Cornerstone
2. **Botulinum toxin (Botox)** - Focal spasticity, 3-6 month duration
3. **Intrathecal baclofen** - Generalized spasticity (pump implant)
4. **Selective dorsal rhizotomy (SDR)** - Permanent reduction in spasticity
5. **Orthopedic surgery** - Address fixed contractures

### Upper Extremity

| Deformity | Cause | Treatment |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Shoulder adduction** | Pec, subscapularis spasticity | Release, lengthening |
| **Elbow flexion** | Biceps, brachialis | Lengthening, fractional |
| **Forearm pronation** | Pronator teres | PT release, rerouting |
| **Wrist flexion** | FCR, FCU | FCU transfer to ECRB |
| **Thumb-in-palm** | Adductor, FPL | Adductor release, EPL rerouting |
| **Swan neck** | Intrinsic spasticity | FDS tenodesis |

**Goal:** Improve function for grasping, hygiene, cosmesis

### Lower Extremity

#### Hip Problems (Most Important!)

**Hip Displacement** - Progressive, due to:
- Adductor/flexor spasticity
- Abnormal femoral anteversion
- Acetabular dysplasia
- GMFCS Level IV-V at highest risk

**Reimers Migration Index:**
- % of femoral head outside acetabulum on AP X-ray
- <30%: Normal
- 30-50%: Subluxation (needs close monitoring)
- 50-90%: Progressive subluxation (usually needs surgery)
- 100%: Dislocation

**Treatment by Migration Index:**
| MI | Status | Treatment |
|----|--------|-----------|
| <30% | Normal | Observation |
| 30-40% | At risk | Botox, bracing, soft tissue surgery |
| 40-60% | Subluxation | VDRO + soft tissue release |
| >60% | Severe | VDRO + pelvic osteotomy |
| 100% dislocated | | VDRO + pelvic osteotomy (if painful) or salvage |

**Soft Tissue Procedures:**
- Adductor release (longus ± brevis)
- Iliopsoas release (at lesser trochanter)

**Bony Procedures:**
- **VDRO** (Varus Derotation Osteotomy) - Corrects coxa valga and anteversion
- **Pelvic osteotomy** (Dega, Pemberton, San Diego) - Improves coverage

#### Knee Deformities

| Deformity | Cause | Treatment |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Flexion contracture** | Hamstring spasticity | Hamstring lengthening |
| **Recurvatum** | Weak hamstrings, strong quads | Hamstring transfer, brace |
| **Stiff knee gait** | Rectus femoris overactivity | Rectus transfer to hamstrings |

**Crouch Gait:** Flexion at hip, knee, and ankle
- Common in diplegics
- Treatment: Multi-level surgery (hip flexor release, hamstring lengthening, ankle correction)

#### Foot and Ankle

| Deformity | Muscles Involved | Treatment |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| **Equinus** | Gastrocnemius, soleus | Gastrocnemius recession, TAL |
| **Equinovarus** | Tibialis posterior, anterior | Split tibialis anterior transfer, TAL |
| **Equinovalgus** | Peroneals, weak tibialis posterior | Peroneus brevis lengthening, calcaneal osteotomy |
| **Planovalgus** | Peroneal overactivity | Lateral column lengthening, subtalar fusion |

**Surgical Principles:**
- Avoid overlengthening (calcaneus gait, crouch)
- Preserve motor control when possible
- **Split transfers** better than releases

### Spine

**Scoliosis:**
- Common in non-ambulatory (GMFCS IV-V): 60-80%
- Usually long, C-shaped curve
- Pelvic obliquity common
- Affects sitting, breathing, pain

**Treatment:**
- Bracing: May slow progression, won't prevent
- Surgery: Posterior spinal fusion (often to pelvis)
- Indications: >40-50° curve, progression, sitting imbalance

## Gait Analysis

**Essential for surgical planning in ambulatory CP**

**Types of Gait:**
| Pattern | Features | Level |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **Crouch gait** | Flexion at hip, knee, ankle | Common in diplegia |
| **Jump gait** | Hip/knee flexion + equinus | Diplegia |
| **Apparent equinus** | Knee flexion makes ankle look equinus | Diplegia |
| **True equinus** | Ankle plantarflexion in stance | Variable |
| **Stiff knee gait** | Decreased knee flexion in swing | Rectus overactivity |

**3D Gait Analysis:**
- Kinematics (joint angles)
- Kinetics (forces, moments)
- EMG (muscle timing)
- Guides surgical decision-making

## Timing of Surgery

**Single Event Multi-Level Surgery (SEMLS):**
- Address all levels in one surgery
- Typically age 6-10 years
- Better outcomes than staged procedures
- Requires extensive rehabilitation

**Indications for Surgery:**
- Fixed contractures limiting function
- Progressive deformity
- Gait deterioration
- Pain
- Hygiene/care issues

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **CP = Static brain lesion, progressive musculoskeletal problems**
2. **Spastic type** is most common (70-80%)
3. **GMFCS** predicts walking ability and hip displacement risk
4. **Hip surveillance** is essential - Reimers Migration Index >30% needs intervention
5. **VDRO** corrects coxa valga and femoral anteversion
6. **Crouch gait** = Flexion at hip, knee, ankle - common in diplegics
7. **Rectus transfer** for stiff knee gait (overactive rectus)
8. **Split tibialis anterior transfer** for equinovarus foot
9. **Single event multi-level surgery (SEMLS)** - age 6-10, address all levels at once
10. **Scoliosis** in 60-80% of non-ambulators (GMFCS IV-V)
11. **Botox duration** = 3-6 months (temporary)
12. **Intrathecal baclofen** for generalized spasticity

## Hip Surveillance Protocol

| Age | Frequency | GMFCS I-II | GMFCS III-V |
|-----|-----------|------------|-------------|
| 0-2 years | q6 months | Clinical exam | X-ray |
| 2-4 years | q6-12 months | X-ray if concern | X-ray |
| 4-8 years | q12 months | Clinical | X-ray |
| >8 years | q1-2 years | Clinical | X-ray |

**Goal:** Detect displacement early, intervene before dislocation
