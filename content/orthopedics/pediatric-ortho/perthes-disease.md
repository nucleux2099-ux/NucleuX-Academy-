# Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease

> 📚 **Sources:** Maheshwari Orthopedics 7th Ed, Tachdjian's Pediatric Orthopaedics, Campbell's Operative
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Perthes disease is **idiopathic avascular necrosis (AVN) of the femoral head** in children. The blood supply to the femoral epiphysis is interrupted, leading to necrosis, fragmentation, and eventual revascularization and reossification. The goal of treatment is to **maintain a spherical femoral head contained within the acetabulum** during healing.

**Key Principle:** The acetabulum acts as a mold. If the femoral head stays spherical and contained, it will heal with good shape. If it subluxates or becomes aspherical, arthritis develops.

## Key Points

- **Age:** 4-10 years (peak 5-7 years)
- **Male:Female = 4-5:1**
- **Bilateral** in 10-15% (usually sequential)
- **Self-limiting** disease (heals spontaneously in 2-4 years)
- **Prognosis depends on:** Age at onset, extent of head involvement, head containment
- **Younger children (<6 years)** have better prognosis (more remodeling potential)
- **"The head bone is connected to the hip bone"** - Final head shape determines adult hip function

## Etiology

**Unknown, but theories include:**
- Vascular abnormality (single vessel supply, thrombophilia)
- Hypercoagulable states
- Trauma (transient synovitis)
- Delayed bone age
- Passive smoking

**Not related to:** Infection, tumor, or significant trauma

## Natural History

### Stages of Disease (Waldenstrom Classification)

| Stage | Duration | Pathology | X-ray Findings |
|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Initial (Necrosis)** | 6 months | Ischemia, necrosis | Increased density, subchondral fracture (crescent sign) |
| **Fragmentation** | 8-12 months | Resorption, revascularization | Head appears fragmented, lytic areas |
| **Reossification** | 12-24 months | New bone formation | Progressive ossification |
| **Healed/Remodeling** | Variable | Final head shape achieved | Residual deformity or normal |

**Total disease course:** 2-4 years

## Clinical Features

### History
- Painless limp (most common presentation)
- Hip or knee pain (referred pain common!)
- Symptoms for weeks to months
- Activity-related worsening
- No systemic symptoms

### Examination

**Inspection:**
- Antalgic gait
- Short leg gait (if head collapse)
- Thigh atrophy

**Palpation:**
- May have mild tenderness over hip

**Range of Motion:**
- **Decreased internal rotation and abduction** (earliest signs)
- Flexion usually preserved
- Pain at extremes of motion
- Positive Trendelenburg (abductor weakness)

**Limb Length:**
- May be shortened (head collapse)

### Red Flags (Not Perthes)
- Bilateral simultaneous onset
- Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
- Age <3 or >12 years
- Known steroid use, sickle cell disease

## Imaging

### X-ray (AP + Frog-leg Lateral)

**Early Findings:**
- Smaller, denser epiphysis
- Widened joint space (cartilage hypertrophy)
- **Crescent sign** (subchondral fracture) - best seen on frog-leg lateral

**Later Findings:**
- Fragmentation of epiphysis
- Metaphyseal cysts
- Lateral subluxation
- Femoral head collapse, flattening
- Coxa magna, coxa breva, coxa plana

### MRI
- **Most sensitive** for early detection
- Shows extent of necrosis
- Useful when X-ray normal but clinical suspicion high
- Perfusion MRI can show revascularization

### Bone Scan
- Cold area initially (decreased uptake)
- Later shows increased uptake with revascularization
- Largely replaced by MRI

## Classification Systems

### Catterall Classification (Historical)

| Group | Head Involvement | Prognosis |
|-------|------------------|-----------|
| I | <25% (anterior) | Excellent |
| II | 25-50% (anterior + central) | Good |
| III | 50-75% (large sequestrum) | Fair |
| IV | >75% (total head) | Poor |

### Herring Lateral Pillar Classification (Most Used Today)

**Based on lateral pillar height during fragmentation stage**

| Grade | Lateral Pillar Height | Prognosis |
|-------|----------------------|-----------|
| **A** | Normal height (100%) | Excellent |
| **B** | >50% height maintained | Good (age-dependent) |
| **B/C** | 50% height (borderline) | Intermediate |
| **C** | <50% height | Poor |

**Herring C or B/C in child >8 years = Poor prognosis without treatment**

### Stulberg Classification (Outcome at Maturity)

| Class | Femoral Head | Acetabulum | Prognosis |
|-------|--------------|------------|-----------|
| I | Spherical | Normal | Excellent (no arthritis) |
| II | Spherical | Coxa magna/brevis | Good |
| III | Non-spherical but congruent | Matches head shape | Fair |
| IV | Flat head, non-congruent | Normal | Poor |
| V | Flat head, non-congruent | Abnormal | Worst (early OA) |

**Stulberg I-II:** Good long-term outcome
**Stulberg III-V:** Risk of early osteoarthritis

## Prognostic Factors

### Good Prognosis
- Age <6 years at onset
- Less head involvement (Catterall I-II, Herring A-B)
- Good range of motion maintained
- Head remains contained

### Poor Prognosis ("Head at Risk" Signs)
1. **Gage sign** - V-shaped lucency in lateral epiphysis
2. **Calcification lateral to epiphysis**
3. **Lateral subluxation**
4. **Horizontal physis**
5. **Metaphyseal cyst** (Caffey sign)

## Management

### Goals
1. **Relieve symptoms** (pain, spasm)
2. **Maintain range of motion** (especially abduction, rotation)
3. **Contain the femoral head** within acetabulum (mold a spherical head)
4. **Prevent femoral head deformation**

### Treatment Algorithm

| Age | Herring Grade | Treatment |
|-----|---------------|-----------|
| <6 years | Any | Observation, maintain ROM |
| 6-8 years | A, B | Observation, PT, ± bracing |
| 6-8 years | B/C, C | Containment surgery |
| >8 years | B, B/C, C | Containment surgery |

### Non-Operative Treatment
- **Activity modification** (avoid impact activities)
- **Physical therapy** (ROM exercises, stretching)
- **NSAIDs** for pain
- **Traction** (for acute synovitis, to regain motion)
- **Abduction bracing** (controversial, inconsistent evidence)

**Petrie casts:** Abduction casts to contain head (used less now)

### Surgical Treatment

**Containment Procedures:**

| Procedure | Description | Indication |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Femoral varus osteotomy** | Brings head deeper into acetabulum | Standard containment |
| **Shelf acetabuloplasty** | Adds lateral coverage | Lateral uncoverage |
| **Pelvic osteotomy** (Salter, triple) | Redirects acetabulum | Acetabular dysplasia |
| **Combined** | Femoral + pelvic | Severe subluxation |

**Salvage Procedures (Healed with Deformity):**
- Femoral head reduction osteotomy (Morscher)
- Cheilectomy (remove osteophytes)
- Hip arthrodiastasis
- Eventually: Hip arthroplasty in adulthood

### Indications for Surgery
- Age >8 years with significant involvement
- Herring B/C or C
- "Head at risk" signs
- Subluxation that doesn't reduce with abduction
- Hinge abduction (late complication)

## Complications

| Complication | Cause | Management |
|--------------|-------|------------|
| **Coxa magna** | Enlarged, deformed head | May need reduction osteotomy |
| **Coxa plana** | Flattened head | Symptomatic treatment |
| **Hinge abduction** | Lateral head incongruency | Valgus osteotomy, shelf |
| **Leg length discrepancy** | Growth arrest | Equalization at maturity |
| **Early osteoarthritis** | Incongruent hip | THR in adulthood |

**Hinge Abduction:** During abduction, lateral extrusion causes the femoral head to "hinge" on acetabular rim instead of entering acetabulum. Paradoxically, abduction makes things worse. Treatment: Valgus osteotomy.

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Perthes = AVN of femoral head in children** (4-10 years)
2. **Male:Female = 4-5:1**
3. **Bilateral** in 10-15% (usually sequential, not simultaneous)
4. **Earliest clinical sign** = Decreased internal rotation and abduction
5. **Crescent sign** = Subchondral fracture (frog-leg lateral view)
6. **Herring lateral pillar classification** is most useful prognostically
7. **Age >8 + Herring C** = Poor prognosis without surgery
8. **"Head at risk" signs**: Gage sign, lateral calcification, subluxation, horizontal physis, metaphyseal cyst
9. **Containment** is the principle of treatment - keep head in acetabulum
10. **Younger age** (<6 years) = Better prognosis (more remodeling)
11. **Total disease course** = 2-4 years (self-limiting)
12. **Stulberg I-II** at maturity = Good long-term outcome

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Transient synovitis** | Acute onset, resolves in days, normal X-ray |
| **Septic arthritis** | Fever, elevated WBC/ESR, refuses to move |
| **JIA** | Other joints, morning stiffness, systemic |
| **SCFE** | Older child (10-14), obese, ice cream cone on frog-leg |
| **DDH** | Infant presentation, different X-ray findings |
| **Multiple epiphyseal dysplasia** | Bilateral symmetric, other epiphyses involved |

## Perthes vs Transient Synovitis

| Feature | Perthes | Transient Synovitis |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| Duration | Months | Days |
| Age | 4-10 years | 3-8 years |
| X-ray | Abnormal (changes over time) | Normal |
| Labs | Normal | May have mild elevation |
| Recurrence | Persistent | May recur |
| Treatment | Long-term | Rest, NSAIDs |
