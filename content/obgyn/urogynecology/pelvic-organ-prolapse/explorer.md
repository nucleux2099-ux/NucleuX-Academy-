# Pelvic Organ Prolapse

## Overview

Pelvic organ prolapse (POP) is the descent of pelvic organs (uterus, vaginal walls, bladder, rectum) from their normal position due to weakness of pelvic floor support. It is a common condition affecting up to 50% of parous women. Management ranges from conservative (pelvic floor exercises, pessaries) to surgical repair depending on severity and symptoms.

## Key Points

- **Vaginal delivery** is the most important risk factor
- Cardinal ligaments and uterosacral ligaments are the primary supports of the uterus
- POP-Q (Pelvic Organ Prolapse Quantification) is the standardized staging system
- **Cystocele** is the most common type of prolapse
- Pessary is the first-line non-surgical treatment
- Manchester repair preserves the uterus; vaginal hysterectomy with pelvic floor repair is definitive

## Definition

Pelvic organ prolapse is the herniation of pelvic organs to or beyond the vaginal walls. Classified by compartment:

| Compartment | Structure | Prolapse |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Anterior | Bladder | Cystocele |
| Anterior | Urethra | Urethrocele |
| Middle | Uterus/cervix | Uterine prolapse |
| Middle | Vault (post-hysterectomy) | Vault prolapse |
| Posterior | Rectum | Rectocele |
| Posterior | Pouch of Douglas | Enterocele |

## Etiology

### Pelvic Floor Support
- **Level I**: Cardinal and uterosacral ligaments (suspend uterus/upper vagina)
- **Level II**: Paravaginal attachments to arcus tendineus (mid-vagina)
- **Level III**: Perineal body, urogenital diaphragm (distal vagina)

### Risk Factors
- **Vaginal delivery** (most important) — especially prolonged labor, macrosomia, instrumental
- Multiparity
- Chronic straining (constipation, chronic cough, heavy lifting)
- Menopause/estrogen deficiency
- Obesity
- Connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos)
- Previous pelvic surgery
- Aging

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Sensation of "something coming down" — most common complaint
- Dragging sensation, heaviness in pelvis
- Low backache, worse by end of day, relieved by lying down
- Urinary symptoms: frequency, incomplete emptying, SUI
- Difficulty with defecation (need to digitally reduce rectocele)
- Dyspareunia
- Ulceration of exposed prolapse (decubitus ulcer)

### Signs by Degree (Shaw's Classification)
| Degree | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| First | Descent within vagina, not reaching introitus |
| Second | Descent to introitus |
| Third | Descent outside introitus (partial procidentia) |
| Fourth | Complete eversion (procidentia) |

### POP-Q Staging (ICS Standard)
- Uses 6 defined points (Aa, Ba, C, D, Ap, Bp) measured relative to hymen
- Stage 0: No prolapse
- Stage I: Most distal point >1 cm above hymen
- Stage II: Most distal point within ±1 cm of hymen
- Stage III: Most distal point >1 cm below hymen but <(TVL-2) cm
- Stage IV: Complete eversion

### Examination
- Examine standing and in left lateral (Sims) position
- Use Sims speculum to assess each compartment separately
- Ask patient to strain/cough
- Assess perineal body, cervical descent
- Bimanual examination for associated pathology

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical diagnosis** — history and examination
- POP-Q for standardized documentation
- Urodynamic studies if urinary symptoms present
- Pelvic ultrasound/MRI — if associated pathology suspected
- Renal function tests — if complete procidentia (possible ureteric kink)

## Management

### Conservative Management
- **Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT/Kegel exercises)** — first-line for mild prolapse
- **Pessary** — ring pessary (most common), shelf/Gellhorn pessary for severe prolapse
  - Ideal for: elderly/unfit for surgery, pregnancy, patient preference
  - Change every 3-6 months; watch for ulceration, discharge
- Lifestyle: Weight loss, treat chronic cough/constipation, avoid heavy lifting
- Vaginal estrogen cream — improves tissue quality

### Surgical Management

#### For Uterine Prolapse
- **Vaginal hysterectomy + pelvic floor repair** — gold standard/definitive
- **Manchester (Fothergill) repair** — amputation of elongated cervix + cardinal ligament shortening (preserves uterus)
- **Sacrohysteropexy** — uterus-preserving, mesh suspension to sacral promontory (abdominal/laparoscopic)
- **Sacrospinous fixation** — vaginal route uterine suspension
- **Le Fort's colpocleisis** — obliteration of vaginal canal; for elderly unfit patients who are not sexually active

#### For Cystocele
- **Anterior colporrhaphy (anterior repair)** — plication of pubocervical fascia

#### For Rectocele
- **Posterior colporrhaphy (posterior repair)** — plication of rectovaginal fascia

#### For Vault Prolapse
- **Sacrocolpopexy** — gold standard for vault prolapse (mesh to sacral promontory)
- **Sacrospinous fixation** — vaginal route

#### For Enterocele
- **McCall culdoplasty** — obliteration of cul-de-sac
- **Moschcowitz procedure** — abdominal approach

### Special Situations
- **Procidentia with decubitus ulcer**: Treat ulcer first (local estrogen, antiseptic dressing, pessary reduction), then surgery
- **Concomitant SUI**: Address at same surgery (mid-urethral sling)
- **Young/nulliparous**: Uterus-preserving surgery preferred

## Complications

- Decubitus ulceration (chronic exposed prolapse)
- Urinary retention, UTI, hydronephrosis (in severe prolapse)
- Incarceration (rarely strangulation)
- Recurrence after surgery (10-30%)
- Mesh complications (erosion, pain, infection) — if mesh used
- Sexual dysfunction
