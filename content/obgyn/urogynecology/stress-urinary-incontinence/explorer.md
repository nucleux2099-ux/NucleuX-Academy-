# Stress Urinary Incontinence

## Overview

Stress urinary incontinence (SUI) is the involuntary leakage of urine on effort, exertion, sneezing, or coughing. It is the most common type of urinary incontinence in women. SUI results from urethral hypermobility and/or intrinsic sphincter deficiency. Pelvic floor exercises are first-line; mid-urethral sling (TVT/TOT) is the gold standard surgical treatment.

## Key Points

- **SUI** is the most common type of urinary incontinence in younger women
- **Urge incontinence** is more common in elderly
- **Mixed incontinence** (SUI + urge) is the most common overall presentation
- Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) is **first-line** treatment for all SUI
- **Mid-urethral sling (TVT/TOT)** is the gold standard surgery
- Urodynamic studies confirm diagnosis and differentiate types

## Definition

SUI is the complaint of involuntary loss of urine on physical effort, exertion, coughing, or sneezing (ICS definition). It is a symptom, a sign (demonstrable on examination), and a urodynamic diagnosis.

### Types of Urinary Incontinence
| Type | Mechanism | Feature |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Stress | Urethral hypermobility/ISD | Leak with effort |
| Urge | Detrusor overactivity | Leak with urgency |
| Mixed | Both mechanisms | Both symptoms |
| Overflow | Underactive detrusor/obstruction | Constant dribbling |
| Fistula | Abnormal communication | Continuous leak |

## Etiology

### Pathophysiology
Two mechanisms:
1. **Urethral hypermobility** — loss of support to urethra and bladder neck; rotational descent during straining
2. **Intrinsic sphincter deficiency (ISD)** — weak urethral sphincter itself; more severe, poor coaptation

### Risk Factors
- Vaginal delivery (pudendal nerve damage, fascial tears)
- Multiparity
- Menopause (estrogen deficiency → urethral mucosal atrophy)
- Obesity
- Chronic cough, constipation
- Prior pelvic surgery (hysterectomy)
- Aging
- Smoking
- High-impact exercise

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Urine leakage with coughing, sneezing, laughing, lifting, exercise
- No urgency or urge component (in pure SUI)
- Predictable — related to physical activity
- Severity varies from drops to complete emptying

### Examination
- **Cough stress test** — ask patient to cough with full bladder; observe leakage
- **Bonney's test** (Marshall test) — elevate bladder neck with fingers; leak stops (tests hypermobility)
- **Q-tip test** — cotton swab in urethra; straining angle >30° = urethral hypermobility
- Assess for prolapse (commonly associated)
- Vaginal examination for pelvic floor strength (Oxford grading)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment
- **Bladder diary** (3-day) — frequency, volume, leak episodes, fluid intake
- **Pad test** (1-hour or 24-hour) — quantifies urine loss
- **Cough stress test** — clinical demonstration
- **Quality of life questionnaires** (ICIQ-SF, UDI-6)

### Investigations
- **Urinalysis/culture** — rule out UTI
- **Post-void residual** (PVR) — rule out overflow
- **Urodynamic studies** — gold standard for diagnosis
  - Filling cystometry: Demonstrates SUI (leak with Valsalva without detrusor contraction)
  - Abdominal Leak Point Pressure (ALPP): <60 cmH₂O = ISD; >90 = hypermobility
  - Valsalva Leak Point Pressure (VLPP)
- **Pelvic ultrasound** — bladder neck mobility, PVR
- **Cystoscopy** — if hematuria or suspected fistula

## Management

### Conservative (First-Line)
- **Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT/Kegels)** — supervised, 3 months minimum
  - Contract pelvic floor for 8-10 seconds, relax, repeat 8-12 times, 3x/day
  - Cure/improvement in 50-70% of mild-moderate SUI
- **Biofeedback** — augments PFMT with visual/auditory feedback
- **Electrical stimulation** — for patients unable to contract pelvic floor
- **Vaginal cones** — weighted cones held in vagina
- **Lifestyle modifications**: Weight loss (5-10% reduces episodes by 50%), fluid management, smoking cessation
- **Vaginal estrogen** — improves urethral mucosal coaptation in postmenopausal women
- **Continence pessary** — support bladder neck

### Pharmacological
- **Duloxetine** (SNRI) — only drug with evidence for SUI; increases urethral sphincter tone
- No first-line pharmacological agent; drugs are adjunct

### Surgical Management

#### Mid-Urethral Sling (Gold Standard)
- **TVT (Tension-free Vaginal Tape)** — retropubic route
- **TOT (Trans-Obturator Tape)** — obturator foramen route
- Mechanism: Provides suburethral support; reinforces hammock effect
- Success rate: 80-90%
- **TVT**: Higher risk of bladder perforation; better for ISD
- **TOT**: Lower risk of bladder injury; higher groin pain; better for hypermobility

#### Other Surgical Options
- **Burch colposuspension** — retropubic, sutures paravaginal tissue to Cooper's ligament; historical gold standard
- **Autologous fascial sling** — for recurrent SUI or ISD
- **Periurethral bulking agents** (Macroplastique, Bulkamid) — injection around urethra; for ISD, poor surgical candidates
- **Artificial urinary sphincter** — severe ISD, refractory cases

### For Intrinsic Sphincter Deficiency
- Bulking agents, autologous sling, or artificial sphincter preferred over standard mid-urethral sling

## Complications

- Recurrence (10-20% after surgery)
- Mesh erosion/exposure (sling procedures)
- Bladder perforation (TVT)
- Voiding difficulty post-surgery
- De novo urgency/urge incontinence after SUI surgery
- Groin pain (TOT)
- UTI

## Key Differentials

| Feature | Stress | Urge | Overflow |
|---------|--------|------|----------|
| Trigger | Effort/cough | Urgency | None (constant) |
| Volume | Small | Large | Dribbling |
| Frequency | With activity | Day and night | Constant |
| PVR | Normal | Normal | High |
| Urodynamics | No detrusor contraction | Detrusor overactivity | High PVR, low flow |
