# Emergency Contraception

## Overview

Emergency contraception (EC) refers to methods used to prevent pregnancy after unprotected sexual intercourse or contraceptive failure. It is not intended as a regular method of contraception but serves as a backup option. EC works primarily by delaying or inhibiting ovulation and does not disrupt an established pregnancy.

The most commonly used methods include hormonal pills (levonorgestrel and ulipristal acetate) and the copper intrauterine device (Cu-IUD). The Cu-IUD is the most effective form of emergency contraception and can also serve as ongoing contraception. Timely administration is critical — efficacy decreases with delay.

Emergency contraception is an important public health tool in reducing unintended pregnancies. It is available over-the-counter in many countries. Understanding the mechanisms, timing, and efficacy of different methods is essential for clinical practice and examinations.

## Key Points

- Cu-IUD is the most effective method of emergency contraception
- Levonorgestrel (LNG) is most effective within 72 hours of unprotected intercourse
- Ulipristal acetate (UPA) is effective up to 120 hours (5 days)
- Cu-IUD can be inserted up to 5 days after unprotected intercourse
- EC works primarily by delaying/inhibiting ovulation — NOT abortifacient
- Mifepristone in low doses can also be used as EC

## Definition & Classification

Emergency contraception is defined as any method used after unprotected intercourse to prevent pregnancy.

**Types:**
1. **Hormonal methods:**
   - Levonorgestrel (LNG) 1.5 mg single dose — Plan B
   - Ulipristal acetate (UPA) 30 mg single dose — EllaOne
   - Yuzpe regimen (historical): Combined ethinyl estradiol + levonorgestrel
   - Mifepristone (low dose, 25-50 mg)

2. **Non-hormonal method:**
   - Copper IUD (Cu-T 380A) — inserted within 5 days

**Yuzpe regimen (largely replaced):**
- Ethinyl estradiol 100 μg + LNG 0.5 mg, repeated after 12 hours
- Higher side effects (nausea, vomiting) than LNG alone

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Indications for EC:**
- Unprotected intercourse
- Condom rupture or slippage
- Missed OCPs (≥2 pills)
- Failed withdrawal method
- Sexual assault

**Mechanisms of action:**
- **LNG:** Delays or inhibits ovulation by suppressing LH surge; thickens cervical mucus; NO effect after ovulation has occurred
- **UPA:** Selective progesterone receptor modulator; delays ovulation even after LH surge has begun (more effective than LNG in late follicular phase)
- **Cu-IUD:** Toxic to sperm and ovum; prevents fertilization; may also prevent implantation; creates sterile inflammatory reaction in endometrium
- **Mifepristone:** Delays ovulation; alters endometrial receptivity

## Clinical Features

Not applicable as this is a preventive intervention. However, side effects include:

**LNG side effects:**
- Nausea (less than Yuzpe)
- Irregular bleeding
- Headache
- Breast tenderness
- Next period may be early or delayed

**UPA side effects:**
- Similar to LNG
- Headache most common
- Should not be used with hormonal contraception for 5 days after

**Cu-IUD side effects:**
- Cramping during insertion
- Heavier periods
- Risk of infection, perforation, expulsion

## Diagnosis

No diagnostic workup needed for EC administration. However:
- Exclude existing pregnancy before Cu-IUD insertion
- Pregnancy test if period is delayed by >1 week after EC use
- No routine investigations required for oral EC

## Management

**Levonorgestrel 1.5 mg:**
- Single oral dose as soon as possible
- Most effective within 72 hours; some efficacy up to 96 hours
- Available OTC
- Can be used more than once in a cycle if needed
- Efficacy: 85% if taken within 72 hours

**Ulipristal acetate 30 mg:**
- Single oral dose within 120 hours (5 days)
- More effective than LNG especially on days 4-5
- Prescription required in most countries
- Avoid hormonal contraception for 5 days after UPA
- Efficacy: maintains effectiveness throughout 120-hour window

**Copper IUD:**
- Most effective EC method (failure rate <0.1%)
- Can be inserted up to 5 days (120 hours) after unprotected intercourse
- Can be left in place for ongoing contraception (up to 10 years)
- Requires trained provider for insertion

**After EC:**
- Advise reliable contraception going forward
- Follow-up pregnancy test if no period within 3 weeks
- LNG-IUS (Mirena) is NOT effective as emergency contraception

## Complications & Prognosis

- EC does not cause abortion — does not affect established pregnancy
- No teratogenic effects if pregnancy occurs despite EC
- Ectopic pregnancy should be excluded if pregnancy occurs after EC
- No long-term effects on fertility
- Repeated use of hormonal EC is safe but not recommended as regular method
- Cu-IUD: small risk of PID, perforation, expulsion
