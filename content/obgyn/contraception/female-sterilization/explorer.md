# Female Sterilization

## Overview

Female sterilization is a permanent method of contraception that involves occlusion or removal of the fallopian tubes to prevent the ovum from being fertilized by sperm. It is one of the most widely used contraceptive methods globally, especially in India where tubectomy is the most popular family planning method.

The procedure can be performed postpartum, post-abortal, or as an interval procedure. Various techniques include laparoscopic sterilization, minilaparotomy, and hysteroscopic sterilization. The modified Pomeroy technique is the most commonly used method for puerperal sterilization.

While considered permanent, reversal (tubal reanastomosis) is possible but success rates vary. Female sterilization has a Pearl index of 0.5 (failure rate of about 1 in 200 per year), making it highly effective.

## Key Points

- Most popular family planning method in India
- Modified Pomeroy technique — most common method for puerperal sterilization
- Laparoscopic sterilization — most common method for interval sterilization
- Failure rate: 0.5% (Pearl index 0.5)
- If failure occurs, high risk of ectopic pregnancy
- No effect on hormonal function or menstruation

## Definition & Classification

Female sterilization is the permanent surgical interruption of the fallopian tubes.

**By timing:**
- **Puerperal (postpartum):** Within 7 days of delivery (ideally within 48 hours)
- **Post-abortal:** Concurrent with MTP
- **Interval:** Unrelated to pregnancy (in proliferative phase of menstrual cycle)

**By approach:**
- Minilaparotomy (minilap) — most common in India for puerperal sterilization
- Laparoscopy — preferred for interval sterilization
- Hysteroscopy (Essure — now discontinued in many countries)

**By technique:**
- **Pomeroy technique:** Loop of tube ligated and excised
- **Modified Pomeroy:** Most commonly used; plain catgut suture used (absorbs, allowing ends to separate)
- **Parkland method:** Two separate ligatures with excision of intervening segment
- **Irving method:** Proximal stump buried in myometrium (lowest failure rate)
- **Uchida method:** Proximal stump buried in mesosalpinx
- **Fimbriectomy (Kroener):** Removal of fimbrial end
- **Falope ring (Yoon ring):** Applied via laparoscopy
- **Filshie clip:** Most commonly used clip worldwide
- **Hulka-Clemens clip:** Spring-loaded clip
- **Unipolar/Bipolar cautery:** Electrosurgical methods via laparoscopy

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Mechanism:** Physical interruption of the fallopian tube prevents sperm-ovum transport and fertilization.

**Indications:**
- Completed family (woman must be ≥22 years as per Indian law)
- Medical conditions where pregnancy is contraindicated
- Voluntary request with informed consent

**Contraindications:**
- Young age/low parity (relative)
- Unstable medical condition
- Active pelvic infection
- Uncertainty about desire for permanent sterilization

## Clinical Features

Not applicable — this is a surgical procedure. Counseling points include:

- Permanent and generally irreversible
- No effect on hormones, libido, or menstruation
- Does not protect against STIs
- Small failure rate exists
- If failure occurs → high risk of ectopic pregnancy
- Rare post-tubal ligation syndrome (controversial): menstrual irregularity, pelvic pain

## Diagnosis

Pre-operative assessment:
- Confirm completed family and informed consent
- Exclude pregnancy (urine pregnancy test)
- Basic blood investigations (Hb, blood group)
- Consent form signed (mandatory cooling-off period in India)

## Management

**Puerperal sterilization:**
- Modified Pomeroy via minilaparotomy (infraumbilical incision)
- Within 48 hours of delivery preferred
- Uterus is still enlarged, tubes easily accessible
- Under local or spinal anesthesia

**Interval sterilization:**
- Laparoscopic approach preferred
- Falope ring or Filshie clip application
- Done in proliferative phase of cycle
- Under general anesthesia

**Laparoscopic techniques:**
- Mechanical: Falope ring, Filshie clip, Hulka clip
- Electrical: Bipolar cautery (preferred over unipolar — safer)

**Post-operative care:**
- Day care procedure usually
- Resume normal activities in 1-2 days (laparoscopic)
- Follow-up for wound check

**Reversal (tubal reanastomosis):**
- Microsurgical technique
- Success depends on: method used, length of remaining tube, age
- Best results after clip sterilization (least tissue destruction)
- Isthmo-isthmic anastomosis has best results
- Overall pregnancy rate after reversal: 50-80%
- Risk of ectopic pregnancy after reversal: 5-10%

## Complications & Prognosis

**Intraoperative:**
- Hemorrhage
- Bowel/bladder injury (laparoscopy)
- Anesthesia complications
- Failed procedure

**Postoperative:**
- Wound infection
- Failure and pregnancy (0.5%)
- Ectopic pregnancy (if failure occurs — up to 30% of post-sterilization pregnancies)
- Regret (especially if done at young age)

**Long-term:**
- Post-tubal ligation syndrome (controversial)
- May have protective effect against ovarian cancer (reduced risk by 20-30%)

**Mortality:** 1-2 per 100,000 procedures (mostly anesthesia-related)
