# Male Sterilization (Vasectomy)

## Overview

Vasectomy is a permanent method of male contraception involving interruption of the vas deferens to prevent sperm from reaching the ejaculate. It is simpler, safer, and more cost-effective than female sterilization, yet remains underutilized globally, particularly in India where tubectomy predominates.

The procedure can be performed under local anesthesia as an outpatient procedure. The no-scalpel vasectomy (NSV) technique, developed in China, has significantly reduced complications and is now the preferred approach worldwide. Vasectomy is not immediately effective — azoospermia must be confirmed at 3 months or after 20 ejaculations.

Vasectomy has a very low failure rate (Pearl index 0.1) and is the most effective permanent contraceptive method. Despite its safety and efficacy, social and cultural barriers limit its acceptance in many countries.

## Key Points

- Simplest, safest, and most effective permanent contraceptive method
- No-scalpel vasectomy (NSV) — preferred technique
- NOT immediately effective — takes 3 months/20 ejaculations for azoospermia
- Pearl index: 0.1 (lower than female sterilization)
- Minimum age in India: 25 years for vasectomy
- Does not affect testosterone levels, libido, or erections

## Definition & Classification

Vasectomy is the surgical division or occlusion of the vas deferens bilaterally.

**Techniques:**
- **Conventional vasectomy:** Scrotal skin incision, vas isolated, segment excised, ends ligated/cauterized
- **No-scalpel vasectomy (NSV):** Developed by Dr. Li Shunqiang (China, 1974); uses sharp-pointed forceps to puncture scrotal skin; less bleeding, faster recovery
- **Vas occlusion methods:**
  - Ligation and excision
  - Cautery (thermal or electrocautery)
  - Fascial interposition (folding fascia over cut end)
  - Mucosal fulguration
  - Clips (tantalum clips)

**Experimental/reversible:**
- Intra-vas devices
- RISUG (Reversible Inhibition of Sperm Under Guidance) — styrene maleic anhydride injected into vas; developed in India by Prof. S.K. Guha

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Mechanism:** Interruption of vas deferens prevents sperm from entering the ejaculate. Sperm production continues but sperm are reabsorbed.

**Indications:**
- Completed family
- Male age ≥25 years (Indian law)
- Voluntary informed consent

**Contraindications:**
- Active scrotal/inguinal infection
- Large hydrocele or varicocele
- Inguinal hernia
- Bleeding disorders
- Uncertainty about permanent sterilization

## Clinical Features

Pre-procedure counseling:
- Permanent (reversal possible but not guaranteed)
- Not immediately effective — barrier method needed for 3 months
- No effect on erections, ejaculation volume (95% is seminal vesicle/prostate secretion), or testosterone
- Does not protect against STIs
- Semen analysis needed at 3 months to confirm azoospermia

## Diagnosis

**Pre-operative:**
- Clinical examination of scrotum
- Palpation of vas deferens bilaterally
- Exclude local pathology

**Post-operative confirmation:**
- Semen analysis at 3 months (or after 20 ejaculations)
- Must show azoospermia before discontinuing other contraception
- If motile sperm persist → repeat analysis or consider re-operation

## Management

**No-scalpel vasectomy (preferred technique):**
1. Local anesthesia (lidocaine without adrenaline around vas)
2. Vas fixed with ring clamp through scrotal skin
3. Puncture with sharp dissecting forceps (no incision)
4. Vas delivered, segment excised (1-2 cm)
5. Ends cauterized/ligated ± fascial interposition
6. Repeat on other side
7. No sutures needed for skin puncture

**Post-operative care:**
- Scrotal support for 48 hours
- Avoid heavy lifting for 1 week
- Analgesics as needed
- Resume intercourse after 1 week with barrier method
- Semen analysis at 3 months

**Vasectomy reversal (vasovasostomy):**
- Microsurgical reanastomosis
- Success rates: 80-90% patency; 40-70% pregnancy
- Better outcomes if reversal within 10 years
- If epididymal obstruction → vasoepididymostomy needed

## Complications & Prognosis

**Early:**
- Hematoma (most common complication)
- Wound infection
- Scrotal swelling
- Sperm granuloma

**Late:**
- Failure and recanalization (0.1%)
- Chronic scrotal pain (post-vasectomy pain syndrome) — 1-2%
- Sperm granuloma
- Epididymal congestion

**NOT associated with:**
- Prostate cancer (no proven association)
- Cardiovascular disease
- Testicular cancer
- Impotence or decreased libido

**Prognosis:**
- Most effective permanent contraception (Pearl index 0.1)
- Safe with very low mortality
- NSV: faster recovery, fewer complications than conventional
