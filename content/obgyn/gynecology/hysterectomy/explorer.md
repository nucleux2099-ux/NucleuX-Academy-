# Hysterectomy

## Overview

Hysterectomy is the surgical removal of the uterus and is the most common major gynecological surgery worldwide. It can be performed via abdominal, vaginal, laparoscopic, or robotic approach. The choice depends on indication, uterine size, associated pathology, and surgical expertise. It is a definitive treatment for many benign and malignant conditions.

## Key Points

- Most common major gynecological surgery
- Vaginal hysterectomy is the preferred route for benign disease (ACOG)
- Total = uterus + cervix; Subtotal/supracervical = uterus without cervix
- Most common indication: abnormal uterine bleeding / fibroids
- Ureteric injury is the most important surgical complication
- Vault prolapse is a long-term complication

## Definition & Classification

### Types by Extent
| Type | What is Removed |
|------|----------------|
| Total hysterectomy (TAH) | Uterus + cervix |
| Subtotal/supracervical | Uterine body only (cervix preserved) |
| Total + BSO | Uterus + cervix + both tubes and ovaries |
| Radical hysterectomy (Wertheim's) | Uterus + cervix + parametrium + upper vagina + pelvic lymph nodes |
| Extended radical | Above + more extensive parametrial/paravaginal tissue |

### Piver-Rutledge Classification (Radical Hysterectomy)
| Class | Extent |
|-------|--------|
| I | Extrafascial (simple TAH) |
| II | Modified radical — ureter unroofed through tunnel; medial half of cardinal/uterosacral ligaments |
| III | Classical radical (Wertheim's) — complete cardinal/uterosacral ligaments, upper third vagina |
| IV | Extended — entire periureteral tissue, superior vesical artery |
| V | Partial pelvic exenteration |

### Routes
| Route | Advantages | Limitations |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Vaginal | Least invasive, fastest recovery, lowest complication rate | Limited access, no adnexal assessment |
| Abdominal | Best visualization, any size uterus, malignancy staging | Longest recovery, most pain, highest complication rate |
| Laparoscopic (TLH/LAVH) | Minimally invasive, shorter hospital stay | Longer operative time, equipment cost |
| Robotic | Enhanced visualization, ergonomic | Very high cost, long setup |

- **LAVH:** Laparoscopic Assisted Vaginal Hysterectomy — upper pedicles laparoscopic, completed vaginally
- **TLH:** Total Laparoscopic Hysterectomy — entire procedure laparoscopic

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Indications

#### Benign (90% of hysterectomies)
- Leiomyoma (most common overall indication)
- Abnormal uterine bleeding unresponsive to treatment
- Adenomyosis
- Endometriosis
- Pelvic organ prolapse (vaginal hysterectomy + repair)
- Chronic pelvic pain
- Obstetric hemorrhage (peripartum hysterectomy — last resort)

#### Malignant
- Endometrial cancer (TAH + BSO + staging)
- Cervical cancer (radical hysterectomy for Stage IA2-IIA)
- Ovarian cancer (as part of debulking)
- Uterine sarcoma

## Clinical Features

### Pre-Operative Assessment
- Indication confirmed; alternatives exhausted
- Pap smear, endometrial biopsy (as indicated)
- Imaging: USG, MRI for fibroid mapping
- CBC, crossmatch, coagulation
- Informed consent: risks, route selection, ovarian conservation discussion

### Ovarian Conservation
- **Premenopausal women <65 years with benign disease:** Conserve ovaries (reduces cardiovascular, osteoporosis, and cognitive risks)
- **Remove if:** BRCA carrier, family history of ovarian cancer, suspicious appearance

## Diagnosis

Not applicable — surgical procedure.

## Management

### Surgical Steps (Abdominal Total Hysterectomy)
1. Midline or Pfannenstiel incision
2. Exploration and packing of bowels
3. Clamp, cut, and ligate:
   - Round ligament
   - Infundibulopelvic ligament (if BSO) or utero-ovarian ligament (if conserving)
   - Broad ligament
   - Uterine artery at level of internal os
   - Cardinal ligaments
   - Uterosacral ligaments
4. Circumferential vaginal incision below cervix
5. Removal of specimen
6. Vaginal vault closure (continuous or interrupted)
7. Hemostasis, peritoneal check, closure

### Key Surgical Relations
- **Ureter:** Crosses under uterine artery ("water under the bridge") — at risk during uterine artery ligation and cardinal ligament division
- **Bladder:** Anterior to cervix — must be reflected down
- **Rectum:** Posterior — pouch of Douglas must be carefully dissected

### Post-Operative Care
- Early mobilization, DVT prophylaxis
- Catheter for 24 hours (longer after radical)
- Diet advancement
- Vault healing: 6-8 weeks; avoid intercourse and heavy lifting
- Follow-up at 6 weeks

## Complications

### Intraoperative
| Complication | Incidence |
|-------------|-----------|
| Hemorrhage | 1-2% |
| Ureteric injury | 0.5-1% (highest in radical) |
| Bladder injury | 1-2% |
| Bowel injury | 0.1-0.5% |

### Early Postoperative
- Infection (vault cellulitis, UTI, wound infection)
- DVT/PE
- Secondary hemorrhage
- Vault hematoma
- Ileus

### Late
- **Vault prolapse** (especially after TAH without colpopexy)
- Urinary incontinence or retention
- Vaginal shortening / dyspareunia
- Premature ovarian failure (even with conservation — reduced blood supply)
- Psychological / sexual impact
- Fistulae (vesicovaginal, ureterovaginal — rare)

### Ureteric Injury
- Most feared complication
- Sites of risk: infundibulopelvic ligament, uterine artery crossing, cardinal ligament, vaginal angle
- "Water under the bridge" — ureter passes under uterine artery
- Prevention: identify ureter, stay close to uterus, careful dissection
