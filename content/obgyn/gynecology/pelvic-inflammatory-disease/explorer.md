# Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

## Overview

Pelvic inflammatory disease is an ascending infection of the upper female genital tract involving the uterus (endometritis), fallopian tubes (salpingitis), ovaries (oophoritis), and/or pelvic peritoneum. It is most commonly caused by sexually transmitted organisms, particularly Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae. PID is a major cause of infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and chronic pelvic pain.

## Key Points

- Ascending infection from lower to upper genital tract
- Most common cause: Chlamydia trachomatis (often subclinical)
- Polymicrobial in majority of cases
- Most common during/just after menstruation
- Tubal factor infertility is the most important long-term sequela
- Low threshold for empirical treatment (consequences of untreated PID are severe)
- Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome: perihepatitis associated with PID

## Definition & Classification

### Spectrum of Disease
| Condition | Structure Involved |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Endometritis | Uterine endometrium |
| Salpingitis | Fallopian tubes (most common component) |
| Oophoritis | Ovaries |
| Tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA) | Complex mass involving tube and ovary |
| Pelvic peritonitis | Pelvic peritoneum |
| Perihepatitis (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis) | Liver capsule (perihepatic adhesions — "violin string") |

### Classification by Severity
- **Mild:** Ambulatory management; oral antibiotics
- **Moderate:** Hospitalization considered
- **Severe:** TOA, peritonitis, systemic sepsis; hospitalization mandatory

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Causative Organisms
| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Chlamydia trachomatis | Most common STI cause; often silent/subclinical |
| Neisseria gonorrhoeae | More acute presentation |
| Mycoplasma genitalium | Increasingly recognized |
| Anaerobes (Bacteroides, Peptostreptococcus) | Polymicrobial component |
| Facultative aerobes (E. coli, Streptococcus) | Secondary infection |
| Actinomyces israelii | Associated with IUD use |

### Pathogenesis
1. Organisms ascend from vagina/cervix → endometrium → tubes → peritoneum
2. Factors facilitating ascent:
   - Menstruation (cervical mucus disruption, retrograde flow)
   - Instrumentation (D&C, IUD insertion, HSG)
   - Bacterial vaginosis (altered vaginal flora)
3. Tubal damage: mucosal edema → adhesions → tubal occlusion → infertility
4. Fitz-Hugh-Curtis: organisms reach liver capsule → perihepatic inflammation + adhesions

### Risk Factors
- Multiple sexual partners (most important)
- Young age (15-25 years)
- No barrier contraception
- Previous PID
- Recent instrumentation
- IUD insertion (first 20 days only)
- Bacterial vaginosis

### Protective Factors
- Barrier contraception (condoms)
- OCP use (thickens cervical mucus)
- Tubal ligation

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Lower abdominal/pelvic pain (most common — bilateral)
- Abnormal vaginal discharge (purulent)
- Abnormal bleeding (intermenstrual, postcoital)
- Dyspareunia (deep)
- Dysuria
- Fever
- Nausea, vomiting (severe cases)

### Signs
- Lower abdominal tenderness with guarding
- **Cervical motion tenderness** (chandelier sign — most characteristic)
- Adnexal tenderness (bilateral)
- Adnexal mass (TOA)
- Mucopurulent cervical discharge
- Fever >38°C (in severe cases — absent in many)

### Fitz-Hugh-Curtis Syndrome
- Right upper quadrant pain mimicking cholecystitis or pleurisy
- "Violin string" adhesions between liver capsule and anterior abdominal wall
- Associated with both chlamydial and gonococcal PID

## Diagnosis

### CDC Minimum Criteria (Low Threshold for Diagnosis)
At least ONE of:
- Cervical motion tenderness
- Uterine tenderness
- Adnexal tenderness

### Additional Criteria (Support Diagnosis)
- Temperature >38.3°C
- Mucopurulent cervical discharge
- Abundant WBCs on wet mount of vaginal discharge
- Elevated ESR, CRP
- Positive Chlamydia or Gonorrhea test

### Definitive Criteria
- Endometrial biopsy showing endometritis
- Laparoscopy showing inflamed, erythematous tubes with purulent exudate
- Imaging (ultrasound/MRI) showing TOA or thickened fluid-filled tubes

### Investigations
- Endocervical swab: NAAT for Chlamydia and Gonorrhea
- Vaginal swab: wet mount, culture
- Pregnancy test (exclude ectopic — crucial differential)
- CBC, ESR, CRP
- Ultrasound: TOA, pyosalpinx
- Urinalysis (exclude UTI)

### Differential Diagnosis
- Ectopic pregnancy (MUST exclude)
- Appendicitis
- Ovarian cyst torsion/rupture
- Endometriosis
- UTI

## Management

### Outpatient (Mild-Moderate)
CDC recommended regimens:
- **Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose** + **Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days** ± **Metronidazole 500 mg BD × 14 days**
- Alternative: Ofloxacin + metronidazole

### Inpatient (Severe) — Criteria for Hospitalization
- TOA
- Pregnancy
- Failure of oral therapy (48-72 hours)
- Unable to tolerate oral medications
- Surgical emergency not excluded
- Severe illness (high fever, peritonitis)

### Inpatient Regimen
- **Cefoxitin 2g IV q6h** + **Doxycycline 100 mg BD** (IV or oral)
- OR **Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h** + **Gentamicin** loading + maintenance
- Switch to oral after 24-48 hours of clinical improvement
- Complete 14 days total

### TOA Management
- IV antibiotics (as above)
- If no response in 48-72 hours: drainage (USG/CT-guided percutaneous or laparoscopic)
- Rupture of TOA: surgical emergency → laparotomy

### Partner Treatment
- Treat all sexual partners from past 60 days
- Screen and treat for Chlamydia and Gonorrhea
- Abstinence until both partners treated

## Complications

### Acute
- Tubo-ovarian abscess
- Pelvic peritonitis
- Sepsis
- Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome

### Chronic/Long-Term
| Complication | Risk After PID |
|-------------|---------------|
| Tubal factor infertility | 10-15% after 1 episode; 50-75% after 3+ |
| Ectopic pregnancy | 6-10 fold increase |
| Chronic pelvic pain | 20-30% |
| Recurrent PID | 25% |
