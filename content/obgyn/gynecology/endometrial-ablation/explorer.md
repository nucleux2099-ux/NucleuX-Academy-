# Endometrial Ablation

## Overview

Endometrial ablation is a minimally invasive procedure that destroys the endometrial lining to reduce or stop menstrual bleeding. It is an alternative to hysterectomy for women with heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) who have completed childbearing and in whom medical therapy has failed. Both first-generation (hysteroscopic) and second-generation (non-hysteroscopic/global) techniques are available.

## Key Points

- Destroys endometrium to treat heavy menstrual bleeding
- Alternative to hysterectomy — preserves uterus
- **Absolute prerequisite:** Family must be complete (NOT a contraceptive method)
- Concurrent reliable contraception required post-procedure
- Second-generation (global) techniques are simpler and equally effective
- 70-80% satisfaction rate; 20-30% amenorrhea rate
- Endometrial pretreatment thins the endometrium for better results

## Definition & Classification

### First-Generation (Hysteroscopic)
Require direct visualization; operator-dependent; longer learning curve.

| Technique | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| Transcervical resection of endometrium (TCRE) | Resectoscope with loop electrode — gold standard first-gen |
| Rollerball ablation | Electrosurgical rollerball coagulates endometrium |
| Laser ablation | Nd:YAG laser (largely obsolete) |

### Second-Generation (Global/Non-Hysteroscopic)
Do not require hysteroscopic skill; automated; shorter procedure time.

| Technique | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| Thermal balloon (ThermaChoice) | Heated fluid-filled balloon (87°C for 8 min) |
| NovaSure | Bipolar radiofrequency mesh; 90 seconds |
| Microwave (MEA) | Microwave energy via probe |
| HydroThermAblator (HTA) | Free-flowing hot saline (90°C) |
| Cryoablation (Her Option) | Freezing probes |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
- Thermal, electrical, or mechanical energy destroys the endometrium including the basal layer
- Prevents regeneration → reduced or absent menstrual bleeding
- Depth of destruction: 4-6 mm to destroy basalis layer
- Resulting fibrosis and scarring of uterine cavity

### Endometrial Pretreatment (Optional/Recommended)
- Thins endometrium for better ablation results
- Options:
  - GnRH agonist (4-6 weeks prior)
  - Danazol (400-800 mg/day for 4-6 weeks)
  - Progestogens
  - Suction curettage immediately before
  - Timing in early proliferative phase (Day 5-7)

## Clinical Features

### Indications
- Heavy menstrual bleeding unresponsive to medical therapy
- Completed family (no future pregnancy desire)
- Uterine cavity length ≤12 cm (for most devices)
- Normal or near-normal uterine cavity (no significant distortion)
- Benign endometrial histology confirmed

### Contraindications
| Absolute | Relative |
|----------|----------|
| Desire for future pregnancy | Large submucosal fibroids |
| Endometrial malignancy or hyperplasia | Uterine anomalies |
| Active pelvic infection | Previous classical cesarean section |
| Pregnancy | Myometrial thinning |
| Postmenopausal bleeding (until cancer excluded) | IUD in situ |

### Pre-Procedure Requirements
1. Endometrial biopsy to exclude malignancy/hyperplasia
2. Pregnancy test (negative)
3. Reliable contraception plan
4. Counseling: not a contraceptive, pregnancy dangerous post-ablation

## Diagnosis

Not applicable — this is a therapeutic procedure.

### Pre-Procedure Workup
- Transvaginal ultrasound (cavity assessment, fibroid mapping)
- Endometrial biopsy (mandatory)
- Hysteroscopy (first-gen techniques; optional for second-gen)
- CBC, coagulation screen
- Cervical cytology

## Management

### Procedure (Second-Generation — NovaSure Example)
1. Cervical dilation to 6-8 mm
2. Cavity integrity test (CO2 at 50 mmHg — must maintain pressure)
3. Bipolar mesh array inserted and expanded
4. RF energy applied for ~90 seconds
5. Device removed; procedure complete
6. Total procedure time: 5-10 minutes

### Procedure (First-Generation — TCRE)
1. Distension medium: glycine 1.5% or normal saline (bipolar)
2. Resectoscope inserted after dilation
3. Systematic resection of endometrium (fundus → lower segment)
4. Chip collection and histological examination
5. Fluid deficit monitoring (glycine toxicity if >1000-1500 mL absorbed)

### Post-Procedure
- Day-case procedure; discharge same day
- Watery/bloody discharge for 2-4 weeks
- NSAIDs for cramping
- Reliable contraception (tubal ligation or partner vasectomy ideal)
- Follow-up at 6-8 weeks

### Outcomes
| Outcome | Rate |
|---------|------|
| Amenorrhea | 20-40% |
| Significant reduction in bleeding | 70-80% |
| Patient satisfaction | 80-90% |
| Subsequent hysterectomy (within 5 years) | 15-25% |

## Complications

### Immediate
- Uterine perforation (1-2%; higher with first-gen)
- Hemorrhage
- Fluid overload / glycine toxicity (TCRE — hyponatremia, cerebral edema)
- Cervical laceration
- Burns to adjacent organs

### Delayed
- Post-ablation tubal sterilization syndrome (PATSS): cornual hematometra
- Endometritis
- Hematometra
- Failure requiring hysterectomy
- **Pregnancy post-ablation:** High risk — ectopic, placenta accreta, IUGR, preterm

### Fluid Overload (TCRE-specific)
- Glycine absorption → dilutional hyponatremia → cerebral edema
- Monitor fluid deficit: stop if >1000 mL (glycine) or >2500 mL (saline)
- Treatment: IV hypertonic saline, furosemide
