# Menopause

## Overview

Menopause is the permanent cessation of menstruation due to loss of ovarian follicular activity. It is a retrospective diagnosis, confirmed after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea without any other pathological cause. The average age of menopause is 51 years (range 45-55). Perimenopause (menopausal transition) begins years before and continues for 1 year after the final menstrual period.

## Key Points

- Retrospective diagnosis: 12 months of amenorrhea
- Average age: 51 years; premature menopause: <40 years
- Caused by ovarian follicular depletion
- FSH >40 IU/L is confirmatory (but not needed for diagnosis in women >45)
- Estrogen deficiency causes vasomotor, urogenital, skeletal, and cardiovascular effects
- HRT is the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms

## Definition & Classification

### Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Menopause | 12 months amenorrhea from last menstrual period (LMP) |
| Perimenopause | Transition period: irregular cycles → 12 months after LMP |
| Premature menopause (POI) | Menopause before age 40 |
| Early menopause | Age 40-45 |
| Postmenopause | Period after menopause |
| Induced menopause | Surgical (bilateral oophorectomy), chemotherapy, radiation |

### STRAW+10 Staging System
Stages of Reproductive Aging Workshop:
- **-5 to -3:** Reproductive (regular cycles)
- **-2 to -1:** Menopausal transition (variable cycle length, ≥60 days between periods)
- **0:** Final menstrual period (FMP)
- **+1a, +1b:** Early postmenopause (first 5 years)
- **+1c to +2:** Late postmenopause (>5 years)

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
1. Progressive decline in ovarian follicle number (from ~2 million at birth)
2. Fewer follicles → decreased inhibin B → increased FSH (earliest marker)
3. Continued follicular depletion → declining estradiol
4. Eventually no follicles respond → anovulation → amenorrhea
5. Postmenopausal ovary still produces androgens (androstenedione, testosterone)
6. **Estrone** becomes the predominant estrogen (from peripheral aromatization of androstenedione in adipose tissue)

### Hormonal Changes
| Hormone | Change |
|---------|--------|
| FSH | ↑↑ (>40 IU/L) — most reliable marker |
| LH | ↑ |
| Estradiol | ↓↓ |
| Estrone | Becomes predominant estrogen (from adipose) |
| Inhibin B | ↓ (earliest change) |
| AMH | ↓↓ (reflects follicular reserve) |
| Androgens | Mild decrease (ovary still produces) |

### Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI)
- Menopause <40 years
- Causes: idiopathic (most common), autoimmune, Turner syndrome (45,X), Fragile X premutation, iatrogenic (surgery, chemo, radiation), galactosemia
- Associated with: autoimmune thyroiditis, adrenal insufficiency, diabetes

## Clinical Features

### Vasomotor Symptoms (75% of women)
- **Hot flushes:** Sudden heat sensation, sweating, flushing; last 1-5 min; peak 1-2 years post-menopause
- Night sweats
- Sleep disturbance
- Mechanism: narrowed thermoneutral zone due to estrogen withdrawal

### Urogenital Atrophy (Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause — GSM)
- Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia
- Vulvovaginal atrophy, thinning, loss of rugae
- Recurrent UTI, urgency, frequency
- Progressive — does NOT improve without treatment

### Skeletal
- Accelerated bone loss (2-5% per year in first 5 years post-menopause)
- Osteoporosis → fragility fractures (hip, vertebral, wrist)
- Mechanism: estrogen deficiency → increased osteoclast activity

### Cardiovascular
- Loss of cardioprotective effect of estrogen
- Increased risk of CAD, dyslipidemia (increased LDL, decreased HDL)
- Cardiovascular disease becomes leading cause of death postmenopausal

### Psychological
- Mood changes, irritability, anxiety
- Cognitive changes, poor concentration
- Depression (multifactorial)

### Other
- Weight gain (central adiposity)
- Skin thinning, hair changes
- Joint pains

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical diagnosis** in women >45 with typical symptoms + amenorrhea 12 months
- **FSH >40 IU/L:** Needed only if diagnostic uncertainty (age 40-45, post-hysterectomy, atypical)
- **Not needed:** Hormone levels in women >45 with typical presentation
- **POI diagnosis:** FSH >40 IU/L on two occasions ≥4 weeks apart, in women <40

### Investigations
- DEXA scan: bone density (T-score ≤-2.5 = osteoporosis)
- Lipid profile
- Thyroid function (exclude hypothyroidism)
- Blood glucose / HbA1c
- Cervical cytology, mammography (screening continues)

## Management

### Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)
**Most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms.**

#### Types
| Regimen | Who |
|---------|-----|
| Estrogen-only | Women WITHOUT uterus (post-hysterectomy) |
| Combined (estrogen + progestogen) | Women WITH uterus (progestogen prevents endometrial hyperplasia) |
| Sequential combined | Perimenopausal (monthly withdrawal bleed) |
| Continuous combined | >12 months postmenopause (no bleed) |
| Tibolone | Synthetic steroid; estrogenic + progestogenic + androgenic activity |

#### Routes
- Oral (most common), transdermal (patches, gels — preferred if VTE risk), vaginal (local GSM)

#### Benefits
- Vasomotor symptom relief (most effective)
- Prevents osteoporosis
- Improves GSM symptoms
- May reduce colorectal cancer risk

#### Risks
| Risk | Details |
|------|---------|
| VTE | 2-fold increase (oral > transdermal) |
| Breast cancer | Slight increase with combined HRT after 5+ years; NOT with estrogen-only |
| Stroke | Small increased risk |
| Endometrial cancer | Only with unopposed estrogen (prevented by adding progestogen) |
| Gallbladder disease | Increased with oral HRT |

#### WHI Study Key Findings (2002)
- Combined HRT: increased breast cancer, VTE, stroke, CHD
- Estrogen-only: NO increase in breast cancer
- Both: decreased hip fracture and colorectal cancer
- Led to dramatic decline in HRT use (but now recognized as applicable mainly to older women)

#### Current Recommendations
- Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration
- Start within 10 years of menopause or age <60 ("window of opportunity")
- Transdermal preferred if VTE risk factors
- Annual reassessment
- For POI: HRT until at least age 51 (natural menopause age)

### Non-Hormonal Alternatives
- **SSRIs/SNRIs:** Paroxetine, venlafaxine (for hot flushes)
- **Clonidine:** α2-agonist; modest benefit
- **Gabapentin:** For hot flushes
- **Fezolinetant:** NK3 receptor antagonist (newest)
- **Vaginal moisturizers/lubricants** for GSM
- **Lifestyle:** Exercise, weight management, smoking cessation

### Osteoporosis Prevention
- Weight-bearing exercise, calcium (1200 mg/day), vitamin D (800-1000 IU/day)
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate)
- Denosumab, raloxifene (SERM), calcitonin

## Complications

- Osteoporotic fractures (hip fracture mortality 20-30% in first year)
- Cardiovascular disease (leading cause of death)
- Urogenital atrophy (progressive)
- Psychosocial impact
- HRT-related complications (VTE, breast cancer)
