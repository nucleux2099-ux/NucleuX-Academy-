# Laparoscopy in Gynecology

## Overview

Gynecological laparoscopy is a minimally invasive surgical technique that uses a telescope and camera system inserted through small abdominal incisions to visualize and operate on pelvic and abdominal organs. It has revolutionized gynecological surgery, offering advantages of smaller incisions, less pain, shorter hospital stay, and faster recovery compared to open surgery. It is both diagnostic and operative.

## Key Points

- Minimally invasive: "keyhole surgery"
- Pneumoperitoneum created with CO2 gas
- Palmer's point: alternative entry in patients with previous surgery/adhesions
- Veress needle or Hasson's (open) technique for entry
- Trendelenburg position for pelvic visualization
- Most common complication: trocar/Veress needle injury
- CO2 embolism is the most feared (rare) complication

## Definition & Classification

### Types
| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Diagnostic | Visualization: infertility workup, chronic pelvic pain, staging |
| Operative | Therapeutic procedures: cystectomy, myomectomy, hysterectomy, sterilization |

### Entry Techniques
| Technique | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Veress needle (closed) | Blind insertion at umbilicus; insufflation; then trocar |
| Hasson's (open) | Mini-laparotomy at umbilicus; direct trocar insertion under vision |
| Direct trocar entry | Optical trocar inserted without prior insufflation |
| Palmer's point | Left hypochondrium entry (MCL, 3 cm below costal margin) — for adhesions |

### Port Placement
- **Primary port:** Umbilicus (10 mm — camera)
- **Secondary ports:** Suprapubic, bilateral iliac fossa (5 mm — instruments)
- Z-track or perpendicular insertion technique

### Equipment
- Laparoscope: 0° or 30° lens, 5 or 10 mm
- Insufflator: CO2 at 12-15 mmHg pressure
- Light source, camera, monitor
- Instruments: graspers, scissors, bipolar forceps, monopolar hook, suction-irrigation, needle holder, clip applicator
- Energy sources: monopolar, bipolar, ultrasonic (Harmonic), advanced bipolar (LigaSure)

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Physiology of Pneumoperitoneum
- CO2 chosen: rapidly absorbed, non-combustible, inexpensive
- Intra-abdominal pressure: 12-15 mmHg
- **Cardiovascular effects:** Increased SVR, decreased venous return (initially), increased MAP
- **Respiratory effects:** Elevated diaphragm → decreased FRC, hypercarbia (CO2 absorption)
- **Renal effects:** Decreased renal blood flow, oliguria (transient)
- Trendelenburg worsens respiratory effects but improves pelvic visualization

## Clinical Features

### Indications

#### Diagnostic
- Infertility: tubal patency (chromopertubation), endometriosis staging, pelvic anatomy
- Chronic pelvic pain
- Staging of gynecological malignancies
- Evaluation of pelvic masses
- Ectopic pregnancy (diagnosis and management)

#### Operative
| Procedure | Indication |
|-----------|------------|
| Laparoscopic cystectomy | Ovarian cysts |
| Laparoscopic myomectomy | Subserosal/intramural fibroids |
| TLH / LAVH | Hysterectomy |
| Salpingectomy/salpingostomy | Ectopic pregnancy |
| Adhesiolysis | Pelvic adhesions |
| Endometriosis excision/ablation | Endometriosis |
| Tubal ligation | Sterilization (Filshie clips, Fallope rings) |
| Sacrocolpopexy | Vault prolapse |
| Lymphadenectomy | Gynecological cancer staging |

### Contraindications
| Absolute | Relative |
|----------|----------|
| Hemodynamic instability | Multiple previous surgeries (dense adhesions) |
| Severe cardiopulmonary disease | Large pelvic mass (>20 weeks) |
| Generalized peritonitis | Morbid obesity |
| Uncorrected coagulopathy | Late pregnancy |
| Diaphragmatic hernia | |

### Chromopertubation
- Methylene blue or dilute indigo carmine injected through cervix
- Visualize dye spill from fimbrial ends → confirms tubal patency
- Part of infertility workup

## Diagnosis

### Pre-Operative
- Standard pre-anesthetic workup
- Informed consent (conversion to laparotomy risk)
- Imaging (USG, CT/MRI if needed)
- Bowel preparation (optional, for advanced cases)

## Management

### Procedure Steps
1. General anesthesia; orogastric tube; Foley catheter
2. Patient in lithotomy + Trendelenburg position
3. Uterine manipulator placed (if applicable)
4. Veress needle insertion at umbilicus (or Palmer's point/Hasson's)
5. Safety tests: aspiration, saline drop, opening pressure <8 mmHg
6. CO2 insufflation to 12-15 mmHg
7. Primary trocar insertion (10 mm, umbilical)
8. Camera insertion; inspect entry site; secondary ports under vision
9. Systematic inspection of pelvis and abdomen
10. Operative procedure performed
11. Hemostasis, irrigation, specimen retrieval
12. Desufflation; port removal under vision; closure

### Veress Needle Safety Tests
- **Aspiration test:** No blood/bowel contents
- **Saline drop test:** Drop drawn into abdomen by negative pressure
- **Opening pressure:** <8 mmHg confirms peritoneal entry

## Complications

### Entry-Related (Most Common)
- Vascular injury (aorta, iliac vessels, epigastric vessels)
- Bowel injury (small bowel, colon)
- Bladder injury
- Prevention: Hasson's technique safest; Palmer's point if adhesions

### Pneumoperitoneum-Related
- **CO2 embolism** (most feared; rare): sudden cardiovascular collapse
  - Treatment: left lateral decubitus (Durant's maneuver), aspirate gas via central line
- Subcutaneous emphysema
- Pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum
- Cardiac arrhythmias (hypercarbia)
- Hypothermia (cold dry CO2)

### Surgical
- Hemorrhage
- Thermal injury (electrosurgery)
- Ureteric injury
- Nerve injury (positioning)

### Post-Operative
- Port site hernia (>10 mm ports; close fascia)
- Shoulder tip pain (CO2 → diaphragmatic irritation → referred C3-C5)
- Port site infection
- DVT (reduced venous return)
- Port site metastasis (in malignancy)
