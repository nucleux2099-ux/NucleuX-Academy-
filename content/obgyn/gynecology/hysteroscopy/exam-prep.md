# Hysteroscopy — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

Gold standard for intracavitary uterine pathology. Diagnostic or operative. Distension: saline (bipolar), glycine (monopolar), CO2 (diagnostic). Most dangerous complication: fluid overload (glycine → hyponatremia). Best timing: early proliferative phase (Day 6-11). Office hysteroscopy allows "see and treat" without anesthesia. Contraindicated in pregnancy, active PID, uterine cancer.

## High Yield Points ★

- ★ Gold standard for intrauterine pathology evaluation
- ★ Glycine 1.5% used with MONOPOLAR energy; saline with BIPOLAR
- ★ Glycine toxicity: hyponatremia → cerebral edema; stop if deficit >1000 mL
- ★ Saline overload: pulmonary edema; stop if deficit >2500 mL
- ★ Best timing: Day 6-11 (early proliferative — thin endometrium)
- ★ Submucosal fibroid FIGO 0-1: ideal for hysteroscopic resection
- ★ FIGO type 2: challenging, may need staged procedure
- ★ CO2 used for DIAGNOSTIC hysteroscopy only; risk = gas embolism
- ★ Intrauterine pressure: 80-100 mmHg (must exceed tubal opening ~70 mmHg)
- ★ Tubal cannulation: for proximal tubal block in infertility

## Mnemonics

**Distension media — "SGB":** Saline = Bipolar, Glycine = Monopolar, (B)CO2 = diagnostic

**Fluid deficit limits — "1000G, 2500S":** Glycine 1000 mL, Saline 2500 mL

**Operative indications — "PAST-FU":** Polyp, Adhesion, Septum, submucosal Tumor (fibroid), Foreign body, Unblock tubes
