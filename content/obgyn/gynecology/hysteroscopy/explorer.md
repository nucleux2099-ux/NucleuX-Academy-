# Hysteroscopy

## Overview

Hysteroscopy is the direct visualization of the uterine cavity using a hysteroscope (rigid or flexible telescope) passed through the cervix. It is the gold standard for evaluating intrauterine pathology. It can be diagnostic (office hysteroscopy) or operative (resection of polyps, fibroids, adhesions, septa). Advances have made office hysteroscopy without anesthesia increasingly common.

## Key Points

- Gold standard for evaluating intracavitary pathology
- Diagnostic and operative
- Distension media: saline (bipolar), glycine/sorbitol (monopolar), CO2 (diagnostic only)
- Office (outpatient) hysteroscopy: miniature scopes, no anesthesia, "see and treat"
- Fluid overload is the most dangerous complication
- Contraindicated in pregnancy, active PID, and known endometrial/cervical cancer

## Definition & Classification

### Types
| Type | Purpose | Setting |
|------|---------|---------|
| Diagnostic | Visualize cavity; biopsy | Office/outpatient |
| Operative | Resect pathology; therapeutic | Operating room (usually) |
| Office "see and treat" | Combined diagnostic + minor operative | Outpatient |

### Equipment
- **Hysteroscope:** Rigid (most common; 2.9-4 mm diagnostic, 8-10 mm operative) or flexible
- **Light source:** Xenon or LED
- **Camera system**
- **Distension medium delivery system**
- **Operative instruments:** Resectoscope (monopolar/bipolar loop), scissors, graspers, morcellator

### Distension Media
| Medium | Type | System | Risk |
|--------|------|--------|------|
| Normal saline | Electrolyte-containing | Bipolar energy | Volume overload (>2500 mL deficit) |
| Glycine 1.5% | Electrolyte-free | Monopolar energy | Hyponatremia (>1000 mL deficit) |
| Sorbitol/mannitol | Electrolyte-free | Monopolar energy | Similar to glycine |
| CO2 | Gas | Diagnostic only | Gas embolism (rare) |

### Intrauterine Pressure
- Must exceed tubal opening pressure (~70 mmHg) for adequate distension
- Should not exceed mean arterial pressure (avoid intravasation)
- Optimal: 80-100 mmHg

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
- Distension medium expands uterine cavity for visualization
- White light transmitted through scope illuminates cavity
- Operative instruments passed through working channel for interventions

## Clinical Features

### Indications

#### Diagnostic
- Abnormal uterine bleeding (pre- and postmenopausal)
- Abnormal ultrasound findings (thickened endometrium, suspected polyp/fibroid)
- Infertility evaluation (uterine cavity assessment)
- Recurrent pregnancy loss
- Lost IUD retrieval
- Targeted endometrial biopsy
- Evaluation of Asherman syndrome

#### Operative
- Endometrial polypectomy
- Submucosal myomectomy (FIGO 0, 1, 2)
- Adhesiolysis (Asherman syndrome)
- Uterine septum resection
- Endometrial ablation (first-generation)
- Foreign body removal (IUD, bone fragments)
- Tubal cannulation (proximal tubal block)
- Directed biopsy

### Contraindications
| Absolute | Relative |
|----------|----------|
| Pregnancy | Cervical stenosis |
| Active pelvic infection | Heavy bleeding (poor visualization) |
| Known uterine/cervical cancer | Cardiopulmonary disease (fluid overload risk) |
| Recent uterine perforation | |

## Diagnosis

### Pre-Procedure
- Pregnancy test
- Cervical cytology
- Endometrial biopsy (if indicated — can also be done during hysteroscopy)
- Ultrasound
- Timing: Early proliferative phase (Day 6-11) — thinnest endometrium, best visualization

## Management

### Procedure Steps
1. Informed consent; timing in early proliferative phase
2. Anesthesia (GA for operative; paracervical block or none for office)
3. Cervical dilation (may not be needed for miniature scopes)
4. Insert hysteroscope under direct vision ("no touch" technique for office)
5. Distension medium infusion
6. Systematic inspection: tubal ostia, fundus, anterior/posterior/lateral walls, cervical canal
7. Operative intervention if needed
8. Monitor fluid balance throughout
9. Document findings (video/photos)

### Submucosal Myomectomy Classification (Determines Feasibility)
| FIGO Type | Intracavitary | Hysteroscopic Resection |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| 0 | 100% (pedunculated) | Ideal candidate |
| 1 | >50% intracavitary | Good candidate |
| 2 | <50% intracavitary | Challenging; may need 2 stages |
| 3+ | No intracavitary component | Not suitable for hysteroscopic approach |

### Office Hysteroscopy Advantages
- No anesthesia needed
- "See and treat" — biopsy/polypectomy in same sitting
- Cost-effective
- Faster recovery
- Reduced hospital burden

## Complications

### Immediate
- **Fluid overload** (most dangerous)
  - Glycine: hyponatremia → cerebral edema, seizures, cardiac arrest
  - Saline: pulmonary edema, heart failure
  - Prevention: continuous monitoring; stop glycine if deficit >1000 mL, saline >2500 mL
- Uterine perforation (1-2%)
- Hemorrhage
- Gas embolism (CO2 — rare but potentially fatal)
- Cervical laceration
- False passage

### Delayed
- Infection
- Intrauterine adhesions (post-operative)
- Incomplete resection
- Thermal injury to adjacent organs (if perforation + electrosurgery)

### Management of Fluid Overload (Glycine)
- Stop procedure immediately
- IV furosemide
- Hypertonic saline (3%) if symptomatic hyponatremia
- ICU monitoring
- Check serum sodium urgently
