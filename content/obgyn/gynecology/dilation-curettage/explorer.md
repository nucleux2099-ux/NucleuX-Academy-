# Dilation and Curettage (D&C)

## Overview

Dilation and curettage (D&C) is one of the most commonly performed gynecological procedures. It involves dilation of the cervix followed by scraping (curettage) of the uterine endometrium. It serves both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. While largely being replaced by hysteroscopy and outpatient endometrial sampling for diagnosis, D&C remains important in management of incomplete abortion, molar pregnancy, and acute hemorrhage.

## Key Points

- Two components: cervical dilation + endometrial curettage
- Can be diagnostic (endometrial sampling) or therapeutic (evacuation)
- Fractional curettage: separate endocervical and endometrial sampling (for cancer staging)
- Suction evacuation preferred over sharp curettage for pregnancy-related indications
- Complications: perforation, Asherman syndrome, cervical trauma, infection
- Increasingly replaced by office endometrial biopsy (Pipelle) and hysteroscopy

## Definition & Classification

### Types of D&C
| Type | Description | Indication |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Sharp curettage | Metal curette scrapes endometrium | Diagnostic sampling, dysfunctional bleeding |
| Suction curettage | Vacuum aspiration | Incomplete/missed abortion, MTP, molar pregnancy |
| Fractional curettage | Endocervical curettage FIRST, then endometrial | Suspected endometrial/cervical malignancy |

### Instruments
- **Dilators:** Hegar's dilators (graduated, blunt-tipped), Hawkin-Ambler dilators
- **Cervical priming:** Misoprostol (400 μg PV, 3-4 hours prior), osmotic dilators (laminaria)
- **Curettes:** Sharp curette (Sims), blunt curette, suction cannula (Karman's)
- **Others:** Vulsellum/tenaculum, uterine sound, sponge-holding forceps

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
- Dilation: progressive enlargement of cervical os using graduated dilators
- Curettage: systematic scraping of all uterine walls to obtain endometrial tissue
- Suction: negative pressure aspirates contents (more efficient for pregnancy tissue)

### Cervical Ripening
- Misoprostol (PGE1 analogue): softens cervix by collagen remodeling
- Laminaria tents: hygroscopic, gradual dilation over 6-12 hours
- Reduces risk of cervical trauma and perforation

## Clinical Features

### Indications

#### Diagnostic
- Abnormal uterine bleeding (postmenopausal bleeding, failed medical therapy)
- Suspected endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy
- Endometrial dating (infertility workup — largely obsolete)
- Fractional curettage for cervical/endometrial cancer staging

#### Therapeutic
- Incomplete abortion / missed abortion
- Medical termination of pregnancy (MTP)
- Molar pregnancy (suction evacuation)
- Acute uterine hemorrhage unresponsive to medical therapy
- Removal of endometrial polyps (when hysteroscopy unavailable)
- Retained products of conception

### Contraindications
- Desired pregnancy (unless indicated)
- Active pelvic infection (relative — treat first)
- Cervical cancer (relative — may cause hemorrhage)
- Coagulopathy (correct first)
- Uterine anomalies with distorted cavity (higher risk of perforation)

## Diagnosis

Not applicable as this is a procedure. Pre-procedure evaluation includes:
- Pregnancy test
- CBC, coagulation profile
- Ultrasound to assess uterine size, position, and cavity
- Cervical cytology if indicated
- Consent with discussion of risks

## Management

### Procedure Steps
1. **Pre-operative:** Informed consent, cervical priming if needed, antibiotics (if indicated)
2. **Anesthesia:** General anesthesia, paracervical block, or IV sedation
3. **Positioning:** Lithotomy position
4. **Antiseptic preparation** of vulva and vagina
5. **Bimanual examination:** Confirm uterine size and position
6. **Speculum insertion** and cervix visualization
7. **Tenaculum application** on anterior lip of cervix
8. **Uterine sounding:** Determine depth and direction of cavity
9. **Cervical dilation:** Sequential Hegar's dilators (start small, increase by 0.5-1 mm)
10. **Curettage:** Systematic scraping of all walls (anterior → posterior → lateral → fundus)
    - For fractional curettage: endocervical sample FIRST, then dilate and curette endometrium
11. **Suction evacuation:** For pregnancy-related (use appropriate cannula size)
12. **Check completeness:** Gritty sensation indicates complete curettage
13. **Specimen sent** for histopathological examination

### Post-Procedure Care
- Monitor vitals for 2-4 hours
- Mild cramping and spotting normal for few days
- Antibiotics if infection risk
- Follow-up with histopathology results
- Avoid intercourse and tampon use for 2 weeks

## Complications

### Immediate
- **Uterine perforation** (most common serious complication; risk at fundus)
- Cervical laceration/trauma
- Hemorrhage
- Anesthesia complications
- Incomplete evacuation

### Delayed
- **Asherman syndrome** (intrauterine adhesions) — most important long-term complication
- Infection (endometritis)
- Cervical stenosis
- Hematometra

### Uterine Perforation Management
- If suspected: STOP procedure
- Small perforation without visceral injury: observation, antibiotics, monitor
- If visceral injury suspected: laparoscopy/laparotomy

### Asherman Syndrome
- Intrauterine adhesions from aggressive curettage (especially post-pregnancy)
- Presents as: hypomenorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility, recurrent miscarriage
- Diagnosis: hysteroscopy (gold standard), HSG (filling defects)
- Treatment: hysteroscopic adhesiolysis + IUD/balloon + estrogen therapy
