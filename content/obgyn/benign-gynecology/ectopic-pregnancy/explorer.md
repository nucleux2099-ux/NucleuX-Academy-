# Ectopic Pregnancy

## Overview
Ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilized ovum implants outside the uterine cavity, most commonly in the fallopian tube (95%). It is a leading cause of maternal mortality in the first trimester and constitutes a gynecological emergency.

The incidence is approximately 1-2% of all pregnancies and has been rising due to increased prevalence of pelvic inflammatory disease, assisted reproductive techniques, and improved diagnostic capabilities enabling earlier detection.

## Key Points
- Most common site is the ampulla of the fallopian tube (70%)
- Classic triad: amenorrhea, abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding
- Serial beta-hCG levels and transvaginal ultrasound are key diagnostic tools
- Ruptured ectopic is a surgical emergency
- Medical management with methotrexate is an option for unruptured cases
- Risk of recurrence is 10-15%

## Definition & Classification
### By Site
- **Tubal** (95%): Ampullary (70%), Isthmic (12%), Fimbrial (11%), Interstitial (2%)
- **Ovarian** (3%)
- **Abdominal** (1%)
- **Cervical** (<1%)
- **Cesarean scar** (rare but increasing)
- **Heterotopic**: Simultaneous intrauterine and ectopic pregnancy

## Risk Factors
- Previous ectopic pregnancy
- Pelvic inflammatory disease / salpingitis
- Tubal surgery or sterilization
- Intrauterine contraceptive device
- Assisted reproductive techniques (IVF/ICSI)
- Endometriosis
- Smoking
- Age > 35 years

## Pathophysiology
Damage to the tubal epithelium from infection, surgery, or congenital abnormalities impairs ciliary function and tubal motility, delaying ovum transport. The implanting blastocyst invades the tubal wall, which lacks a decidual reaction. Progressive trophoblastic invasion erodes blood vessels, leading to hemorrhage. Without intervention, tubal rupture occurs with potentially life-threatening intraperitoneal bleeding.

## Clinical Features
### Symptoms
- Amenorrhea (6-8 weeks typically)
- Unilateral lower abdominal or pelvic pain
- Vaginal bleeding (usually scanty, dark)
- Shoulder tip pain (diaphragmatic irritation from hemoperitoneum)
- Syncopal episodes (if ruptured)

### Signs
- Adnexal tenderness and mass
- Cervical motion tenderness (chandelier sign)
- Slight uterine enlargement
- Signs of shock in ruptured cases (tachycardia, hypotension, pallor)
- Cullen sign (periumbilical bruising — rare)

## Diagnosis
### Investigations
- **Urine pregnancy test**: Positive in >99% cases
- **Serum beta-hCG**: Suboptimal rise (<66% in 48 hours suggests ectopic)
- **Transvaginal ultrasound**: Empty uterus with adnexal mass, free fluid in POD
- **Discriminatory zone**: beta-hCG >1500-2000 mIU/mL with empty uterus is suggestive
- **Culdocentesis**: Non-clotting blood (less used now with ultrasound availability)
- **Diagnostic laparoscopy**: Gold standard when diagnosis uncertain

### Differential Diagnosis
- Threatened/incomplete abortion
- Ruptured corpus luteum cyst
- Ovarian torsion
- Acute appendicitis
- Pelvic inflammatory disease

## Management
### Emergency (Ruptured)
- ABC resuscitation, IV fluids, cross-match blood
- Emergency laparotomy or laparoscopy
- Salpingectomy (usually) or salpingotomy

### Medical (Unruptured, Criteria Met)
- **Methotrexate** (single dose 50 mg/m² IM)
- Criteria: Hemodynamically stable, unruptured, mass <3.5 cm, no fetal cardiac activity, beta-hCG <5000
- Monitor with serial beta-hCG (day 4 and 7)

### Surgical (Unruptured)
- Laparoscopic salpingectomy (preferred if contralateral tube normal)
- Laparoscopic salpingotomy (if fertility preservation needed)

### Expectant
- Selected cases with declining beta-hCG, low initial levels
- Close monitoring essential

## Complications
- Tubal rupture and hemorrhagic shock
- Recurrent ectopic pregnancy (10-15%)
- Subfertility
- Rh isoimmunization (give anti-D if Rh negative)
- Persistent trophoblast after conservative surgery
