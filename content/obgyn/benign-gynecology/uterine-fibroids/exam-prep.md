# Uterine Fibroids — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

Most common benign tumor of the uterus, arising from smooth muscle (leiomyoma). Estrogen-dependent, occurs in reproductive age, regresses after menopause. Submucosal type causes most symptoms (menorrhagia, infertility).

## High Yield Points

- ★ Most common benign tumor of female genital tract
- ★ Submucosal fibroid — most symptomatic, causes menorrhagia and infertility
- ★ Intramural fibroid — most common type
- ★ Hyaline degeneration — most common degeneration overall
- ★ Red (carneous) degeneration — most common in pregnancy; managed conservatively
- ★ Sarcomatous change occurs in <1% (suspect if rapid growth in postmenopausal)
- ★ GnRH agonist shrinks fibroid 40-60%; used preoperatively; max 6 months
- ★ Whorled pattern of smooth muscle on histology
- ★ Fibroid moves with cervix (vs ovarian mass which doesn't)
- ★ Most common indication for hysterectomy
- ★ FIGO 0-2 submucosal → hysteroscopic myomectomy
- ★ Calcified fibroid — postmenopausal, seen on X-ray

## Mnemonics

**FIBROID — Risk Factors:**
- F — Family history
- I — Increased BMI (obesity)
- B — Black race
- R — Reproductive age
- O — Only child (nulliparity)
- I — Increased estrogen exposure
- D — Delayed childbearing

**Degeneration types (most common in each setting):**
- Overall → Hyaline
- Pregnancy → Red/Carneous
- Postmenopausal → Calcification
- Malignant → Sarcomatous (<1%)

## Common MCQ Topics

- Most common type vs most symptomatic type
- Type of degeneration in pregnancy (red degeneration)
- Differentiating fibroid from ovarian mass on exam
- FIGO classification of submucosal fibroids
- GnRH agonist — mechanism, duration limit, side effects
- Indication for myomectomy vs hysterectomy
- Fibroid polyp = pedunculated submucosal fibroid
- Secondary polycythemia due to erythropoietin secretion (rare association)
- Pseudocapsule — present in fibroid, absent in adenomyosis
