# Uterine Fibroids

## Overview

Uterine fibroids (leiomyomas) are the most common benign tumors of the uterus, arising from smooth muscle cells of the myometrium. They are estrogen and progesterone dependent and occur in 20-40% of women of reproductive age. They are more common in nulliparous women and African-American women.

Fibroids are a leading cause of abnormal uterine bleeding, pelvic pain, and infertility. They are the most common indication for hysterectomy worldwide. While most fibroids are asymptomatic, their clinical significance depends on size, number, and location within the uterus.

The management of fibroids has evolved significantly with the availability of medical therapies and minimally invasive surgical techniques, allowing for uterus-preserving approaches in women desiring future fertility.

## Key Points

- Most common benign tumor of the uterus and female genital tract
- Estrogen and progesterone dependent — grow during reproductive years, regress after menopause
- Classified by location: submucosal, intramural, subserosal
- Submucosal fibroids most commonly cause menorrhagia and infertility
- Whorled pattern of smooth muscle on histology is characteristic
- Red (carneous) degeneration is the most common degeneration during pregnancy

## Definition & Classification

Leiomyomas are benign monoclonal neoplasms arising from uterine smooth muscle. They are surrounded by a pseudocapsule of compressed muscle fibers.

**Classification by location:**
- **Submucosal** — project into uterine cavity; most symptomatic; cause menorrhagia
- **Intramural** — within myometrium; most common type
- **Subserosal** — project from serosal surface; may be pedunculated
- **Cervical** — arise from cervical wall (rare, 1-2%)
- **Broad ligament (intraligamentary)** — between layers of broad ligament

**FIGO Classification (0-8):**
- 0: Pedunculated intracavitary
- 1: >50% intracavitary
- 2: <50% intracavitary
- 3: Contacts endometrium, 100% intramural
- 4: Intramural
- 5: Subserosal, ≥50% intramural
- 6: Subserosal, <50% intramural
- 7: Subserosal pedunculated
- 8: Other (cervical, parasitic)

**Types of degeneration:**
- Hyaline degeneration — most common overall
- Cystic degeneration
- Red/carneous degeneration — most common during pregnancy
- Calcification — most common in postmenopausal women
- Sarcomatous change — rare (<1%), leiomyosarcoma

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Risk factors:**
- Nulliparity
- Early menarche
- Obesity (increased peripheral conversion of androgens to estrogen)
- African-American race
- Family history
- PCOS

**Protective factors:**
- Multiparity
- OCP use
- Smoking (anti-estrogenic effect)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Each fibroid is monoclonal — arises from a single smooth muscle cell
- Growth is estrogen and progesterone dependent
- Express higher levels of estrogen receptors than normal myometrium
- Aromatase activity is increased within fibroids
- Growth factors (TGF-β, bFGF, EGF) play a role

## Clinical Features

**Menstrual symptoms:**
- Menorrhagia (most common symptom) — especially submucosal
- Dysmenorrhea
- Intermenstrual bleeding

**Pressure symptoms:**
- Urinary frequency, urgency (anterior wall fibroids)
- Constipation (posterior wall fibroids)
- Ureteric obstruction → hydronephrosis (rare, cervical/broad ligament fibroids)

**Reproductive:**
- Infertility (submucosal > intramural)
- Recurrent pregnancy loss
- Complications in pregnancy: miscarriage, malpresentation, preterm labor, red degeneration, obstructed labor

**On examination:**
- Uterus is enlarged, firm, irregular, mobile
- Non-tender (unless degeneration)
- Moves with cervix (unlike ovarian mass)

## Diagnosis

**Ultrasound (TVS):** First-line investigation
- Well-defined hypoechoic mass with whorled appearance
- Peripheral vascularity on Doppler

**Saline infusion sonography (SIS):** For submucosal fibroids

**MRI:** Best imaging for mapping fibroids pre-surgery; delineates number, size, location

**Hysteroscopy:** Direct visualization of submucosal fibroids; diagnostic and therapeutic

**Investigations for associated conditions:**
- CBC (anemia from menorrhagia)
- Iron studies
- Endometrial biopsy (if AUB in >40 years to rule out malignancy)

## Management

**Expectant management:** Asymptomatic fibroids — observation with serial ultrasound

**Medical management:**
- **GnRH agonists** (leuprolide): Shrink fibroids by 40-60%; used preoperatively; max 6 months (bone loss)
- **Ulipristal acetate** (selective progesterone receptor modulator): Reduces bleeding and fibroid size
- **Tranexamic acid:** For menorrhagia
- **Mifepristone:** Antiprogestogen; reduces fibroid size
- **LNG-IUS (Mirena):** Reduces menorrhagia (not if cavity distorted)
- **Combined OCPs / progestins:** Symptomatic relief of bleeding

**Surgical management:**
- **Myomectomy:** For women desiring fertility
  - Hysteroscopic myomectomy — for submucosal (FIGO 0-2)
  - Laparoscopic/open myomectomy — for intramural/subserosal
- **Hysterectomy:** Definitive treatment; for completed family
- **Uterine artery embolization (UAE):** Interventional radiology; preserves uterus but NOT recommended before planned pregnancy
- **MRI-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS):** Non-invasive thermal ablation

**In pregnancy:**
- Conservative management for red degeneration (bed rest, analgesics, hydration)
- Myomectomy during pregnancy — avoided unless torsion of pedunculated fibroid
- Cesarean myomectomy — controversial

## Complications & Prognosis

**Complications:**
- Iron deficiency anemia
- Infertility
- Pregnancy complications (miscarriage, preterm labor, malpresentation)
- Torsion of pedunculated fibroid
- Sarcomatous change (<1%)
- Acute urinary retention
- Secondary polycythemia (rare — erythropoietin production)

**Prognosis:**
- Benign condition with excellent prognosis
- Regresses after menopause
- Recurrence after myomectomy: 15-30% over 5 years
- Hysterectomy is curative
