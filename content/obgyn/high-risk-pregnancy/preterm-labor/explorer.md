# Preterm Labor

## Overview

Preterm labor is defined as regular uterine contractions causing progressive cervical change between 20-36+6 weeks of gestation. Preterm birth is the leading cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality worldwide, accounting for approximately 75% of perinatal deaths and 50% of long-term neurological disability. Globally, about 15 million babies are born preterm each year.

Prevention, early recognition, and appropriate management including tocolysis, antenatal corticosteroids, and neuroprotection with magnesium sulfate are key to improving outcomes.

## Key Points

- Preterm labor: regular contractions + cervical change at 20-36+6 weeks
- Leading cause of neonatal mortality and morbidity worldwide
- Spontaneous preterm birth accounts for ~70% of cases
- Fetal fibronectin (fFN) and cervical length are key predictive tools
- Antenatal corticosteroids: single most effective intervention for reducing neonatal mortality
- Tocolysis buys time (48 hours) for steroids, not a definitive treatment
- MgSO4 for neuroprotection if <32 weeks

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
- **Preterm labor**: Regular uterine contractions (≥4 in 20 min or ≥8 in 60 min) with cervical dilatation ≥1 cm or effacement ≥80%, between 20+0 and 36+6 weeks

### Classification by Gestational Age
| Category | Gestational Age | Survival |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| Extremely preterm | <28 weeks | 60-80% (with NICU) |
| Very preterm | 28-31+6 weeks | 90-95% |
| Moderate preterm | 32-33+6 weeks | >95% |
| Late preterm | 34-36+6 weeks | >98% |

### Classification by Etiology
1. **Spontaneous preterm labor** (~40-45%)
2. **PPROM-associated** (~25-30%)
3. **Iatrogenic/indicated** (~30%) — for maternal/fetal indications (preeclampsia, IUGR, abruption)

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Pathophysiology — Four Major Pathways
1. **Infection/Inflammation** — ascending genital tract infection → decidual inflammation → prostaglandin release → contractions + cervical ripening
2. **Decidual hemorrhage** — abruption → thrombin → uterotonic effect
3. **Uterine overdistension** — twins, polyhydramnios → gap junction formation → coordinated contractions
4. **Premature activation of HPA axis** — fetal/maternal stress → CRH → prostaglandins

### Risk Factors
- **Previous preterm birth** (strongest predictor — recurrence 15-50%)
- Short cervical length (<25 mm on TVS)
- Multiple gestation
- Uterine anomalies (bicornuate, septate)
- Cervical insufficiency
- Infections: BV, UTI, chorioamnionitis, periodontal disease
- PPROM
- Polyhydramnios
- Smoking, substance abuse
- Low BMI, poor nutrition
- Extremes of maternal age (<18, >40)
- Short interpregnancy interval (<6 months)
- African American race
- Low socioeconomic status
- Psychological stress

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Regular, painful uterine contractions
- Low backache (dull, rhythmic)
- Pelvic pressure / "heaviness"
- Menstrual-like cramping
- Vaginal discharge (mucus plug, bloody show)
- May have associated PPROM symptoms

### Signs
- Documented regular contractions on tocodynamometry
- Cervical dilatation and/or effacement on speculum/digital exam
- Presenting part low in pelvis

### Assessment Tools
1. **Transvaginal cervical length (CL)**:
   - ≥30 mm → low risk (95% NPV)
   - <25 mm → significant risk
   - <15 mm → very high risk
2. **Fetal fibronectin (fFN)**:
   - Negative (<50 ng/mL) → 99% NPV (won't deliver in 7-14 days)
   - Best used between 22-34 weeks
   - Invalid if digital exam, intercourse, or bleeding in last 24 hours

## Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria
- Gestational age 20-36+6 weeks
- Regular uterine contractions (documented)
- Progressive cervical change (dilatation ≥1 cm or effacement ≥80%)

### Investigations
| Investigation | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|
| Cervical length (TVS) | Predict true preterm labor |
| Fetal fibronectin | High negative predictive value |
| CBC, CRP | Rule out infection |
| Urinalysis + culture | UTI screening |
| GBS culture | Rectovaginal swab |
| Ultrasound | Confirm GA, fetal weight, presentation, AFI |
| CTG | Fetal well-being |
| High vaginal swab | Identify infection |

### Differential Diagnosis
- Braxton-Hicks contractions (irregular, non-progressive)
- UTI
- Round ligament pain
- Placental abruption
- PPROM without labor

## Management

### Prevention Strategies
1. **Progesterone supplementation**:
   - Vaginal progesterone (200 mg/day) if short cervix (<25 mm)
   - 17-alpha hydroxyprogesterone caproate (250 mg IM weekly) — for history of prior preterm birth
2. **Cervical cerclage**: if cervical insufficiency (history-indicated or ultrasound-indicated)
3. **Cervical pessary**: emerging evidence
4. **Treat infections**: BV, UTI, periodontal disease
5. **Smoking cessation**
6. **Adequate interpregnancy interval**

### Acute Management of Preterm Labor

#### Step 1: Confirm True Labor
- TVS cervical length + fFN to avoid unnecessary treatment

#### Step 2: Antenatal Corticosteroids (MOST IMPORTANT)
- **Betamethasone** 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 hours apart (preferred)
- **Dexamethasone** 6 mg IM × 4 doses, 12 hours apart (alternative)
- Effective between 24-34 weeks
- Reduces: RDS (50%), IVH (70%), NEC (50%), neonatal death (40%)
- Optimal benefit: 24 hours to 7 days after completion
- Single rescue course if >14 days since first course and <34 weeks

#### Step 3: Tocolysis
- **Goal**: delay delivery by 48 hours for steroid effect + transfer
- **NOT recommended** beyond 48 hours or after 34 weeks

| Tocolytic | Mechanism | Side Effects |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Nifedipine** (1st line) | CCB → inhibits Ca²⁺ entry | Hypotension, headache, flushing |
| **Atosiban** | Oxytocin receptor antagonist | Minimal side effects (expensive) |
| **Indomethacin** | COX inhibitor → ↓prostaglandins | Premature DA closure (avoid >32 wk), oligohydramnios |
| **MgSO4** | Ca²⁺ antagonist | Flushing, respiratory depression |
| **Terbutaline** | β2-agonist | Tachycardia, pulmonary edema, hyperglycemia |

- **Contraindications to tocolysis**: chorioamnionitis, severe preeclampsia, fetal distress, fetal demise, lethal anomaly, advanced labor (>4 cm)

#### Step 4: MgSO4 for Neuroprotection
- If <32 weeks and delivery expected within 24 hours
- Loading: 4g IV over 20-30 min → maintenance 1g/hr until delivery or 24 hours
- Reduces cerebral palsy by 30-40%

#### Step 5: GBS Prophylaxis
- If GBS status unknown or positive
- Penicillin G 5 million units IV loading → 2.5 million q4h until delivery

#### Step 6: Transfer
- In-utero transfer to facility with appropriate NICU if needed

### Management by Gestational Age
- **<24 weeks**: counseling, limited intervention
- **24-34 weeks**: full management (steroids + tocolysis + MgSO4 if <32 wk)
- **34-37 weeks**: late preterm steroids (if not given), delivery may be considered

## Complications

### Maternal
- Side effects of tocolytics (pulmonary edema with β-agonists)
- Chorioamnionitis
- Operative delivery
- Psychological stress
- Postpartum depression

### Neonatal
- **Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)** — most common
- Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH)
- Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC)
- Neonatal sepsis
- Patent ductus arteriosus
- Retinopathy of prematurity
- Bronchopulmonary dysplasia
- Hypothermia, hypoglycemia
- Feeding difficulties

### Long-term
- Cerebral palsy
- Learning disabilities
- Visual/hearing impairment
- Chronic lung disease
- Behavioral problems
