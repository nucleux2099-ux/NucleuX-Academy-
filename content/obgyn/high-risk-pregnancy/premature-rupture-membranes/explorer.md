# Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM)

## Overview

Premature rupture of membranes (PROM) refers to rupture of fetal membranes before the onset of labor. When it occurs before 37 weeks of gestation, it is termed preterm PROM (PPROM). It is a significant cause of preterm birth, maternal and neonatal morbidity, and necessitates careful clinical decision-making balancing the risks of prematurity against infection.

PROM complicates approximately 8-10% of all pregnancies, while PPROM occurs in 2-4% of singleton pregnancies and is responsible for approximately one-third of all preterm deliveries.

## Key Points

- PROM = rupture of membranes before onset of labor at any gestational age
- PPROM = rupture before 37 weeks gestation
- Prolonged PROM = rupture >18 hours before delivery
- Diagnosis is primarily clinical (history + sterile speculum examination)
- Digital vaginal examination should be AVOIDED until labor is established
- Management depends on gestational age and presence of complications
- Latency period inversely proportional to gestational age
- Chorioamnionitis is the most feared complication

## Definition & Classification

### Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| PROM | Rupture of membranes before onset of labor (≥37 weeks) |
| PPROM | Rupture of membranes before 37 weeks |
| Prolonged PROM | >18 hours between rupture and delivery |
| Previable PROM | Rupture before viability (<24 weeks) |
| Midtrimester PROM | Rupture between 24-34 weeks |
| Near-term PROM | Rupture between 34-37 weeks |

### Classification by Gestational Age
1. **Term PROM** (≥37 weeks) — most common, 80% enter labor within 24 hours
2. **Late preterm PPROM** (34-36+6 weeks) — delivery usually recommended
3. **Remote from term PPROM** (24-33+6 weeks) — expectant management with steroids
4. **Previable PPROM** (<24 weeks) — counseling, high morbidity/mortality

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Normal Membrane Structure
- **Amnion**: inner layer, single epithelial layer + connective tissue
- **Chorion**: outer layer, thicker, contains collagen
- Membranes have tensile strength maintained by collagen cross-links

### Pathophysiology of Rupture
1. **Weakening of collagen** by matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)
2. **Infection** → inflammatory cytokines → MMP activation
3. **Mechanical stress** — polyhydramnios, multiple pregnancy
4. **Apoptosis** of membrane cells in the zone of altered morphology

### Risk Factors
- **Previous PPROM** (strongest risk factor — recurrence 16-32%)
- Genital tract infection (BV, GBS, Chlamydia, Gonorrhea)
- Smoking
- Low socioeconomic status
- Cervical insufficiency / short cervix
- Polyhydramnios
- Multiple pregnancy
- Amniocentesis / cervical cerclage
- Nutritional deficiencies (Vitamin C, copper, zinc)
- Connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos syndrome)
- Antepartum hemorrhage

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Sudden gush of fluid** from vagina — most common presentation
- Continuous leaking/dribbling of clear fluid
- Wet undergarments
- May report "feeling something pop"
- Absence of labor contractions (distinguishes from normal labor)

### Signs on Examination
- **Sterile speculum examination** (GOLD STANDARD for initial assessment):
  - Pooling of fluid in posterior fornix
  - Fluid seen draining from cervical os
  - Cervical dilatation assessment (visual only)
- Fundal height may be reduced (oligohydramnios)

### ⚠️ Important
- **DO NOT perform digital vaginal examination** unless labor is imminent
- Digital examination increases risk of infection and shortens latency

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis (Most Important)
1. **History**: gush of fluid from vagina
2. **Sterile speculum examination**: pooling in posterior fornix

### Confirmatory Tests
| Test | Principle | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Nitrazine test** | Amniotic fluid is alkaline (pH 7.0-7.5), vaginal pH is acidic (4.5-6.0); turns blue | 90-97% |
| **Fern test** | Dried amniotic fluid shows ferning/arborization pattern on microscopy | 95-98% |
| **AmniSure (PAMG-1)** | Detects placental alpha-microglobulin-1 | 98-99% |
| **IGFBP-1 test** | Detects insulin-like growth factor binding protein-1 | 95% |

### False Positives for Nitrazine Test
- Blood, semen, antiseptic solutions, BV discharge, cervical mucus

### Ultrasound
- **Oligohydramnios** supports diagnosis but not confirmatory
- Assess fetal presentation, estimated fetal weight
- AFI <5 cm or single deepest pocket <2 cm

### Additional Investigations
- GBS culture (rectovaginal swab)
- Complete blood count, CRP (to monitor for infection)
- High vaginal swab for culture
- CTG for fetal well-being

## Management

### General Principles
- Confirm diagnosis
- Determine gestational age accurately
- Rule out chorioamnionitis, cord prolapse, fetal distress, placental abruption
- Decision based on gestational age

### Term PROM (≥37 weeks)
- **Induction of labor** recommended (within 12-24 hours)
- Oxytocin or prostaglandins
- 80% will labor spontaneously within 24 hours
- Expectant management up to 24 hours acceptable if no infection

### Late Preterm PPROM (34-36+6 weeks)
- **Delivery recommended** (induction or C-section based on presentation)
- Antenatal corticosteroids if not previously given
- GBS prophylaxis

### Remote from Term PPROM (24-33+6 weeks) — Expectant Management
1. **Hospitalization** — bed rest, monitor for infection
2. **Antenatal corticosteroids** — Betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 hours apart
3. **Antibiotics (latency antibiotics)**:
   - Ampicillin 2g IV q6h × 48 hours → Amoxicillin 250 mg PO q8h × 5 days
   - PLUS Azithromycin 1g PO single dose
   - Prolongs latency by 5-7 days
4. **Tocolysis** — short-term (48 hours) only to allow steroid completion
5. **MgSO4 for neuroprotection** — if <32 weeks and delivery imminent
6. **Monitoring**: daily CTG, weekly BPP, CBC/CRP, temperature charting

### Previable PPROM (<24 weeks)
- Counseling regarding extremely poor prognosis
- Options: expectant management vs. termination
- Pulmonary hypoplasia risk if <20 weeks (lethal)
- Potter sequence risk with severe oligohydramnios
- Limb contractures

### GBS Prophylaxis
- Intrapartum Penicillin G or Ampicillin if GBS positive or unknown

### Indications for Immediate Delivery (Any GA)
- Chorioamnionitis
- Fetal distress
- Cord prolapse
- Placental abruption
- Advanced labor

### Signs of Chorioamnionitis
- Maternal fever ≥38°C
- Maternal/fetal tachycardia
- Uterine tenderness
- Foul-smelling discharge
- Elevated WBC, CRP

## Complications

### Maternal
- **Chorioamnionitis** (most common serious complication)
- Endometritis
- Placental abruption (4-12%)
- Cord prolapse (1-2%)
- Cesarean delivery
- Sepsis (rare but life-threatening)
- Retained placenta

### Fetal/Neonatal
- **Prematurity** and its complications (RDS, IVH, NEC, sepsis)
- Neonatal sepsis
- Cord compression from oligohydramnios
- Pulmonary hypoplasia (especially if <24 weeks)
- Skeletal deformities / contractures (Potter sequence)
- Fetal death
- Cord prolapse

### Long-term Neonatal
- Cerebral palsy
- Chronic lung disease
- Neurodevelopmental delay
