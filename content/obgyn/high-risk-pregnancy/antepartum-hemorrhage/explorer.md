# Antepartum Hemorrhage

## Overview
Antepartum hemorrhage (APH) is defined as bleeding from the genital tract after 28 weeks of gestation (some definitions use 24 weeks) and before delivery. It complicates 3–5% of pregnancies and is a leading cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality.

The two major causes are placenta previa and placental abruption, which together account for about half of all cases. Other causes include vasa previa, local cervical/vaginal lesions, and unexplained APH. The management depends on the cause, amount of bleeding, gestational age, and maternal/fetal condition.

APH is an obstetric emergency that requires rapid assessment, resuscitation, and appropriate intervention. Every case of APH should be managed in a facility with blood transfusion and operative delivery capability.

## Key Points
- Defined as bleeding from genital tract after 28 weeks of gestation
- Two major causes: placenta previa and placental abruption
- Placenta previa = painless bleeding; abruption = painful bleeding (classic distinction)
- Never do vaginal examination in APH until placenta previa is excluded by ultrasound
- Vasa previa: fetal vessels in membranes over internal os — catastrophic fetal hemorrhage
- Kleihauer-Betke test in Rh-negative mothers to calculate anti-D dose

## Definition & Classification
### By Cause
| Cause | Frequency | Key Feature |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Placenta previa | 30% | Painless, bright red, recurrent |
| Placental abruption | 25% | Painful, dark blood, tender uterus |
| Vasa previa | Rare | Fetal blood, rupture of membranes |
| Local causes | 5% | Cervicitis, polyp, carcinoma |
| Unexplained | 40% | No cause identified |

### Placenta Previa Classification
- **Low-lying:** Placental edge within 2 cm of internal os
- **Placenta previa:** Placenta covers internal os (partial or complete)
- Old classification: Type I–IV (lateral, marginal, partial, complete)

### Abruption Grading (Page Classification)
- **Grade 0:** Asymptomatic, diagnosed retrospectively
- **Grade 1:** Mild vaginal bleeding, no fetal distress
- **Grade 2:** Moderate bleeding, fetal distress present
- **Grade 3:** Severe bleeding, fetal death, coagulopathy

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
### Placenta Previa Risk Factors
- Previous cesarean section (most important)
- Multiparity, advanced maternal age
- Multiple pregnancy, smoking
- Previous placenta previa

### Abruption Risk Factors
- Hypertension (most important — chronic or pre-eclamptic)
- Previous abruption, trauma
- Smoking, cocaine use
- Premature rupture of membranes
- Thrombophilia

### Pathophysiology
- **Previa:** Placenta in lower segment → stretched and disrupted as lower segment forms → hemorrhage
- **Abruption:** Spiral artery rupture → retroplacental hematoma → placental separation → fetal compromise + DIC from tissue thromboplastin release
- **Vasa previa:** Fetal vessels unsupported by cord/placenta → rupture at ROM → fetal exsanguination

## Clinical Features
| Feature | Placenta Previa | Abruption |
|---------|----------------|-----------|
| Bleeding | Painless, bright red, recurrent | Painful, dark, may be concealed |
| Uterus | Soft, non-tender, relaxed | Tense, tender, "woody hard" |
| Presentation | Often malpresentation | Usually cephalic |
| Shock | Proportional to visible blood loss | Out of proportion (concealed) |
| Fetal distress | Usually absent initially | Common |
| Coagulopathy | Rare | Common (DIC) |

### Vasa Previa
- Painless vaginal bleeding at ROM
- Rapid fetal heart rate deterioration
- Apt test positive (fetal blood)

## Diagnosis
- **Ultrasound:** First investigation — localize placenta (TAS + TVS)
- **Do NOT do digital vaginal exam** until previa excluded
- **Speculum exam:** After excluding previa — look for local causes
- **Cardiotocography (CTG):** Fetal heart rate monitoring
- **Blood tests:** CBC, cross-match, coagulation profile, fibrinogen, Kleihauer-Betke
- **Apt test / Singer test:** Differentiates fetal from maternal blood (vasa previa)

## Management
### General Measures
1. ABC approach — IV access (2 large-bore), cross-match blood
2. Crystalloids/blood transfusion as needed
3. Continuous fetal monitoring (CTG)
4. Rh-negative mothers: anti-D immunoglobulin

### Placenta Previa
- **Asymptomatic/minor bleed + preterm:** Expectant management, hospitalization, steroids if <34 weeks
- **Major hemorrhage or ≥37 weeks:** Delivery by cesarean section
- **Complete previa:** Always cesarean section
- **Placenta accreta spectrum:** Anticipate if previous CS + previa; plan multidisciplinary surgery

### Abruption
- **Mild + preterm + stable:** Expectant management, steroids, close monitoring
- **Moderate-severe:** Immediate delivery
  - Live fetus + distress → emergency CS
  - Dead fetus → aim vaginal delivery, correct coagulopathy (FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets)
- **DIC management:** FFP, cryoprecipitate (target fibrinogen >200 mg/dL), platelets

### Vasa Previa
- **Diagnosed antenatally:** Elective CS at 35–36 weeks
- **Ruptured at delivery:** Emergency CS — every second counts (fetal blood volume ~250 mL at term)

## Complications & Prognosis
- **Maternal:** Hemorrhagic shock, DIC, renal failure, Sheehan syndrome, hysterectomy, death
- **Fetal:** Prematurity, IUGR, hypoxia, stillbirth
- **Placenta accreta spectrum:** Massive hemorrhage, hysterectomy
- **Couvelaire uterus:** Blood infiltrates myometrium in severe abruption (uterine apoplexy)
- With prompt management, maternal mortality from APH is <1% in well-resourced settings
