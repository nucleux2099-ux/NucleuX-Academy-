# Fetal Distress

## Overview
Fetal distress refers to a state where the fetus is compromised due to inadequate oxygenation, typically manifesting during labor. The preferred modern terminology is "non-reassuring fetal status" (NRFS), as the term "fetal distress" is imprecise. It is a common indication for emergency cesarean section.

Fetal distress can be acute (cord prolapse, abruption, uterine rupture) or chronic (IUGR, pre-eclampsia). Intrapartum fetal monitoring using cardiotocography (CTG) is the primary tool for detecting fetal compromise during labor. Fetal scalp blood sampling for pH/lactate provides confirmatory evidence.

Early recognition and appropriate intervention (intrauterine resuscitation or expedited delivery) are critical to prevent neonatal hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy, cerebral palsy, and stillbirth.

## Key Points
- Modern term: non-reassuring fetal status (NRFS)
- Meconium-stained liquor is a warning sign but not diagnostic alone
- CTG is the primary monitoring tool; interpretation uses DR C BRAVADO
- Normal FHR baseline: 110–160 bpm
- Late decelerations and reduced variability are ominous CTG patterns
- Fetal scalp blood pH <7.20 = acidosis → expedite delivery

## Definition & Classification
### Acute Fetal Distress
- Sudden compromise during labor
- Causes: cord prolapse, abruption, uterine hyperstimulation, maternal hypotension

### Chronic Fetal Compromise
- Gradual deterioration over days/weeks
- Causes: IUGR, placental insufficiency, post-dates

### CTG Classification (NICE)
| Category | Features |
|----------|---------|
| Normal | Baseline 110–160, variability 5–25, accelerations present, no decelerations |
| Suspicious | One non-reassuring feature |
| Pathological | Two non-reassuring features OR one abnormal feature |

### Decelerations
- **Early:** Mirror contractions; head compression — benign
- **Variable:** Cord compression — variable shape
- **Late:** Uteroplacental insufficiency — ominous
- **Prolonged:** >3 min — requires urgent action if >5 min

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
### Causes of Fetal Hypoxia
1. **Reduced uteroplacental blood flow:** Pre-eclampsia, abruption, maternal hypotension, uterine hyperstimulation
2. **Reduced umbilical blood flow:** Cord compression, prolapse, true knot
3. **Reduced fetal oxygen-carrying capacity:** Severe fetal anemia, fetomaternal hemorrhage
4. **Maternal hypoxia:** Respiratory failure, severe anemia, cardiac arrest

### Fetal Response to Hypoxia
- Initial: redistribution of blood flow (brain-sparing)
- Progressive: anaerobic metabolism → metabolic acidosis
- Late: myocardial depression → bradycardia → multi-organ failure → death

## Clinical Features
### Warning Signs
- Meconium-stained amniotic fluid (especially thick meconium)
- Abnormal CTG patterns
- Decreased fetal movements (antepartum)
- Abnormal Doppler studies (antepartum)

### CTG Abnormalities
- Fetal tachycardia (>160 bpm) — infection, hypoxia, maternal fever
- Fetal bradycardia (<110 bpm) — cord compression, abruption, prolonged
- Reduced/absent variability (<5 bpm for >50 min)
- Late decelerations — uteroplacental insufficiency
- Sinusoidal pattern — severe fetal anemia (ominous)

## Diagnosis
### Intrapartum Monitoring
1. **Intermittent auscultation:** Low-risk labor; every 15 min in 1st stage, every 5 min in 2nd stage
2. **Continuous CTG:** High-risk pregnancies; interpretation by DR C BRAVADO:
   - **D**efine **R**isk, **C**ontractions, **B**aseline **R**ate, **A**ccelerations, **V**ariability, **D**ecelerations, **O**verall impression

### Confirmatory Tests
- **Fetal scalp blood sampling:** pH — normal >7.25, pre-acidosis 7.20–7.25, acidosis <7.20
- **Fetal scalp lactate:** >4.8 mmol/L = acidosis
- **Fetal pulse oximetry:** Not widely used
- **ST analysis (STAN):** ECG-based adjunct to CTG

### Cord Blood Gas at Delivery
- Umbilical artery pH <7.0 and base deficit >12 = significant birth asphyxia

## Management
### Intrauterine Resuscitation
1. Left lateral position (relieve aortocaval compression)
2. IV fluids (correct maternal hypotension)
3. Stop oxytocin if running
4. Tocolysis with terbutaline 0.25 mg SC (relax uterus)
5. Oxygen supplementation (debated but commonly used)
6. Amnioinfusion for variable decelerations (if available)

### Delivery Decision
- **Normal CTG:** Continue labor
- **Suspicious CTG:** Increase monitoring, consider scalp blood sampling
- **Pathological CTG:** If no rapid improvement with intrauterine resuscitation → expedite delivery
- **Prolonged bradycardia >3 min:** Category 1 CS (within 30 minutes)
- **Cord prolapse:** Immediate CS; relieve cord compression by elevating presenting part

### Mode of Delivery
- Vaginal delivery if cervix fully dilated and head low → instrumental (forceps/vacuum)
- CS if not suitable for vaginal delivery or deteriorating

## Complications & Prognosis
- **Neonatal:** Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy (HIE), meconium aspiration syndrome, cerebral palsy, neonatal death
- **Long-term:** Neurodevelopmental disability if severe HIE
- **Therapeutic hypothermia:** For moderate-severe HIE — initiated within 6 hours of birth
- Prognosis depends on duration and severity of hypoxia and rapidity of intervention
