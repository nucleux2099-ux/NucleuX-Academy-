# Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension (PIH)

> 📚 **Sources:** Williams Obstetrics 26th Ed, Ch 40; DC Dutta's Textbook of Obstetrics 9th Ed
> **Level:** UG | PG | SS

## Core Concept

Pregnancy-induced hypertension encompasses a spectrum of hypertensive disorders unique to pregnancy, ranging from gestational hypertension to preeclampsia and eclampsia. It is defined as new-onset hypertension (BP ≥140/90 mmHg) after 20 weeks of gestation in a previously normotensive woman. The pathophysiology centers on abnormal placentation leading to widespread endothelial dysfunction, vasospasm, and multi-organ involvement.

## Key Points

- **Definition**: BP ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions 4 hours apart after 20 weeks gestation
- **Preeclampsia**: Hypertension + proteinuria (≥300mg/24h) OR end-organ dysfunction
- **Eclampsia**: Preeclampsia + new-onset seizures (not attributable to other causes)
- **HELLP Syndrome**: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets
- **Risk factors**: Nulliparity, extremes of age, multiple gestation, chronic HTN, diabetes, obesity, antiphospholipid syndrome
- **Pathophysiology**: Defective trophoblastic invasion → inadequate spiral artery remodeling → placental ischemia → endothelial dysfunction

## Classification

| Type | BP Criteria | Proteinuria | Other Features |
|------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Gestational HTN | ≥140/90 | Absent | After 20 weeks, resolves postpartum |
| Preeclampsia | ≥140/90 | ≥300mg/24h | Or end-organ dysfunction |
| Severe Preeclampsia | ≥160/110 | Present | + severe features |
| Eclampsia | Variable | Usually present | + seizures |
| Chronic HTN | ≥140/90 | Variable | Before 20 weeks or persists >12 weeks postpartum |
| Superimposed Preeclampsia | Worsening | New/worsening | Chronic HTN + new preeclampsia features |

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Often asymptomatic initially
- Headache (frontal, throbbing, not relieved by analgesics)
- Visual disturbances (scotomata, blurred vision, diplopia)
- Epigastric/RUQ pain (liver capsule distension)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Oliguria
- Sudden weight gain, facial edema

### Signs
- Elevated BP (≥140/90 or ≥160/110 for severe)
- Edema (non-dependent, facial, generalized)
- Brisk deep tendon reflexes, clonus
- Papilledema (rare)
- Epigastric tenderness

### Severe Features (any one = severe preeclampsia)
- BP ≥160/110 mmHg
- Thrombocytopenia (<100,000/μL)
- Liver enzymes >2× normal
- Renal insufficiency (Cr >1.1 or doubling)
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset cerebral or visual symptoms

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Finding | Significance |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Urine protein | ≥300mg/24h or P:C ratio ≥0.3 | Diagnostic criterion |
| CBC | ↓Platelets, ↑Hct (hemoconcentration) | Severity marker |
| LFT | ↑AST, ↑ALT, ↑LDH | HELLP, liver involvement |
| RFT | ↑Creatinine, ↑Uric acid | Renal involvement |
| Coagulation | ↓Fibrinogen, ↑PT/aPTT | DIC screening |
| Peripheral smear | Schistocytes, helmet cells | Hemolysis (HELLP) |
| USG | IUGR, oligohydramnios | Fetal compromise |
| Doppler | Abnormal umbilical artery flow | Placental insufficiency |

### Diagnostic Criteria for Preeclampsia
BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks PLUS either:
1. Proteinuria ≥300mg/24h, OR
2. Any of: thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, liver dysfunction, pulmonary edema, cerebral/visual symptoms

## Management

### Prevention
- **Low-dose aspirin** (75-150mg/day) starting 12-16 weeks in high-risk women
- **Calcium supplementation** (1.5-2g/day) if dietary intake low
- Regular antenatal surveillance

### Antihypertensive Therapy

#### Acute Severe Hypertension (BP ≥160/110)
| Drug | Dose | Route | Notes |
|------|------|-------|-------|
| **Labetalol** | 20mg IV, then 40-80mg q10min (max 300mg) | IV | First-line |
| **Hydralazine** | 5mg IV, then 5-10mg q20min | IV | Alternative |
| **Nifedipine** | 10-20mg PO q30min | Oral | Immediate-release |

#### Maintenance Therapy
- **Labetalol**: 100-400mg BD-TDS
- **Nifedipine**: 10-20mg BD-TDS (extended-release)
- **Methyldopa**: 250-500mg TDS (safe, oldest)
- **ACE inhibitors/ARBs**: CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy

### Seizure Prophylaxis (Magnesium Sulfate)

#### Pritchard Regimen (IM)
- Loading: 4g IV (20% solution over 15-20 min) + 10g IM (5g each buttock)
- Maintenance: 5g IM every 4 hours

#### Zuspan Regimen (IV)
- Loading: 4g IV over 15-20 minutes
- Maintenance: 1-2g/hour IV infusion

#### Monitoring for MgSO₄ Toxicity
| Parameter | Normal | Toxicity |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Patellar reflex | Present | Absent (first sign) |
| Respiratory rate | >16/min | <16/min → STOP |
| Urine output | >25mL/hr | <25mL/hr |
| Serum Mg | 4-7 mEq/L | >7 mEq/L toxic |

**Antidote**: Calcium gluconate 10% 10mL IV slowly

### Definitive Treatment: DELIVERY
- **Timing depends on severity and gestational age**
- Gestational HTN/mild preeclampsia: Deliver at 37 weeks
- Severe preeclampsia <34 weeks: Stabilize, steroids, deliver 34 weeks or earlier if unstable
- Severe preeclampsia ≥34 weeks: Deliver after stabilization
- Eclampsia/HELLP: Deliver after maternal stabilization regardless of GA

### Eclampsia Management
1. **Protect airway**, lateral position
2. **MgSO₄** for seizure control (NOT diazepam as first-line)
3. **Antihypertensives** for severe BP
4. **Deliver** after maternal stabilization (within 24 hours)

## Complications

### Maternal
- Eclampsia, status epilepticus
- HELLP syndrome
- Placental abruption
- DIC
- Acute renal failure
- Pulmonary edema
- Cerebral hemorrhage/stroke
- Liver rupture (rare)

### Fetal
- IUGR
- Oligohydramnios
- Preterm birth
- Intrauterine fetal demise
- Perinatal asphyxia

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **MgSO₄ is the drug of choice** for seizure prophylaxis AND treatment in eclampsia (NOT diazepam/phenytoin)
2. **Antidote for MgSO₄ toxicity**: Calcium gluconate 10% 10mL IV
3. **First sign of MgSO₄ toxicity**: Loss of patellar reflex
4. **Uric acid** is the earliest biochemical marker to rise in preeclampsia
5. **Low-dose aspirin** before 16 weeks reduces preeclampsia risk by 17%
6. **HELLP triad**: Hemolysis (↑LDH, schistocytes), Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets
7. **Only cure for preeclampsia**: Delivery (placenta removal)
8. **Postpartum eclampsia** can occur up to 4-6 weeks after delivery
9. **BP target in acute severe HTN**: 140-150/90-100 (avoid rapid drops → fetal distress)
10. **ACE inhibitors are CONTRAINDICATED** - cause fetal renal agenesis, oligohydramnios
