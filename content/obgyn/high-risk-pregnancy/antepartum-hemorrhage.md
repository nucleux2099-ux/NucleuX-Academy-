# Antepartum Hemorrhage (APH)

> 📚 **Sources:** Williams Obstetrics 26th Ed, Ch 41-42; DC Dutta's Textbook of Obstetrics 9th Ed
> **Level:** UG | PG | SS

## Core Concept

Antepartum hemorrhage is defined as bleeding from the genital tract after 28 weeks of gestation (some define as after 20 weeks) and before delivery of the baby. It complicates 3-5% of pregnancies and is a leading cause of maternal and perinatal mortality. The two major causes—placenta previa and placental abruption—account for approximately 50% of cases and have distinctly different presentations requiring tailored management.

## Key Points

- **Definition**: Vaginal bleeding from 28 weeks gestation to delivery
- **Incidence**: 3-5% of pregnancies
- **Major causes**: Placenta previa, placental abruption (50% combined)
- **Minor causes**: Vasa previa, cervical lesions, local trauma
- **"Revealed" vs "Concealed"**: External bleeding vs internal blood accumulation
- **APH is always an emergency** until proven otherwise

## Etiology

| Cause | Frequency | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Placenta previa** | 20-30% | Placenta implanted over/near cervical os |
| **Placental abruption** | 25-30% | Premature separation of normally implanted placenta |
| **Vasa previa** | Rare (<1%) | Fetal vessels crossing internal os |
| **Local causes** | 5-10% | Cervical polyp, ectropion, carcinoma, trauma |
| **Indeterminate** | 40-50% | No cause found |

## Placenta Previa

### Definition
Placenta implanted partially or completely over the internal cervical os.

### Classification
| Type | Description | Management Implication |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Low-lying** | Edge within 2 cm of os but not covering | May allow vaginal delivery if >2 cm at term |
| **Marginal** | Edge reaches internal os | Cesarean usually needed |
| **Partial** | Partially covers internal os | Cesarean mandatory |
| **Complete/Total** | Completely covers internal os | Cesarean mandatory |

### Risk Factors
- Previous cesarean section (MOST IMPORTANT)
- Previous placenta previa (recurrence 4-8%)
- Multiple pregnancy
- Multiparity
- Advanced maternal age
- Smoking
- Previous uterine surgery (myomectomy, D&C)
- Assisted reproduction

### Clinical Features
**Classic Presentation**: Painless, bright red, recurrent bleeding
- Sudden onset, often at rest or during sleep
- No abdominal pain or tenderness
- Soft, non-tender uterus
- Malpresentation common (breech, transverse)
- High presenting part (not engaged)
- FHR usually normal (until massive hemorrhage)

### Diagnosis
- **Transabdominal USG**: First-line, safe, 95% accuracy
- **Transvaginal USG**: Safe (does NOT induce bleeding), more accurate for posterior placenta
- **MRI**: If USG inconclusive, for placenta accreta spectrum

⚠️ **NEVER perform digital vaginal examination in suspected placenta previa!**

### Management

#### Expectant Management (if stable, preterm)
- Hospitalization after first bleed
- IV access, crossmatch blood
- Steroids (if <34 weeks)
- Tocolytics (controversial, may use if no active bleeding)
- Serial growth scans
- Plan delivery at 36-37 weeks (complete previa) or 37-38 weeks (partial)

#### Emergency Management (massive hemorrhage)
1. Resuscitation (ABC)
2. IV access (two large-bore cannulas)
3. Crossmatch 4-6 units PRBC
4. Immediate cesarean section (regardless of gestation)
5. Prepare for massive transfusion protocol
6. Inform anesthesia and NICU

#### Delivery
- **Cesarean section** for complete, partial, or symptomatic marginal previa
- **Classical CS** may be needed if anterior placenta previa
- **Vaginal delivery** possible ONLY if low-lying (>2 cm from os at term) and no active bleeding

### Placenta Accreta Spectrum
| Type | Depth of Invasion |
|------|------------------|
| Accreta | Attaches to myometrium (no intervening decidua) |
| Increta | Invades into myometrium |
| Percreta | Penetrates through myometrium to serosa/bladder |

**Risk**: Massive hemorrhage, hysterectomy (often needed)
**Risk factors**: Placenta previa + previous CS (60% risk if ≥3 prior CS)

## Placental Abruption (Abruptio Placentae)

### Definition
Premature separation of a normally implanted placenta before delivery of the fetus.

### Classification by Severity
| Grade | Clinical Features |
|-------|-------------------|
| **0 (Asymptomatic)** | Diagnosed only after delivery (retroplacental clot) |
| **1 (Mild)** | Vaginal bleeding, no fetal/maternal distress |
| **2 (Moderate)** | Vaginal bleeding, fetal distress, uterine tenderness |
| **3 (Severe)** | Fetal death, maternal shock, coagulopathy |

### Classification by Type
| Type | Revealed | Concealed | Mixed |
|------|----------|-----------|-------|
| Bleeding | External | Internal (behind placenta) | Both |
| Severity appearance | Corresponds to loss | Underestimates severity | Variable |

### Risk Factors
- Hypertensive disorders (MOST IMPORTANT - 50% association)
- Previous abruption (recurrence 10-15%)
- Trauma (MVA, assault, falls)
- Cocaine/amphetamine use
- Smoking
- Preterm premature rupture of membranes
- Polyhydramnios (rapid decompression)
- Short umbilical cord
- Thrombophilias

### Clinical Features
**Classic Presentation**: Painful, dark bleeding, tender uterus

| Feature | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| Pain | Sudden, severe, constant abdominal pain |
| Bleeding | Dark red (may be absent in concealed) |
| Uterus | Tense, tender, "woody hard" |
| Contractions | High-frequency, low-amplitude (tetanic) |
| FHR | Often abnormal (distress, absent) |
| Maternal | Shock out of proportion to visible bleeding |

### Couvelaire Uterus
- Blood infiltrates myometrium
- Purple, ecchymotic uterus seen at cesarean
- May cause uterine atony → PPH

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical diagnosis primarily**
- **USG**: Only 25% sensitive (fresh blood similar echogenicity to placenta)
  - Retroplacental hematoma (when visible)
  - Rules out placenta previa
- **CTG**: Fetal distress, sinusoidal pattern, absent FHR
- **Labs**: CBC, coagulation profile (PT, aPTT, fibrinogen), crossmatch

### Complications

#### Maternal
- Hypovolemic shock
- DIC (10% of severe abruption)
- Acute renal failure (acute tubular necrosis)
- Couvelaire uterus
- PPH
- Sheehan syndrome (pituitary necrosis)

#### Fetal
- Fetal distress
- IUFD (15-20% overall, 50% in severe)
- Preterm birth
- Anemia
- Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy

### Management

#### Initial Stabilization
1. Two large-bore IV cannulas
2. Crystalloid resuscitation
3. Crossmatch ≥4 units PRBC
4. Insert Foley catheter (monitor UOP)
5. Coagulation profile
6. Continuous CTG

#### Definitive Management
| Scenario | Management |
|----------|------------|
| Mild abruption, term, stable | Vaginal delivery (expedite with ARM/oxytocin) |
| Moderate abruption, stable | Expedite delivery (vaginal if favorable, else CS) |
| Severe abruption, fetal distress | Emergency cesarean section |
| Fetal demise | Vaginal delivery (aim within 6 hours) |
| Maternal DIC | Correct coagulopathy, deliver |

**Target Hb >10 g/dL, Fibrinogen >200 mg/dL, Platelets >50,000**

## Vasa Previa

### Definition
Fetal blood vessels crossing the internal os, unprotected by placenta or cord.

### Types
- **Type 1**: Velamentous cord insertion with vessels over os
- **Type 2**: Vessels connecting succenturiate/bilobed placenta over os

### Risk Factors
- Velamentous cord insertion
- Succenturiate placenta
- Low-lying placenta in second trimester (resolved)
- IVF pregnancies
- Multiple gestation

### Clinical Features
- **Painless vaginal bleeding at membrane rupture**
- Fetal distress/death out of proportion to maternal blood loss
- Rapid fetal exsanguination (fetal blood volume only 80-100 mL/kg)

### Diagnosis
- **Transvaginal USG with color Doppler**: Can detect vessels over os
- **Apt test/Kleihauer-Betke**: Differentiates fetal from maternal blood
- **Sinusoidal FHR pattern**: Suggestive of fetal anemia

### Management
- **Antenatal diagnosis**: Planned cesarean at 34-36 weeks (after steroids)
- **Emergency**: Immediate cesarean if diagnosed in labor
- **Neonatal resuscitation**: Prepare for fetal hypovolemia/anemia

## Comparison: Placenta Previa vs Placental Abruption

| Feature | Placenta Previa | Placental Abruption |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Bleeding** | Painless, bright red | Painful, dark red |
| **Onset** | Sudden, often during rest | Associated with HTN/trauma |
| **Uterus** | Soft, non-tender | Tense, tender, woody |
| **Fetal parts** | Easily palpable | Difficult to palpate |
| **FHR** | Usually normal initially | Often abnormal |
| **Presentation** | Malpresentation common | Variable |
| **Shock** | Proportional to visible loss | Out of proportion |
| **Coagulopathy** | Rare | Common (20% DIC) |
| **USG** | Diagnostic (95%) | Low sensitivity (25%) |
| **P/V exam** | CONTRAINDICATED | Safe to perform |
| **Delivery** | Usually cesarean | Often vaginal possible |

## General Approach to APH

### History
- Amount of bleeding (pads soaked, clots)
- Color (bright vs dark)
- Pain (present/absent, character)
- Trauma, recent intercourse
- Prior bleeding episodes
- Gestational age confirmation
- Risk factors

### Examination
- General: Pallor, pulse, BP, respiratory rate
- Abdominal: Fundal height, uterine tone, tenderness, FHR
- **NEVER perform vaginal exam until placenta previa excluded by USG**
- Speculum exam (after USG) if local cause suspected

### Investigations
| Investigation | Purpose |
|---------------|---------|
| CBC | Hemoglobin, platelets |
| Blood group, crossmatch | Prepare for transfusion |
| Coagulation profile | DIC screening |
| USG | Placental localization, fetal status |
| CTG | Fetal well-being |
| Kleihauer-Betke | Fetomaternal hemorrhage |

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Placenta previa**: Painless, bright red, recurrent bleeding; NEVER do P/V exam
2. **Placental abruption**: Painful, dark red, tender uterus, associated with PIH
3. **Couvelaire uterus**: Blood infiltrates myometrium → purple, ecchymotic appearance
4. **Vasa previa**: Rapid fetal exsanguination at ROM; fetal mortality 60% if undiagnosed
5. **USG sensitivity**: 95% for previa, only 25% for abruption
6. **DIC** is common in severe abruption, rare in previa
7. **Risk factors**: Previous CS (previa), HTN (abruption), IVF (vasa previa)
8. **Concealed abruption**: Shock out of proportion to visible bleeding
9. **Apt test**: Distinguishes fetal (pink) from maternal (brown) blood
10. **Management**: Resuscitate → Diagnose → Deliver (timing based on stability and gestation)
11. **Placenta accreta spectrum**: Suspect if previa + previous CS; prepare for massive hemorrhage
12. **Sheehan syndrome**: Postpartum pituitary necrosis from APH-related shock
