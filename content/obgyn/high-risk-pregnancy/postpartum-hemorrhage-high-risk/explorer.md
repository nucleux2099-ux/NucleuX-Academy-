# Postpartum Hemorrhage (High-Risk Perspective)

## Overview
Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is defined as blood loss ≥500 mL after vaginal delivery or ≥1000 mL after cesarean section. It is the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, responsible for approximately 25% of all maternal deaths, with the majority occurring in low-resource settings.

PPH is classified as primary (within 24 hours of delivery) or secondary (24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum). The most common cause is uterine atony (70–80%). High-risk pregnancies — including multiple pregnancy, polyhydramnios, grand multiparity, prolonged labor, and coagulopathies — carry significantly increased risk.

Active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) is the single most important intervention to prevent PPH. When PPH occurs, a systematic approach using the "4 T's" framework ensures rapid identification and treatment.

## Key Points
- Definition: blood loss ≥500 mL (vaginal) or ≥1000 mL (CS)
- Leading cause of maternal mortality globally
- Most common cause: uterine atony (70–80%)
- "4 T's": Tone, Trauma, Tissue, Thrombin
- AMTSL prevents PPH: oxytocin + controlled cord traction + uterine massage
- Massive PPH protocol: call for help, resuscitate, identify cause, intervene

## Definition & Classification
### Primary PPH
- Within 24 hours of delivery
- Minor: 500–1000 mL
- Major: >1000 mL
- Massive: >2000 mL or requiring >4 units blood

### Secondary PPH
- 24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum
- Usually due to retained products, endometritis, or subinvolution of placental site

### The "4 T's"
| Cause | Frequency | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **T**one (atony) | 70% | Uterine atony |
| **T**rauma | 20% | Genital tract lacerations, uterine rupture |
| **T**issue | 10% | Retained placenta/membranes |
| **T**hrombin | 1% | DIC, coagulopathy |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
### Risk Factors for Atony
- Over-distended uterus (multiple pregnancy, polyhydramnios, macrosomia)
- Prolonged/augmented labor, precipitate labor
- Grand multiparity (≥5)
- Chorioamnionitis
- Uterine fibroids, previous PPH
- General anesthesia (halothane), tocolytics

### Risk Factors for Trauma
- Instrumental delivery, episiotomy
- Rapid delivery, macrosomia
- Cervical lacerations (precipitate labor)
- Uterine rupture (previous CS, obstructed labor)

### Pathophysiology
- After placental separation, myometrial contraction compresses spiral arteries ("living ligatures")
- Atony: failure of myometrial contraction → uncontrolled bleeding from placental site
- DIC: triggered by abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, retained dead fetus, sepsis

## Clinical Features
- Excessive vaginal bleeding after delivery
- Boggy, enlarged uterus (atony) vs well-contracted uterus (trauma/tissue)
- Signs of hypovolemic shock: tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, altered consciousness
- Visible genital tract lacerations
- Undelivered or incomplete placenta

### Shock Index
- Heart rate / systolic BP; normal <0.9
- >0.9 suggests significant hemorrhage even before overt shock

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical diagnosis:** Based on estimated blood loss and maternal condition
- **Visual estimation** underestimates blood loss — use calibrated drapes or weighing
- **Assess uterine tone:** Bimanual palpation
- **Inspect genital tract:** Under adequate anesthesia for lacerations
- **Examine placenta:** For completeness
- **Coagulation screen:** If DIC suspected (PT, aPTT, fibrinogen, platelets, D-dimer)

## Management
### Prevention — AMTSL
1. **Oxytocin 10 IU IM** immediately after delivery of baby (most important step)
2. Controlled cord traction (Brandt-Andrews maneuver)
3. Uterine massage after placental delivery

### Stepwise Management of PPH
**Step 1: Resuscitate**
- Call for help, 2 large-bore IV lines
- Crystalloids → blood products (cross-matched or O-negative)
- Massive transfusion protocol: RBCs : FFP : Platelets = 1:1:1

**Step 2: Identify Cause (4 T's)**

**Step 3: Uterine Atony Management (Escalating)**
1. Bimanual uterine compression (rubbing up the fundus)
2. Oxytocin infusion: 20–40 IU in 500 mL NS
3. Ergometrine 0.2 mg IM/IV (contraindicated in hypertension)
4. Carboprost (15-methyl PGF2α) 250 μg IM every 15 min (max 8 doses) — contraindicated in asthma
5. Misoprostol 800 μg sublingual/rectal
6. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV
7. Intrauterine balloon tamponade (Bakri balloon, condom catheter)
8. Uterine compression sutures (B-Lynch suture)
9. Uterine artery ligation / internal iliac artery ligation
10. Hysterectomy (life-saving, last resort)

**Step 4: Correct Coagulopathy**
- FFP, cryoprecipitate (fibrinogen >200), platelets

### Retained Placenta
- Controlled cord traction → manual removal under anesthesia
- Placenta accreta: may require hysterectomy

### Secondary PPH
- Antibiotics (endometritis)
- Ultrasound to check for retained products
- Evacuation if retained products present

## Complications & Prognosis
- Hemorrhagic shock, multi-organ failure, death
- Sheehan syndrome (pituitary necrosis from hypovolemia)
- Renal failure (acute tubular necrosis)
- DIC, ARDS
- Need for hysterectomy (loss of fertility)
- Asherman syndrome (after vigorous curettage)
- With prompt AMTSL and management, PPH mortality is preventable
