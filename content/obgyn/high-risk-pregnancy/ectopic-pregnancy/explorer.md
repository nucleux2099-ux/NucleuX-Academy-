# Ectopic Pregnancy

## Overview
Ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilized ovum implants outside the uterine cavity. It is the leading cause of maternal mortality in the first trimester, accounting for 1–2% of all pregnancies. The most common site is the fallopian tube (95%), specifically the ampulla (70%).

Early diagnosis has improved with high-resolution transvaginal ultrasound and serial β-hCG measurements. Management has evolved from solely surgical to include medical (methotrexate) and expectant approaches for selected patients.

Rupture of an ectopic pregnancy is a life-threatening emergency presenting with acute abdomen, hypovolemic shock, and requiring immediate surgical intervention. Risk factors include previous ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, IUD use, and assisted reproduction.

## Key Points
- Most common site: ampulla of fallopian tube (70%)
- Classic triad: amenorrhea + vaginal bleeding + abdominal pain
- β-hCG discriminatory zone: 1500–2000 mIU/mL (TVS should show intrauterine pregnancy)
- β-hCG doubling time <66% in 48 hours suggests abnormal pregnancy
- Methotrexate for unruptured ectopic with specific criteria
- Ruptured ectopic = emergency laparotomy/laparoscopy

## Definition & Classification
**Ectopic pregnancy:** Implantation of fertilized ovum at any site other than the endometrial cavity.

### By Location
| Site | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| Tubal — Ampullary | 70% |
| Tubal — Isthmic | 12% |
| Tubal — Fimbrial | 11% |
| Tubal — Interstitial (cornual) | 2% |
| Ovarian | 3% |
| Cervical | <1% |
| Abdominal | 1% |
| Cesarean scar | Increasing |
| Heterotopic | 1:30,000 (higher with ART) |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
### Risk Factors
- **High risk:** Previous ectopic, tubal surgery, tubal sterilization failure, IUD in situ, DES exposure
- **Moderate risk:** PID/salpingitis, infertility, ART (especially IVF)
- **Low risk:** Previous pelvic surgery, smoking, age >35, multiple sexual partners

### Pathophysiology
- Impaired tubal motility or anatomical distortion → ovum trapped in tube
- Trophoblast invades tubal wall → tubal distension → rupture
- Rupture → intraperitoneal hemorrhage → hypovolemic shock
- Interstitial ectopic ruptures later (8–16 weeks) with massive hemorrhage due to rich blood supply

## Clinical Features
### Unruptured
- Amenorrhea (6–8 weeks)
- Unilateral lower abdominal pain
- Irregular vaginal bleeding (usually scanty, dark)
- Adnexal tenderness, possible mass
- Uterus slightly enlarged but smaller than expected for dates

### Ruptured
- Sudden severe abdominal pain
- Shoulder tip pain (diaphragmatic irritation from blood)
- Syncope, dizziness
- Signs of hypovolemic shock (tachycardia, hypotension, pallor)
- Abdominal distension, guarding, rigidity
- Cervical excitation (chandelier sign)
- Fullness in POD (cul-de-sac)

## Diagnosis
### β-hCG
- Positive pregnancy test
- Serial levels: normal IUP doubles every 48 hours (≥66% rise)
- Abnormal rise (<66%) or plateau suggests ectopic/non-viable pregnancy
- Discriminatory zone: β-hCG >1500 mIU/mL + empty uterus on TVS = likely ectopic

### Ultrasound (TVS)
- Empty uterus with positive β-hCG above discriminatory zone
- Adnexal mass (ring of fire on Doppler)
- Free fluid in POD
- Pseudo-gestational sac (decidual reaction) — differentiate from true GS

### Other
- Culdocentesis: non-clotting blood = hemoperitoneum (less used now)
- Diagnostic laparoscopy: gold standard when diagnosis uncertain

## Management
### Expectant Management
- Criteria: asymptomatic, declining β-hCG, level <1000 mIU/mL
- Close follow-up with serial β-hCG until undetectable

### Medical Management — Methotrexate
**Criteria for single-dose MTX:**
- Hemodynamically stable
- No rupture
- β-hCG <5000 mIU/mL (ideally <3000)
- Ectopic <4 cm, no cardiac activity
- Able to follow up
- Normal liver and renal function, WBC >1500, platelets >100,000

**Protocol:** MTX 50 mg/m² IM single dose
- Check β-hCG on days 4 and 7: must drop ≥15% between days 4–7
- If inadequate drop → repeat dose
- Follow β-hCG weekly until undetectable

### Surgical Management
**Indications:**
- Ruptured ectopic (emergency)
- Hemodynamically unstable
- Contraindication to MTX
- Failed medical management

**Procedures:**
- **Salpingectomy:** Removal of tube — preferred if ruptured or damaged tube
- **Salpingostomy:** Tube-conserving — preferred if other tube absent/damaged and future fertility desired
- **Laparoscopy** preferred over laparotomy when stable

### Ruptured Ectopic
- Emergency resuscitation: IV fluids, blood transfusion
- Emergency laparotomy (or laparoscopy if expertise available)
- Salpingectomy is procedure of choice

### Rh-negative patients: Anti-D immunoglobulin

## Complications & Prognosis
- **Rupture:** Hemorrhagic shock, death if untreated
- **Recurrence:** 10–15% after one ectopic
- **Fertility:** ~60% intrauterine pregnancy rate after one ectopic
- **Persistent trophoblast:** After salpingostomy — follow β-hCG until negative
- **MTX side effects:** Stomatitis, GI upset, transient LFT derangement
- Early diagnosis and treatment have reduced ectopic-related maternal mortality significantly
