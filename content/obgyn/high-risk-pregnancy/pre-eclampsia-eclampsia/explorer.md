# Pre-eclampsia & Eclampsia

## Overview
Pre-eclampsia is a multisystem disorder of pregnancy characterized by new-onset hypertension (BP ≥140/90 mmHg) and proteinuria (≥300 mg/24h) after 20 weeks of gestation. Eclampsia is the occurrence of generalized tonic-clonic seizures in a pre-eclamptic woman not attributable to other causes.

Pre-eclampsia complicates 2-8% of pregnancies and is a leading cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality worldwide. Early recognition and timely management are critical to preventing progression to eclampsia, HELLP syndrome, and other life-threatening complications.

## Key Points
- Definitive treatment is delivery of the fetus and placenta
- Magnesium sulfate is the drug of choice for seizure prophylaxis and treatment
- HELLP syndrome: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets
- Aspirin 75-150 mg from 12 weeks reduces risk in high-risk women
- Eclampsia can occur antepartum (38%), intrapartum (18%), or postpartum (44%)
- Proteinuria is no longer mandatory for diagnosis if other organ dysfunction present

## Definition & Classification
### Pre-eclampsia
- **Mild**: BP 140-159/90-109, proteinuria <2g/24h, no symptoms
- **Severe**: BP ≥160/110, proteinuria ≥2g/24h, or end-organ damage

### Severe Features
- Systolic BP ≥160 or diastolic ≥110 on two occasions
- Thrombocytopenia (<100,000/μL)
- Elevated liver transaminases (2x normal)
- Renal insufficiency (creatinine >1.1 mg/dL)
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache or visual disturbances

### Eclampsia
- Seizures in pre-eclamptic patient without other neurological cause

### HELLP Syndrome
- Hemolysis (LDH >600, schistocytes)
- Elevated Liver enzymes (AST >70)
- Low Platelets (<100,000)

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
### Two-Stage Model
1. **Stage 1** (Placental): Defective trophoblast invasion → inadequate remodeling of spiral arteries → placental ischemia
2. **Stage 2** (Maternal): Release of anti-angiogenic factors (sFlt-1, sEng) → widespread endothelial dysfunction → vasoconstriction, increased permeability, coagulation activation

### Key Pathological Features
- Generalized vasospasm
- Glomerular endotheliosis (kidney)
- Hepatic periportal necrosis/hemorrhage
- Cerebral edema and microhemorrhages
- Placental infarction and abruption

### Risk Factors
- Nulliparity
- Previous pre-eclampsia
- Multiple pregnancy
- Chronic hypertension
- Diabetes mellitus
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Autoimmune conditions (SLE, APLA)
- Family history
- Extremes of reproductive age
- Donor egg/sperm pregnancy

## Clinical Features
### Symptoms
- Headache (frontal, severe, persistent)
- Visual disturbances (scotomata, blurred vision, photopsia)
- Epigastric/right upper quadrant pain (liver capsule distension)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Sudden edema (face, hands)
- Decreased urine output
- Hyperreflexia with clonus

### Eclamptic Seizure
- Tonic-clonic, generalized
- Usually self-limiting (60-90 seconds)
- May be preceded by prodromal symptoms (headache, visual changes)
- Risk of aspiration, tongue bite, placental abruption

## Diagnosis
### Investigations
- **BP monitoring**: Repeated measurements, 4-6 hourly
- **Urine**: Dipstick (≥2+), spot protein:creatinine ratio (≥30 mg/mmol), 24-hour urine (≥300 mg)
- **Blood**: FBC (platelets), LFT (AST/ALT), renal function (creatinine, uric acid), LDH
- **Coagulation**: PT, aPTT, fibrinogen
- **Fetal assessment**: CTG, growth scan, umbilical artery Doppler, amniotic fluid index

### Diagnostic Criteria (ISSHP 2018)
- New hypertension after 20 weeks PLUS one of:
  - Proteinuria
  - Organ dysfunction (renal, hepatic, neurological, hematological)
  - Uteroplacental dysfunction (FGR, abnormal Doppler)

## Management
### Prevention
- Low-dose aspirin (75-150 mg) from 12-36 weeks in high-risk women
- Calcium supplementation (1.5-2g/day) if dietary intake low

### Mild Pre-eclampsia
- Close monitoring (BP, urine, blood tests twice weekly)
- Fetal surveillance (CTG, growth scans)
- Delivery at 37 weeks if stable
- No antihypertensives needed if BP <150/100

### Severe Pre-eclampsia
- **Stabilize**: IV magnesium sulfate (Pritchard or Zuspan regimen)
- **Control BP**: Labetalol IV, nifedipine oral, or hydralazine IV (target <150/100)
- **Fluid management**: Restrict to 80 mL/hour, monitor output
- **Delivery**: Within 24-48 hours after stabilization
- **Corticosteroids**: Betamethasone if <34 weeks for fetal lung maturity

### Magnesium Sulfate Regimens
- **Pritchard**: 4g IV + 10g IM loading, then 5g IM every 4 hours
- **Zuspan**: 4g IV loading, then 1-2g/hour IV infusion
- Monitor: Patellar reflex, respiratory rate (>16/min), urine output (>25 mL/hr)
- Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1g IV (for toxicity)

### Eclampsia Management
1. Protect airway, left lateral position
2. MgSO4 loading dose (if not already on it)
3. Control BP
4. Deliver after stabilization (not during seizure)
5. Monitor for complications (DIC, renal failure, pulmonary edema)

## Complications
### Maternal
- Eclampsia, status epilepticus
- HELLP syndrome
- Placental abruption
- DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)
- Acute renal failure
- Pulmonary edema
- Cerebral hemorrhage
- Hepatic rupture
- Cortical blindness (usually reversible)

### Fetal
- Intrauterine growth restriction
- Prematurity
- Oligohydramnios
- Intrauterine fetal death
- Neonatal complications of prematurity

### Long-term
- Increased cardiovascular risk in later life
- Recurrence risk: 16% for pre-eclampsia, 2% for eclampsia
