# Puerperal Sepsis

## Overview

Puerperal sepsis (postpartum infection) is infection of the genital tract occurring after delivery, typically within the first 6 weeks postpartum. It remains a major cause of maternal morbidity and mortality, especially in resource-limited settings. The most common cause is endometritis (infection of the endometrium and myometrium). Risk factors include cesarean section, prolonged labor, PROM, and retained products. Early recognition and prompt broad-spectrum antibiotics are lifesaving.

## Key Points

- Definition (WHO): Infection of the genital tract occurring at any time between rupture of membranes/onset of labor and 42 days postpartum
- Temperature ≥ 38°C on any 2 of the first 10 days postpartum (excluding first 24 hours) — classical definition
- Most common cause: endometritis (polymicrobial)
- Most common organism: Group A Streptococcus (historically); now polymicrobial (E. coli, anaerobes, GBS)
- CS is the single greatest risk factor (5–20× higher than vaginal delivery)
- Treatment: IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (clindamycin + gentamicin ± ampicillin — "triple regimen")

## Definition & Classification

### Classical Definition (Puerperal Pyrexia)

- Temperature ≥ 38°C (100.4°F) on any 2 of the first 10 days postpartum, excluding the first 24 hours

### WHO Definition

- Infection of the genital tract occurring at any time between rupture of membranes or labor and 42 days postpartum, with ≥ 2 of: pelvic pain, fever ≥ 38°C, abnormal vaginal discharge (foul-smelling), delay in uterine involution

### Types of Puerperal Infection

| Type | Site |
|------|------|
| **Endometritis** | Endometrium + myometrium (most common) |
| **Wound infection** | CS incision or episiotomy |
| **Parametritis** | Pelvic connective tissue (broad ligament) |
| **Peritonitis** | Peritoneal cavity (extension from uterus) |
| **Pelvic abscess** | Pelvic collection (pouch of Douglas) |
| **Septic pelvic thrombophlebitis** | Thrombosis of pelvic veins (diagnosis of exclusion) |
| **Necrotizing fasciitis** | Deep soft tissue; rare but fatal |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Organisms

| Type | Organisms |
|------|----------|
| **Aerobic gram-positive** | Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes), Group B Streptococcus, Enterococcus |
| **Aerobic gram-negative** | E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus |
| **Anaerobes** | Bacteroides fragilis, Peptostreptococcus, Clostridium |
| **Others** | Chlamydia trachomatis (late-onset endometritis), Mycoplasma, MRSA |

- Most puerperal infections are **polymicrobial** (ascending from lower genital tract)
- Endogenous flora ascend through the open cervix into the uterine cavity

### Risk Factors

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Delivery-related** | Cesarean section (#1 risk factor), prolonged labor, multiple vaginal exams, operative vaginal delivery |
| **Membrane-related** | PROM > 18 hours, prolonged ROM, chorioamnionitis |
| **Tissue-related** | Retained products, manual removal of placenta, episiotomy |
| **Host-related** | Anemia, malnutrition, diabetes, HIV, obesity |
| **Other** | Internal fetal monitoring, poor hygiene, low socioeconomic status |

### Pathogenesis

1. Disrupted uterine/cervical/vaginal barriers after delivery
2. Ascending infection from vaginal flora
3. Raw placental site provides excellent culture medium (blood, necrotic tissue)
4. Endometritis → parametritis → peritonitis → septicemia (progressive spread)

## Clinical Features

### Endometritis

- **Onset**: Usually day 2–5 postpartum (earlier after CS)
- Fever ≥ 38°C, often spiking
- Uterine tenderness (on palpation)
- Foul-smelling, purulent lochia
- Subinvolution of uterus (larger than expected)
- Tachycardia

### Wound Infection

- Erythema, induration, discharge from CS incision or episiotomy
- Usually day 3–7
- Wound dehiscence if severe

### Septic Pelvic Thrombophlebitis

- Persistent spiking fever despite adequate antibiotics ("picket-fence fever")
- No obvious focus of infection
- Diagnosis of exclusion; CT/MRI may show pelvic vein thrombus
- Treatment: heparin + antibiotics → dramatic response (diagnostic and therapeutic)

### Progression to Sepsis

| Stage | Features |
|-------|----------|
| **SIRS** | ≥ 2 of: Temp > 38 or < 36°C, HR > 90, RR > 20, WBC > 12,000 or < 4,000 |
| **Sepsis** | SIRS + confirmed/suspected infection |
| **Severe sepsis** | Sepsis + organ dysfunction (hypotension, oliguria, ↑ lactate) |
| **Septic shock** | Sepsis + refractory hypotension despite fluid resuscitation |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

- Fever + uterine tenderness + foul lochia = endometritis until proven otherwise
- Diagnosis is primarily clinical

### Investigations

| Investigation | Purpose |
|---------------|---------|
| CBC | Leukocytosis; left shift |
| Blood cultures (×2) | Before starting antibiotics |
| Endometrial/wound culture | Guide targeted therapy |
| CRP / Procalcitonin | Inflammatory markers |
| Urine culture | Rule out UTI |
| Pelvic ultrasound | Retained products, pelvic collection |
| CT/MRI | Pelvic abscess, septic thrombophlebitis |
| Coagulation profile | DIC screen if severe |
| Lactate | Tissue perfusion (sepsis marker) |

## Management

### Antibiotic Regimens

| Severity | Regimen |
|----------|---------|
| **Mild endometritis** | Oral amoxicillin-clavulanate OR oral clindamycin + ciprofloxacin |
| **Moderate–severe** | IV clindamycin 900 mg q8h + IV gentamicin 5 mg/kg/day ("gold standard") |
| **No response in 48h** | Add IV ampicillin 2 g q6h (triple regimen — covers enterococcus) |
| **MRSA suspected** | Add vancomycin |
| **Anaerobic predominance** | IV metronidazole 500 mg q8h |

### Duration

- IV antibiotics until afebrile for 24–48 hours
- Switch to oral antibiotics to complete 7–10 days total
- If good response to IV, oral step-down may not always be needed (varies by guideline)

### Surgical Intervention

| Indication | Procedure |
|-----------|-----------|
| Retained products | Suction evacuation (under antibiotic cover) |
| Wound abscess | Incision and drainage; secondary closure |
| Pelvic abscess | CT/US-guided drainage or surgical drainage |
| Necrotizing fasciitis | Aggressive surgical debridement (emergency) |
| Peritonitis (generalized) | Exploratory laparotomy |

### Septic Pelvic Thrombophlebitis

- Add therapeutic anticoagulation (heparin)
- Continue antibiotics
- If dramatic defervescence within 48–72 hours → confirms diagnosis

### Sepsis Management (Surviving Sepsis)

- Early recognition → "Sepsis Six" / "Hour-1 Bundle"
- IV fluids (30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus)
- Blood cultures before antibiotics
- Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour
- Measure lactate (repeat if > 2 mmol/L)
- Vasopressors if hypotension persists (noradrenaline first line)
- Source control (drain abscess, remove retained products)

## Complications

- **Sepsis / septic shock** → multi-organ failure → death
- **Pelvic abscess**
- **Peritonitis**
- **Septic pelvic thrombophlebitis** → pulmonary embolism
- **DIC**
- **Asherman syndrome** (intrauterine adhesions from aggressive curettage)
- **Infertility** (tubal damage from pelvic infection)
- **Chronic pelvic pain**
- **Maternal death** (4th most common cause of maternal mortality globally)
