# Diagnosis of Pregnancy

## Overview

Diagnosis of pregnancy is one of the most fundamental skills in obstetrics. It involves recognizing the signs and symptoms of pregnancy, confirming it with biochemical and imaging modalities, and establishing gestational age. Early and accurate diagnosis is essential for proper antenatal care planning, dating, and identification of ectopic or molar pregnancies.

## Key Points

- Pregnancy can be diagnosed by clinical signs, biochemical markers (β-hCG), and ultrasonography
- β-hCG is detectable in serum as early as 8–10 days post-conception
- Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS) can detect gestational sac by 4.5–5 weeks
- Presumptive, probable, and positive signs form a clinical framework
- Pregnancy of unknown location (PUL) requires serial β-hCG and follow-up imaging
- Ectopic pregnancy must always be excluded when β-hCG is positive without intrauterine sac

## Definition & Classification

### Signs of Pregnancy

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Presumptive (Subjective)** | Amenorrhea, nausea/vomiting, breast tenderness, urinary frequency, fatigue |
| **Probable (Objective)** | Uterine enlargement, Hegar's sign, Chadwick's sign, positive pregnancy test |
| **Positive (Definitive)** | Fetal heart sounds, fetal movements felt by examiner, ultrasound visualization of fetus |

### Classical Signs

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Hegar's sign** | Softening of the isthmus (6–8 weeks) |
| **Chadwick's sign** | Bluish discoloration of vagina and cervix |
| **Goodell's sign** | Softening of the cervix |
| **Osiander's sign** | Pulsation felt through lateral fornix |
| **Palmer's sign** | Regular rhythmic uterine contractions (Braxton Hicks) |
| **Jacquemier's sign** | Bluish discoloration of anterior vaginal wall |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Hormonal Basis

- **β-hCG**: Produced by syncytiotrophoblast; doubles every 48–72 hours in early normal pregnancy
  - Peaks at 8–10 weeks, then declines
  - Discriminatory level for TVS: 1500–2000 mIU/mL
- **Progesterone**: Maintains endometrium; produced by corpus luteum → placenta (luteo-placental shift at 7–9 weeks)
- **Estrogen**: Rises throughout pregnancy; responsible for uterine growth, breast changes

### Implantation

- Occurs 6–7 days after fertilization
- β-hCG enters maternal circulation shortly after implantation
- Decidual reaction transforms endometrium

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms (by gestational age)

| Weeks | Symptom |
|-------|---------|
| 4–5 | Missed period |
| 5–6 | Nausea, breast tenderness |
| 6–8 | Urinary frequency, fatigue |
| 8–12 | Nausea peaks, uterus palpable above symphysis at 12 weeks |
| 16–20 | Quickening (primigravida ~20 wk, multigravida ~16 wk) |

### Examination Findings

- Uterine size assessment:
  - 8 weeks: size of an orange
  - 12 weeks: reaches above symphysis pubis
  - 20 weeks: at umbilicus
  - 36 weeks: at xiphisternum
  - 40 weeks: drops (lightening in primigravida)

## Diagnosis

### Biochemical Tests

| Test | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Urine pregnancy test (UPT)** | Detects β-hCG ≥ 20–25 mIU/mL; positive by day of missed period |
| **Serum β-hCG** | Quantitative; detects pregnancy 8–10 days post-conception |
| **Serial β-hCG** | Normal doubling time: 48–72 hours; slower rise suggests ectopic/non-viable |

### Ultrasonography

| Landmark | Gestational Age (TVS) |
|----------|----------------------|
| Gestational sac | 4.5–5 weeks |
| Yolk sac | 5–5.5 weeks |
| Fetal pole with cardiac activity | 6–6.5 weeks |
| CRL measurement for dating | 7–13 weeks (most accurate) |

### Criteria for Non-Viability (ACOG)

- Mean sac diameter ≥ 25 mm without embryo
- CRL ≥ 7 mm without cardiac activity
- Absent embryo with heartbeat ≥ 2 weeks after sac seen
- Absent embryo with heartbeat ≥ 11 days after sac with yolk sac

### Differential Diagnosis of Positive β-hCG

- Intrauterine pregnancy (viable/non-viable)
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Gestational trophoblastic disease
- Biochemical pregnancy / early pregnancy loss
- Phantom hCG (heterophilic antibodies — rare)

## Management

### Confirming Intrauterine Pregnancy

1. Positive UPT → Confirm with TVS when β-hCG reaches discriminatory level
2. If no IUP seen → Serial β-hCG every 48 hours
3. Inappropriately rising or plateauing β-hCG → Suspect ectopic; evaluate with TVS ± diagnostic laparoscopy

### Dating the Pregnancy

- **First trimester CRL** (7–13 weeks): Most accurate (±5–7 days)
- **LMP-based (Naegele's rule)**: EDD = LMP + 9 months + 7 days
- If discrepancy > 7 days between LMP and CRL → Use CRL dating

### Pregnancy of Unknown Location (PUL)

- β-hCG positive but no IUP or ectopic on TVS
- Manage expectantly with serial β-hCG
- Rising: repeat scan in 7–14 days
- Falling: likely early pregnancy loss; follow to zero
- Plateauing: consider ectopic; may need methotrexate or surgery

## Complications

- Delayed diagnosis → late antenatal care initiation
- Missed ectopic pregnancy → rupture, hemorrhagic shock
- False-negative UPT (dilute urine, too early testing)
- Heterotopic pregnancy (rare, higher with ART): coexisting IUP + ectopic
- Misdiagnosis of molar pregnancy as normal pregnancy
