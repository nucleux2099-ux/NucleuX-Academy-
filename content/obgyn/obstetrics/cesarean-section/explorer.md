# Cesarean Section

## Overview

Cesarean section (CS) is the surgical delivery of a fetus through incisions in the abdominal wall and uterus. It is the most common major surgical procedure performed worldwide in women. The global CS rate has risen significantly, from ~6% in 1990 to over 21% in 2021. While CS can be life-saving, unnecessary cesarean deliveries carry risks without benefits. This topic covers the obstetric perspective on indications, decision-making, technique, and outcomes.

## Key Points

- WHO ideal rate: 10-15%; actual rates much higher globally
- Most common overall indication: previous cesarean section
- Most common emergency indication: non-reassuring fetal status
- Standard technique: Pfannenstiel + lower segment transverse uterine incision
- Prophylactic antibiotic before skin incision (Cefazolin 2g IV)
- Regional anesthesia preferred (spinal > epidural > GA)
- VBAC success: 60-80% with one prior low transverse CS
- Each additional CS increases risk of placenta accreta spectrum

## Definition & Classification

### Classification (Lucas Classification / NICE)
| Category | Urgency | Target |
|----------|---------|--------|
| 1 | Immediate threat to life | DDI <30 min |
| 2 | Maternal/fetal compromise | DDI <75 min |
| 3 | Early delivery needed, no compromise | Scheduled |
| 4 | Elective | Timed |

### Uterine Incision Types
- **Lower segment transverse (LSCS)**: standard, lowest rupture risk (0.5-1%)
- **Classical (upper segment vertical)**: high rupture risk (4-9%), specific indications
- **Low vertical**: for poorly formed lower segment
- **T/J incision**: extension when needed

## Etiology / Indications

### Absolute Indications
- Complete placenta previa
- Vasa previa
- Transverse/oblique lie (persistent)
- Cord prolapse with live fetus
- Prior classical CS or uterine rupture

### Common Indications
- Previous CS (most common)
- Non-reassuring fetal status
- Failure to progress / arrest of labor
- Malpresentation (breech in primigravida)
- CPD
- Failed induction
- Macrosomia (>4.5 kg in DM, >5 kg otherwise)
- Multiple pregnancy (certain configurations)
- Placental abruption (with live fetus)
- Maternal request (after counseling)

## Clinical Features / Preoperative Assessment

### Preoperative
- Informed consent with risk discussion
- NPO: 6h solids, 2h clear fluids (elective)
- Investigations: CBC, blood group, crossmatch
- Antacid prophylaxis (ranitidine + sodium citrate)
- Antibiotic prophylaxis: Cefazolin 2g IV before skin incision
- VTE risk assessment → TED stockings ± LMWH
- Urinary catheter inserted

### Elective CS Timing
- **39 completed weeks** (to reduce neonatal respiratory morbidity)
- Earlier if indicated (placenta previa with bleeding, etc.)

## Diagnosis

Not applicable — CS is a therapeutic procedure.

## Management

### Surgical Technique (Standard LSCS)
1. Pfannenstiel skin incision
2. Dissect through layers: subcutaneous fat → rectus sheath → separate rectus muscles → peritoneum
3. Create bladder flap, reflect bladder
4. Transverse curvilinear uterine incision in lower segment
5. Deliver fetus and placenta
6. Uterine closure (continuous, usually 2-layer)
7. Check hemostasis, peritoneal toilet
8. Close abdomen layer by layer
9. Subcuticular skin closure

### Joel-Cohen Technique
- Straight transverse skin incision 3 cm above symphysis
- Blunt dissection of all layers
- Faster, less blood loss, less postoperative pain

### Intraoperative
- Oxytocin 5 IU slow IV after delivery (prevent atony)
- Ensure complete placental removal
- Inspect adnexa (incidental findings)
- Swab and instrument count

### Postoperative Care
- Analgesia: multimodal (paracetamol + NSAID + opioid PRN)
- Early feeding: clear fluids 2-4h, diet 6h post-op
- Catheter removal: 12-24 hours
- Early mobilization: 6-12 hours
- VTE prophylaxis: LMWH based on risk
- Wound care: inspect day 1-2
- Breastfeeding: encourage immediately
- Discharge: day 2-3 if uncomplicated

### VBAC Considerations
- One prior low transverse CS: VBAC success 60-80%
- Uterine rupture risk: 0.5-1%
- Contraindicated: prior classical CS, >2 prior CS (relative), prior rupture
- Requires continuous CTG monitoring
- Must deliver in facility with immediate CS capability

## Complications

### Intraoperative
- Hemorrhage (500-1000 mL average)
- Bladder injury (most common organ)
- Bowel injury, ureteric injury
- Uterine incision extension
- Anesthetic complications
- Fetal laceration (1-2%)

### Postoperative
- Wound infection (3-15%)
- Endometritis
- UTI
- VTE (DVT/PE)
- Ileus
- Wound dehiscence/burst abdomen
- Secondary PPH

### Long-term
- Adhesion formation
- Placenta accreta spectrum (increasing with each CS)
- Cesarean scar ectopic
- Scar dehiscence/rupture in future
- Isthmocele (niche defect)
- Chronic pelvic pain

### Neonatal
- TTN (transient tachypnea of newborn) — most common
- Fetal laceration
- Iatrogenic prematurity
- Delayed breastfeeding initiation
