# Postpartum Hemorrhage

## Overview

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, responsible for approximately 25% of all maternal deaths. It is defined as blood loss ≥ 500 mL after vaginal delivery or ≥ 1000 mL after cesarean section. PPH is classified as primary (within 24 hours) or secondary (24 hours to 12 weeks). The most common cause is uterine atony (70–80%), and active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) is the key preventive strategy.

## Key Points

- PPH = blood loss ≥ 500 mL (vaginal) or ≥ 1000 mL (CS)
- Most common cause: uterine atony (70–80%)
- "4 Ts" of PPH: Tone, Trauma, Tissue, Thrombin
- AMTSL prevents PPH (oxytocin 10 IU IM)
- Stepwise management: uterotonic drugs → tamponade → surgical → hysterectomy
- Massive PPH: activate massive transfusion protocol; 1:1:1 PRBC:FFP:platelets
- Secondary PPH: most common cause is endometritis ± retained products

## Definition & Classification

### Definitions

| Type | Timing | Definition |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **Primary PPH** | Within 24 hours of delivery | Blood loss ≥ 500 mL (vaginal); ≥ 1000 mL (CS) |
| **Secondary PPH** | 24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum | Abnormal/excessive bleeding |
| **Major PPH** | — | > 1000 mL |
| **Massive PPH** | — | > 2000 mL OR rate > 150 mL/min OR requiring > 4 units PRBC |

### The "4 Ts" — Causes of PPH

| Cause | Frequency | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **T**one (atony) | 70–80% | Uterine atony |
| **T**rauma | 10–20% | Lacerations, episiotomy, uterine rupture, uterine inversion |
| **T**issue | 5–10% | Retained placenta/membranes, placenta accreta |
| **T**hrombin | 1–2% | Coagulopathy (DIC, preeclampsia, HELLP, AFE, abruption) |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors for Uterine Atony

| Category | Risk Factors |
|----------|-------------|
| **Overdistension** | Multiple pregnancy, polyhydramnios, macrosomia |
| **Uterine exhaustion** | Prolonged labor, augmented labor, rapid labor |
| **Infection** | Chorioamnionitis |
| **Drugs** | MgSO₄, nifedipine, halogenated anesthetics |
| **Structural** | Fibroids, uterine anomalies |
| **Other** | Grand multiparity, previous PPH, placenta previa |

### Pathophysiology

- After placental delivery, myometrial contractions compress spiral arteries ("living ligature")
- If uterus fails to contract → spiral arteries remain open → massive hemorrhage
- Blood loss can reach 500–1000 mL within minutes
- Compensatory mechanisms (tachycardia, vasoconstriction) may mask hypovolemia until > 1500 mL lost

### Coagulopathy in PPH

- Massive hemorrhage → consumptive coagulopathy (DIC)
- Dilutional coagulopathy from crystalloid/colloid resuscitation
- Hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy = "lethal triad"
- Pre-existing: HELLP, placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism

## Clinical Features

### Clinical Assessment

| Blood Loss | Symptoms | Signs |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| 500–1000 mL | Mild weakness | Tachycardia (90–100) |
| 1000–1500 mL | Anxiety, thirst | Tachycardia (100–120), ↓ BP |
| 1500–2000 mL | Confusion, restlessness | Tachycardia > 120, hypotension, oliguria |
| > 2500 mL | Collapse | Profound shock, anuria |

### Identifying the Cause

- **Atony**: Uterus soft, boggy, above umbilicus
- **Trauma**: Bleeding despite well-contracted uterus → examine genital tract
- **Retained tissue**: Incomplete placenta on examination
- **Coagulopathy**: Oozing from multiple sites, non-clotting blood; check labs

## Diagnosis

### Estimation of Blood Loss

- Visual estimation is inaccurate (underestimates by 30–50%)
- Weigh swabs and drapes (1 g = 1 mL blood)
- Calibrated drapes / collection bags
- Shock index (HR/SBP) > 0.9 = significant hemorrhage

### Investigations

| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| CBC | Hb, platelet count |
| Coagulation (PT, aPTT, fibrinogen) | Coagulopathy; fibrinogen < 200 mg/dL → poor prognosis |
| Blood group and crossmatch | Transfusion readiness |
| Renal function | Assess for AKI (Sheehan syndrome risk) |
| Lactate, ABG | Assess tissue perfusion |
| Ultrasound | Retained products, uterine inversion |

## Management

### Stepwise Approach to PPH

#### Step 1: First-Line Uterotonics

| Drug | Dose | Route | Notes |
|------|------|-------|-------|
| **Oxytocin** | 10 IU IM or 20–40 IU in 1L NS IV | IM/IV | First line; can cause water intoxication |
| **Ergometrine** | 0.2 mg IM | IM | Avoid in hypertension, cardiac disease |
| **Carboprost (15-methyl PGF₂α)** | 250 µg IM | IM (intramyometrial) | Every 15 min (max 8 doses); avoid in asthma |
| **Misoprostol** | 800–1000 µg | Sublingual/rectal | When others unavailable; causes fever |
| **Tranexamic acid** | 1 g IV | IV | Give within 3 hours (WOMAN trial); antifibrinolytic |

#### Step 2: Tamponade

- **Uterine massage**: Bimanual compression
- **Balloon tamponade**: Bakri balloon or condom catheter (inflate with 300–500 mL saline)
- "Tamponade test": If bleeding stops with balloon → tamponade effective; if not → surgical

#### Step 3: Surgical

| Procedure | Technique |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Uterine compression sutures** | B-Lynch suture (most common); Cho, Hayman |
| **Uterine artery ligation** | Bilateral; stepwise devascularization |
| **Internal iliac artery ligation** | Reduces pulse pressure by 85% |
| **Uterine artery embolization** | Interventional radiology; if available and patient stable |

#### Step 4: Hysterectomy

- Last resort (life-saving)
- Subtotal or total hysterectomy
- Definitive treatment especially for placenta accreta spectrum

### Massive Transfusion Protocol

- Activate early if > 1500 mL loss or ongoing hemorrhage
- **1:1:1 ratio** — PRBC : FFP : Platelets
- Target: Hb > 8 g/dL, fibrinogen > 200 mg/dL, platelets > 50,000
- Cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen < 200
- Calcium replacement (citrate in blood products chelates Ca²⁺)
- Keep warm (prevent hypothermia → lethal triad)

### Secondary PPH Management

- Antibiotics (endometritis is most common cause)
- Ultrasound to assess for retained products
- If retained products → suction evacuation
- Uterotonics as needed

## Complications

- Hypovolemic shock → multi-organ failure
- DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)
- **Sheehan syndrome**: Pituitary necrosis from severe PPH → panhypopituitarism (failure of lactation is earliest sign)
- Renal failure (acute tubular necrosis)
- ARDS (from massive transfusion/shock)
- Asherman syndrome (from aggressive curettage)
- Death
