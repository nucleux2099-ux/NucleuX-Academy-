# Maternal Adaptations in Pregnancy

## Overview

Pregnancy induces profound physiological changes in virtually every organ system to support fetal growth and prepare for delivery. These adaptations begin early in the first trimester and progressively increase, peaking in the third trimester. Understanding normal maternal adaptations is essential for differentiating physiological changes from pathology and for safe management of medical conditions in pregnancy.

## Key Points

- Blood volume increases by 40–50% (peaks at 32–34 weeks)
- Physiological anemia of pregnancy: plasma volume rises more than RBC mass
- Cardiac output increases by 30–50%; heart rate increases by 15–20 bpm
- GFR increases by 50% → lower creatinine and urea levels are normal
- Peripheral vascular resistance DECREASES (progesterone + nitric oxide)
- Respiratory: tidal volume increases (progesterone effect); functional residual capacity decreases
- Hypercoagulable state: increased fibrinogen, factors VII, VIII, X, vWF; decreased protein S

## Definition & Classification

### System-wise Adaptations Summary

| System | Key Change |
|--------|-----------|
| Cardiovascular | ↑ CO, ↑ blood volume, ↓ SVR, ↓ BP in 2nd trimester |
| Hematological | ↑ Plasma volume > ↑ RBC mass → dilutional anemia; hypercoagulable |
| Respiratory | ↑ Tidal volume, ↓ FRC; minute ventilation ↑ 40% |
| Renal | ↑ GFR 50%, ↑ renal blood flow; glycosuria/aminoaciduria may be normal |
| GI | Relaxation of LES → GERD; delayed gastric emptying; constipation |
| Endocrine | ↑ hCG, hPL, estrogen, progesterone, cortisol, thyroid-binding globulin |
| Musculoskeletal | Lordosis, relaxin → joint laxity, symphysis widening |
| Skin | Hyperpigmentation (linea nigra, melasma), striae |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Cardiovascular Adaptations

- **Blood volume**: Increases 40–50% (from ~4L to ~5.5–6L); peaks 32–34 weeks
  - Plasma volume: ↑ 45–50%
  - RBC mass: ↑ 20–30%
  - Net effect: dilutional/physiological anemia (Hb nadir at 28–34 weeks)
- **Cardiac output**: ↑ 30–50% (from ~5 to ~7 L/min)
  - ↑ Heart rate: 15–20 bpm above baseline
  - ↑ Stroke volume: mainly first half
- **Blood pressure**: Falls in 1st and 2nd trimester (nadir at 24–28 weeks); returns to pre-pregnancy by term
  - Due to ↓ SVR (progesterone, prostacyclin, NO)
- **Heart**: Displaced upward and left by gravid uterus; apex beat shifts laterally
- **Auscultation**: S3 may be physiological; ejection systolic murmur common; mammary souffle

### Hematological Adaptations

| Parameter | Change |
|-----------|--------|
| Hb | ↓ to ~11 g/dL (dilutional) |
| WBC | ↑ (up to 15,000; in labor up to 25,000) |
| Platelets | Slight ↓ (gestational thrombocytopenia if > 70,000) |
| ESR | Markedly elevated (not reliable in pregnancy) |
| Fibrinogen | ↑ 50% (300–600 mg/dL) |
| Factors VII, VIII, X, vWF | Increased |
| Protein S | Decreased |
| D-dimer | Elevated (not useful for DVT diagnosis) |

### Respiratory Adaptations

- **Progesterone** → central respiratory stimulant → ↑ tidal volume by 40%
- Minute ventilation ↑ 40% → respiratory alkalosis (PaCO₂ ~30 mmHg; compensated by renal HCO₃⁻ excretion)
- **FRC ↓ 20%** (diaphragm elevation)
- Vital capacity: unchanged
- Oxygen consumption: ↑ 20%
- Dyspnea of pregnancy: common, physiological (progesterone effect on respiratory center)

### Renal Adaptations

- **GFR ↑ 50%** → serum creatinine FALLS (normal: 0.4–0.8 mg/dL in pregnancy)
- Renal plasma flow ↑ 50–80%
- **Glycosuria**: May be physiological (↑ filtered glucose > tubular reabsorption capacity)
- **Hydroureter/hydronephrosis**: Physiological; right > left (dextrorotated uterus compresses right ureter)
- BUN ↓, uric acid ↓ (rises again in 3rd trimester; elevated in preeclampsia)

### Gastrointestinal Adaptations

- ↓ LES tone (progesterone) → GERD
- Delayed gastric emptying → nausea, aspiration risk
- ↓ Gallbladder motility → cholesterol gallstones
- Constipation (progesterone effect on smooth muscle)
- Hemorrhoids (↑ venous pressure + constipation)
- Liver: slight ↑ ALP (placental origin); albumin falls; unchanged ALT/AST

### Endocrine Adaptations

| Hormone | Change | Clinical Significance |
|---------|--------|----------------------|
| hCG | Peaks 8–10 weeks | Maintains corpus luteum; nausea |
| Progesterone | ↑ throughout | Smooth muscle relaxation; immunomodulation |
| Estrogen | ↑ throughout | Uterine growth, breast development |
| hPL | ↑ with placental mass | Anti-insulin; diabetogenic effect |
| Cortisol | ↑ (bound + free) | Insulin resistance |
| Thyroid | ↑ TBG → ↑ total T4; free T4 normal | hCG has TSH-like activity (1st trimester) |
| Insulin | ↑ production but ↑ resistance | Diabetogenic state (2nd/3rd trimester) |

### Musculoskeletal Adaptations

- **Relaxin** → ligament laxity → symphysis pubis widening, SI joint mobility
- Progressive lumbar lordosis → back pain
- Carpal tunnel syndrome (edema)
- Diastasis recti

## Clinical Features

### Normal Findings That May Mimic Disease

| Finding | Physiological Basis | Differential |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Ankle edema | ↑ Venous pressure, ↓ albumin | Preeclampsia |
| Dyspnea | ↑ Tidal volume, progesterone | PE, cardiac disease |
| Systolic murmur | ↑ CO, ↑ blood volume | Valvular disease |
| Glycosuria | ↑ GFR | GDM |
| Elevated WBC | Physiological leukocytosis | Infection |
| ↑ ALP | Placental origin | Liver disease |
| ↓ Hb | Dilutional anemia | IDA, B12/folate deficiency |

## Diagnosis

### Modified Normal Values in Pregnancy

| Parameter | Non-Pregnant | Pregnant |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Hb (g/dL) | 12–16 | ≥ 11 (WHO); nadir 28–34 wk |
| Creatinine (mg/dL) | 0.6–1.2 | 0.4–0.8 |
| PaCO₂ (mmHg) | 38–42 | ~30 |
| Fibrinogen (mg/dL) | 200–400 | 300–600 |
| Heart rate (bpm) | 60–100 | 75–115 |
| Cardiac output (L/min) | 4.5–5.5 | 6–7 |

## Management

- Know physiological ranges to avoid unnecessary investigation
- Screen for anemia: Hb at booking, 28, and 36 weeks
- Screen for GDM at 24–28 weeks (diabetogenic milieu)
- Iron + folic acid supplementation routinely
- DVT prophylaxis in high-risk (hypercoagulable state)
- GERD: lifestyle measures, antacids, PPIs if needed
- Back pain: physiotherapy, support belt
- Compression stockings for varicosities and edema

## Complications

- Failure to recognize physiological changes → unnecessary investigations/treatment
- Supine hypotension syndrome (aortocaval compression by gravid uterus in supine position)
- Venous thromboembolism (hypercoagulable state)
- Aspiration pneumonitis (Mendelson syndrome) — delayed gastric emptying + anesthesia
- Cardiomyopathy may be unmasked by increased hemodynamic demand
- Gestational diabetes (insulin resistance)
