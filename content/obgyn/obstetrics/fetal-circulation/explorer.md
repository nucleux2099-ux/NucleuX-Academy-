# Fetal Circulation

## Overview

Fetal circulation is a unique hemodynamic arrangement designed to bypass the non-functional lungs and deliver oxygenated blood from the placenta to the vital organs. It features three shunts — ductus venosus, foramen ovale, and ductus arteriosus — that close at birth during the transition to neonatal circulation. Understanding fetal circulation is essential for interpreting congenital heart defects and neonatal resuscitation.

## Key Points

- The placenta functions as the organ of gas exchange (not the lungs)
- Umbilical vein carries oxygenated blood FROM placenta TO fetus
- Three shunts: ductus venosus, foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus
- The most oxygenated blood is directed to the brain and heart
- At birth, shunts close due to increased PaO₂ and decreased pulmonary vascular resistance
- Failure of shunt closure → persistent fetal circulation / PDA / ASD

## Definition & Classification

### Three Fetal Shunts

| Shunt | Location | Function | Postnatal Remnant |
|-------|----------|----------|-------------------|
| **Ductus venosus** | Liver (bypasses hepatic sinusoids) | Shunts oxygenated blood from UV to IVC | Ligamentum venosum |
| **Foramen ovale** | Interatrial septum | R→L shunt: RA to LA | Fossa ovalis |
| **Ductus arteriosus** | Connects PA to aorta | Shunts blood away from lungs | Ligamentum arteriosum |

### Postnatal Remnants of Fetal Structures

| Fetal Structure | Adult Remnant |
|----------------|---------------|
| Umbilical vein | Ligamentum teres (round ligament of liver) |
| Umbilical arteries (distal) | Medial umbilical ligaments |
| Ductus venosus | Ligamentum venosum |
| Foramen ovale | Fossa ovalis |
| Ductus arteriosus | Ligamentum arteriosum |
| Allantois | Median umbilical ligament (urachus) |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Pathway of Fetal Circulation

1. **Placenta** → oxygenated blood (SpO₂ ~80%) enters via **umbilical vein** (single)
2. **Ductus venosus**: ~50% of UV blood bypasses liver → enters **IVC** directly
3. **IVC → Right atrium**: Mixed blood; oxygenated stream directed by Eustachian valve (crista dividens) through **foramen ovale → Left atrium**
4. **LA → LV → Ascending aorta**: Most oxygenated blood supplies **brain and coronary arteries**
5. **SVC → RA → RV → Pulmonary artery**: Deoxygenated blood from upper body
6. **Ductus arteriosus**: ~90% of PA blood shunts to **descending aorta** (bypasses lungs)
7. **Descending aorta → Umbilical arteries (×2)** → back to placenta for re-oxygenation

### Oxygen Saturations in Fetal Circulation

| Site | SpO₂ (%) |
|------|----------|
| Umbilical vein | 80 |
| IVC (after mixing) | 67 |
| Ascending aorta (post-LV) | 65 |
| Descending aorta | 58 |
| Umbilical arteries | 58 |
| Pulmonary artery | 52 |

### Why Fetal Hemoglobin (HbF) is Important

- HbF has **higher O₂ affinity** than HbA (left-shifted O₂ dissociation curve)
- Contains **2α + 2γ** globin chains (vs 2α + 2β in HbA)
- Does NOT bind 2,3-DPG effectively → holds O₂ more tightly
- Facilitates O₂ transfer across placenta from maternal HbA to fetal HbF
- HbF constitutes ~60–80% at birth; replaced by HbA by 6 months

### Regulation of Shunts

- **Ductus arteriosus patency**: Maintained by prostaglandins (PGE₂) and low PaO₂
- **Foramen ovale patency**: Maintained by higher RA pressure than LA pressure
- **Ductus venosus**: Regulated by sphincter mechanism; closes passively after cord clamping

## Clinical Features

### Transition at Birth

| Event | Mechanism | Result |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| First breath | Lung expansion → ↓ PVR | Blood flows to lungs |
| Cord clamping | Loss of low-resistance placental circuit | ↑ SVR |
| ↑ PaO₂ | Oxygen constricts ductus arteriosus | DA closure |
| ↑ LA pressure | Exceeds RA pressure | Foramen ovale closure |
| Loss of UV flow | No blood through ductus venosus | DV closure |

### Timeline of Closure

- **Foramen ovale**: Functional closure within hours; anatomical by 3–12 months (25% probe-patent in adults)
- **Ductus arteriosus**: Functional closure 10–15 hours; anatomical by 2–3 weeks
- **Ductus venosus**: Closes within days of cord clamping

## Diagnosis

### Assessment of Fetal Circulation

- **Doppler ultrasound**: Evaluates blood flow in umbilical artery, MCA, ductus venosus
- **Umbilical artery Doppler**: Assesses placental resistance; absent/reversed end-diastolic flow → compromise
- **Middle cerebral artery (MCA) Doppler**: Brain-sparing effect → low PI indicates redistribution
- **Ductus venosus Doppler**: Absent/reversed a-wave → cardiac dysfunction; predictor of adverse outcome
- **Fetal echocardiography**: Detailed cardiac anatomy and flow patterns

### Key Doppler Indices

- **Cerebroplacental ratio (CPR)** = MCA PI / UA PI; < 1 → brain sparing / redistribution
- **UA S/D ratio**: Normally decreases with gestation; elevated → ↑ placental resistance
- **DV PIV**: Elevated with reversed a-wave in severe IUGR / hydrops

## Management

### Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)

- Common in preterm infants
- Indomethacin / Ibuprofen → inhibit prostaglandins → promote closure
- If large/hemodynamically significant → surgical ligation
- **Prostaglandin E₁ (alprostadil)**: Used to KEEP DA open in duct-dependent cardiac lesions (TGA, coarctation, critical PS)

### Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of Newborn (PPHN)

- Failure of transition: persistent fetal-type circulation with R→L shunting
- Causes: MAS, sepsis, CDH, idiopathic
- Treatment: Inhaled nitric oxide (iNO), high-frequency ventilation, ECMO

### Duct-Dependent Lesions

| Dependent on PDA for: | Lesions |
|----------------------|---------|
| Pulmonary blood flow | Critical PS, pulmonary atresia, tricuspid atresia |
| Systemic blood flow | HLHS, critical coarctation, interrupted aortic arch |
| Mixing | TGA (also needs atrial septostomy) |

## Complications

- **PDA**: Heart failure, pulmonary overcirculation (preterm)
- **Persistent foramen ovale (PFO)**: Paradoxical embolism, cryptogenic stroke in adults
- **PPHN**: Severe hypoxemia, multi-organ failure
- **Duct-dependent CHD**: Collapse when DA closes → prostaglandin-dependent survival
- **Fetal anemia** (Rh disease): Hydrops from high-output cardiac failure
