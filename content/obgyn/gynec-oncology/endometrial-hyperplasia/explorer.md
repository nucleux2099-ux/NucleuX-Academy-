# Endometrial Hyperplasia

## Overview

Endometrial hyperplasia is an abnormal proliferation of the endometrial glands relative to stroma, resulting from unopposed estrogen stimulation. It represents a spectrum from benign proliferation to a precancerous condition that can progress to endometrial carcinoma, particularly when cytological atypia is present.

The WHO 2014 classification simplifies previous systems into two categories: hyperplasia without atypia and atypical hyperplasia (endometrial intraepithelial neoplasia). This distinction is clinically critical — hyperplasia without atypia has <5% risk of progression to cancer and is managed with progestins, while atypical hyperplasia has a 25-30% risk of concurrent or future endometrial cancer and is best managed with hysterectomy.

Understanding endometrial hyperplasia is essential as it bridges benign gynecology with gynecological oncology. Any condition causing unopposed estrogen — anovulation, obesity, PCOS, estrogen-only HRT, tamoxifen — can lead to hyperplasia.

## Key Points

- Caused by unopposed estrogen stimulation of endometrium
- WHO 2014: two categories — without atypia vs atypical hyperplasia
- Atypia is the most important factor determining cancer risk
- Hyperplasia without atypia: <5% cancer risk → progestins
- Atypical hyperplasia: 25-30% cancer risk → hysterectomy
- Most common presentation: abnormal uterine bleeding

## Definition & Classification

**WHO 2014 Classification (current):**
1. **Hyperplasia without atypia:** Increased gland-to-stroma ratio; no cytological atypia; benign; <5% progression risk
2. **Atypical hyperplasia / Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia (EIN):** Cytological atypia present; precancerous; 25-30% progression risk

**Previous WHO 1994 Classification (historical):**
- Simple hyperplasia without atypia (1% cancer risk)
- Complex hyperplasia without atypia (3% cancer risk)
- Simple hyperplasia with atypia (8% cancer risk)
- Complex hyperplasia with atypia (29% cancer risk)

**EIN system (Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia):**
- Molecular and morphometric-based classification
- EIN = precancer; non-EIN = benign
- Correlates with atypical hyperplasia in WHO system

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Causes of unopposed estrogen:**
- **Anovulation:** PCOS, perimenopause
- **Obesity:** Peripheral aromatization of androgens to estrone in adipose tissue
- **Exogenous estrogen:** Estrogen-only HRT without progestogen
- **Tamoxifen:** Estrogenic effect on endometrium
- **Estrogen-secreting tumors:** Granulosa cell tumor, thecoma
- **Early menarche / late menopause**
- **Nulliparity**

**Pathogenesis:**
- Continuous estrogen stimulation without cyclic progesterone opposition
- Progesterone normally induces secretory differentiation and limits proliferation
- Without progesterone → glandular proliferation → architectural crowding → eventually cytological atypia → carcinoma

**Molecular changes:**
- PTEN loss — most common genetic alteration in atypical hyperplasia and Type I endometrial cancer
- Microsatellite instability
- PAX2 loss
- These changes bridge hyperplasia to carcinoma (hyperplasia-carcinoma sequence)

## Clinical Features

- **Abnormal uterine bleeding** — most common presentation
  - Menorrhagia in premenopausal women
  - Postmenopausal bleeding
  - Irregular, unpredictable bleeding
- Often asymptomatic (incidental finding)
- May present with infertility (anovulation)
- No specific physical examination findings

## Diagnosis

**Transvaginal ultrasound:**
- Thickened endometrium
- Postmenopausal: ET >4 mm warrants biopsy
- Premenopausal: less reliable cutoff

**Endometrial sampling:**
- **Pipelle biopsy:** Office-based, first-line investigation; 90-95% sensitivity
- **Fractional curettage:** Under anesthesia; if pipelle inadequate
- **Hysteroscopy + directed biopsy:** Best method; allows direct visualization

**Histopathology determines:**
- Gland-to-stroma ratio
- Glandular crowding and complexity
- Presence or absence of cytological atypia (KEY finding)

## Management

**Hyperplasia WITHOUT atypia:**
- **First-line:** Progestins
  - LNG-IUS (Mirena) — most effective local progestin delivery
  - Oral medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA) 10-20 mg/day for 12-14 days/cycle
  - Oral norethisterone
- **Duration:** 6 months minimum
- **Follow-up:** Endometrial biopsy at 6 months
- **Address underlying cause:** Weight loss, treat PCOS
- **Regression rate:** 80-90% with progestin therapy
- **Hysterectomy:** If persistent despite progestins, or if compliance is poor

**Atypical hyperplasia:**
- **Standard treatment:** Total hysterectomy (TAH-BSO preferred)
  - 25-30% have concurrent endometrial cancer at hysterectomy
  - Thorough pathological examination of specimen essential
- **Fertility-sparing (selected cases):**
  - Young women desiring future pregnancy
  - LNG-IUS or high-dose progestins (megestrol acetate 160 mg/day)
  - Endometrial biopsy every 3 months
  - Hysterectomy recommended after completion of family
  - Close surveillance essential

## Complications & Prognosis

**Complications:**
- Progression to endometrial carcinoma
- Heavy menstrual bleeding → iron deficiency anemia
- Infertility (if anovulatory)

**Prognosis:**
- Hyperplasia without atypia: excellent; 80-90% regress with progestins
- Atypical hyperplasia: 25-30% risk of concurrent cancer; requires definitive treatment
- Early treatment prevents progression to invasive cancer
- Long-term surveillance needed if fertility-sparing approach chosen

**Progression rates (untreated):**
- Without atypia: <5% progress to cancer over 20 years
- With atypia: 25-30% progress to cancer (many already have concurrent cancer)
