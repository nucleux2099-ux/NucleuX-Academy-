# Vaginal Cancer

## Overview

Primary vaginal cancer is one of the rarest gynecological malignancies, accounting for only 1-2% of all female genital tract cancers. Most vaginal malignancies are metastatic (from cervix, endometrium, vulva, or choriocarcinoma). Squamous cell carcinoma is the most common histological type. It predominantly affects postmenopausal women (60-70 years). The vagina is more commonly a site of secondary than primary cancer.

## Key Points

- Rarest primary gynecological malignancy (1-2%)
- SCC is the most common type (80-90%)
- Posterior wall of upper third is the most common site
- HPV-related (same risk factors as cervical cancer)
- Staged **clinically** (FIGO), NOT surgically
- DES exposure in utero → clear cell adenocarcinoma in young women
- If tumor involves cervix → classified as cervical cancer; if involves vulva → vulvar cancer

## Definition & Classification

### Classification Rules
- A tumor involving the cervix = cervical cancer
- A tumor involving the vulva = vulvar cancer
- A metastatic tumor = not primary vaginal cancer
- Must be confined to vagina to be classified as primary vaginal cancer

### Histological Types
| Type | Frequency | Key Features |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Squamous cell carcinoma | 80-90% | Older women; HPV-related; upper posterior vagina |
| Adenocarcinoma | 5-10% | DES-related (clear cell); or from Gartner duct |
| Clear cell adenocarcinoma | Rare | DES exposure in utero; young women (15-25 yrs) |
| Melanoma | 3% | Second most common; distal vagina; poor prognosis |
| Sarcoma (embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma) | Rare | Children <5 years; "grape-like" — sarcoma botryoides |
| Endodermal sinus tumor | Very rare | Children |

### FIGO Staging (Clinical)

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| I | Confined to vaginal wall |
| II | Involves subvaginal tissue but not pelvic sidewall |
| III | Extends to pelvic sidewall |
| IVA | Involves bladder/rectal mucosa or extends beyond true pelvis |
| IVB | Distant metastasis |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **HPV infection** (types 16, 18) — most important for SCC
- Prior cervical intraepithelial neoplasia or cervical cancer
- Prior hysterectomy (vaginal cuff recurrence of cervical disease)
- Prior pelvic radiation
- **DES (diethylstilbestrol) exposure in utero** — clear cell adenocarcinoma
- Smoking
- Immunosuppression
- Vaginal intraepithelial neoplasia (VAIN) — precursor lesion

### DES Story
- DES was a synthetic estrogen given to pregnant women (1940s-1970s) to prevent miscarriage
- Daughters exposed in utero developed vaginal adenosis and clear cell adenocarcinoma
- Peak incidence at age 19-20 years
- Associated with vaginal adenosis (columnar epithelium in vagina), T-shaped uterus, cervical hood

### Precursor Lesion
- **VAIN (Vaginal Intraepithelial Neoplasia):** VAIN 1, 2, 3
- Similar to CIN; HPV-related
- VAIN 3 = carcinoma in situ

## Clinical Features

- **Painless vaginal bleeding** (most common symptom, especially postmenopausal)
- Vaginal discharge (watery or blood-stained)
- Dyspareunia
- Urinary symptoms (frequency, dysuria) — if anterior wall involvement
- Rectal symptoms (tenesmus, constipation) — if posterior wall involvement
- Pelvic pain (advanced disease)
- Fistulae (vesicovaginal, rectovaginal) — late

### Sarcoma Botryoides
- Embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma in children (<5 years)
- Grape-like polypoid mass protruding from vagina
- Pathognomonic: **cambium layer** (dense subepithelial zone of rhabdomyoblasts)

## Diagnosis

- **Speculum examination:** Visualize lesion; most common on posterior wall of upper third
- **Biopsy:** Punch biopsy or excisional biopsy for histological confirmation
- **Colposcopy with acetic acid:** For VAIN detection
- **Pap smear:** May detect incidentally (especially post-hysterectomy vault smear)

### Staging Workup
- Examination under anesthesia
- Cystoscopy and proctoscopy (for Stage IV assessment)
- Chest X-ray
- CT/MRI pelvis (for treatment planning, not for staging per se)
- MRI preferred for local extent assessment

## Management

### VAIN (Precancerous)
- VAIN 1: Observation (high regression rate)
- VAIN 2/3: Laser ablation, excision, topical 5-FU, or imiquimod
- Intracavitary radiation for refractory cases

### Invasive Cancer
| Stage | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| I (superficial, <0.5 cm thick) | Wide local excision or partial vaginectomy |
| I (deeper) | Radiation therapy (EBRT + brachytherapy) |
| II | Radiation therapy (primary treatment) |
| III-IVA | Chemoradiation (cisplatin-based concurrent chemo + radiation) |
| IVB | Systemic chemotherapy ± palliative radiation |

### Radiation Therapy
- **Mainstay of treatment** for most stages
- Combination of external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) and intracavitary/interstitial brachytherapy
- Better functional outcomes than radical surgery in most cases

### Surgery
- Limited role; mainly for Stage I superficial lesions
- Radical vaginectomy with reconstruction for selected cases
- Pelvic exenteration for central recurrence after radiation

### Special Situations
- **Clear cell adenocarcinoma (DES):** Radical surgery preferred in young patients (preserve ovarian function)
- **Sarcoma botryoides:** Chemotherapy (VAC regimen — vincristine, actinomycin D, cyclophosphamide) + conservative surgery
- **Melanoma:** Wide excision ± radiation; very poor prognosis

## Complications

- Radiation-related: Vaginal stenosis, cystitis, proctitis, fistulae
- Surgical: Urinary/fecal incontinence, sexual dysfunction
- Disease: Fistulae, renal failure (ureteric obstruction), hemorrhage
- Psychosexual morbidity
