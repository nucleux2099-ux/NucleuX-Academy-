# Uterine Sarcoma — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

Rare (3-7%) but aggressive uterine malignancies. LMS is most common type — arises de novo (NOT from fibroids), spreads hematogenously to lungs. Cannot be reliably diagnosed preoperatively. Morcellation is contraindicated. Treatment: TAH+BSO; lymphadenectomy not routine for LMS. Low-grade ESS is hormone-sensitive (progestins/AI). Carcinosarcoma now reclassified as metaplastic carcinoma.

## High Yield Points ★

- ★ LMS does NOT arise from leiomyoma (de novo from myometrium)
- ★ LMS diagnostic triad: atypia + ≥10 mitoses/10 HPF + coagulative necrosis
- ★ Most common site of metastasis = LUNGS (hematogenous)
- ★ Lymphadenectomy NOT routinely needed in LMS
- ★ FDA black box warning against morcellation (2014)
- ★ Low-grade ESS: JAZF1-SUZ12 fusion; ER/PR positive → hormonal therapy
- ★ Avoid tamoxifen and HRT in low-grade ESS (estrogen-dependent)
- ★ Carcinosarcoma (MMMT) = now classified as carcinoma, NOT sarcoma
- ★ Carcinosarcoma: homologous (native tissue) vs heterologous (cartilage/bone/muscle)
- ★ STUMP = smooth muscle tumor of uncertain malignant potential
- ★ Tamoxifen and prior pelvic radiation are risk factors

## Mnemonics

**LMS diagnostic triad — "MAN":** Mitoses (≥10), Atypia, Necrosis (coagulative)

**Sarcoma vs Carcinoma spread:** "Sarcoma = Stream (hematogenous/blood), Carcinoma = Channel (lymphatic)"

**Low-grade ESS treatment — "PAL":** Progestins, Aromatase inhibitors, Long follow-up

## Common MCQ Topics

1. LMS does NOT arise from leiomyoma (most tested misconception)
2. Morcellation contraindication and FDA warning
3. Route of spread: LMS → hematogenous; endometrial carcinoma → lymphatic
4. Carcinosarcoma reclassification (metaplastic carcinoma)
5. Homologous vs heterologous elements in MMMT
6. Low-grade ESS: hormone receptor positive, responds to progestins
7. Red flags suggesting sarcoma in a "fibroid"
8. Lymphadenectomy not needed for LMS
9. STUMP definition and management
10. Prior pelvic radiation as risk factor (latency 5-25 years)
