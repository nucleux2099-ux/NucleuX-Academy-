# Uterine Sarcoma

## Overview

Uterine sarcomas are rare, aggressive mesenchymal tumors of the uterus, accounting for 3-7% of all uterine malignancies. They arise from the myometrium or endometrial stroma, unlike the more common endometrial carcinomas which are epithelial. They carry a worse prognosis than endometrial carcinomas of similar stage due to early hematogenous spread.

## Key Points

- Rare (3-7% of uterine malignancies) but aggressive
- Three main types: leiomyosarcoma (LMS), endometrial stromal sarcoma (ESS), undifferentiated uterine sarcoma
- Leiomyosarcoma is the **most common** uterine sarcoma
- Hematogenous spread predominates (lungs are the most common site of metastasis)
- Cannot reliably distinguish LMS from leiomyoma preoperatively
- Morcellation of unsuspected LMS worsens prognosis (FDA black box warning)
- Poor response to radiation therapy

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Classification (2020)

#### 1. Leiomyosarcoma (LMS)
- Most common uterine sarcoma (60%)
- Arises from myometrial smooth muscle
- **Does NOT arise from pre-existing leiomyoma** (de novo)
- Criteria: ≥2 of 3 features — coagulative necrosis, diffuse moderate-severe atypia, ≥10 mitoses/10 HPF

#### 2. Endometrial Stromal Tumors
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| Endometrial stromal nodule (ESN) | Benign; well-circumscribed; no myometrial invasion |
| Low-grade ESS | Indolent; myometrial/vascular invasion; estrogen/progesterone receptor positive; JAZF1-SUZ12 fusion |
| High-grade ESS | Aggressive; YWHAE-NUTM2 fusion |
| Undifferentiated uterine sarcoma | Most aggressive; no specific differentiation |

#### 3. Adenosarcoma
- Mixed tumor: benign epithelial + malignant stromal component
- Müllerian origin
- Polypoid mass protruding through cervix
- Sarcomatous overgrowth variant has worse prognosis

#### 4. Carcinosarcoma (MMMT — Malignant Mixed Müllerian Tumor)
- Previously classified as sarcoma; NOW classified as **metaplastic carcinoma** (epithelial)
- Contains both carcinomatous and sarcomatous elements
- Homologous (uterine tissue elements) vs heterologous (cartilage, bone, skeletal muscle)
- Most aggressive uterine malignancy
- Treated as high-grade endometrial carcinoma

### Smooth Muscle Tumors of Uncertain Malignant Potential (STUMP)
- Cannot definitively classify as benign or malignant
- Close follow-up required

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Tamoxifen use** (especially for endometrial stromal sarcoma)
- Prior pelvic radiation (5-25 years latency)
- African American ethnicity (LMS)
- Age: LMS peaks at 50-55 years
- Hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell cancer (HLRCC) syndrome — fumarate hydratase mutation

### Pathophysiology
- LMS: Complex karyotype, TP53 and RB mutations, chromosomal instability
- Low-grade ESS: Chromosomal translocations (JAZF1-SUZ12)
- Hematogenous spread is early → lungs most common distant site
- Lymphatic spread is uncommon (unlike endometrial carcinoma)

## Clinical Features

- **Abnormal uterine bleeding** (most common symptom)
- Rapidly enlarging uterus (especially postmenopausal)
- Pelvic pain or pressure
- Pelvic mass
- Adenosarcoma: Polypoid mass through cervix (grape-like)

### Red Flags for Sarcoma in a "Fibroid"
- Rapid growth (especially postmenopausal)
- Postmenopausal uterine enlargement
- Pain disproportionate to size
- Irregular margins on imaging
- Central necrosis on MRI

## Diagnosis

### Imaging
- **USG:** Non-specific; cannot distinguish LMS from leiomyoma
- **MRI:** Best for preoperative assessment; irregular margins, central necrosis, high signal on DWI
- **CT chest:** For pulmonary metastases
- **PET-CT:** For staging and recurrence

### Histopathology
- Definitive diagnosis is **post-surgical** in most cases
- LMS criteria: atypia + mitoses (≥10/10 HPF) + coagulative necrosis
- Endometrial sampling may diagnose ESS or adenosarcoma but often misses LMS

### No reliable serum tumor markers
- LDH may be elevated in LMS (non-specific)

## Management

### Surgery (Mainstay)
- **Total abdominal hysterectomy ± bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy**
- Lymphadenectomy NOT routinely required (LMS spreads hematogenously)
- Lymphadenectomy may be considered in ESS and carcinosarcoma
- **Avoid morcellation** — FDA black box warning (2014)
- En bloc removal preferred

### Adjuvant Therapy
| Type | Adjuvant |
|------|----------|
| LMS | Chemotherapy (doxorubicin ± ifosfamide or gemcitabine-docetaxel); radiation controversial |
| Low-grade ESS | Hormonal therapy (progestins, aromatase inhibitors); avoid tamoxifen and HRT |
| High-grade ESS | Chemotherapy |
| Adenosarcoma | Surgery; chemo if sarcomatous overgrowth |
| Carcinosarcoma | Carboplatin-paclitaxel (treated as carcinoma) |

### Recurrent/Metastatic Disease
- LMS: Gemcitabine + docetaxel; trabectedin; pazopanib
- Low-grade ESS: Hormonal manipulation (long disease-free intervals possible)
- Immunotherapy: Limited role currently

## Complications

- Pulmonary metastases (most common distant site)
- Local recurrence
- Bowel/bladder involvement
- Complications of morcellation: peritoneal dissemination
- Treatment toxicity

## Prognosis

| Type | 5-Year Survival (All Stages) |
|------|------------------------------|
| LMS | 40-50% (Stage I: 50-70%) |
| Low-grade ESS | 80-90% |
| High-grade ESS | 40-50% |
| Undifferentiated | <30% |
| Carcinosarcoma | 30-40% |
