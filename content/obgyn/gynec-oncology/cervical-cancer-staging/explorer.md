# Cervical Cancer Staging

## Overview

Cervical cancer is one of the few gynecological cancers that is clinically staged using the FIGO (International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics) system. Unlike ovarian and endometrial cancers which are surgically staged, cervical cancer staging relies on clinical examination, often performed under anesthesia (EUA), along with basic investigations.

The 2018 FIGO revision was a landmark update that incorporated imaging (CT, MRI, PET-CT) and pathological findings (lymph node status) into the staging system for the first time. This was a major departure from the purely clinical staging used since 1958. The staging determines treatment approach and prognosis.

Accurate staging is crucial as it dictates whether a patient receives surgery (early stages) or chemoradiation (advanced stages). The cutoff between surgical and radiation management is at Stage IIB — parametrial involvement.

## Key Points

- Cervical cancer is CLINICALLY staged (FIGO system)
- 2018 FIGO update now allows imaging and lymph node pathology
- EUA is mandatory for staging
- Parametrial involvement (Stage IIB) is the critical treatment decision point
- Per rectal examination is essential for parametrial assessment
- Stage IIIC introduced in 2018 for lymph node involvement

## Definition & Classification

**FIGO 2018 Staging:**

**Stage I — Confined to cervix:**
- IA: Microscopic (diagnosed only by microscopy)
  - IA1: Stromal invasion ≤3 mm depth
  - IA2: Stromal invasion >3 mm and ≤5 mm depth
- IB: Clinically visible OR microscopic >IA2
  - IB1: ≤2 cm
  - IB2: >2 cm and ≤4 cm
  - IB3: >4 cm

**Stage II — Beyond cervix, not to pelvic wall or lower third of vagina:**
- IIA: Upper 2/3 vagina, no parametrial involvement
  - IIA1: ≤4 cm
  - IIA2: >4 cm
- IIB: Parametrial involvement (NOT reaching pelvic wall)

**Stage III — Lower third vagina and/or pelvic wall and/or hydronephrosis and/or lymph nodes:**
- IIIA: Lower third of vagina
- IIIB: Pelvic wall involvement OR hydronephrosis/non-functioning kidney
- IIIC: Lymph node involvement (NEW in 2018)
  - IIIC1: Pelvic lymph nodes
  - IIIC2: Para-aortic lymph nodes

**Stage IV — Beyond true pelvis or involving bladder/rectum mucosa:**
- IVA: Spread to adjacent pelvic organs (bladder/rectal mucosa involvement)
  - Bullous edema alone is NOT Stage IVA
- IVB: Distant metastases

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Rationale for clinical staging:**
- Cervical cancer is most prevalent in developing countries
- Surgical staging facilities not universally available
- Clinical staging allows uniform comparison worldwide
- 2018 update acknowledges modern imaging availability

**Routes of spread:**
- **Direct extension:** Parametrium → pelvic wall, vagina, bladder, rectum
- **Lymphatic:** Paracervical → obturator → internal/external iliac → common iliac → para-aortic
- **Hematogenous:** Lung, liver, bone (late)

## Clinical Features

Staging examination findings:
- **Per speculum:** Size of cervical growth, vaginal involvement
- **Per vaginal:** Cervical size, vaginal fornices, parametrial thickening
- **Per rectal (essential):** Parametrial involvement, rectal mucosal involvement, pelvic wall fixation

**Signs by stage:**
- Frozen pelvis — tumor fixed to pelvic wall (Stage IIIB)
- Leg edema — lymphatic obstruction
- Hydronephrosis — ureteric involvement (Stage IIIB)

## Diagnosis

**Permitted staging investigations (FIGO):**

*Always allowed:*
- Examination under anesthesia (EUA) — bimanual + rectal
- Punch biopsy / cone biopsy (histological confirmation)
- Chest X-ray
- IVU / IVP (intravenous urography)
- Cystoscopy (biopsy-proven mucosal involvement for Stage IVA)
- Proctoscopy (biopsy-proven mucosal involvement for Stage IVA)
- Skeletal X-rays
- Barium studies

*Now allowed (2018 update):*
- CT scan
- MRI — best for parametrial assessment and tumor measurement
- PET-CT — best for lymph node assessment
- Surgical/pathological lymph node assessment

**NOT used for staging (but useful clinically):**
- Tumor markers (SCC antigen)
- Laparoscopy findings do not change FIGO stage

## Management

**Treatment by stage:**
- Stage IA1: Cone biopsy / simple hysterectomy
- Stage IA2: Modified radical hysterectomy + LND
- Stage IB1-IIA1: Radical hysterectomy + LND OR primary RT
- Stage IB2-IIA2: Concurrent chemoradiation preferred
- Stage IIB-IVA: Concurrent chemoradiation (CCRT)
- Stage IVB: Palliative chemotherapy ± palliative RT

**The IIB watershed:**
- Stage IIB = parametrial involvement
- This is the dividing line between surgery and radiation
- Once parametrium is involved → surgery not curative → CCRT

## Complications & Prognosis

**5-year survival (approximate):**
| Stage | Survival |
|-------|----------|
| IA    | >95%     |
| IB    | 80-90%   |
| IIA   | 70-80%   |
| IIB   | 60-65%   |
| IIIA  | 40-45%   |
| IIIB  | 30-35%   |
| IVA   | 15-20%   |
| IVB   | <10%     |

**Prognostic factors:**
- Stage (most important)
- Lymph node status
- Tumor size
- Histological type (adenocarcinoma worse than SCC)
- Lymphovascular space invasion (LVSI)
- Depth of stromal invasion
