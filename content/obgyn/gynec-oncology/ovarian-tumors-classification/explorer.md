# Ovarian Tumors — Classification

## Overview

Ovarian tumors are one of the most diverse groups of neoplasms in the human body, arising from three main cell types: surface epithelium, germ cells, and sex cord-stromal cells. Classification is essential for management and prognosis. The WHO histological classification is the gold standard. Ovarian tumors can be benign, borderline (low malignant potential), or malignant.

## Key Points

- Epithelial tumors = 60-70% of all ovarian tumors, 90% of malignant ones
- Germ cell tumors are the most common in **young women and children**
- Sex cord-stromal tumors are often **hormone-producing**
- Dermoid cyst (mature cystic teratoma) is the most common benign ovarian tumor in reproductive age
- Serous cystadenoma is the most common benign epithelial tumor
- Each tumor type has characteristic tumor markers

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Classification of Ovarian Tumors

#### I. Epithelial Tumors (Surface Epithelium-Stromal)

| Type | Benign | Borderline | Malignant |
|------|--------|------------|-----------|
| Serous | Cystadenoma | Serous borderline tumor | Serous carcinoma (high-grade/low-grade) |
| Mucinous | Cystadenoma | Mucinous borderline | Mucinous carcinoma |
| Endometrioid | Endometrioid cystadenoma | Borderline | Endometrioid carcinoma |
| Clear cell | Benign clear cell | Borderline | Clear cell carcinoma |
| Brenner (Transitional) | Brenner tumor | Borderline Brenner | Malignant Brenner |
| Seromucinous | Cystadenoma | Borderline | Carcinoma |
| Undifferentiated | — | — | Undifferentiated carcinoma |

**Key Features:**
- **Serous:** Most common epithelial tumor; psammoma bodies on histology
- **Mucinous:** Largest ovarian tumors (can be >30 cm); pseudomyxoma peritonei
- **Endometrioid:** Associated with endometriosis; 15-20% have synchronous endometrial cancer
- **Clear cell:** Associated with endometriosis; poor prognosis; hobnail cells
- **Brenner:** Coffee bean/grooved nuclei; Walthard cell rests

#### II. Germ Cell Tumors

| Tumor | Key Feature |
|-------|-------------|
| Mature teratoma (dermoid cyst) | Most common; contains all 3 germ layers; Rokitansky protuberance |
| Immature teratoma | Graded I-III based on immature neural tissue |
| Dysgerminoma | Most common malignant germ cell tumor; equivalent of seminoma; LDH marker; radiosensitive |
| Yolk sac tumor (EST) | Schiller-Duval bodies; AFP marker; most common in children |
| Choriocarcinoma | β-hCG marker; hemorrhagic |
| Embryonal carcinoma | AFP + β-hCG |
| Mixed germ cell | Combination of above |
| Gonadoblastoma | In dysgenetic gonads (46,XY DSD); bilateral prophylactic gonadectomy |
| Struma ovarii | Thyroid tissue in teratoma; can cause hyperthyroidism |
| Carcinoid | Can cause carcinoid syndrome without liver metastases |

#### III. Sex Cord-Stromal Tumors

| Tumor | Key Feature |
|-------|-------------|
| Granulosa cell tumor (adult) | Most common malignant; Call-Exner bodies; estrogen-producing; inhibin marker; late recurrence |
| Granulosa cell tumor (juvenile) | Pre-pubertal girls; precocious puberty |
| Thecoma | Estrogen-producing; benign; associated with Meigs syndrome |
| Fibroma | Meigs syndrome (fibroma + ascites + pleural effusion); benign |
| Sertoli-Leydig cell tumor | Testosterone-producing; virilization; Reinke crystals |
| Gynandroblastoma | Both granulosa and Sertoli-Leydig elements |
| Steroid cell tumor | Virilization |
| Sclerosing stromal tumor | Young women; benign |

#### IV. Metastatic Tumors
- **Krukenberg tumor:** Signet ring cells; usually from GI tract (stomach); bilateral
- Other primaries: Breast, colon, appendix, endometrium

### Borderline Tumors (Low Malignant Potential)
- Epithelial proliferation WITHOUT stromal invasion
- Excellent prognosis (95% 5-year survival)
- Serous borderline is the most common
- Fertility-sparing surgery is appropriate

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Theories of Origin
1. **Incessant ovulation theory** — repeated ovulatory trauma to surface epithelium
2. **Gonadotropin theory** — excess FSH/LH stimulation
3. **Tubal origin theory** — HGSC from fallopian tube fimbrial epithelium (STIC)
4. **Endometriosis-associated** — endometrioid and clear cell carcinomas
5. **Germ cell origin** — from primordial germ cells
6. **Stromal origin** — from specialized ovarian stroma

## Clinical Features

### Based on Tumor Type
- **Epithelial:** Abdominal mass, ascites, CA-125 elevation
- **Germ cell:** Young patient, rapid growth, acute pain (torsion)
- **Granulosa cell:** Estrogen effects — menorrhagia, postmenopausal bleeding, endometrial hyperplasia/cancer, precocious puberty in children
- **Sertoli-Leydig:** Virilization — hirsutism, voice change, clitoromegaly
- **Fibroma/Thecoma:** Meigs syndrome

### Complications of Any Ovarian Tumor
- Torsion, rupture, hemorrhage, infection
- Malignant transformation (1-2% of dermoid cysts → squamous cell carcinoma)

## Diagnosis

### Tumor Markers Summary
| Marker | Tumor |
|--------|-------|
| CA-125 | Epithelial (serous) |
| CA 19-9 | Mucinous |
| AFP | Yolk sac, immature teratoma |
| β-hCG | Choriocarcinoma |
| LDH | Dysgerminoma |
| Inhibin B | Granulosa cell tumor |
| Testosterone | Sertoli-Leydig |
| HE4 | Epithelial |

### Imaging
- **USG (TVS):** First-line — assess morphology, IOTA rules
- **CT/MRI:** For characterization and staging
- **Dermoid cyst on USG:** Rokitansky protuberance, tip-of-iceberg sign, dermoid mesh

### Histopathological Hallmarks
- Psammoma bodies → Serous tumors
- Schiller-Duval bodies → Yolk sac tumor
- Call-Exner bodies → Granulosa cell tumor
- Signet ring cells → Krukenberg tumor
- Coffee bean nuclei → Brenner tumor
- Hobnail cells → Clear cell carcinoma
- Reinke crystals → Sertoli-Leydig cell tumor

## Management

### Benign Tumors
- Cystectomy (fertility preservation) or oophorectomy
- Laparoscopic approach preferred for small, benign-appearing tumors

### Borderline Tumors
- Surgical excision; fertility-sparing in young patients
- No adjuvant chemotherapy needed
- Long-term follow-up required

### Malignant Epithelial Tumors
- Complete surgical staging + debulking
- Adjuvant chemotherapy: Paclitaxel + Carboplatin

### Malignant Germ Cell Tumors
- Fertility-sparing surgery (unilateral SO) — even in advanced stage
- Adjuvant chemotherapy: BEP regimen (Bleomycin, Etoposide, Cisplatin)
- Excellent prognosis with chemotherapy

### Granulosa Cell Tumor
- Surgery (TAH + BSO preferred)
- Inhibin B for follow-up
- Late recurrence possible (10-20 years)

## Complications

- Malignant transformation of benign tumors
- Torsion (especially dermoid cysts)
- Pseudomyxoma peritonei (mucinous tumors)
- Paraneoplastic syndromes
- Recurrence
