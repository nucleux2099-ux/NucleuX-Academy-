# Cervical Cancer Screening

## Overview

Cervical cancer screening aims to detect preinvasive cervical lesions (CIN) before progression to invasive cancer. HPV-based screening is replacing cytology as the primary modality. Organized screening programs have dramatically reduced cervical cancer incidence and mortality in developed countries. India uses VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid) as a resource-appropriate screening method.

## Key Points

- Cervical cancer is the **4th most common cancer** in women worldwide; **2nd most common** in India
- **HPV 16 and 18** cause ~70% of cervical cancers
- Screening reduces cervical cancer mortality by up to **80%**
- **Pap smear** (cytology) has been the traditional screening test
- **HPV DNA testing** is more sensitive than cytology and is now preferred primary screening
- **VIA** (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid) — screen-and-treat approach in low-resource settings
- Screening begins at age **21** (ACOG) or **25** (WHO 2021)

## Definition

Cervical cancer screening is the systematic application of a test to identify preinvasive cervical lesions in asymptomatic women, allowing early treatment and prevention of invasive cancer.

### Terminology for Preinvasive Lesions
| Cytology (Bethesda) | Histology (CIN) | WHO 2-tier |
|---------------------|-----------------|------------|
| ASC-US | — | — |
| LSIL | CIN 1 | Low-grade |
| HSIL | CIN 2/3 | High-grade |
| ASC-H | CIN 2/3 possible | — |

## Etiology — Why Screen?

### Natural History of HPV and Cervical Cancer
- HPV infection → most clear spontaneously (90% within 2 years)
- Persistent high-risk HPV → CIN 1 → CIN 2 → CIN 3 → Invasive cancer
- Progression takes **10-20 years** (long preinvasive phase = ideal for screening)
- CIN 1 regresses spontaneously in ~60%; CIN 3 progresses to cancer in ~30% if untreated

### High-Risk HPV Types
- HPV 16, 18 (most oncogenic — 70% of cancers)
- Other high-risk: 31, 33, 35, 39, 45, 51, 52, 56, 58, 59, 68

## Screening Methods

### 1. Pap Smear (Cytology)
- **Conventional Pap**: Cells from SCJ spread on slide, fixed, stained (Papanicolaou)
- **Liquid-based cytology (LBC)**: ThinPrep, SurePath — fewer unsatisfactory results, allows HPV co-testing
- Sensitivity: 50-70% (per single test); Specificity: 95%+
- Bethesda System for reporting

### 2. HPV DNA Testing
- Detects high-risk HPV DNA (PCR-based: Cobas, Hybrid Capture 2)
- **More sensitive** than cytology (>90%); slightly lower specificity
- Can be used as primary screening or co-test with cytology
- Self-collection possible (increases access)

### 3. VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid)
- Apply 3-5% acetic acid to cervix → acetowhite areas = positive
- Simple, low-cost, immediate result
- **Screen-and-treat** approach (VIA + cryotherapy/thermal ablation)
- Sensitivity: 60-80%; lower specificity
- WHO recommended for low-resource settings

### 4. VILI (Visual Inspection with Lugol's Iodine)
- Schiller's test: Normal glycogenated epithelium stains brown; abnormal = non-staining (Schiller positive)
- Used as adjunct to VIA

## Screening Guidelines

### WHO 2021 Recommendations
- **Primary HPV DNA testing** starting at age 25
- Screen every 5-10 years with HPV test
- Screen-and-treat approach preferred
- VIA as alternative in settings without HPV testing

### ACOG/ACS Guidelines
| Age | Recommendation |
|-----|---------------|
| <21 | No screening |
| 21-29 | Cytology alone every 3 years |
| 30-65 | HPV + cytology co-testing every 5 years OR HPV primary every 5 years OR cytology every 3 years |
| >65 | Stop if adequate prior negative screening |
| Post-hysterectomy | Stop (if for benign cause, no CIN history) |

### India (FOGSI/Govt)
- Screening from age 30-65
- VIA-based screen-and-treat in national program
- HPV testing where available

## Diagnosis & Follow-Up

### Abnormal Screening Results
- **ASC-US**: HPV triage → if HPV+, colposcopy; if HPV−, repeat in 3 years
- **LSIL**: Colposcopy (or HPV triage if age 25-29)
- **HSIL**: Colposcopy + biopsy; expedited treatment (LEEP) acceptable
- **ASC-H**: Colposcopy
- **AGC**: Colposcopy + endocervical curettage ± endometrial sampling

### Colposcopy
- Magnified examination of cervix after acetic acid application
- Identifies acetowhite areas, mosaicism, punctation, atypical vessels
- Directed biopsy of suspicious areas
- Swede score or Reid's Colposcopic Index for grading

## Management of CIN

- **CIN 1**: Observation (most regress); treat if persistent >2 years
- **CIN 2/3**: Treatment required
  - **Ablative**: Cryotherapy, thermal ablation, CO₂ laser
  - **Excisional**: LEEP/LLETZ (most common), cold knife conization
  - LEEP preferred — provides specimen for histology
  - Cold knife conization for adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS)

## Complications

- False positives leading to unnecessary colposcopy/treatment
- Anxiety and psychological impact of abnormal results
- LEEP/conization complications: Cervical stenosis, preterm birth (cervical shortening)
- Over-treatment of regressing CIN 1 lesions
- Missed adenocarcinoma (cytology less sensitive for glandular lesions)
