# Vulvar Cancer

> 📚 **Sources:** Williams Gynecology 4th Ed; Shaw's Textbook of Gynaecology 17th Ed
> **Level:** UG | PG | SS

## Core Concept

Vulvar cancer is a relatively rare gynecologic malignancy accounting for 4-5% of female genital tract cancers. It predominantly affects elderly women and presents as a visible lesion on the vulva. Two distinct pathways exist: HPV-related (younger women, associated with VIN) and HPV-independent (older women, associated with lichen sclerosus). Squamous cell carcinoma accounts for 90% of cases, and treatment is primarily surgical.

## Key Points

- **Epidemiology**: 4-5% of gynecologic cancers; median age 65-70 years
- **Histology**: SCC (90%), Melanoma (5%), Basal cell (2%), Others
- **Two pathways**: HPV-related (VIN, younger) vs HPV-independent (lichen sclerosus, older)
- **Most common site**: Labia majora > labia minora > clitoris
- **Treatment**: Wide local excision ± inguinofemoral lymphadenectomy
- **Lymphatic drainage**: Inguinofemoral → pelvic nodes

## Risk Factors

| HPV-Related | HPV-Independent |
|-------------|-----------------|
| HPV 16, 18 infection | Lichen sclerosus |
| Cervical/vaginal dysplasia | Chronic inflammation |
| VIN (HSIL) | Squamous hyperplasia |
| Smoking | Advanced age |
| Immunosuppression | |
| Younger age (40-60) | Older age (>70) |

## Precursor Lesions

### Vulvar Intraepithelial Neoplasia (VIN)

| Old Classification | New (LAST) Classification | Features |
|-------------------|---------------------------|----------|
| VIN 1 | Low-grade SIL (LSIL) | HPV effect, regresses |
| VIN 2-3 | High-grade SIL (HSIL) | HPV-related, precancer |
| Differentiated VIN | dVIN | HPV-independent, with lichen sclerosus |

**Progression risk:**
- HSIL (uVIN): 5-10% progress to cancer
- dVIN: Higher progression rate (30-50%)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Pruritus vulvae** (most common, 70%)
- Vulvar mass or lump
- Vulvar bleeding
- Pain, burning
- Dysuria (if urethral involvement)
- Discharge
- Often delayed presentation (embarrassment)

### Signs
- Visible lesion (ulcer, nodule, warty growth, white patch)
- May be unifocal or multifocal
- Labia majora most common site
- Inguinal lymphadenopathy (if advanced)

### Lesion Characteristics
| Type | Appearance |
|------|------------|
| **Exophytic** | Cauliflower-like, warty |
| **Ulcerative** | Raised edges, necrotic center |
| **Nodular** | Firm, fixed mass |
| **Flat** | Leukoplakia, erythematous patch |

## Diagnosis

### Biopsy (Essential)
- **Punch biopsy** or **excisional biopsy** of suspicious lesion
- Multiple biopsies if multifocal
- Include margin of normal tissue

### Staging Workup
| Investigation | Purpose |
|---------------|---------|
| Examination under anesthesia | Full extent of disease |
| Colposcopy | Cervix, vagina involvement |
| Cystoscopy/Proctoscopy | If bladder/rectal involvement suspected |
| CT/MRI pelvis | Nodal assessment, local extent |
| PET-CT | Advanced disease |
| Chest X-ray | Distant metastases |

## FIGO Staging (2009)

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **IA** | Tumor ≤2 cm confined to vulva/perineum, stromal invasion ≤1 mm |
| **IB** | Tumor >2 cm OR stromal invasion >1 mm, confined to vulva/perineum |
| **II** | Tumor of any size with extension to lower urethra, lower vagina, or anus |
| **IIIA** | 1 lymph node metastasis ≥5 mm OR 1-2 metastases <5 mm |
| **IIIB** | ≥2 lymph node metastases ≥5 mm OR ≥3 metastases <5 mm |
| **IIIC** | Extracapsular spread in lymph nodes |
| **IVA** | Upper urethra, bladder mucosa, rectal mucosa, or fixed to pelvic bone |
| **IVB** | Distant metastases including pelvic lymph nodes |

## Management

### VIN (HSIL) Treatment
| Option | Indication |
|--------|------------|
| **Wide local excision** | Preferred, especially unifocal |
| **CO2 laser ablation** | Multifocal, non-suspicious areas |
| **Imiquimod 5%** | Medical option, 16 weeks |
| **Close surveillance** | Young women, small lesions |

### Invasive Cancer Treatment

#### Stage IA (Minimal Invasion)
- **Wide local excision** (1 cm margins)
- NO lymphadenectomy needed (LN metastasis risk <1%)

#### Stage IB-II
- **Radical local excision** (1-2 cm margins) OR
- **Modified radical vulvectomy**
- **Inguinofemoral lymphadenectomy** (bilateral if midline or >1 cm from midline)

#### Stage III-IV
- **Radical vulvectomy** + bilateral inguinofemoral LND
- ± **Adjuvant radiation** (positive margins, LN+)
- ± **Chemoradiation** (unresectable, bulky nodes)
- **Pelvic exenteration** (selected IVA)

### Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy
- Alternative to full inguinofemoral LND in early-stage (unifocal, <4 cm)
- Reduces lymphedema
- Negative SLN: Omit further LND
- Positive SLN: Complete inguinofemoral LND or radiation

### Radiation Therapy
| Setting | Indication |
|---------|------------|
| **Adjuvant** | Positive margins, LN metastases, extracapsular extension |
| **Primary/Neoadjuvant** | Unresectable tumor, organ preservation |
| **Chemoradiation** | Bulky disease, cisplatin-based |

### Reconstruction
- Primary closure if possible
- Skin grafts, local flaps, myocutaneous flaps for larger defects

## Vulvar Melanoma

- 5% of vulvar cancers
- Similar to cutaneous melanoma
- **Staging**: AJCC (not FIGO)
- **Treatment**: Wide local excision (margins based on Breslow depth)
- Lymphadenectomy controversial
- Poor prognosis (30-40% 5-year survival)

## Bartholin Gland Carcinoma

- Rare (<5% of vulvar cancers)
- Arises from Bartholin gland
- **Types**: Adenocarcinoma, SCC, adenoid cystic
- **Treatment**: Radical local excision + inguinofemoral LND

## Paget's Disease of Vulva

- Intraepithelial adenocarcinoma
- "Strawberry and cream" appearance
- Intense pruritus
- **20% have underlying invasive adenocarcinoma**
- Treatment: Wide local excision (margins often positive)
- High recurrence rate

## Prognosis

| Stage | 5-Year Survival |
|-------|-----------------|
| I | 90% |
| II | 80% |
| III | 50-60% |
| IV | 15-20% |

**Lymph node status is the most important prognostic factor**

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Vulvar pruritus** is the most common presenting symptom
2. **Two pathways**: HPV-related (younger) vs Lichen sclerosus-related (older)
3. **SCC accounts for 90%** of vulvar cancers
4. **Labia majora** is the most common site
5. **Stage IA**: Wide excision only (no LND needed)
6. **Sentinel lymph node biopsy**: Reduces lymphedema in early stages
7. **Inguinofemoral LND**: Required for all >Stage IA
8. **Bilateral LND**: If lesion is midline or within 1 cm of midline
9. **Lymph node status = most important prognostic factor**
10. **Paget's disease**: "Strawberry and cream"; 20% have underlying adenocarcinoma
11. **dVIN (differentiated VIN)**: Associated with lichen sclerosus; higher malignant potential
12. **HPV vaccine may reduce** HPV-related vulvar cancer in future
