# HPV Vaccination

## Overview

Human Papillomavirus (HPV) vaccination is the primary prevention strategy against cervical cancer and other HPV-related malignancies. HPV types 16 and 18 are responsible for approximately 70% of cervical cancers, while types 6 and 11 cause 90% of genital warts. Prophylactic HPV vaccines have been available since 2006 and have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in preventing HPV infection and associated precancerous lesions.

Three vaccines are available: bivalent (Cervarix), quadrivalent (Gardasil), and nonavalent (Gardasil 9). India introduced HPV vaccination in the national immunization program in 2023 with the indigenous quadrivalent vaccine Cervavac. The WHO recommends vaccination of girls aged 9-14 years before sexual debut for maximum efficacy.

HPV vaccines are prophylactic (preventive), not therapeutic — they do not treat existing HPV infections or precancerous lesions. Vaccination does not eliminate the need for cervical screening, as the vaccines do not cover all oncogenic HPV types.

## Key Points

- Prophylactic vaccines — prevent HPV infection, NOT therapeutic
- Target age: 9-14 years (before sexual debut)
- HPV 16 & 18 cause 70% of cervical cancers
- HPV 6 & 11 cause 90% of genital warts
- Nonavalent vaccine covers 9 HPV types and prevents ~90% of cervical cancers
- Vaccination does NOT replace cervical screening

## Definition & Classification

**Available vaccines:**

| Vaccine | Brand | HPV Types | Covers |
|---------|-------|-----------|--------|
| Bivalent | Cervarix (GSK) | 16, 18 | Cervical cancer (70%) |
| Quadrivalent | Gardasil (Merck) | 6, 11, 16, 18 | Cancer (70%) + warts (90%) |
| Nonavalent | Gardasil 9 (Merck) | 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58 | Cancer (~90%) + warts |
| Cervavac | SII (India) | 6, 11, 16, 18 | Quadrivalent (indigenous) |

**Vaccine composition:**
- Virus-like particles (VLPs) — L1 capsid protein
- Non-infectious (no viral DNA)
- Adjuvants: AS04 (Cervarix), AAHS (Gardasil)
- VLPs produced by recombinant DNA technology

**Dosing schedule:**
- Age 9-14 years: 2 doses (0, 6 months)
- Age 15+ years: 3 doses
  - Cervarix: 0, 1, 6 months
  - Gardasil: 0, 2, 6 months
- WHO 2022: single-dose schedule may be sufficient (emerging evidence)

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**HPV virology:**
- Double-stranded DNA virus; Papillomaviridae family
- >200 types; ~40 infect genital tract
- High-risk types: 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58
- Low-risk types: 6, 11 (warts, RRP)
- Most common HPV type in cervical cancer: HPV 16
- Most common HPV type in adenocarcinoma: HPV 18

**Mechanism of vaccine:**
- VLPs mimic native virus → strong antibody response
- Neutralizing antibodies prevent HPV attachment and entry
- Antibody levels after vaccination are 10-100x higher than natural infection
- Cell-mediated immunity also plays a role
- Cross-protection: some protection against closely related non-vaccine types

**HPV-related cancers prevented:**
- Cervical cancer
- Vulvar cancer
- Vaginal cancer
- Anal cancer
- Oropharyngeal cancer
- Penile cancer

## Clinical Features

Not applicable — this is a preventive intervention.

**Target population:**
- Primary target: Girls aged 9-14 years
- Catch-up: Women up to 26 years (some guidelines up to 45 years)
- Males: Recommended in many countries (prevents anal cancer, genital warts, oropharyngeal cancer, and reduces HPV transmission)

**Contraindications:**
- Severe allergic reaction to previous dose or vaccine component
- Pregnancy (defer vaccination; not teratogenic but precautionary)
- Acute severe febrile illness (defer)

**NOT contraindications:**
- Breastfeeding
- Immunocompromised status (can be given; may have reduced efficacy)
- Previous abnormal Pap smear
- Current HPV infection (protects against types not yet acquired)

## Management

**Vaccination in India:**
- Cervavac (SII) — quadrivalent, affordable
- Introduced in national immunization schedule 2023
- Target: girls aged 9-14 years
- 2-dose schedule (0, 6 months)

**Key counseling points:**
- Vaccination is most effective before sexual debut
- Does not treat existing infections
- Continue cervical screening even after vaccination
- Safe — no evidence of serious adverse effects in large studies
- Common side effects: injection site pain, mild fever
- No association with autoimmune diseases or infertility

**Cost-effectiveness:**
- HPV vaccination is highly cost-effective, especially in LMIC
- Single-dose schedule could further reduce costs
- Combined with screening → greatest impact on cervical cancer elimination

**WHO Global Strategy (90-70-90 targets by 2030):**
- 90% of girls vaccinated by age 15
- 70% of women screened with high-performance test by 35 and 45
- 90% of women with pre-cancer/cancer treated

## Complications & Prognosis

**Vaccine safety:**
- Excellent safety profile (>300 million doses administered globally)
- Common: injection site reactions (pain, swelling, redness)
- Uncommon: syncope (fainting) — observe for 15 minutes post-injection
- No causal link with: Guillain-Barré syndrome, autoimmune conditions, infertility
- Anaphylaxis: extremely rare

**Impact (in countries with high coverage):**
- HPV 16/18 prevalence reduced by 83%
- Genital warts reduced by 90%
- CIN 2+ reduced by 51%
- Expected reduction in cervical cancer cases within 20-30 years
