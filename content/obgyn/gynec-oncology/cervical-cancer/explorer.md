# Cervical Cancer

## Overview

Cervical cancer is the most common gynecological malignancy in developing countries and the second most common cancer in women worldwide. In India, it remains the leading cause of cancer-related deaths among women. The vast majority (99.7%) of cervical cancers are caused by persistent infection with high-risk Human Papillomavirus (HPV), particularly types 16 and 18.

The disease progresses through well-defined precancerous stages (CIN 1-3), making it uniquely amenable to screening and prevention. The Pap smear, VIA, and HPV testing have dramatically reduced cervical cancer incidence in countries with organized screening programs. HPV vaccination offers primary prevention.

Squamous cell carcinoma accounts for 80-85% of cervical cancers, with adenocarcinoma comprising most of the remainder. Treatment depends on the FIGO stage and ranges from surgery for early disease to concurrent chemoradiation for advanced stages.

## Key Points

- Most common gynecological cancer in India
- HPV 16 and 18 cause 70% of cervical cancers
- Squamous cell carcinoma is the most common histological type (80-85%)
- Clinically staged (NOT surgically staged) — FIGO staging
- Stage IB-IIA: Wertheim's radical hysterectomy OR radiation
- Stage IIB onwards: Concurrent chemoradiation (cisplatin-based)

## Definition & Classification

**Histological types:**
- **Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC):** 80-85%
  - Large cell keratinizing
  - Large cell non-keratinizing (most common subtype)
  - Small cell (worst prognosis)
- **Adenocarcinoma:** 15-20% (increasing incidence; arises from endocervical glands)
- **Adenosquamous carcinoma**
- **Small cell neuroendocrine** (rare, very aggressive)

**Precancerous lesions:**
- CIN 1 (mild dysplasia) — LSIL
- CIN 2 (moderate dysplasia) — HSIL
- CIN 3 (severe dysplasia/CIS) — HSIL

**Squamocolumnar junction (SCJ):**
- Transformation zone — where SCC arises
- Active metaplasia at SCJ is the target of HPV infection

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**HPV and cervical cancer:**
- HPV 16 — most common type in SCC
- HPV 18 — more common in adenocarcinoma
- HPV 16 & 18 together cause ~70% of cervical cancers
- E6 protein inactivates p53 (tumor suppressor)
- E7 protein inactivates Rb (retinoblastoma protein)
- Persistent infection → CIN → invasive cancer (takes 10-20 years)

**Risk factors:**
- Early onset of sexual activity
- Multiple sexual partners
- HPV infection (high-risk types: 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58)
- Immunosuppression (HIV)
- Smoking
- High parity
- Long-term OCP use (>5 years)
- Co-infection with Chlamydia or HSV-2

**Protective factors:**
- HPV vaccination
- Condom use (partial protection)
- Circumcision of male partner
- Screening and treatment of precancerous lesions

## Clinical Features

**Early stage:**
- Often asymptomatic (detected on screening)
- Post-coital bleeding (most common early symptom)
- Irregular vaginal bleeding
- Blood-stained vaginal discharge

**Advanced stage:**
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Pelvic pain
- Low back pain (sacral nerve involvement)
- Leg edema (lymphatic/venous obstruction)
- Urinary symptoms (frequency, hematuria, ureteric obstruction)
- Rectal symptoms (tenesmus, rectal bleeding)
- Renal failure (bilateral ureteric obstruction — most common cause of death)

**On examination:**
- Speculum: Growth on cervix (exophytic/ulcerative/infiltrative)
- Per vaginal: Hard, irregular cervix; parametrial involvement assessed
- Per rectal: Essential for staging — parametrial and rectal involvement

## Diagnosis

**Screening** (leads to early detection — covered in cervical-screening topic)

**Diagnostic workup:**
- **Punch biopsy:** Gold standard for diagnosis; done under colposcopic guidance
- **Colposcopy:** To identify abnormal areas for biopsy
- **Cone biopsy / LEEP:** If colposcopy is inadequate or microinvasion suspected

**Staging investigations (FIGO allows):**
- Examination under anesthesia (EUA) — mandatory
- Chest X-ray
- IVU (intravenous urography) — for ureteric obstruction
- Cystoscopy and proctoscopy (if stage III-IV suspected)
- CT/MRI/PET-CT — used clinically but NOT for official FIGO staging (2018 update now allows imaging)

**Tumor markers:**
- SCC antigen — for squamous cell carcinoma (monitoring, not diagnosis)
- CEA — may be elevated in adenocarcinoma

## Management

**Stage IA1 (microinvasive, ≤3mm depth, no LVSI):**
- Cone biopsy or simple hysterectomy
- Cone biopsy alone if fertility desired

**Stage IA2 (3-5mm depth):**
- Modified radical hysterectomy + pelvic lymphadenectomy
- Or radical trachelectomy if fertility desired

**Stage IB1-IIA1 (≤4cm):**
- Wertheim's radical hysterectomy (Type III) + bilateral pelvic lymphadenectomy
- OR primary radiation therapy (equally effective)
- Adjuvant chemoradiation if high-risk features on pathology

**Stage IB2-IIA2 (>4cm):**
- Primary concurrent chemoradiation preferred
- Or neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by surgery

**Stage IIB-IVA:**
- Concurrent chemoradiation (CCRT)
- Cisplatin 40 mg/m² weekly during EBRT
- Followed by brachytherapy (intracavitary radiation)

**Stage IVB (distant metastases):**
- Palliative chemotherapy
- Palliative radiation for symptom relief
- Bevacizumab may be added

**Radiation therapy:**
- External beam radiation therapy (EBRT) — teletherapy
- Intracavitary brachytherapy (ICBT) — using tandem and ovoids
- Point A dose: 80-85 Gy total

**Radical hysterectomy (Wertheim's):**
- Uterus + cervix + upper vagina (2 cm) + parametrium + uterosacral ligaments + pelvic lymph nodes
- Ovaries may be preserved in young women (SCC rarely metastasizes to ovaries)

## Complications & Prognosis

**Treatment complications:**
- Surgery: ureteric injury, bladder dysfunction, lymphocyst, DVT
- Radiation: radiation cystitis, proctitis, vaginal stenosis, fistulae, bone marrow suppression

**5-year survival by stage:**
- Stage I: 80-90%
- Stage II: 60-75%
- Stage III: 30-40%
- Stage IV: <15%

**Most common cause of death:** Uremia (bilateral ureteric obstruction)

**Recurrence:** Most within 2 years; pelvic exenteration for central pelvic recurrence
