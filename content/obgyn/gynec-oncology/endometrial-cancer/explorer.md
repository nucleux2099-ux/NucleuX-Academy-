# Endometrial Cancer

## Overview

Endometrial cancer is the most common gynecological malignancy in developed countries and the second most common worldwide after cervical cancer. It predominantly affects postmenopausal women, with the peak incidence between 55-65 years. Postmenopausal bleeding is the most common presenting symptom, leading to early detection in most cases.

The classic risk factor is unopposed estrogen stimulation of the endometrium. Two distinct types are recognized: Type I (estrogen-dependent, endometrioid) which accounts for 80% of cases and has a favorable prognosis, and Type II (non-estrogen-dependent, serous/clear cell) which is aggressive with poor prognosis.

Unlike cervical cancer, endometrial cancer is surgically staged (FIGO 2009). Total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy is the cornerstone of treatment. Adjuvant therapy depends on stage and risk factors.

## Key Points

- Most common gynecological cancer in developed countries
- Postmenopausal bleeding is the most common symptom
- Unopposed estrogen is the primary risk factor (Type I)
- Surgically staged (unlike cervical cancer)
- Endometrioid adenocarcinoma is the most common histological type
- Type I (80%) vs Type II (20%) — different pathogenesis and prognosis

## Definition & Classification

**Dualistic model (Bokhman):**

| Feature | Type I | Type II |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Histology | Endometrioid | Serous, clear cell |
| Percentage | 80% | 20% |
| Estrogen-dependent | Yes | No |
| Precursor | Atypical hyperplasia | Endometrial intraepithelial carcinoma (EIC) |
| Grade | Low (G1-G2) | High (G3) |
| Molecular | PTEN mutation, MSI | p53 mutation, HER2 |
| Prognosis | Good | Poor |
| Patient profile | Obese, young(er) | Thin, older, atrophic endometrium |

**Histological types:**
- Endometrioid adenocarcinoma (most common — 75-80%)
- Serous papillary carcinoma (aggressive)
- Clear cell carcinoma (aggressive)
- Mucinous carcinoma
- Squamous differentiation
- Undifferentiated carcinoma
- Carcinosarcoma (MMMT — now classified as carcinoma)

**FIGO Grading:**
- Grade 1: ≤5% solid (non-squamous) component
- Grade 2: 6-50% solid component
- Grade 3: >50% solid component

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Risk factors (Type I — estrogen-related):**
- Obesity (most important modifiable risk factor — aromatase in adipose tissue)
- Nulliparity
- Early menarche / late menopause
- Unopposed estrogen therapy (without progesterone)
- Tamoxifen use (estrogenic on endometrium)
- PCOS (anovulation → unopposed estrogen)
- Estrogen-secreting tumors (granulosa cell tumor)
- Diabetes mellitus, hypertension

**Protective factors:**
- Combined OCPs (50% risk reduction)
- Multiparity
- Progesterone/progestins
- Smoking (anti-estrogenic — reduces risk by 50%)
- Physical activity

**Genetic syndromes:**
- Lynch syndrome (HNPCC) — lifetime risk 40-60%; most common extracolonic cancer
- Cowden syndrome (PTEN mutation)

**Pathogenesis:**
- Unopposed estrogen → endometrial hyperplasia → atypical hyperplasia → endometrioid carcinoma
- Type II: arises de novo from atrophic endometrium; p53 mutation; no estrogen relationship

## Clinical Features

- **Postmenopausal bleeding** — most common presentation (90%)
- **Abnormal uterine bleeding** in premenopausal women
- Watery or blood-tinged vaginal discharge
- Pelvic pain (advanced disease)
- Pyometra (cervical stenosis in elderly)
- Enlarged uterus on examination

**Red flag:** Any postmenopausal bleeding must be investigated to rule out endometrial cancer

## Diagnosis

**Initial workup:**
- **Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS):** Endometrial thickness
  - Postmenopausal: ET >4 mm → requires biopsy
  - Premenopausal: assessed in early proliferative phase
- **Endometrial biopsy:** Pipelle aspiration (office-based, 90-95% sensitivity)
- **Fractional curettage:** If pipelle inadequate
- **Hysteroscopy + directed biopsy:** Best diagnostic method

**Staging investigations (surgical staging):**
- Chest X-ray
- CT abdomen-pelvis
- MRI — best for myometrial invasion depth assessment
- CA-125 — may be elevated in advanced disease

**FIGO Surgical Staging (2009):**
- **Stage I:** Confined to uterine corpus
  - IA: No or <50% myometrial invasion
  - IB: ≥50% myometrial invasion
- **Stage II:** Cervical stromal invasion
- **Stage III:** Local/regional spread
  - IIIA: Serosa and/or adnexal involvement
  - IIIB: Vaginal and/or parametrial involvement
  - IIIC1: Pelvic lymph node metastasis
  - IIIC2: Para-aortic lymph node metastasis
- **Stage IV:** Bladder/bowel or distant metastases
  - IVA: Bladder/bowel mucosa involvement
  - IVB: Distant metastases (including inguinal LN)

## Management

**Surgery (cornerstone):**
- Total abdominal hysterectomy + bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (TAH-BSO)
- Peritoneal washings for cytology
- Pelvic ± para-aortic lymphadenectomy (for staging)
- Omentectomy (for Type II — serous/clear cell)

**Adjuvant therapy based on risk:**
- **Low risk** (Stage IA, G1-2, endometrioid): Observation only
- **Intermediate risk** (Stage IA G3, Stage IB G1-2): Vaginal cuff brachytherapy
- **High-intermediate risk:** Pelvic EBRT ± brachytherapy
- **High risk** (Stage II-III, serous/clear cell): Pelvic EBRT + chemotherapy (carboplatin/paclitaxel)
- **Stage IVB:** Systemic chemotherapy (carboplatin/paclitaxel) ± hormonal therapy

**Fertility-preserving management (selected cases):**
- Young women with Grade 1 endometrioid, Stage IA, no myometrial invasion
- High-dose progestins (medroxyprogesterone acetate or megestrol) for 6 months
- Regular endometrial sampling during treatment
- Hysterectomy recommended after childbearing

**Hormonal therapy for recurrence:**
- Progesterone (medroxyprogesterone acetate)
- Best response in well-differentiated, PR-positive tumors

## Complications & Prognosis

**5-year survival:**
- Stage I: 85-90%
- Stage II: 70-80%
- Stage III: 40-60%
- Stage IV: 15-20%

**Prognostic factors:**
- Stage (most important)
- Grade
- Histological type (serous/clear cell → poor)
- Depth of myometrial invasion
- Lymphovascular space invasion
- Lymph node status
- Age

**Recurrence:** Usually within 3 years; vaginal cuff is the most common site of local recurrence
