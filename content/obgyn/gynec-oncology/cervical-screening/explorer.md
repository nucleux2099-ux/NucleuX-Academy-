# Cervical Screening

## Overview

Cervical screening aims to detect precancerous lesions (CIN) before they progress to invasive cancer. Cervical cancer is unique among malignancies because it has a long precancerous phase (10-20 years), allowing ample opportunity for screening and early intervention. Effective screening programs have reduced cervical cancer incidence and mortality by up to 80% in developed countries.

The three main screening methods are cytology (Pap smear), visual inspection with acetic acid (VIA), and HPV DNA testing. The WHO recommends HPV testing as the primary screening method where resources allow. In low-resource settings like rural India, VIA is widely used as a single-visit approach (screen and treat).

The Bethesda System is the standard reporting system for cervical cytology. Colposcopy is the next step for abnormal screening results, followed by biopsy for histological confirmation.

## Key Points

- Pap smear — most widely used screening test for cervical cancer
- HPV DNA testing — most sensitive screening method
- VIA — best for low-resource settings (single-visit approach)
- Bethesda System — standard reporting for Pap smear
- Screening starts at age 21 (or 3 years after sexual debut)
- Colposcopy is NOT a screening test — it's a diagnostic test

## Definition & Classification

**Screening methods:**

1. **Pap smear (cervical cytology):**
   - Conventional Pap: cells scraped from SCJ, fixed on slide
   - Liquid-based cytology (LBC): ThinPrep/SurePath — better quality, allows HPV co-testing
   - Ayre's spatula (ectocervix) + cytobrush (endocervix)
   - Sensitivity: 50-80%; Specificity: 85-95%

2. **HPV DNA testing:**
   - Detects high-risk HPV types
   - Hybrid Capture 2 (HC2) — most validated test
   - Most SENSITIVE screening method (>95%)
   - Can be done on self-collected samples

3. **VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid):**
   - 3-5% acetic acid applied to cervix
   - Acetowhite areas = positive (abnormal)
   - Results available immediately
   - Sensitivity: 70-80%; low specificity
   - Ideal for screen-and-treat in low-resource settings

4. **VILI (Visual Inspection with Lugol's Iodine):**
   - Schiller's test: normal epithelium stains brown (glycogen-rich)
   - Non-staining (Schiller-positive) areas are abnormal
   - Complementary to VIA

**Bethesda System (2014) for cervical cytology reporting:**
- **Specimen adequacy:** Satisfactory / Unsatisfactory
- **Negative for intraepithelial lesion or malignancy (NILM)**
- **Epithelial cell abnormalities:**
  - ASC-US: Atypical squamous cells of undetermined significance
  - ASC-H: Atypical squamous cells, cannot exclude HSIL
  - LSIL: Low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (= CIN 1)
  - HSIL: High-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (= CIN 2/3)
  - SCC: Squamous cell carcinoma
  - AGC: Atypical glandular cells
  - AIS: Adenocarcinoma in situ

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

**Rationale for screening:**
- HPV infection → CIN → invasive cancer takes 10-20 years
- CIN is reversible (especially CIN 1)
- Early detection and treatment prevents progression to cancer
- CIN 3 has ~30% chance of progressing to invasive cancer if untreated

**Natural history of CIN:**
| CIN Grade | Regression | Persistence | Progression to CIS | Progression to Invasion |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| CIN 1     | 60%       | 30%         | 10%                | 1%                    |
| CIN 2     | 40%       | 35%         | 20%                | 5%                    |
| CIN 3     | 33%       | 56%         | —                  | >12%                  |

## Clinical Features

Screening is done in asymptomatic women. There are no clinical features for CIN — it is entirely screen-detected.

**Who to screen:**
- Women aged 21-65 years
- Earlier if HIV-positive or immunocompromised
- Not needed if total hysterectomy (with cervix removal) for benign disease and no history of CIN

**Screening intervals:**
- Age 21-29: Pap smear every 3 years
- Age 30-65: Pap + HPV co-testing every 5 years (preferred) OR Pap alone every 3 years
- HPV primary screening every 5 years (if available)

## Diagnosis

**Management of abnormal Pap smear:**

- **ASC-US:** HPV co-testing (if HPV+: colposcopy; if HPV−: repeat in 1 year) OR repeat Pap in 6-12 months
- **LSIL:** Colposcopy (most will regress)
- **ASC-H:** Colposcopy
- **HSIL:** Colposcopy + biopsy ± LEEP
- **AGC:** Colposcopy + endocervical curettage + endometrial biopsy (if >35 years)

**Colposcopy:**
- Examination of cervix under magnification (6-40x)
- Acetic acid (3-5%) applied — acetowhite areas, punctation, mosaic pattern
- Abnormal findings → directed biopsy
- Satisfactory colposcopy: entire SCJ visualized

**Colposcopic findings (Reid's Colposcopic Index):**
- Acetowhite epithelium
- Punctation (fine or coarse)
- Mosaic pattern (fine or coarse)
- Atypical vessels (suggest invasion)

## Management

**Treatment of CIN:**

- **CIN 1:** Observation (60% regress spontaneously); follow-up with Pap/HPV at 6-12 months
- **CIN 2-3:** Treatment required
  - **Ablative:** Cryotherapy, laser ablation, cold coagulation
  - **Excisional:** LEEP/LLETZ (most common), cone biopsy (cold knife), laser conization
  - LEEP = Loop Electrosurgical Excision Procedure
  - Cone biopsy — provides specimen for histology; indicated when invasion cannot be excluded

**Screen-and-treat approach (WHO):**
- VIA positive → immediate cryotherapy (same visit)
- Reduces loss to follow-up
- Ideal for low-resource settings

**Post-treatment follow-up:**
- Pap smear and/or HPV test at 6 and 12 months
- If negative → annual screening for 3 years then routine screening

## Complications & Prognosis

**Complications of treatment:**
- LEEP/cone biopsy: Cervical stenosis, cervical incompetence (preterm birth risk), hemorrhage
- Cryotherapy: Watery discharge for 2-4 weeks
- False positives leading to unnecessary anxiety and procedures

**Prognosis:**
- CIN 1: 60% regress spontaneously
- CIN 2-3 treatment: >95% cure rate
- Regular screening reduces cervical cancer mortality by 80%
- Even one lifetime screening (at age 35-40) significantly reduces risk
