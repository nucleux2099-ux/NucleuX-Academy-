# Fetal Hydrops

## Overview
Fetal hydrops is a serious condition characterized by abnormal accumulation of fluid in two or more fetal body compartments, including ascites, pleural effusion, pericardial effusion, and skin edema. It is classified as immune or non-immune based on etiology.

Immune hydrops results from red cell alloimmunization (most commonly Rh isoimmunization), leading to fetal hemolytic anemia. Non-immune hydrops (NIHF) accounts for approximately 90% of cases today due to successful prevention of Rh disease with anti-D immunoglobulin. NIHF has diverse etiologies including cardiac, chromosomal, infectious, and hematological causes.

Hydrops fetalis carries a high perinatal mortality rate (50–98% depending on etiology and gestational age). Early detection, identification of the underlying cause, and appropriate intervention (including intrauterine transfusion for immune hydrops) can improve outcomes.

## Key Points
- Defined as fluid accumulation in ≥2 fetal body compartments
- Immune (Rh disease) vs Non-immune (90% of cases now)
- Most common cause of immune hydrops: Rh(D) alloimmunization
- Most common cause of NIHF: cardiovascular anomalies
- Diagnosis by ultrasound showing effusions + edema
- MCA-PSV Doppler is key for detecting fetal anemia non-invasively

## Definition & Classification
**Hydrops fetalis:** Abnormal fluid accumulation in ≥2 of the following:
- Ascites
- Pleural effusion
- Pericardial effusion
- Skin edema (>5 mm thickness)

**Associated:** Polyhydramnios, placentomegaly (placental thickness >4 cm)

### Immune Hydrops
- Anti-D (most common), anti-c, anti-Kell antibodies
- Mechanism: maternal IgG crosses placenta → fetal RBC hemolysis → anemia → high-output cardiac failure → hydrops

### Non-Immune Hydrops — Causes
| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Cardiovascular (20–25%) | SVT, structural defects, cardiomyopathy |
| Chromosomal (15%) | Turner syndrome (45,X), trisomy 21, 18 |
| Hematological (10%) | Alpha-thalassemia, G6PD, fetomaternal hemorrhage |
| Infections (8%) | Parvovirus B19, CMV, toxoplasma, syphilis |
| Twin-related | TTTS, acardiac twin |
| Thoracic | CCAM, CDH, chylothorax |
| Idiopathic (15–25%) | No cause identified |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
The final common pathway in hydrops is an imbalance between interstitial fluid production and lymphatic return:
- **Anemia** → ↑ cardiac output → cardiac failure → ↑ venous pressure → edema
- **Cardiac failure** → ↑ CVP → fluid transudation
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (hepatic dysfunction) → ↓ oncotic pressure
- **Lymphatic obstruction** (Turner syndrome, thoracic masses) → impaired drainage
- **Capillary leak** (infections, hypoxia) → increased permeability

## Clinical Features
- **Maternal:** Polyhydramnios, rapidly increasing fundal height, "mirror syndrome" (maternal edema mimicking fetal hydrops — rare)
- **Fetal (on ultrasound):** Ascites, pleural/pericardial effusion, scalp edema, placentomegaly, polyhydramnios
- **Neonatal:** Severe pallor, respiratory distress, hepatosplenomegaly, anasarca

## Diagnosis
### Ultrasound
- Identify fluid collections in ≥2 compartments
- Detailed fetal anatomical survey for structural anomalies
- Fetal echocardiography for cardiac causes
- MCA-PSV Doppler: peak systolic velocity >1.5 MoM suggests moderate-severe anemia

### Laboratory Workup
- Maternal blood group + antibody screen (indirect Coombs test)
- Kleihauer-Betke test (fetomaternal hemorrhage)
- TORCH titers, Parvovirus B19 IgM/IgG
- Hb electrophoresis (alpha-thalassemia)
- Karyotype (amniocentesis or cordocentesis)
- If cordocentesis: fetal Hb, blood group, reticulocyte count

## Management
### Immune Hydrops
1. **Prevention:** Anti-D immunoglobulin (300 μg) at 28 weeks and within 72 hours postpartum
2. **Monitoring:** Serial MCA-PSV Doppler every 1–2 weeks
3. **Intrauterine transfusion (IUT):** When MCA-PSV >1.5 MoM or fetal Hb <2 SD
   - O Rh-negative, cross-matched, irradiated, CMV-negative packed RBCs
   - Intravascular (cordocentesis) preferred over intraperitoneal
4. **Delivery:** Timed based on gestational age and fetal condition; typically by 37–38 weeks

### Non-Immune Hydrops
1. **Identify cause:** Detailed ultrasound, echocardiography, karyotype, infection screen
2. **Treat underlying cause:**
   - SVT → transplacental digoxin or flecainide
   - Parvovirus B19 → IUT if severe anemia
   - TTTS → laser ablation
   - Chylothorax → thoracoamniotic shunt
3. **Supportive:** Serial drainage of large effusions, antenatal steroids if preterm delivery likely
4. **Counseling:** Prognosis depends on etiology; chromosomal causes have poor prognosis

## Complications & Prognosis
- Overall perinatal mortality 50–98% for NIHF
- Immune hydrops: survival >90% with timely IUT in experienced centers
- Prognosis of NIHF depends on etiology:
  - SVT with hydrops: good if converted
  - Chromosomal: generally poor
  - Parvovirus: good with IUT
  - Idiopathic: variable
- Mirror syndrome: rare maternal complication mimicking pre-eclampsia
