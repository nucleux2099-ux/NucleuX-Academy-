# Puberty — Precocious and Delayed

## Overview

Puberty is the process of physical maturation leading to reproductive capability. Normal puberty in girls begins between 8-13 years. Precocious puberty is onset before 8 years; delayed puberty is absence of secondary sexual characteristics by 13 years or menarche by 15 years. Early recognition and management prevent compromised adult height, psychological distress, and identify underlying pathology.

## Key Points

- Normal puberty in girls: **8-13 years**; first sign is **thelarche** (breast budding)
- Tanner staging: 5 stages for breast and pubic hair development
- **Precocious puberty**: Secondary sexual characters before age 8 in girls
- **Delayed puberty**: No breast development by 13 or no menarche by 15
- GnRH-dependent (central) precocious puberty is **most common** and **idiopathic** in girls
- GnRH agonists (leuprolide) are treatment for central precocious puberty

## Definition

### Normal Puberty in Girls
- **Thelarche** (breast budding) → first sign, average age 10-11
- **Adrenarche/pubarche** → pubic hair
- **Growth spurt** → peak height velocity
- **Menarche** → average age 12-13 (2-2.5 years after thelarche)
- Sequence: Thelarche → Pubarche → Growth spurt → Menarche

### Tanner Staging (Breast)
| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| I | Prepubertal |
| II | Breast bud (thelarche) |
| III | Enlargement of breast and areola |
| IV | Areola forms secondary mound |
| V | Adult contour |

## Precocious Puberty

### Classification

#### Central (GnRH-Dependent / True / Isosexual)
- Premature activation of HPO axis
- **Idiopathic** (80-90% in girls) — most common
- CNS lesions: Hypothalamic hamartoma (most common CNS cause), tumors, hydrocephalus
- Post-infection, post-radiation, post-trauma

#### Peripheral (GnRH-Independent / Pseudo)
- Autonomous sex steroid production, HPO axis not activated
- Ovarian cysts or tumors (granulosa cell tumor)
- Adrenal tumors or CAH
- McCune-Albright syndrome (café-au-lait spots + polyostotic fibrous dysplasia + precocious puberty)
- Exogenous estrogen exposure
- Hypothyroidism (Van Wyk-Grumbach syndrome — rare)

#### Incomplete/Variants
- **Premature thelarche** — isolated breast development, no other pubertal signs, self-limiting
- **Premature adrenarche** — isolated pubic/axillary hair, benign
- **Premature menarche** — isolated vaginal bleeding, rare

### Clinical Features of Precocious Puberty
- Early breast development, pubic hair, axillary hair
- Growth acceleration with advanced bone age
- Early menarche
- Compromised final adult height (early epiphyseal closure)
- Psychological and social issues

### Diagnosis
1. **Bone age** (X-ray left wrist) — advanced in true precocious puberty
2. **Basal LH, FSH** — elevated LH:FSH ratio in central PP
3. **GnRH stimulation test** — gold standard; LH rise >5 IU/L = central PP
4. **Estradiol** — elevated
5. **Pelvic ultrasound** — ovarian and uterine size (pubertal changes)
6. **MRI brain** — mandatory in all central PP (rule out CNS lesion)
7. **17-OHP, DHEA-S** — if peripheral cause suspected
8. **Thyroid function** — rule out hypothyroidism

### Management
#### Central Precocious Puberty
- **GnRH agonists** — leuprolide (depot), triptorelin
- Mechanism: Initial stimulation then desensitization → suppresses gonadotropins
- Continue until appropriate age for puberty (age 11-12)
- Goals: Arrest progression, improve adult height, address psychosocial concerns
- Treat underlying cause if identified

#### Peripheral Precocious Puberty
- Treat underlying cause (tumor excision, etc.)
- **Aromatase inhibitors** (letrozole) — for McCune-Albright
- **Anti-androgens** (cyproterone acetate)
- **Tamoxifen** — for McCune-Albright

## Delayed Puberty

### Definition
- No breast development by age **13**
- No menarche by age **15** (primary amenorrhea)
- No menarche within **5 years** of thelarche

### Causes

#### Hypogonadotropic Hypogonadism (Low FSH/LH)
- **Constitutional delay** — most common cause overall; family history+, bone age delayed
- Functional: Anorexia, excessive exercise, chronic illness, stress
- Kallmann syndrome (anosmia + hypogonadotropic hypogonadism)
- Pituitary tumors (craniopharyngioma)
- Hyperprolactinemia

#### Hypergonadotropic Hypogonadism (High FSH/LH)
- **Turner syndrome (45,X)** — most common pathological cause in girls
- Gonadal dysgenesis
- Premature ovarian insufficiency
- Chemotherapy/radiation-induced gonadal failure
- Galactosemia

#### Eugonadism with Anatomical Causes
- Müllerian agenesis (MRKH syndrome)
- Imperforate hymen
- Transverse vaginal septum

### Diagnosis
1. **FSH, LH** — distinguish hypo- from hypergonadotropic
2. **Karyotype** — essential (Turner syndrome, Swyer)
3. **Bone age** — delayed in constitutional delay
4. **Pelvic ultrasound** — uterine/ovarian assessment
5. **MRI brain** — if hypogonadotropic (rule out pituitary/hypothalamic pathology)
6. **Prolactin, thyroid function**
7. **Estradiol**

### Management
- **Constitutional delay**: Reassurance, watchful waiting; low-dose estrogen to initiate puberty if needed
- **Hypogonadotropic**: Treat underlying cause; estrogen replacement → add progesterone later
- **Hypergonadotropic**: Estrogen-progesterone replacement (HRT); cannot restore fertility naturally
- **Turner syndrome**: Growth hormone + estrogen replacement
- **Anatomical causes**: Surgical correction

## Complications

- Compromised adult height (precocious puberty)
- Psychological distress and social difficulties
- Osteoporosis (delayed puberty with prolonged hypoestrogenism)
- Infertility
- Increased risk of abuse in precocious puberty
