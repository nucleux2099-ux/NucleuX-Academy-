# Menopause Management

## Overview

Menopause is the permanent cessation of menstruation due to loss of ovarian follicular activity, diagnosed retrospectively after 12 months of amenorrhea. Average age is 51 years (in India ~47 years). Management focuses on vasomotor symptoms, urogenital atrophy, osteoporosis prevention, and cardiovascular risk modification. Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) remains the most effective treatment for menopausal symptoms.

## Key Points

- Menopause = 12 months of amenorrhea with no other cause
- **Perimenopause** = transition period with irregular cycles and symptoms
- FSH >40 IU/L is diagnostic (but not always needed clinically)
- HRT is the **gold standard** for vasomotor symptoms
- HRT should be started within **10 years of menopause** or before age **60** (window of opportunity)
- Combined estrogen-progesterone for women with uterus; estrogen alone for hysterectomized women
- WHI trial changed HRT prescribing practices globally

## Definition

- **Menopause**: Permanent cessation of menstruation; last menstrual period (retrospective diagnosis after 12 months)
- **Premature menopause (POI)**: Menopause before age **40**
- **Early menopause**: Menopause between 40-45 years
- **Perimenopause**: Period of menstrual irregularity preceding menopause + 1 year after FMP
- **Postmenopause**: Period after confirmed menopause

### STRAW+10 Staging System
Standardized staging of reproductive aging:
- Stage -2: Early menopausal transition (variable cycle length, ≥7 days change)
- Stage -1: Late menopausal transition (≥60 days amenorrhea)
- Stage 0: Final menstrual period
- Stages +1a, +1b, +1c: Early postmenopause
- Stage +2: Late postmenopause

## Etiology

### Pathophysiology
- Depletion of ovarian follicles → decreased estrogen and inhibin B
- Loss of negative feedback → **elevated FSH and LH**
- Estradiol levels fall; androgen production continues (adrenal, stromal)
- Relative hyperandrogenism may occur

### Causes of Premature Ovarian Insufficiency
- Idiopathic (most common)
- Autoimmune (thyroid, adrenal, polyglandular)
- Genetic (Turner syndrome, Fragile X premutation)
- Iatrogenic (chemotherapy, radiation, surgery)
- Infections (mumps oophoritis)

## Clinical Features

### Vasomotor Symptoms (Most Common)
- Hot flushes (75% of women) — sudden warmth, flushing, sweating
- Night sweats and sleep disturbance
- Duration: Average 7 years, may persist >10 years

### Urogenital Symptoms
- Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia
- Recurrent UTIs, urinary urgency/frequency
- Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) — progressive, does not resolve without treatment

### Psychological Symptoms
- Mood changes, irritability, anxiety
- Decreased concentration and memory
- Decreased libido

### Long-term Consequences
- **Osteoporosis** — accelerated bone loss in first 5-7 years post-menopause
- **Cardiovascular disease** — loss of cardioprotective effect of estrogen
- **Cognitive decline** — controversial association with dementia
- Skin and hair changes (thinning, dryness)

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical diagnosis** in women >45 years with typical symptoms and amenorrhea
- **FSH >40 IU/L** (on two occasions, 4-6 weeks apart) — needed in women <45 or after hysterectomy
- Estradiol <20 pg/mL
- AMH — low/undetectable (useful in POI assessment)
- No routine investigation needed for typical presentation in appropriate age

### Investigations to Consider
- Thyroid function tests (rule out thyroid disease)
- DEXA scan — baseline bone density assessment
- Lipid profile, fasting glucose
- Endometrial assessment if abnormal bleeding

## Management

### Lifestyle Modifications
- Regular weight-bearing exercise
- Calcium (1000-1200 mg/day) and Vitamin D (800-1000 IU/day)
- Smoking cessation, alcohol moderation
- Healthy diet, weight management
- Layered clothing, cool environment for hot flushes

### Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)

#### Indications
- Moderate-severe vasomotor symptoms (primary indication)
- Premature ovarian insufficiency (essential, at least until age of natural menopause)
- Urogenital atrophy not responding to local therapy
- Osteoporosis prevention when other agents not suitable

#### Types of HRT
| Type | Indication |
|------|-----------|
| Estrogen alone | Hysterectomized women |
| Combined continuous (E+P) | Postmenopausal women with uterus |
| Combined sequential (E+cyclical P) | Perimenopausal women with uterus |
| Local vaginal estrogen | GSM/urogenital symptoms only |
| Tibolone | Synthetic steroid with E+P+A activity |

#### Routes
- Oral: Conjugated equine estrogen (CEE), estradiol
- Transdermal: Patches, gel (preferred — avoids first-pass, lower VTE risk)
- Vaginal: Cream, pessary, ring (for local symptoms)

#### Contraindications to HRT
- Undiagnosed vaginal bleeding
- Known/suspected breast cancer
- Active VTE or history of VTE
- Active liver disease
- Known thrombophilia
- Coronary heart disease, stroke

### Non-Hormonal Alternatives
- **SSRIs/SNRIs**: Paroxetine (FDA-approved for hot flushes), venlafaxine
- **Fezolinetant**: Neurokinin 3 receptor antagonist (newest)
- **Clonidine**: Modest benefit for hot flushes
- **Gabapentin**: Effective for night sweats
- **Ospemifene**: SERM for dyspareunia
- **Vaginal moisturizers and lubricants**

### Osteoporosis Prevention
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate)
- Denosumab (RANK-L inhibitor)
- SERMs (raloxifene — reduces vertebral fractures)
- Calcitonin (limited use)
- HRT (effective but not first-line for osteoporosis alone)

## Important Trials

### Women's Health Initiative (WHI) — 2002
- Combined CEE + MPA increased breast cancer, CHD, stroke, VTE
- Estrogen-alone arm: No increase in breast cancer
- Led to "lowest dose, shortest duration" principle
- Later reanalysis: Benefits outweigh risks if started <60 years or within 10 years of menopause

## Complications of HRT

- Breast cancer (small increased risk with combined HRT >5 years)
- VTE (2-3x increased risk, mainly in first year; lower with transdermal route)
- Stroke (small increased risk with oral HRT)
- Endometrial cancer (if unopposed estrogen in women with uterus)
- Gallbladder disease
