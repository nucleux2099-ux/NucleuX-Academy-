# Medical Disorders in Pregnancy — Overview

## Overview
Medical disorders complicating pregnancy represent a major cause of maternal morbidity and mortality. These include pre-existing conditions that may worsen during pregnancy and conditions that develop de novo during gestation. The physiological changes of pregnancy can unmask latent disease or exacerbate pre-existing conditions.

Common medical disorders include hypertensive disorders, diabetes mellitus, anemia, thyroid disorders, heart disease, epilepsy, renal disease, and infections. Management requires a multidisciplinary approach involving the obstetrician, physician, and relevant specialists.

Understanding the interaction between pregnancy physiology and disease pathophysiology is essential for safe maternal and fetal outcomes. Many drugs used in non-pregnant patients are contraindicated in pregnancy, necessitating careful therapeutic choices.

## Key Points
- Hypertensive disorders complicate 5–10% of all pregnancies
- Anemia is the most common medical disorder in pregnancy in India
- Gestational diabetes affects 3–14% of pregnancies
- Pre-existing heart disease carries significant mortality risk
- Thyroid disorders require dose adjustments in pregnancy
- Teratogenicity of drugs must be considered in all prescriptions

## Definition & Classification
### By Pre-existing vs Gestational
- **Pre-existing:** Chronic hypertension, type 1/2 DM, epilepsy, heart disease, SLE, renal disease
- **Gestational:** Pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, gestational thyrotoxicosis, HELLP syndrome

### Major Categories
1. **Hypertensive disorders:** Chronic HTN, gestational HTN, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia
2. **Diabetes:** Pre-gestational (Type 1, 2), gestational diabetes
3. **Hematological:** Iron deficiency anemia, megaloblastic anemia, sickle cell, ITP, VTE
4. **Thyroid:** Hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, postpartum thyroiditis
5. **Cardiac:** Rheumatic heart disease, congenital heart disease, peripartum cardiomyopathy
6. **Renal:** CKD, UTI, acute kidney injury
7. **Hepatic:** Intrahepatic cholestasis, acute fatty liver, HELLP
8. **Infections:** UTI, malaria, hepatitis B, HIV, TORCH
9. **Autoimmune:** SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome
10. **Neurological:** Epilepsy, migraine

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
Pregnancy induces profound physiological changes that interact with disease:
- **Cardiovascular:** ↑ blood volume by 40%, ↑ cardiac output, ↓ SVR — stresses diseased hearts
- **Hematological:** Dilutional anemia, hypercoagulable state — ↑ VTE risk
- **Renal:** ↑ GFR by 50%, physiological hydronephrosis — ↑ UTI risk
- **Endocrine:** Anti-insulin hormones (hPL, cortisol) → insulin resistance → GDM
- **Hepatic:** ↑ estrogen → cholestasis; HELLP from endothelial dysfunction
- **Immune:** Th2 shift — some autoimmune conditions improve (RA), others flare (SLE)

## Clinical Features
### Red Flags in Pregnancy
- BP ≥160/110 with headache/visual disturbance → severe pre-eclampsia
- Seizures → eclampsia
- Severe anemia (Hb <7) → high-output cardiac failure
- Persistent tachycardia → thyrotoxicosis or cardiac decompensation
- Jaundice + coagulopathy → HELLP or acute fatty liver
- Fever + rigors → sepsis, malaria, pyelonephritis

## Diagnosis
- **Hypertension:** BP ≥140/90 on two occasions; pre-eclampsia = HTN + proteinuria after 20 weeks
- **GDM:** 75g OGTT at 24–28 weeks (IADPSG/WHO criteria)
- **Anemia:** Hb <11 g/dL (mild), <7 g/dL (severe); peripheral smear, iron studies
- **Thyroid:** TSH + free T4 (trimester-specific ranges)
- **Heart disease:** Echocardiography, ECG
- **VTE:** D-dimer unreliable in pregnancy; Doppler US for DVT, CTPA for PE

## Management
### General Principles
- Preconception counseling for known medical disorders
- Multidisciplinary team approach
- Drug safety: avoid teratogens (warfarin, ACE inhibitors, methotrexate, valproate)
- Regular monitoring with increased visit frequency
- Timing and mode of delivery based on maternal and fetal condition

### Specific Management Highlights
| Condition | Key Drug(s) | Contraindicated |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Chronic HTN | Labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa | ACE inhibitors, ARBs |
| Pre-eclampsia | MgSO4 (seizure prophylaxis), labetalol | — |
| GDM | Insulin (first-line), metformin | Oral sulfonylureas (relative) |
| Hypothyroid | Levothyroxine (↑ dose by 30–50%) | — |
| Hyperthyroid | PTU (1st tri), methimazole (2nd/3rd tri) | Radioactive iodine |
| Epilepsy | Levetiracetam, lamotrigine | Valproate, phenytoin |
| DVT/PE | LMWH (enoxaparin) | Warfarin (1st trimester) |
| Heart disease | Depends on lesion; avoid fluid overload | — |

## Complications & Prognosis
- Uncontrolled hypertension → abruption, stroke, IUGR, stillbirth
- Uncontrolled diabetes → macrosomia, shoulder dystocia, neonatal hypoglycemia, congenital malformations
- Severe anemia → preterm birth, LBW, maternal cardiac failure
- Thyroid disease → miscarriage, preterm birth, fetal goiter
- Heart disease → maternal mortality (especially Eisenmenger, pulmonary HTN, severe AS)
- With proper management, most medical disorders have favorable outcomes
