# Vaccination in Pregnancy

## Overview
Vaccination during pregnancy is a key strategy to protect both the mother and the newborn from vaccine-preventable diseases. Maternal immunization provides passive immunity to the neonate through transplacental transfer of IgG antibodies during the third trimester.

The primary vaccines recommended during pregnancy are tetanus toxoid (TT/Td) and influenza vaccine. More recently, the Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis) vaccine has been recommended in many countries to protect neonates from pertussis in early life before they can be immunized.

Live vaccines are generally contraindicated in pregnancy due to theoretical risk to the fetus, while inactivated vaccines are considered safe. Understanding which vaccines are safe, recommended, or contraindicated is essential for clinical practice and examinations.

## Key Points
- Tetanus toxoid is the most important vaccine in pregnancy (universal recommendation)
- Inactivated vaccines are generally safe; live vaccines are contraindicated
- Tdap recommended between 27–36 weeks in each pregnancy
- Influenza (inactivated) vaccine is safe and recommended in any trimester
- COVID-19 mRNA vaccines are considered safe in pregnancy
- Rubella and varicella vaccines should be given pre-conception (live vaccines)

## Definition & Classification
### By Safety in Pregnancy
**Recommended/Safe:**
| Vaccine | Type | Timing |
|---------|------|--------|
| Tetanus Toxoid (TT/Td) | Toxoid | As per schedule |
| Tdap | Inactivated | 27–36 weeks |
| Influenza (inactivated) | Inactivated | Any trimester |
| Hepatitis B | Inactivated | If indicated |
| Pneumococcal | Inactivated | If indicated |
| COVID-19 (mRNA) | mRNA | Any trimester |

**Contraindicated (Live vaccines):**
| Vaccine | Type | Recommendation |
|---------|------|---------------|
| MMR | Live | Give 1 month before conception |
| Varicella | Live | Give 1 month before conception |
| BCG | Live | Contraindicated |
| Oral Polio (OPV) | Live | Avoid (IPV can be used if needed) |
| Yellow Fever | Live | Avoid unless high risk |
| HPV | Non-live but not studied | Defer until postpartum |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology
- **Passive immunity:** IgG crosses the placenta (especially in 3rd trimester) via FcRn receptors
- **Neonatal protection:** Maternal antibodies protect the newborn for the first 3–6 months
- **Pertussis rationale:** Neonates cannot receive DTaP until 6 weeks; maternal Tdap bridges this gap
- **Tetanus:** Prevents neonatal tetanus from contaminated umbilical cord care
- **Influenza:** Pregnant women have higher morbidity/mortality from influenza due to immunological and cardiopulmonary changes

## Clinical Features
Vaccination is a preventive measure; clinical features relate to the diseases prevented:
- **Neonatal tetanus:** Trismus, generalized spasms, high mortality
- **Pertussis in neonates:** Paroxysmal cough, apnea, can be fatal
- **Influenza in pregnancy:** Pneumonia, preterm labor, ICU admission

## Diagnosis
Not applicable — vaccination is a preventive strategy. However:
- Check vaccination status at booking visit
- Rubella IgG: check immunity; if non-immune, vaccinate postpartum
- Hepatitis B: HBsAg screening; if negative and at risk, vaccinate

## Management
### Tetanus Toxoid Schedule (India)
| Dose | Timing | Protection |
|------|--------|-----------|
| TT-1 | At first contact or early pregnancy | — |
| TT-2 | 4 weeks after TT-1 | 3 years |
| TT-Booster | In subsequent pregnancy | 5 years |
| 5 doses total | Over reproductive years | 20+ years |

- If previously immunized with 5 doses, no further doses needed

### Tdap
- One dose between 27–36 weeks (ideally 27–32 weeks) in every pregnancy
- Provides maximum antibody transfer to neonate before birth

### Influenza
- One dose of inactivated influenza vaccine in any trimester
- Especially important during flu season

### Postpartum Vaccination
- MMR if rubella non-immune
- Varicella if non-immune
- HPV if age-appropriate and not previously vaccinated

### Anti-D Immunoglobulin
- Not a vaccine but important: give to Rh-negative unsensitized mothers at 28 weeks and within 72 hours of delivery of Rh-positive baby

## Complications & Prognosis
- Inactivated vaccines: mild local reactions, low-grade fever (self-limiting)
- No evidence of teratogenicity with recommended vaccines
- Live vaccines: theoretical risk of fetal infection (hence contraindicated)
- Inadvertent rubella vaccination in pregnancy — NOT an indication for termination (no cases of CRS reported)
- Maternal vaccination programs have dramatically reduced neonatal tetanus mortality globally
