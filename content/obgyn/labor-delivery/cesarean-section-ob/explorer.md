# Cesarean Section (Obstetric)

## Overview

Cesarean section (CS) is the delivery of a fetus through surgical incisions in the abdominal wall (laparotomy) and uterine wall (hysterotomy). It is one of the most commonly performed major surgical procedures worldwide, with rates ranging from 15-35% in most developed countries. While it can be life-saving for both mother and baby, it carries significant surgical risks and implications for future pregnancies.

Understanding indications, techniques, and complications is essential for safe obstetric practice.

## Key Points

- Most common major surgery performed on women worldwide
- Ideal CS rate: 10-15% (WHO recommendation)
- Most common incision: Pfannenstiel (skin) + lower segment transverse (uterus)
- Most common indication: previous cesarean section (repeat CS)
- Most common emergency indication: fetal distress / non-reassuring fetal status
- Classical CS (vertical uterine incision) — higher rupture risk in future pregnancies
- VBAC success rate: 60-80% with proper selection
- Regional anesthesia (spinal/epidural) preferred over general anesthesia

## Definition & Classification

### Types by Timing
| Category | Definition | Decision-to-Delivery |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Category 1** | Immediate threat to life of mother/fetus | <30 minutes |
| **Category 2** | Maternal/fetal compromise, not immediately life-threatening | <75 minutes |
| **Category 3** | No compromise but early delivery needed | Planned schedule |
| **Category 4** | Elective, at a time to suit mother and team | Scheduled |

### Types by Uterine Incision
1. **Lower segment cesarean section (LSCS)** — transverse incision in lower segment (standard)
2. **Classical CS** — vertical incision in upper segment
3. **Inverted T / J incision** — extension of lower segment incision
4. **Lower segment vertical** — vertical incision confined to lower segment

## Etiology / Indications

### Maternal Indications
- Previous CS (most common overall indication)
- Cephalopelvic disproportion (CPD)
- Previous uterine surgery (myomectomy)
- Obstructive lesions (fibroids, ovarian tumors)
- Cervical carcinoma
- Previous vaginal reconstruction
- Maternal cardiac disease (some cases)
- Maternal request

### Fetal Indications
- **Non-reassuring fetal status** (most common emergency indication)
- Malpresentation (breech, transverse lie)
- Multiple pregnancy (some cases)
- Cord prolapse
- Macrosomia (estimated >4.5 kg in diabetic, >5 kg in non-diabetic)
- Fetal anomalies (hydrocephalus, sacrococcygeal teratoma)

### Uteroplacental Indications
- Placenta previa (major/complete)
- Placental abruption (with live fetus)
- Vasa previa
- Failed induction of labor
- Arrest of labor (failure to progress)

### Absolute Contraindications to Vaginal Delivery
- Complete placenta previa
- Vasa previa
- Transverse lie
- Cord prolapse with live fetus
- Prior classical CS or T-incision

## Clinical Features / Preoperative Assessment

### Preoperative Preparation
- Informed consent (risks, benefits, alternatives)
- NPO status (6 hours solids, 2 hours clear liquids for elective)
- Blood group, crossmatch, CBC
- Antacid prophylaxis (ranitidine + sodium citrate)
- Prophylactic antibiotics (Cefazolin 2g IV before skin incision)
- Thromboprophylaxis assessment
- Urinary catheter
- Fetal heart rate monitoring

### Anesthesia
- **Regional (spinal/epidural)** — preferred (safer, mother awake)
- **General anesthesia** — for emergencies, contraindications to regional
- Failed intubation is a leading cause of anesthesia-related maternal death

## Diagnosis

Not applicable as a diagnostic entity. CS is a therapeutic procedure indicated by specific obstetric conditions.

## Management / Surgical Technique

### Standard LSCS Technique
1. **Skin incision**: Pfannenstiel (transverse suprapubic) — cosmetic, stronger
2. **Rectus sheath**: transverse incision
3. **Rectus muscles**: separated in midline (not cut)
4. **Peritoneum**: opened
5. **Uterovesical fold**: identified, bladder reflected downward
6. **Uterine incision**: transverse curvilinear in lower segment
7. **Delivery**: baby delivered by fundal pressure + hand extraction
8. **Placenta**: delivered by controlled cord traction
9. **Uterine closure**: usually 2-layer closure (debated: single vs. double)
10. **Abdominal closure**: layer by layer
11. **Skin**: subcuticular suture or staples

### Classical CS Indications
- Transverse lie with back down
- Extreme prematurity (<26 weeks, poorly formed lower segment)
- Anterior placenta previa
- Dense adhesions over lower segment
- Lower segment fibroids
- Post-mortem cesarean

### Joel-Cohen Technique
- Modified approach with blunt dissection — faster, less blood loss

### Perioperative Measures
- Active management of third stage (oxytocin 5 IU IV slow)
- Antibiotic prophylaxis (single dose cefazolin)
- Thromboprophylaxis (LMWH + stockings based on risk)
- Early mobilization
- Adequate analgesia (multimodal: paracetamol + NSAID + opioid PRN)
- Early feeding (6 hours post-op)
- Catheter removal (12-24 hours)

### VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean)
- **Criteria for VBAC**: one prior low transverse CS, no other uterine scar, adequate pelvis, no contraindications
- **Success rate**: 60-80%
- **Risk of uterine rupture**: 0.5-1% with prior LSCS, 4-9% with prior classical CS
- **Contraindications**: prior classical/T-incision, prior rupture, >2 prior CS (relative), fundal myomectomy

## Complications

### Intraoperative
- Hemorrhage (average blood loss: 500-1000 mL)
- Bladder injury (most common organ injured)
- Ureteric injury
- Bowel injury
- Extension of uterine incision
- Anesthetic complications
- Amniotic fluid embolism (rare)

### Early Postoperative
- Wound infection (3-15%)
- Endometritis
- UTI
- Venous thromboembolism (DVT/PE)
- Ileus
- Wound dehiscence
- Secondary PPH

### Late Complications
- Adhesions
- Incisional hernia
- Cesarean scar ectopic pregnancy
- **Abnormal placentation in future pregnancy** (accreta spectrum)
- Uterine rupture in subsequent labor
- Chronic pelvic pain
- Niche/isthmocele formation

### Neonatal Complications
- Transient tachypnea of newborn (TTN) — most common
- Iatrogenic prematurity (if dates incorrect)
- Fetal laceration (1-2%)
- Respiratory distress (if no labor before elective CS)

### Placenta Accreta Spectrum Risk with Prior CS
| Number of Prior CS | Risk of Accreta |
|-------------------|----------------|
| 1 | 0.3% |
| 2 | 0.6% |
| 3 | 2.4% |
| 4 | 6.7% |
| ≥5 | 6.7% |
