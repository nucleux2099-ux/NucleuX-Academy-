# Stages of Labor

## Overview

Labor is the process by which the products of conception are expelled from the uterus through the birth canal. It is divided into four distinct stages, each with specific characteristics, expected durations, and management priorities. Understanding the stages of labor is fundamental to monitoring progress, identifying abnormalities, and ensuring safe delivery for both mother and baby. The partograph is the key tool for monitoring labor progress.

## Key Points

- Labor has four stages: 1st (cervical dilatation), 2nd (expulsion), 3rd (placental delivery), 4th (observation)
- First stage has latent and active phases
- Active management of labor: ARM + oxytocin if progress slow
- Partograph: essential monitoring tool, alert and action lines
- Active management of 3rd stage (AMTSL) reduces PPH
- Duration varies: nullipara longer than multipara
- Friedman's curve describes normal cervical dilatation pattern

## Definition & Classification

### The Four Stages of Labor

| Stage | Definition | Duration (Nullipara) | Duration (Multipara) |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **1st** | Onset of true labor → full cervical dilatation (10 cm) | 12-16 hours | 6-8 hours |
| **2nd** | Full dilatation → delivery of baby | 1-2 hours | 15-45 min |
| **3rd** | Delivery of baby → delivery of placenta | 15-30 min | 5-15 min |
| **4th** | Delivery of placenta → 1-2 hours postpartum | 1-2 hours | 1-2 hours |

### First Stage Subdivisions
| Phase | Cervical Dilatation | Duration | Rate |
|-------|-------------------|----------|------|
| **Latent** | 0-4 cm | Up to 20 hours (nullipara) | Slow, variable |
| **Active** | 4-10 cm | ~6 hours (nullipara) | ≥1 cm/hour |

### Friedman's Curve (Active Phase)
- Acceleration phase (4-5 cm)
- Phase of maximum slope (5-8 cm) — 3 cm/hr nullipara, 5 cm/hr multipara
- Deceleration phase (8-10 cm)
- *Note: WHO/ACOG now use simplified criteria — active labor from 6 cm*

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Onset of Labor — Theories
1. **Progesterone withdrawal**: functional withdrawal → uterine contractility
2. **Oxytocin stimulation**: increased oxytocin receptors → coordinated contractions
3. **Prostaglandin release**: PGE2 and PGF2α → cervical ripening + contractions
4. **Fetal HPA axis**: fetal cortisol → placental CRH → prostaglandins
5. **Mechanical stretch**: uterine distension → gap junctions → coordinated activity
6. **Inflammatory pathway**: cervical remodeling via inflammatory mediators

### True vs False Labor
| Feature | True Labor | False Labor (Braxton-Hicks) |
|---------|------------|---------------------------|
| Contractions | Regular, increasing | Irregular |
| Interval | Decreasing | Variable |
| Intensity | Increasing | Stays same |
| Cervical change | Progressive dilatation/effacement | None |
| Location | Back → abdomen | Front only |
| Sedation effect | No relief | May stop |

### Uterine Contractions in Labor
- Frequency: 3-5 per 10 minutes in active labor
- Duration: 45-60 seconds
- Intensity: 40-60 mmHg above resting tone
- Resting tone: 8-12 mmHg
- **Polarity**: fundal dominance — contractions strongest at fundus

## Clinical Features

### First Stage of Labor

#### Latent Phase (0-4 cm)
- Irregular to regular contractions
- Cervical effacement and early dilatation
- Mucus plug/bloody show may occur
- Management: supportive care, hydration, mobilization
- Prolonged latent phase: >20 hours (nullipara), >14 hours (multipara)

#### Active Phase (4-10 cm)
- Regular, strong contractions (3-5 in 10 min)
- Progressive cervical dilatation ≥1 cm/hour
- Descent of presenting part
- Monitoring: partograph, CTG, maternal vitals q1-2h
- **Abnormalities**:
  - Protraction: slow progress (<1 cm/hr)
  - Arrest: no progress for ≥4 hours with adequate contractions (or ≥6 hours with inadequate)

### Second Stage of Labor

#### Passive Phase
- Full dilatation but no urge to push
- Head descending through pelvis
- Allow descent before active pushing (especially with epidural)

#### Active Phase
- Maternal bearing down (pushing) with contractions
- Visible descent of presenting part
- Crowning → delivery
- **Management**: coached pushing, perineal support, controlled delivery of head
- **Prolonged**: >3 hours with epidural, >2 hours without (nullipara)

### Third Stage of Labor

#### Signs of Placental Separation
1. **Gush of blood** from vagina
2. **Cord lengthening** (cord appears to advance)
3. **Uterine fundus rises** and becomes globular and firm
4. **Suprapubic bulge** (placenta in lower segment)

#### Mechanisms of Separation
- **Schultze mechanism** (80%): fetal surface first (shiny), retroplacental hematoma pushes center
- **Matthews Duncan mechanism** (20%): maternal surface first (rough, dirty), slides out laterally

### Fourth Stage of Labor
- Close observation for PPH
- Monitor: uterine tone, vital signs, bleeding, perineal repair
- Early breastfeeding and skin-to-skin contact
- Bladder emptying encouraged

## Diagnosis

### Diagnosis of Labor Onset
- Regular painful contractions + progressive cervical change
- Cervix ≥4 cm with regular contractions = active labor

### Partograph
- WHO partograph: graphical recording of labor progress
- **Alert line**: expected progress (1 cm/hr from 4 cm)
- **Action line**: 4 hours to the right of alert line
- Crossing action line = intervention needed (augmentation/CS)
- Records: cervical dilatation, descent, contractions, FHR, maternal vitals, urine output

### Monitoring Parameters
| Parameter | Frequency (1st stage) | Frequency (2nd stage) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| FHR | Every 15-30 min (intermittent) | Every 5 min |
| Contractions | Every 30 min | Continuous |
| Maternal BP/pulse | Every 1-2 hours | Every 15-30 min |
| Vaginal exam | Every 4 hours | As needed |
| Temperature | Every 4 hours | — |

## Management

### Active Management of Labor (O'Driscoll Protocol)
1. Strict diagnosis of labor
2. Early amniotomy (ARM) if not already ruptured
3. Oxytocin augmentation if progress <1 cm/hour
4. One-to-one midwifery support
5. Aim: delivery within 12 hours

### Management by Stage

#### First Stage
- Supportive care: hydration, nutrition, mobility, pain relief
- Monitoring: partograph, fetal heart, maternal vitals
- Pain relief: non-pharmacological → Entonox → epidural
- ARM if indicated
- Oxytocin if inadequate progress (with ruptured membranes)

#### Second Stage
- Position of choice (upright, lateral, lithotomy)
- Directed pushing with contractions
- Perineal support and controlled head delivery
- Episiotomy if indicated
- Hands on vs hands poised for head delivery

#### Third Stage — Active Management (AMTSL)
1. **Uterotonic** within 1 minute of delivery (Oxytocin 10 IU IM — 1st line)
2. **Controlled cord traction** (Brandt-Andrews maneuver)
3. **Uterine massage** after placental delivery
- Reduces PPH by 60%
- Physiological management: allow spontaneous placental separation (longer, more bleeding)

#### Fourth Stage
- Monitor uterine tone every 15 minutes × 2 hours
- Check vital signs
- Assess bleeding
- Encourage breastfeeding
- Repair episiotomy/tears

## Complications

### First Stage
- Prolonged latent phase
- Protraction/arrest disorders
- Cord prolapse (with ARM)
- Fetal distress
- Chorioamnionitis

### Second Stage
- Prolonged second stage → maternal exhaustion
- Perineal trauma
- Shoulder dystocia
- Fetal distress → operative delivery
- Cord complications

### Third Stage
- **Postpartum hemorrhage** (most dangerous)
- Retained placenta
- Uterine inversion
- Genital tract trauma

### Fourth Stage
- Secondary PPH
- Uterine atony
- Perineal hematoma
- Urinary retention
- Eclampsia (can occur postpartum)
