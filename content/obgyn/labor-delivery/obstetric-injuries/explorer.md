# Obstetric Injuries

## Overview

Obstetric injuries encompass the spectrum of trauma sustained by the mother during childbirth, affecting the genital tract, pelvic floor, and surrounding structures. These injuries range from minor perineal tears to life-threatening uterine rupture. Early recognition and appropriate management are critical to prevent long-term morbidity including urinary/fecal incontinence, pelvic organ prolapse, and fistula formation.

Birth canal injuries are a significant cause of postpartum hemorrhage and must be systematically excluded after every delivery.

## Key Points

- Birth canal injuries are the second most common cause of PPH (after uterine atony)
- Systematic examination of birth canal mandatory after every delivery
- Cervical tears most commonly at 3 o'clock and 9 o'clock positions
- Third/fourth degree perineal tears require repair by experienced surgeon in OT
- Genital tract fistulae: devastating complication of obstructed labor
- Pelvic floor damage may manifest years later as prolapse/incontinence
- Risk factors: operative delivery, macrosomia, prolonged labor, precipitate labor

## Definition & Classification

### Classification of Obstetric Injuries by Site

#### 1. Vulvar and Vaginal Injuries
- Perineal tears (1st to 4th degree)
- Vaginal wall lacerations
- Vulvar/vaginal hematoma
- Labial tears

#### 2. Cervical Injuries
- Cervical lacerations/tears
- Annular detachment of cervix (rare)

#### 3. Uterine Injuries
- Uterine rupture
- Uterine inversion
- Broad ligament hematoma

#### 4. Pelvic Floor Injuries
- Levator ani damage
- Pubocervical fascia injury
- Nerve injuries (pudendal, obturator)

#### 5. Urinary Tract Injuries
- Bladder injury
- Urethral injury
- Vesicovaginal fistula

#### 6. Bowel Injuries
- Rectovaginal fistula
- Rectal injury

### Perineal Tear Classification
| Degree | Structure Involved |
|--------|-------------------|
| 1st | Perineal skin and vaginal mucosa |
| 2nd | Perineal muscles (bulbospongiosus, transverse perineal) |
| 3rd | Anal sphincter complex (3a: <50% EAS; 3b: >50% EAS; 3c: IAS) |
| 4th | Anal/rectal epithelium |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors for Obstetric Injury
**Maternal factors:**
- Primigravida
- Rigid perineum (elderly primigravida)
- Short perineum
- Previous scarring
- Tissue edema
- Vulvar varicosities

**Fetal factors:**
- Macrosomia (>4 kg)
- Malpresentation (face, brow, OP)
- Shoulder dystocia
- Congenital anomalies (hydrocephalus)

**Labor factors:**
- Prolonged labor / obstructed labor
- Precipitate labor (rapid, uncontrolled delivery)
- Operative vaginal delivery (forceps > vacuum)
- Inadequate episiotomy
- Oxytocin augmentation (tumultuous contractions)

**Iatrogenic:**
- Forceps application
- Destructive operations
- Internal podalic version
- Manual removal of placenta

## Clinical Features

### Perineal Tears
- Visible tear on perineal inspection
- Bleeding from wound
- Must assess depth by systematic examination
- Rectal exam mandatory to exclude sphincter injury

### Cervical Tears
- PPH despite well-contracted uterus (key clinical clue)
- Bright red bleeding
- Diagnosed on speculum/ring forceps examination
- Most common at 3 and 9 o'clock (lateral angles)
- Can extend to lower uterine segment

### Vaginal Hematoma
- **Vulvar hematoma**: visible, painful swelling of vulva
- **Paravaginal hematoma**: may be concealed; presents with perineal pain, pressure, urinary retention
- **Broad ligament hematoma**: presents with shock out of proportion to visible bleeding
- Expanding hematoma = surgical emergency

### Pelvic Floor Injury
- May be occult at delivery
- Long-term: stress urinary incontinence, pelvic organ prolapse, fecal incontinence
- Pudendal nerve injury: perineal numbness, incontinence

### Vesicovaginal Fistula (VVF)
- Continuous urinary leakage from vagina
- Develops 7-14 days after obstructed labor
- Due to pressure necrosis of bladder base
- Devastating social consequences (especially in developing countries)

## Diagnosis

### Systematic Post-Delivery Examination
1. **Inspect vulva and perineum** — tears, hematoma
2. **Vaginal walls** — lacerations (use retractors, good lighting)
3. **Cervix** — examine with ring forceps (sponge forceps on anterior and posterior lips, inspect circumference)
4. **Rectal examination** — mandatory to exclude 3rd/4th degree tears
5. **Uterus** — check tone and integrity

### Investigation
- Hemoglobin if significant blood loss
- Coagulation profile if ongoing hemorrhage
- Ultrasound for concealed hematoma
- CT/MRI for broad ligament or retroperitoneal hematoma
- Dye test for suspected fistula

## Management

### Perineal Tears
| Degree | Management |
|--------|-----------|
| 1st | May heal spontaneously; suture if bleeding |
| 2nd | Layered repair with absorbable suture (Vicryl) |
| 3rd/4th | Repair in OT under regional/GA by experienced surgeon |

### 3rd/4th Degree Tear Repair
- OT setting, regional/general anesthesia
- Identify and repair rectal mucosa first (4th degree) — interrupted Vicryl
- Internal anal sphincter: repair with interrupted mattress sutures (PDS/Vicryl)
- External anal sphincter: end-to-end or overlap technique
- Postop: broad-spectrum antibiotics (metronidazole + cephalosporin), laxatives ×2 weeks
- Follow-up in dedicated perineal clinic

### Cervical Tears
- Grasp cervix with ring forceps
- Identify apex of tear
- Suture from apex downward with continuous absorbable suture
- If extends to lower segment → may need laparotomy

### Vaginal Hematoma Management
| Type | Small | Large/Expanding |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| Vulvar | Ice packs, observe | Incision, drainage, ligation, pack |
| Paravaginal | Observe if stable | Surgical drainage, pack vagina |
| Broad ligament | — | Laparotomy, may need internal iliac ligation/embolization |

### Vesicovaginal Fistula
- Small: continuous catheter drainage ×4-6 weeks (may heal spontaneously)
- Large: surgical repair after 3-6 months (tissue inflammation subsides)
- O'Connor technique or Latzko technique

### Pelvic Floor Rehabilitation
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises)
- Physiotherapy
- Biofeedback
- Surgery for severe prolapse/incontinence (delayed)

## Complications

### Immediate
- Postpartum hemorrhage (birth canal trauma = 2nd most common cause)
- Hemorrhagic shock
- Infection

### Short-term
- Wound infection/dehiscence
- Secondary PPH
- Urinary retention
- Perineal pain
- Dyspareunia

### Long-term
- **Urinary incontinence** (stress incontinence — pudendal nerve damage)
- **Fecal/flatal incontinence** (anal sphincter damage)
- **Pelvic organ prolapse** (cystocele, rectocele, uterine prolapse)
- **Vesicovaginal fistula** / rectovaginal fistula
- **Dyspareunia**
- **Psychological trauma** (fear of future deliveries — tocophobia)
