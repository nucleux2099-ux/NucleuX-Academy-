# Uterine Inversion

## Overview

Uterine inversion is a rare but life-threatening obstetric emergency where the uterine fundus collapses into the endometrial cavity and may protrude through the cervix or even outside the vulva. It occurs in approximately 1 in 2,000-20,000 deliveries. The condition causes severe hemorrhage and neurogenic shock (due to traction on peritoneal structures and ovaries) and requires immediate recognition and management. Delay in treatment significantly increases morbidity and mortality.

## Key Points

- Rare but life-threatening — mortality up to 15% if untreated
- Most common cause: mismanaged third stage (excessive cord traction)
- Characterized by hemorrhage + shock (neurogenic + hypovolemic)
- Shock often out of proportion to visible blood loss (neurogenic component)
- Immediate manual replacement is the definitive treatment
- Do NOT remove placenta before replacement (increases bleeding)
- Johnson's maneuver: push fundus through cervix back to normal position
- O'Sullivan's hydrostatic method: alternative if manual replacement fails

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Inversion of the uterus where the fundus turns inside out, partially or completely, through the cervix.

### Classification by Degree
| Degree | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **1st (Incomplete)** | Fundus reaches internal os but does not protrude through cervix |
| **2nd (Complete)** | Fundus protrudes through cervix into vagina |
| **3rd (Prolapsed)** | Fundus protrudes outside the vulva |
| **4th (Total)** | Vagina also inverted along with uterus |

### Classification by Timing
| Type | Timing |
|------|--------|
| **Acute** | Within 24 hours of delivery (most common) |
| **Subacute** | 24 hours to 4 weeks |
| **Chronic** | >4 weeks postpartum |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
1. **Mismanaged third stage** (most common):
   - Excessive cord traction before placental separation
   - Fundal pressure (Credé's maneuver) on relaxed uterus
   - Cord traction with fundal placenta
2. **Short umbilical cord**
3. **Fundal attachment of placenta** (especially placenta accreta)
4. **Uterine atony** (relaxed uterus)
5. **Connective tissue disorders** (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos)
6. **Rapid delivery**
7. **Multiparity** (relaxed uterine musculature)

### Pathophysiology
- Traction on cord → fundus dimples → inverts progressively
- Once fundus inverts through cervix → cervix contracts around inverted fundus (constriction ring)
- Constriction ring makes replacement increasingly difficult with time
- Peritoneal traction → **neurogenic (parasympathetic) shock** — bradycardia, hypotension
- Hemorrhage → **hypovolemic shock**
- Combined shock mechanism → severity out of proportion to blood loss

## Clinical Features

### Classic Triad
1. **Hemorrhage** (massive PPH)
2. **Shock** (out of proportion to visible blood loss)
3. **Severe lower abdominal pain**

### Signs
- **Abdominal**: fundus not palpable at usual location (dimple or cup-shaped depression)
- **Vaginal**: mass felt in vagina or visible at vulva (if 2nd/3rd degree)
- **Hemorrhage**: active bleeding, may be profuse
- **Shock**: pallor, tachycardia (or bradycardia in neurogenic shock), hypotension, collapse

### Key Diagnostic Feature
- **Fundus not palpable abdominally** + mass in vagina/at vulva + hemorrhage + shock

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis (primarily clinical — no time for imaging)
- Inability to palpate fundus abdominally
- Smooth mass felt in vagina or at vulva
- Hemorrhage with shock
- Placenta may still be attached

### Differential Diagnosis
- Uterine atony with PPH
- Cervical/vaginal mass (fibroid polyp)
- Prolapsed uterus
- Large cervical tear

## Management

### Immediate Resuscitation (Simultaneous with Replacement)
- **Call for help** — obstetric emergency
- Two large-bore IV cannulae
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation (crystalloid → blood)
- Crossmatch blood urgently
- **Avoid ergometrine/oxytocin** until after replacement (will cause cervical constriction)
- Atropine 0.6 mg IV if bradycardia (neurogenic shock)

### Step 1: Manual Replacement (Johnson's Maneuver)
1. **Do NOT remove placenta** if still attached (increases hemorrhage)
2. Grasp the fundus with palm of hand
3. Push the fundus through the cervix, directing it toward the umbilicus
4. Push the last part that inverted first (reverse the inversion)
5. Once replaced, maintain hand in uterus as a fist for 3-5 minutes
6. Start oxytocin infusion AFTER replacement to maintain tone
7. Then remove placenta

### Step 2: If Manual Replacement Fails — Uterine Relaxation
- **General anesthesia with halothane** (relaxes uterus) — then reattempt
- **MgSO4** 4g IV
- **Terbutaline** 0.25 mg IV/SC
- **Nitroglycerin** 50-200 μg IV (rapid onset)
- After relaxation → reattempt manual replacement

### Step 3: O'Sullivan's Hydrostatic Method
- Patient in lithotomy with Trendelenburg
- Seal vulva with hand or silastic ventouse cup
- Infuse warm saline (2-5 liters) into vagina
- Hydrostatic pressure distends vagina and pushes fundus back
- Effective and can be life-saving

### Step 4: Surgical Options (if all above fail)
- **Huntington's operation** (abdominal approach):
  - Laparotomy → identify cup of inversion
  - Grasp round ligaments with Allis forceps
  - Apply gentle upward traction to progressively pull fundus out
- **Haultain's operation** (abdominal approach):
  - Incision through posterior cervical ring (constriction ring)
  - Then manual reposition
  - Repair the incision
- **Spinelli's operation** (vaginal approach):
  - Anterior colpotomy → incise cervical ring anteriorly
  - Reposition uterus
  - Less commonly performed

### Post-Replacement Care
- Oxytocin infusion (40 IU in 500 mL NS over 4 hours)
- Antibiotics (broad-spectrum)
- Monitor for re-inversion (hand in uterus or intrauterine balloon)
- Blood transfusion as needed
- Monitor in ICU/HDU
- Check for uterine atony, DIC

## Complications

- Hemorrhagic shock / death
- Neurogenic shock
- DIC
- Renal failure (hypovolemia)
- Re-inversion
- Infection / sepsis
- Need for hysterectomy (rare, if all replacement fails)
- Sheehan syndrome (pituitary necrosis from shock)
- Psychological trauma
