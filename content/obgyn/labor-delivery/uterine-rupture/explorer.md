# Uterine Rupture

## Overview

Uterine rupture is a full-thickness disruption of the uterine wall during pregnancy or labor. It is a catastrophic obstetric emergency associated with significant maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality. The most common cause in developed countries is rupture of a previous cesarean scar during trial of labor (TOLAC), while in developing countries it is more commonly due to obstructed labor. Rapid diagnosis and surgical intervention are essential for survival.

## Key Points

- Full-thickness disruption of myometrium ± serosa
- Most common cause (developed): previous CS scar rupture during TOLAC
- Most common cause (developing): obstructed labor in unscarred uterus
- Classical CS scar rupture risk: 4-9% vs LSCS scar: 0.5-1%
- Classic presentation: sudden severe abdominal pain, cessation of contractions, fetal distress
- Scar dehiscence ≠ rupture (dehiscence = incomplete, serosa intact, usually asymptomatic)
- Emergency laparotomy is mandatory
- Fetal mortality: 50-75% if rupture with extrusion

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Complete disruption of all layers of the uterine wall (endometrium, myometrium, and serosa) with or without expulsion of fetal parts into the peritoneal cavity.

### Rupture vs Dehiscence
| Feature | Rupture | Dehiscence |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Layers involved | All (full-thickness including serosa) | Incomplete (serosa intact) |
| Symptoms | Severe pain, shock, fetal distress | Often asymptomatic |
| Hemorrhage | Significant | Minimal |
| Peritoneal cavity | Communication (fetus may extrude) | No communication |
| Management | Emergency surgery | Observation or elective repair |

### Classification
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Complete** | All layers disrupted, peritoneal communication |
| **Incomplete (dehiscence)** | Serosa intact, no peritoneal communication |
| **Spontaneous** | Without prior scar |
| **Traumatic** | Due to external injury/iatrogenic |
| **Scar rupture** | At site of previous uterine surgery |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes

#### Scarred Uterus (Most Common in Developed Countries)
- Previous cesarean section (especially classical)
- Previous myomectomy (especially fundal, full-thickness)
- Previous uterine perforation (D&C, hysteroscopy)
- Previous rupture repair

#### Unscarred Uterus
- **Obstructed labor** (most common in developing countries)
- Grand multiparity (≥5)
- Oxytocin/prostaglandin misuse (hyperstimulation)
- Malpresentation (transverse lie, neglected shoulder)
- Fetal anomaly (hydrocephalus)
- Placenta percreta
- Uterine anomalies (rudimentary horn pregnancy)
- Trauma (abdominal, MVA)
- Invasive mole / choriocarcinoma
- Connective tissue disorders

### Risk Factors for Scar Rupture
- Classical/inverted T incision (highest risk: 4-9%)
- Short inter-delivery interval (<18-24 months)
- Single-layer uterine closure
- Induction/augmentation with prostaglandins (especially misoprostol)
- >2 prior cesarean sections
- Infection of previous scar
- Uterine overdistension

### Pathophysiology
- Weakened uterine wall → contractions exceed wall strength → rupture
- Scarred uterus: scar tissue has reduced elasticity and vascularity
- Obstructed labor: pathological retraction ring (Bandl's ring) forms → lower segment thins → ruptures

## Clinical Features

### Impending Rupture (Warning Signs)
- **Bandl's ring**: visible/palpable retraction ring rising in abdomen
- Severe continuous abdominal pain (not just with contractions)
- Maternal tachycardia and anxiety
- Fetal distress on CTG
- Hematuria (bladder involvement)
- Tender lower segment
- Frequent strong contractions

### Complete Rupture — Classic Presentation
1. **Sudden severe abdominal pain** (tearing sensation) — may improve briefly as uterus stops contracting
2. **Cessation of uterine contractions** (uterus relaxes)
3. **Vaginal bleeding** (may be absent if bleeding is intraperitoneal)
4. **Fetal parts palpable superficially** through abdomen (if fetus extruded)
5. **Loss of fetal heart sounds** or severe bradycardia
6. **Recession of presenting part** on vaginal exam
7. **Maternal shock** — tachycardia, hypotension, pallor
8. **Abdominal distension** (hemoperitoneum)

### Scar Dehiscence
- Often asymptomatic — discovered incidentally at repeat CS
- Mild tenderness over scar
- No acute emergency features

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis (primarily clinical — time-critical)
- Sudden pain → cessation of contractions → fetal distress/death → shock
- Abnormal CTG: prolonged bradycardia most reliable sign in scarred uterus
- Loss of station on vaginal examination

### Investigations (do not delay surgery)
- Hemoglobin / crossmatch urgently
- Bedside ultrasound may show:
  - Free fluid (hemoperitoneum)
  - Fetus in peritoneal cavity
  - Uterine wall defect
- Post-delivery: DIC screen (PT, aPTT, fibrinogen, platelets)

## Management

### Emergency Management
1. **Call for help** — senior obstetrician, anesthetist, blood bank
2. **Resuscitation**: 2 large-bore IV, crystalloid/colloid, crossmatch 4-6 units
3. **Emergency laparotomy** — do NOT delay
4. **Delivery of fetus** (if still in utero)
5. **Assessment of rupture site and extent**

### Surgical Options

#### Repair of Rupture (Preferred if possible)
- **Indications**: young patient, desires future fertility, clean tear, accessible edges
- Debride edges → two-layer closure with absorbable suture
- Counsel regarding risk of recurrence (recommend elective CS in future)

#### Hysterectomy
- **Indications**: extensive rupture, uncontrollable hemorrhage, cervical extension, infection, completed family
- Subtotal hysterectomy usually faster and sufficient
- Total hysterectomy if cervical involvement

#### Additional Procedures
- Bladder repair if involved
- Internal iliac artery ligation (if ongoing hemorrhage)
- Uterine artery embolization (if stable enough for IR)
- Blood transfusion (often massive transfusion protocol)

### Postoperative Care
- ICU/HDU monitoring
- Blood products (packed RBC, FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate)
- Antibiotics (broad-spectrum)
- Monitor for DIC, renal failure
- Thromboprophylaxis
- Counseling regarding future pregnancies
- If repaired: mandatory elective CS at 36-37 weeks in future pregnancy

## Complications

### Maternal
- Hemorrhagic shock and death (maternal mortality 1-10%)
- DIC
- Hysterectomy (loss of fertility)
- Bladder/ureteric injury
- Broad ligament hematoma
- Renal failure (ATN from hypovolemia)
- Sheehan syndrome (pituitary necrosis)
- Sepsis
- VTE
- Asherman syndrome (if repaired)

### Fetal
- Fetal death (50-75% if fetus extruded into peritoneal cavity)
- Severe birth asphyxia → HIE → cerebral palsy
- Prematurity complications (if early delivery for scar tenderness)
