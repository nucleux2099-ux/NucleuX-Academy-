# Forceps Delivery

## Overview

Forceps delivery is an operative vaginal delivery technique using specially designed instruments to assist extraction of the fetal head. It is one of the oldest obstetric procedures, dating back centuries, and remains an essential skill in modern obstetrics. Forceps application requires precise knowledge of pelvic anatomy, fetal head positioning, and strict adherence to prerequisites. When correctly applied, forceps delivery is safe and effective; incorrect application can cause significant maternal and fetal injury.

## Key Points

- Operative vaginal delivery using metal instruments applied to fetal head
- Requires fully dilated cervix, ruptured membranes, engaged head, known position
- Classification: outlet, low, mid-cavity (high forceps abandoned)
- Most commonly used: Wrigley's (outlet/low), Neville-Barnes/Simpson (low-mid), Kielland's (rotation)
- Kielland's forceps: for rotation of occipitoposterior/transverse to OA
- Trial of forceps: attempted in OT with readiness for immediate CS
- Declining use globally, being replaced by vacuum extraction
- Episiotomy usually performed with forceps delivery

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Application of specifically designed instruments to the fetal head to assist vaginal delivery by traction and/or rotation.

### Classification by Station (ACOG)
| Type | Station | Rotation |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Outlet** | Scalp visible, head on perineum, sagittal suture in AP or <45° | ≤45° rotation |
| **Low** | Station ≥+2 (not on pelvic floor) | ≤45° or >45° rotation |
| **Mid-cavity** | Station 0 to +2, head engaged but above +2 | Any rotation |
| **High** | Head not engaged | **ABANDONED — never attempted** |

### Types of Forceps
| Forceps | Features | Use |
|---------|----------|-----|
| **Wrigley's** | Short, light shanks | Outlet / lift-out / at CS |
| **Simpson's** | Separated shanks, cephalic curve | Molded head, traction |
| **Neville-Barnes** | Similar to Simpson | Low/mid-cavity traction |
| **Kielland's** | Minimal pelvic curve, sliding lock | Rotation (OP to OA) |
| **Piper's** | Long, reversed pelvic curve | After-coming head in breech |
| **Barton's** | Hinged anterior blade | Transverse arrest (deep transverse) |

### Parts of Forceps
1. **Blade**: cephalic curve (fits head) + pelvic curve (fits birth canal)
2. **Shank**: connects blade to handle
3. **Lock**: English lock (most), sliding lock (Kielland's)
4. **Handle**: for grip and traction

## Etiology / Indications

### Fetal Indications
- Non-reassuring fetal status (fetal distress)
- After-coming head in breech (Piper's forceps)

### Maternal Indications
- Prolonged second stage of labor
  - Nullipara: >3 hours with regional, >2 hours without
  - Multipara: >2 hours with regional, >1 hour without
- Maternal exhaustion
- Medical conditions contraindicating pushing:
  - Cardiac disease (avoid Valsalva)
  - Hypertensive crisis
  - Cerebrovascular disease
  - Myasthenia gravis

### Prerequisites — **"ABCDEFGH"**
- **A**nalgesia adequate (pudendal/epidural/spinal)
- **B**ladder empty (catheterized)
- **C**ervix fully dilated
- **D**etermination of position (exact fetal head position known)
- **E**ngagement confirmed (head engaged, station ≥0)
- **F**orceps appropriate for situation
- **G**entle traction (no excessive force)
- **H**ead presentation (cephalic, not face with mentoposterior)

Additional prerequisites:
- Membranes ruptured
- No CPD
- Operator experienced
- Informed consent
- Readiness for CS if forceps fail

## Clinical Features

### Assessment Before Application
- Confirm full dilatation (digital exam)
- Determine exact position of fetal head (sutures, fontanelles)
- Assess station and degree of molding
- Rule out CPD
- Empty bladder

### Technique of Application
1. **Check forceps**: assemble outside to confirm matching pair
2. **Apply left blade first** (held in left hand, inserted on left side of pelvis)
3. **Apply right blade** (held in right hand, inserted on right side)
4. **Lock the blades**
5. **Check application**: sagittal suture perpendicular to plane of shanks, posterior fontanelle 1 cm above and midway between blades
6. **Traction**: during contractions with maternal effort, following pelvic curve (Pajot's maneuver)
7. **Delivery**: extension of head, controlled delivery
8. **Remove forceps**: after head delivered to prevent perineal injury

### Pajot's Maneuver
- Combined downward and outward traction following the pelvic curve
- One hand applies traction, other directs force along pelvic axis

## Diagnosis

Not a diagnostic entity. Correct diagnosis of fetal position and station is critical before forceps application.

### Confirming Correct Application
- **Biparietal application**: blades symmetrically over parietal bones
- Posterior fontanelle palpable 1 cm above shanks, centered
- Sagittal suture perpendicular to shanks
- If application not correct → remove and reapply or abandon

## Management

### Trial of Forceps
- Attempted in operating theater with full preparation for CS
- If delivery not easily accomplished with gentle traction → abandon and CS
- Maximum 3 pulls recommended; some say 1-2
- Pop-offs or slipping = warning sign

### Failed Forceps
- Failure to deliver with appropriate application and reasonable force
- Must proceed to CS immediately
- Sequential use of vacuum then forceps (or vice versa) increases neonatal risk — avoid

### Kielland's Forceps — Rotation
- Used for malposition (OT or OP)
- Direct application or wandering technique
- Minimal pelvic curve allows rotation without maternal injury
- Requires experienced operator
- Higher complication rate — some units have abandoned it

### Post-Delivery Care
- Examine for maternal injuries (cervical tears, vaginal lacerations, episiotomy extension)
- Examine neonate (facial marks, cephalohematoma)
- Document procedure details, indication, position, number of pulls
- Postnatal debriefing with mother

## Complications

### Maternal
- Perineal trauma (3rd/4th degree tears — higher than vacuum)
- Vaginal/cervical lacerations
- Episiotomy extension
- Urinary retention / bladder injury
- Anal sphincter injury → fecal incontinence
- Postpartum hemorrhage
- Hematoma (paravaginal, vulvar)
- Dyspareunia
- Urinary incontinence

### Fetal/Neonatal
- Facial marks/bruising (common, transient)
- Facial nerve palsy (usually transient)
- Cephalohematoma
- Subgaleal hemorrhage (rare but serious)
- Skull fracture (rare)
- Intracranial hemorrhage (rare)
- Retinal hemorrhage
- Brachial plexus injury

### Forceps vs Vacuum
| Feature | Forceps | Vacuum |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Maternal trauma | Higher | Lower |
| Neonatal scalp injury | Lower | Higher (cephalohematoma, subgaleal) |
| Facial injury | Higher | None |
| Failed delivery | Less likely | More likely (pop-offs) |
| Rotation capability | Yes (Kielland's) | Limited |
| Anesthesia needed | More | Less |
