# Episiotomy

## Overview

Episiotomy is a surgical incision made in the perineum during the second stage of labor to enlarge the vaginal outlet and facilitate delivery. Once the most commonly performed surgical procedure in obstetrics, its routine use has declined significantly based on evidence showing selective episiotomy leads to better outcomes than routine episiotomy. Current practice favors restrictive use for specific indications.

## Key Points

- Deliberate incision of perineum to widen vaginal introitus during delivery
- Restrictive (selective) episiotomy is now standard — NOT routine
- Mediolateral episiotomy preferred in most countries (lower risk of 3rd/4th degree tears)
- Median (midline) episiotomy — easier repair, better healing, but higher risk of extension
- Best timing: when perineum is distended and thinned during crowning
- Local anesthesia: pudendal nerve block or local infiltration
- Repair with continuous absorbable suture (Vicryl Rapide preferred)

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
A planned surgical incision of the perineum and posterior vaginal wall during the second stage of labor.

### Types of Episiotomy
| Type | Direction | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Mediolateral** | 45-60° from midline toward ischial tuberosity | Lower extension risk, protects anal sphincter | More painful, more blood loss, harder to repair |
| **Median (Midline)** | Straight down from fourchette toward anus | Easy repair, less pain, less blood loss, better healing | High risk of 3rd/4th degree tears |
| **Lateral** | Transverse from vaginal introitus | Rarely used | Damage to Bartholin's gland, poor healing |
| **J-shaped** | Starts midline, curves laterally | Theoretical benefits | Limited use |

### Degrees of Perineal Tears
| Degree | Structure Involved |
|--------|-------------------|
| 1st | Vaginal mucosa / perineal skin only |
| 2nd | Perineal muscles (equivalent to episiotomy) |
| 3rd | Anal sphincter (3a: <50% EAS, 3b: >50% EAS, 3c: IAS involved) |
| 4th | Anal/rectal mucosa |

## Etiology / Indications

### Current Accepted Indications (Selective/Restrictive)
- **Fetal distress** requiring expedited delivery
- **Operative vaginal delivery** (forceps > vacuum)
- **Shoulder dystocia** (to create room for maneuvers)
- **Breech delivery** — to prevent after-coming head entrapment
- **Rigid perineum** preventing delivery
- **Previous 3rd/4th degree repair** (debated)
- **Preterm delivery** — to reduce head compression (debated)

### No Longer Routine Indications
- Primigravida (routine episiotomy NOT recommended)
- Prevention of perineal tears (evidence shows it doesn't help)
- Previous episiotomy

## Pathophysiology

### Anatomy of the Perineum
Layers cut in mediolateral episiotomy:
1. Vaginal mucosa
2. Perineal skin
3. Subcutaneous tissue
4. Bulbospongiosus muscle
5. Transverse perineal muscle (superficial)
6. Part of pubococcygeus (levator ani)

### Perineal Body
- Central tendon of the perineum
- Convergence point of perineal muscles
- Critical for pelvic floor support
- Located between vagina and anus (3-4 cm length)

## Clinical Features

### When to Perform
- During the second stage of labor
- When perineum is stretched and thinned
- During a contraction with maternal pushing effort
- Head visible at introitus (crowning)

### Technique
1. Adequate analgesia (pudendal block or local infiltration with 1% lidocaine)
2. Two fingers placed between fetal head and perineum (to protect baby)
3. Single clean cut with sharp scissors (Mayo/episiotomy scissors)
4. Mediolateral: 45-60° from midline, starting at fourchette
5. Incision length: 3-4 cm typically
6. Timing: at crowning, during a contraction

## Diagnosis

Not a diagnostic entity. Recognition of perineal trauma and correct classification of tears (1st-4th degree) is essential after delivery.

### Post-delivery Assessment
- Examine systematically: vaginal walls, cervix, perineum, anal sphincter, rectal mucosa
- Rectal examination MANDATORY to exclude 3rd/4th degree tears
- Button-hole tears of rectum can be missed without rectal exam

## Management

### Repair Technique
1. **Analgesia**: ensure adequate (top-up epidural or local infiltration)
2. **Positioning**: lithotomy, good lighting
3. **Suture material**: continuous absorbable polyglactin (Vicryl Rapide 2-0)
4. **Technique** (3-layer repair):
   - **Layer 1**: Vaginal mucosa — continuous non-locking suture from apex
   - **Layer 2**: Perineal muscles — continuous or interrupted sutures
   - **Layer 3**: Perineal skin — continuous subcuticular suture
5. **Continuous suturing** preferred over interrupted (less pain)
6. **Rectal check** after repair — ensure no suture through rectal mucosa

### 3rd/4th Degree Tear Repair
- Should be repaired by experienced operator
- In operating theater under regional/general anesthesia
- Anal sphincter: end-to-end or overlap technique with PDS/Vicryl
- 4th degree: rectal mucosa repaired first (submucous interrupted Vicryl)
- Postoperative: broad-spectrum antibiotics + laxatives

### Postoperative Care
- Ice packs (first 24-48 hours)
- Analgesics: paracetamol + NSAID (diclofenac)
- Sitz baths
- Perineal hygiene
- Pelvic floor exercises (after healing)
- Review at 6-week postnatal check
- Follow-up in perineal clinic if 3rd/4th degree tear

## Complications

### Immediate
- Hemorrhage / hematoma
- Extension to 3rd/4th degree tear (especially midline)
- Pain

### Early
- Wound infection
- Wound dehiscence
- Perineal abscess
- Difficulty with breastfeeding posture (pain)

### Late
- Dyspareunia (pain during intercourse) — most common late complaint
- Perineal pain
- Fecal incontinence / flatal incontinence (especially after 3rd/4th degree)
- Rectovaginal fistula (rare)
- Granulation tissue at wound site
- Endometriosis in scar (rare)
- Psychological/sexual dysfunction
- Pelvic floor weakness

### Evidence Against Routine Episiotomy
- Cochrane Review (2017): restrictive episiotomy results in:
  - Less severe perineal trauma
  - Less suturing
  - Fewer complications
  - No difference in severe vaginal/perineal trauma
  - No benefit for fetal outcomes
