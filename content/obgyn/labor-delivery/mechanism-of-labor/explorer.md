# Mechanism of Labor

## Overview

The mechanism of labor (cardinal movements) describes the series of passive movements the fetus undergoes as it navigates through the maternal pelvis during labor. These movements are a result of the interaction between the fetal head (passenger), the bony pelvis and soft tissues (passage), and uterine contractions (power). Understanding these movements is fundamental to managing normal and abnormal labor.

The most common presentation is vertex with left occipitoanterior (LOA) position, and all cardinal movements are described relative to this position.

## Key Points

- Seven cardinal movements occur in a specific sequence (with overlap)
- Movements are passive — result of forces acting on fetus
- Described for vertex presentation in LOA position (most common)
- Engagement is the first critical step
- Internal rotation brings the occiput from transverse/oblique to direct OA
- The widest diameter must align with the widest pelvic diameter at each level
- Suboccipitobregmatic (9.5 cm) is the presenting diameter in well-flexed vertex

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
The mechanism of labor refers to the series of changes in position and attitude of the fetus during its passage through the birth canal.

### The Seven Cardinal Movements
1. Engagement
2. Descent
3. Flexion
4. Internal rotation
5. Extension
6. Restitution (external rotation)
7. Expulsion

### Important Fetal Head Diameters
| Diameter | Measurement | Presentation |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Suboccipitobregmatic | 9.5 cm | Fully flexed vertex (ideal) |
| Suboccipitofrontal | 10 cm | Partially flexed vertex |
| Occipitofrontal | 11.5 cm | Deflexed (military) |
| Mentovertical | 13.5 cm | Brow (largest, cannot deliver) |
| Submentobregmatic | 9.5 cm | Face (mentoanterior) |
| Biparietal | 9.5 cm | Widest transverse diameter |

### Pelvic Diameters
| Level | Widest Diameter | Measurement |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| Inlet | Transverse | 13 cm |
| Mid-pelvis | AP (interspinous = narrowest: 10 cm) | 12 cm AP |
| Outlet | AP | 13 cm (with coccyx extension) |

## Etiology / Pathophysiology

### Why These Movements Occur
The fetal head must adapt to the changing diameters of the pelvis:
- At the **inlet**: widest diameter is transverse → head enters in transverse/oblique
- At **mid-pelvis**: widest diameter is AP → head must rotate to AP
- At **outlet**: widest diameter is AP → head exits in AP

### The Three P's
1. **Passenger**: fetal head size, attitude, presentation, position
2. **Passage**: bony pelvis + soft tissues
3. **Power**: uterine contractions + maternal expulsive effort

## Clinical Features — The Cardinal Movements in Detail

### 1. Engagement
- **Definition**: widest transverse diameter (biparietal, 9.5 cm) passes through pelvic inlet
- **Clinical assessment**: head is engaged when lowest bony point is at ischial spines (station 0) or when ≤2/5 palpable abdominally
- In nulliparas: often occurs 2-3 weeks before labor
- In multiparas: may occur during labor
- Head enters in transverse or oblique diameter of inlet

### 2. Descent
- Progressive downward movement of presenting part through pelvis
- Occurs throughout labor but most prominent in 2nd stage
- Caused by: uterine contractions, maternal bearing down, gravity
- Assessed by station (ischial spines = 0)

### 3. Flexion
- Chin brought to chest → presents smallest diameter (suboccipitobregmatic 9.5 cm)
- Occurs passively as head meets pelvic floor resistance
- Incomplete flexion → larger diameters present → prolonged labor
- **Lever action**: occipital condyles are posterior to center of head → resistance causes flexion

### 4. Internal Rotation
- **Most critical movement**
- Occiput rotates from transverse/oblique to direct anterior (OA)
- Rotation of 45° (from LOA) or 90° (from LOT)
- Occurs at level of ischial spines (mid-pelvis)
- Caused by: gutter-shaped pelvic floor (levator ani), head meets pelvic floor
- Brings AP diameter of head to align with AP diameter of outlet
- Sagittal suture now in AP diameter

### 5. Extension
- Occiput escapes under symphysis pubis (acts as fulcrum)
- Head extends as it sweeps over perineum
- Nape of neck pivots under symphysis
- Delivery sequence: occiput → bregma → forehead → face → chin
- This is when **crowning** occurs

### 6. Restitution (External Rotation)
- Head returns to normal alignment with shoulders (un-twists)
- Head rotates 45° externally to realign with shoulders
- Simultaneously, shoulders undergo internal rotation to align AP at outlet
- Anterior shoulder comes under symphysis

### 7. Expulsion
- Anterior shoulder delivered under symphysis (by lateral flexion downward)
- Posterior shoulder delivered over perineum (by lateral flexion upward)
- Rest of body follows easily

## Diagnosis

### Assessment During Labor
- **Abdominal palpation**: fifths of head palpable above brim (5/5 to 0/5)
- **Vaginal examination**: station, position (sutures/fontanelles), degree of flexion, caput, molding
- **Engagement confirmed**: ≤2/5 palpable abdominally or station 0

### Station Assessment
| Station | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| -5 to -1 | Above ischial spines |
| 0 | At ischial spines (engaged) |
| +1 to +5 | Below ischial spines |

### Molding Assessment
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | Bones normally separated |
| + | Bones touching |
| ++ | Bones overlapping but reducible |
| +++ | Bones overlapping, irreducible (significant CPD) |

## Management

### Normal Progress Assessment
- Plot on partograph
- Cervical dilatation: 1 cm/hr in active labor (active management)
- Descent should accompany dilatation
- Monitor mechanism with regular vaginal examinations (4-hourly)

### When Mechanism is Abnormal
| Abnormality | Cause | Management |
|-------------|-------|-----------|
| **Failure to engage** | CPD, malpresentation | Assess, may need CS |
| **Failure to flex** | Deflexed head (OP) | Longer labor, may rotate |
| **Failure to rotate** | Deep transverse arrest | Manual rotation / Kielland's / CS |
| **OP position persistent** | Weak pelvic floor, android pelvis | Face-to-pubes delivery or rotation |
| **Arrest of descent** | CPD, inadequate contractions | Oxytocin augmentation or CS |

### Occipitoposterior (OP) Position
- Occurs in 10-25% of labors at onset
- Most (90%) rotate to OA spontaneously (long internal rotation)
- Persistent OP: face-to-pubes delivery (larger diameter, more perineal trauma)
- Options: manual rotation, Kielland's forceps, vacuum rotation, CS

## Complications

### From Abnormal Mechanism
- Prolonged labor
- Obstructed labor
- Cervical lacerations
- Perineal tears (especially OP delivery)
- Maternal exhaustion
- Postpartum hemorrhage
- Operative delivery (forceps/vacuum/CS)

### Fetal
- Caput succedaneum (edema of scalp from pressure)
- Cephalohematoma (subperiosteal hemorrhage)
- Excessive molding → intracranial hemorrhage
- Fetal distress from prolonged labor
- Erb's palsy (if shoulder dystocia)

### Caput vs Cephalohematoma
| Feature | Caput Succedaneum | Cephalohematoma |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Location | Subcutaneous | Subperiosteal |
| Crosses sutures | Yes | No |
| Present at birth | Yes | May appear later |
| Resolution | 24-48 hours | Weeks to months |
