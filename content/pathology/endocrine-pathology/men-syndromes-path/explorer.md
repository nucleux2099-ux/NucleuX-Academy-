# Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) Syndromes — Pathology

## Overview
MEN syndromes are a group of inherited autosomal dominant disorders characterized by hyperplasia and/or neoplasia of multiple endocrine organs. Three major types: MEN 1, MEN 2A, and MEN 2B. Early recognition is critical for screening family members.

## Key Points
- All are **autosomal dominant** with high penetrance
- MEN 1 = 3 P's (Pituitary, Parathyroid, Pancreas)
- MEN 2A = Medullary thyroid carcinoma + Pheochromocytoma + Parathyroid hyperplasia
- MEN 2B = Medullary thyroid carcinoma + Pheochromocytoma + Mucosal neuromas + Marfanoid habitus
- MEN 1 gene = **Menin** (chromosome 11q13); MEN 2 gene = **RET proto-oncogene** (chromosome 10q11.2)

## Definition & Classification

| Feature | MEN 1 | MEN 2A (Sipple) | MEN 2B |
|---------|-------|-----------------|--------|
| **Gene** | Menin (11q13) — tumor suppressor | RET (10q11.2) — proto-oncogene | RET (10q11.2) |
| **Inheritance** | AD | AD | AD (often de novo) |
| **Parathyroid** | Hyperplasia (95%) | Hyperplasia (50%) | Absent |
| **Thyroid** | — | Medullary carcinoma (100%) | Medullary carcinoma (100%) |
| **Adrenal** | — | Pheochromocytoma (50%) | Pheochromocytoma (50%) |

| **Pituitary** | Adenoma (30%) | — | — |
| **Other** | — | — | Mucosal neuromas, Marfanoid habitus |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### MEN 1 — Menin Gene
- **Menin** = tumor suppressor gene on chromosome 11q13
- Loss of function → Knudson's two-hit hypothesis
- Menin protein normally inhibits cell proliferation (interacts with JunD)
- Loss → uncontrolled proliferation of endocrine cells

### MEN 2A & 2B — RET Proto-oncogene
- **RET** = receptor tyrosine kinase on chromosome 10q11.2
- **Gain-of-function** mutations → constitutive activation
- MEN 2A: mutations in extracellular cysteine-rich domain (codon 634 most common)
- MEN 2B: mutations in intracellular tyrosine kinase domain (codon 918, M918T)
- MEN 2B is more aggressive, earlier onset

## Morphology

### MEN 1 Components
**Parathyroid:**
- Multiglandular hyperplasia (all 4 glands); chief cell predominant
- May progress to adenoma

**Pancreatic islet tumors:**
- **Gastrinoma** (most common, 60%) → Zollinger-Ellison syndrome
- Insulinoma, VIPoma, glucagonoma
- Often multicentric and malignant

**Pituitary adenoma:**
- Prolactinoma most common; also GH-secreting, non-functioning

### MEN 2A/2B Components
**Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC):**
- Arises from parafollicular C cells
- Bilateral and multicentric (unlike sporadic MTC which is unilateral)
- Produces **calcitonin** (tumor marker)
- **Amyloid deposits** in stroma (derived from calcitonin) — Congo red positive
- Preceded by C-cell hyperplasia

**Pheochromocytoma:**
- Bilateral in 50-80% of MEN cases (sporadic = usually unilateral)
- Adrenal medullary tumor → catecholamine excess
- "Rule of 10s" modified in MEN (bilateral, less often malignant)

**Mucosal neuromas (MEN 2B):**
- Lips, tongue, eyelids, GI tract
- Prominent nerve bundles with Schwann cell proliferation
- Marfanoid body habitus (tall, thin, long limbs)

## Clinical Features

### MEN 1
- **Hypercalcemia** (primary hyperparathyroidism — earliest and most common manifestation)
- **Zollinger-Ellison syndrome** (recurrent peptic ulcers, diarrhea)
- **Hypoglycemia** (insulinoma)
- **Galactorrhea, amenorrhea** (prolactinoma)
- **Acromegaly** (GH adenoma)

### MEN 2A
- **Medullary thyroid carcinoma** — neck mass, diarrhea (calcitonin effect)
- **Hypertension, palpitations** (pheochromocytoma)
- **Hypercalcemia** (parathyroid hyperplasia)

### MEN 2B
- **MTC** — aggressive, early onset (may present in childhood)
- **Pheochromocytoma**
- **Mucosal neuromas** — bumpy lips, tongue nodules
- **Marfanoid habitus** — but NO lens subluxation (unlike true Marfan)
- **GI ganglioneuromatosis** — constipation, megacolon

## Diagnosis
- **Genetic testing:** RET mutation analysis (MEN 2); Menin gene (MEN 1)
- **Calcitonin:** Tumor marker for MTC; pentagastrin stimulation test
- **Serum calcium, PTH:** Hyperparathyroidism
- **Plasma/urinary metanephrines:** Pheochromocytoma
- **Imaging:** CT/MRI for pancreatic, adrenal, pituitary tumors
- **Family screening:** Genetic testing for first-degree relatives

## Management (Brief)
- **MEN 1:** Parathyroidectomy, pancreatic tumor resection, pituitary surgery/medical therapy
- **MEN 2A/2B:** **Prophylactic thyroidectomy** (based on RET mutation risk category)
  - MEN 2B: thyroidectomy within first year of life
  - MEN 2A: thyroidectomy by age 5 (high-risk) or later (moderate-risk)
- **Pheochromocytoma:** ALWAYS remove BEFORE thyroidectomy (hypertensive crisis risk)
- **Genetic counseling** for families

## Complications
1. Malignant transformation (MTC, pancreatic islet cell tumors)
2. Metastatic disease (liver, lymph nodes, bone)
3. Hypertensive crisis (undiagnosed pheochromocytoma during surgery)
4. Recurrence after surgery (multicentric nature)
5. Zollinger-Ellison syndrome → severe peptic ulceration, perforation
