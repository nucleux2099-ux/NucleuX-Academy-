# Anemias — Overview

## Overview
Anemia is defined as a reduction in the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood, usually due to decreased hemoglobin concentration, RBC count, or hematocrit below normal for age and sex. It is the most common hematological disorder worldwide.

## Key Points
- WHO definition: Hb <13 g/dL (males), <12 g/dL (females), <11 g/dL (pregnancy)
- Classified by morphology (MCV), etiology, or pathophysiology
- Reticulocyte count differentiates production defects from destruction/loss
- Iron deficiency anemia = most common anemia worldwide
- Peripheral smear examination is key to morphological classification

## Definition & Classification

### By MCV (Morphological)
| Type | MCV | Examples |
|------|-----|---------|
| **Microcytic** (<80 fL) | ↓ | Iron deficiency, thalassemia, sideroblastic, chronic disease |
| **Normocytic** (80-100 fL) | Normal | Acute blood loss, anemia of chronic disease, aplastic, hemolytic |
| **Macrocytic** (>100 fL) | ↑ | Megaloblastic (B12/folate), liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS |

### By Pathophysiology
| Mechanism | Examples |
|-----------|---------|
| **↓ Production** | Iron deficiency, B12/folate deficiency, aplastic anemia, anemia of chronic disease |
| **↑ Destruction (hemolytic)** | Hereditary spherocytosis, SCD, G6PD, autoimmune hemolytic |
| **Blood loss** | Acute hemorrhage, chronic GI bleeding |

### By Reticulocyte Count
- **Low reticulocyte count** (<2%) → production problem (marrow failure, nutritional deficiency)
- **High reticulocyte count** (>2%) → appropriate marrow response (hemolysis, blood loss)

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Iron Deficiency Anemia (Most Common)
- Causes: Chronic blood loss (GI, menstrual), poor intake, malabsorption (celiac)
- ↓Iron → ↓heme synthesis → microcytic hypochromic anemia
- Lab: ↓ferritin, ↓serum iron, ↑TIBC, ↓transferrin saturation

### Megaloblastic Anemia (B12/Folate)
- Impaired DNA synthesis → nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony
- Causes: Pernicious anemia (B12), tropical sprue, dietary deficiency
- Hypersegmented neutrophils (≥5 lobes) = hallmark

### Hemolytic Anemias
- Intravascular: Complement-mediated, mechanical (microangiopathic), PNH
- Extravascular: Splenic sequestration (spherocytosis, SCD, antibody-coated RBCs)
- Features: ↑reticulocytes, ↑LDH, ↑unconjugated bilirubin, ↓haptoglobin

## Morphology

### Peripheral Smear Findings — HIGH YIELD
| Finding | Associated Condition |
|---------|---------------------|
| Microcytic hypochromic | Iron deficiency, thalassemia |
| Target cells | Thalassemia, liver disease, HbC |
| Sickle cells | Sickle cell disease |
| Spherocytes | Hereditary spherocytosis, autoimmune hemolytic |
| Schistocytes (fragmented) | DIC, TTP-HUS, mechanical valves |
| Hypersegmented neutrophils | Megaloblastic anemia |
| Teardrop cells (dacrocytes) | Myelofibrosis |
| Howell-Jolly bodies | Post-splenectomy, functional asplenia |
| Basophilic stippling | Lead poisoning, thalassemia |
| Rouleaux formation | Multiple myeloma |
| Heinz bodies | G6PD deficiency (oxidized Hb) |
| Bite cells | G6PD deficiency |
| Pencil cells (elliptocytes) | Iron deficiency |

### Bone Marrow
- **Iron deficiency:** ↓/absent iron stores (Prussian blue stain)
- **Megaloblastic:** Megaloblasts, giant metamyelocytes, hypercellular
- **Aplastic:** Hypocellular, fatty marrow
- **Sideroblastic:** Ring sideroblasts (iron-laden mitochondria around nucleus)

## Clinical Features
- **General:** Fatigue, pallor, dyspnea, tachycardia, palpitations
- **Specific:**
  - Iron deficiency: Koilonychia, pica, Plummer-Vinson syndrome, angular cheilitis
  - B12 deficiency: Subacute combined degeneration (dorsal + lateral columns), glossitis
  - Hemolytic: Jaundice, splenomegaly, dark urine, pigmented gallstones

## Diagnosis
- **CBC with indices:** Hb, MCV, MCH, MCHC, RDW
- **Reticulocyte count:** Production vs destruction
- **Iron studies:** Ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation
- **Peripheral smear:** Morphology
- **B12, folate levels**
- **LDH, haptoglobin, unconjugated bilirubin:** Hemolysis markers
- **Bone marrow biopsy:** When needed (aplastic, MDS, infiltrative)
- **Hb electrophoresis:** Hemoglobinopathies

## Management (Brief)
- **Iron deficiency:** Oral iron (ferrous sulfate); treat underlying cause
- **B12 deficiency:** IM cyanocobalamin (especially if pernicious anemia)
- **Folate deficiency:** Oral folic acid
- **Hemolytic:** Treat cause; steroids (autoimmune); splenectomy; transfusion support
- **Aplastic:** Immunosuppression or bone marrow transplant

## Complications
1. High-output cardiac failure (severe chronic anemia)
2. Growth retardation (children)
3. Subacute combined degeneration (B12 deficiency)
4. Iron overload from repeated transfusions
5. Aplastic crisis (parvovirus B19 in hemolytic anemias)
6. Pigmented gallstones (chronic hemolysis)
