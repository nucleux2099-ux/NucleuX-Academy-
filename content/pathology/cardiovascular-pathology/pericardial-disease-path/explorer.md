# Pericardial Disease — Pathology

## Overview
Pericardial diseases encompass a spectrum of conditions affecting the pericardium including pericarditis (acute, chronic, constrictive), pericardial effusion, and cardiac tamponade. They can be infectious, autoimmune, neoplastic, or idiopathic in origin.

## Key Points
- Pericardium has two layers: visceral (epicardium) and parietal, with 15-50 mL fluid normally
- Acute pericarditis is the most common pericardial disease
- Pericardial effusion → tamponade depends on RATE of accumulation, not just volume
- Constrictive pericarditis mimics restrictive cardiomyopathy
- "Bread and butter" appearance = fibrinous pericarditis (classic gross finding)

## Definition & Classification

### Types of Pericarditis
| Type | Characteristics |
|------|----------------|
| **Serous** | Clear, straw-colored fluid; SLE, uremia, viral |
| **Fibrinous** | Fibrin deposits; "bread and butter" appearance; post-MI (Dressler), uremia, rheumatic fever |
| **Purulent (suppurative)** | Bacterial infection; thick pus; high mortality |
| **Hemorrhagic** | Blood-tinged; TB, malignancy, post-cardiac surgery |
| **Caseous** | TB pericarditis; may → constrictive |
| **Constrictive** | Dense fibrosis/calcification encasing heart; TB most common cause |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes of Acute Pericarditis
- **Idiopathic** (most common, likely viral)
- **Viral:** Coxsackie B, echovirus, influenza, HIV
- **Bacterial:** Staph, Strep, TB
- **Uremia** (renal failure)
- **Post-MI:** Early (1-3 days, reactive) and late (Dressler, 2-10 weeks, autoimmune)
- **Autoimmune:** SLE, rheumatic fever, rheumatoid arthritis
- **Neoplastic:** Metastatic carcinoma (lung, breast), lymphoma
- **Post-cardiac surgery/radiation**

### Pathogenesis
- Inflammation of pericardial layers → exudation of fluid/fibrin
- Tamponade: rapid accumulation → ↑ intrapericardial pressure → ↓ ventricular filling → ↓ cardiac output
- Constrictive: chronic inflammation → fibrosis → calcification → rigid shell → impaired diastolic filling

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Fibrinous:** "Bread and butter" pericarditis — rough, shaggy fibrin strands on both surfaces
- **Serous:** Clear yellow fluid; smooth, glistening pericardial surfaces
- **Purulent:** Thick, creamy pus; pericardial surfaces dull and erythematous
- **Hemorrhagic:** Blood-stained fluid; seen in TB and malignancy
- **Constrictive:** Thickened, white, fibrotic, calcified pericardium (up to 1 cm thick); "armor heart"

### Microscopic Features
- **Fibrinous:** Dense fibrin deposits, inflammatory cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes), reactive mesothelial cells
- **Purulent:** Heavy neutrophilic infiltrate, necrotic debris, bacteria
- **TB:** Caseating granulomas with Langhans giant cells, AFB on Ziehl-Neelsen stain
- **Constrictive:** Dense collagenous fibrosis, calcification, minimal inflammation
- **Malignant effusion:** Atypical cells in cytology

## Clinical Features

### Acute Pericarditis
- **Chest pain:** Sharp, pleuritic; worse on inspiration and lying flat; relieved by sitting forward
- **Pericardial friction rub:** Scratchy, high-pitched; best heard with diaphragm; has 3 components
- **ECG:** Diffuse ST elevation (concave up), PR depression
- **Fever, malaise** in infectious causes

### Pericardial Effusion / Tamponade
- **Beck's triad:** Hypotension + distended neck veins (↑JVP) + muffled heart sounds
- **Pulsus paradoxus:** >10 mmHg drop in systolic BP during inspiration
- **Electrical alternans** on ECG
- **"Water-bottle" shaped heart** on CXR

### Constrictive Pericarditis
- Signs of right heart failure: JVP↑, hepatomegaly, ascites, peripheral edema
- **Kussmaul sign:** Paradoxical rise in JVP on inspiration
- **Pericardial knock:** Early diastolic sound
- Calcified pericardium on CT/X-ray

## Diagnosis
- **ECG:** Diffuse ST elevation (acute); low voltage, electrical alternans (effusion)
- **Echocardiography:** Gold standard for effusion; diastolic collapse of RA/RV in tamponade
- **CT/MRI:** Pericardial thickening, calcification (constrictive)
- **Pericardiocentesis:** Diagnostic and therapeutic; fluid analysis (cytology, culture, ADA for TB)
- **Pericardial biopsy:** For chronic/undiagnosed cases

## Management (Brief)
- **Acute pericarditis:** NSAIDs + colchicine (first-line); corticosteroids if refractory
- **Tamponade:** Emergency pericardiocentesis
- **Purulent:** Surgical drainage + antibiotics
- **Constrictive:** Pericardiectomy (definitive treatment)
- **TB pericarditis:** ATT (anti-tubercular therapy)

## Complications
1. **Cardiac tamponade** — life-threatening; requires emergent drainage
2. **Constrictive pericarditis** — late sequel of chronic inflammation (especially TB)
3. **Recurrent pericarditis** — 15-30% of idiopathic cases
4. **Pericardial calcification** — "armor heart"
5. **Myopericarditis** — extension to myocardium
