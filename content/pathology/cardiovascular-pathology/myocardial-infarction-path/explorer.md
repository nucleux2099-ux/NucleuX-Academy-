# Myocardial Infarction — Pathology

## Overview
Myocardial infarction (MI) is ischemic necrosis of the heart muscle resulting from an acute reduction in coronary blood flow. It is the most important form of ischemic heart disease and the leading cause of death in developed countries.

## Key Points
- Caused by coronary artery atherosclerosis with superimposed thrombosis in >90% cases
- Transmural (STEMI) vs subendocardial (NSTEMI) based on extent
- Sequential morphological changes from 0–6 weeks are highly examinable
- Cardiac biomarkers: Troponin I/T (most specific), CK-MB
- Complications depend on time since infarction

## Definition & Classification

### By Extent of Necrosis
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Transmural** | Full thickness of ventricular wall; single coronary territory; ST elevation (STEMI) |
| **Subendocardial** | Inner 1/3 to 1/2 of wall; circumferential; non-ST elevation (NSTEMI) |

### By Clinical Classification (Universal)
- **Type 1:** Spontaneous MI (plaque rupture/erosion → thrombosis)
- **Type 2:** MI due to supply-demand mismatch (vasospasm, anemia, hypotension)
- **Type 3:** MI resulting in death before biomarker sampling
- **Type 4:** MI associated with PCI
- **Type 5:** MI associated with CABG

### Coronary Artery Territory
| Artery | Area Supplied | % of MI |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| LAD | Anterior wall, anterior septum, apex | 40-50% |
| RCA | Posterior wall, posterior septum, RV | 30-40% |
| LCx | Lateral wall | 15-20% |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes
1. **Coronary atherosclerosis with thrombosis** (>90%)
2. Coronary vasospasm (Prinzmetal angina)
3. Coronary embolism
4. Vasculitis (Kawasaki, PAN)
5. Dissection of coronary artery/aorta

### Sequence of Events
1. Atherosclerotic plaque rupture/erosion
2. Platelet adhesion and aggregation
3. Activation of coagulation cascade
4. Occlusive thrombus formation
5. Myocardial ischemia → injury → necrosis

### Wave-Front Phenomenon
- Necrosis begins at subendocardium (most vulnerable to ischemia)
- Progresses transmurally over 3-6 hours ("wavefront of necrosis")
- Subendocardium is watershed zone with least collateral supply

## Morphology

### Sequential Morphological Changes — HIGH YIELD

| Time | Gross | Microscopy |
|------|-------|------------|
| 0-4 hrs | No visible change | No change (± wavy fibers) |
| 4-12 hrs | Dark mottling (± TTC stain) | Early coagulative necrosis, edema, hemorrhage |
| 12-24 hrs | Dark mottling | Coagulative necrosis, pyknosis, early neutrophil infiltration |
| 1-3 days | Yellow-tan center with hyperemic border | Intense neutrophilic infiltration, coagulative necrosis |
| 3-7 days | Yellow-tan, softening (WEAKEST POINT) | Macrophage phagocytosis begins, granulation tissue at margins |
| 7-14 days | Red-gray, depressed borders | Prominent granulation tissue, neovascularization |
| 2-8 weeks | Gray-white scar forming | Progressive fibrosis, collagen deposition |
| >2 months | White, firm scar | Dense collagenous scar |

### Key Histological Features
- **Wavy fibers:** Earliest change (stretched non-contractile dead fibers pulled by viable adjacent ones)
- **Contraction band necrosis:** Hypereosinophilic bands of hypercontracted sarcomeres (seen with reperfusion)
- **Coagulative necrosis:** Cell outlines preserved, nuclei lost
- **Neutrophilic infiltration:** Peaks at 1-3 days
- **Granulation tissue:** Starts at margins ~day 3-7

## Clinical Features
- **Chest pain:** Severe, crushing, substernal; >30 min; not relieved by rest/nitroglycerin; radiates to left arm, jaw, back
- **Autonomic symptoms:** Diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting
- **Dyspnea** (if LV failure)
- **Silent MI:** In diabetics (autonomic neuropathy), elderly
- **Physical signs:** S4 gallop, hypotension, tachycardia, new murmur

## Diagnosis
- **ECG:** ST elevation (STEMI), Q waves, T-wave inversion
- **Cardiac biomarkers:**
  - Troponin I/T — rises 3-6 hrs, peaks 24 hrs, remains elevated 7-10 days (most specific)
  - CK-MB — rises 3-8 hrs, peaks 24 hrs, normalizes 48-72 hrs (useful for reinfarction)
- **Echocardiography:** Regional wall motion abnormalities
- **Coronary angiography:** Gold standard for identifying culprit vessel

## Management (Brief)
- **Acute:** MONA (Morphine, Oxygen, Nitroglycerin, Aspirin)
- **Reperfusion:** Primary PCI (preferred) or thrombolysis within window
- **Medications:** Dual antiplatelet, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins
- **Long-term:** Cardiac rehabilitation, risk factor modification

## Complications — By Timeline

### Early (0-4 days):
1. **Arrhythmias** — most common cause of death in first few hours (VF)
2. **Cardiogenic shock** — >40% LV mass lost
3. **Acute LV failure** → pulmonary edema

### Intermediate (4-7 days):
4. **Myocardial rupture** (weakest at 3-7 days) → cardiac tamponade
5. **Papillary muscle rupture** → acute mitral regurgitation
6. **Interventricular septal rupture** → L-to-R shunt

### Late (weeks-months):
7. **Ventricular aneurysm** — thin scar bulges during systole
8. **Mural thrombus** → systemic thromboembolism
9. **Dressler syndrome** — autoimmune pericarditis (2-10 weeks post-MI)
10. **Heart failure** — chronic, progressive
