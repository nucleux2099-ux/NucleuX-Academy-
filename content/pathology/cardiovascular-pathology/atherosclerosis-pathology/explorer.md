# Atherosclerosis — Pathology

## Overview
Atherosclerosis is a chronic inflammatory disease of large and medium-sized arteries characterized by the formation of atheromatous plaques (atheromas) in the intima. It is the leading cause of coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, and peripheral vascular disease worldwide.

## Key Points
- Most common cause of morbidity and mortality in developed countries
- Affects **large elastic arteries** (aorta, carotid, iliac) and **medium muscular arteries** (coronary, popliteal, cerebral)
- "Response to injury" hypothesis is the cornerstone of pathogenesis
- Endothelial dysfunction is the initiating event
- Lipid accumulation + chronic inflammation = plaque formation

## Definition & Classification
**Atherosclerosis** = hardening and narrowing of arteries due to buildup of plaque (lipids, fibrous tissue, calcification) within the intimal layer.

### Types of Lesions (AHA Classification):
| Type | Lesion | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| I | Initial lesion | Isolated macrophage foam cells |
| II | Fatty streak | Intracellular lipid accumulation |
| III | Intermediate | Type II + small extracellular lipid pools |
| IV | Atheroma | Type II + core of extracellular lipid |
| V | Fibroatheroma | Lipid core + fibrous cap |
| VI | Complicated | Surface defect, hematoma, thrombus |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Risk Factors
**Non-modifiable:** Age, Male sex, Family history, Genetic factors
**Modifiable:** Hyperlipidemia (↑LDL, ↓HDL), Hypertension, Smoking, Diabetes mellitus, Obesity, Sedentary lifestyle, CRP/Homocysteine elevation

### Pathogenesis — Response to Injury Hypothesis
1. **Endothelial injury/dysfunction** → ↑ permeability, leukocyte adhesion, thrombosis
2. **Lipid accumulation** → LDL enters intima → oxidized LDL (oxLDL)
3. **Monocyte recruitment** → monocytes → macrophages → engulf oxLDL → **foam cells**
4. **Smooth muscle proliferation** → migrate from media to intima, produce ECM
5. **Plaque formation** → lipid core covered by fibrous cap
6. **Plaque complications** → rupture, thrombosis, hemorrhage, calcification

### Key Molecular Players
- **oxLDL** — toxic to endothelium, chemotactic for monocytes
- **Scavenger receptors (CD36, SR-A)** — unregulated uptake of oxLDL
- **PDGF, FGF** — smooth muscle proliferation
- **MMP (matrix metalloproteinases)** — weaken fibrous cap → rupture

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Fatty streaks:** Yellow, flat, along intima (seen even in children/teens)
- **Atheromatous plaque:** Raised, white-yellow, firm; eccentric; protrudes into lumen
- **Complicated plaque:** Ulcerated surface, overlying thrombus, calcification ("pipe-stem" arteries)

### Microscopic Features
- **Fibrous cap:** Smooth muscle cells, collagen, elastic fibers, macrophages, T-lymphocytes
- **Necrotic/lipid core:** Cholesterol crystals (needle-shaped clefts), foam cells, necrotic debris, calcium deposits
- **Shoulder region:** Active inflammation (macrophages, T-cells) — most vulnerable to rupture
- **Neovascularization** of plaque from vasa vasorum
- **Calcification** — dystrophic type

### Vulnerable vs Stable Plaque
| Feature | Vulnerable (Unstable) | Stable |
|---------|----------------------|--------|
| Fibrous cap | Thin | Thick |
| Lipid core | Large | Small |
| Inflammation | ↑↑ | Minimal |
| SMC content | Low | High |
| Risk | Rupture → thrombosis | Gradual stenosis |

## Clinical Features
- **Coronary arteries** → Angina, MI, sudden cardiac death
- **Carotid/cerebral arteries** → TIA, stroke
- **Aorta** → Aneurysm (especially abdominal aortic aneurysm)
- **Peripheral arteries** → Intermittent claudication, gangrene
- **Renal arteries** → Renovascular hypertension
- **Mesenteric arteries** → Intestinal ischemia

## Diagnosis
- **Lipid profile:** Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides
- **Imaging:** Doppler ultrasound, CT angiography, coronary angiography
- **Biomarkers:** hs-CRP, homocysteine (risk assessment)
- **Ankle-brachial index (ABI):** For peripheral arterial disease
- **Intravascular ultrasound (IVUS):** Plaque characterization

## Management (Brief)
- **Lifestyle:** Diet, exercise, smoking cessation, weight control
- **Statins:** First-line pharmacotherapy (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors)
- **Antiplatelets:** Aspirin, clopidogrel
- **Control risk factors:** Antihypertensives, antidiabetics
- **Revascularization:** PCI with stenting, CABG for severe disease

## Complications
1. **Plaque rupture** → acute thrombosis → MI/stroke
2. **Plaque hemorrhage** → rapid expansion
3. **Aneurysm formation** (especially abdominal aorta)
4. **Thromboembolism** → distal infarction
5. **Chronic ischemia** → organ atrophy/fibrosis
6. **Calcification** → "pipe-stem" rigidity
