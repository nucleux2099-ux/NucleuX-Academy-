# Acute Pancreatitis — Pathology

## Overview
Acute pancreatitis is an acute inflammatory condition of the pancreas caused by autodigestion of pancreatic tissue by its own activated enzymes. It ranges from mild edematous (interstitial) to severe hemorrhagic (necrotizing) pancreatitis with high mortality.

## Key Points
- Gallstones and alcohol = two most common causes (together ~80%)
- Autodigestion by prematurely activated pancreatic enzymes (especially trypsin)
- Serum amylase and lipase elevated (lipase more specific)
- Fat necrosis with saponification = characteristic finding
- Ranson's criteria and APACHE II for severity assessment

## Definition & Classification
- **Interstitial edematous pancreatitis** (80%) — mild, self-limiting
- **Necrotizing pancreatitis** (20%) — severe, hemorrhagic; high mortality (30%)

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes — "I GET SMASHED"
- **I**diopathic
- **G**allstones (40%) — most common overall
- **E**thanol/Alcohol (30-40%) — most common in males
- **T**rauma
- **S**teroids
- **M**umps and other infections
- **A**utoimmune
- **S**corpion sting, Spider bite
- **H**yperlipidemia, Hypothermia, Hypercalcemia
- **E**RCP
- **D**rugs (azathioprine, valproic acid, thiazides, estrogens)

### Pathogenesis
1. **Duct obstruction** (gallstone) → ↑ intraductal pressure → enzyme activation
2. **Direct acinar cell injury** (alcohol, drugs, ischemia)
3. **Defective intracellular transport** → premature trypsinogen → trypsin activation
4. Trypsin activates other enzymes: **elastase** (vessel damage), **phospholipase A2** (fat necrosis), **lipase** (fat digestion)
5. **Autodigestion cascade** → edema, hemorrhage, necrosis, fat saponification

### Key Enzyme Roles
| Enzyme | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Trypsin | Activates all other enzymes; activates complement, kinin |
| Elastase | Destroys blood vessel walls → hemorrhage |
| Lipase/Phospholipase | Fat necrosis |
| Kallikrein | Vasodilation, ↑ permeability → shock |

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Mild:** Edematous, swollen pancreas; interstitial edema
- **Severe (hemorrhagic):** Dark red-black hemorrhagic areas; chalky white foci of **fat necrosis** (saponification) in pancreas, mesentery, omentum
- **Fat necrosis:** White, chalky deposits = calcium soaps (Ca²⁺ + fatty acids from lipase action)

### Microscopic Features
- **Interstitial edema** and inflammatory infiltrate
- **Coagulative necrosis** of acinar tissue
- **Fat necrosis** — ghostly outlines of fat cells with basophilic calcium deposits (saponification)
- **Hemorrhage** — from elastase-mediated vessel wall destruction
- **Neutrophilic infiltration**
- **Pseudocyst formation** (liquefied necrotic tissue enclosed by fibrous wall, NO epithelial lining)

## Clinical Features
- **Severe epigastric pain** radiating to back ("boring"); relieved by leaning forward
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- **Abdominal tenderness, guarding**
- **Grey Turner's sign** — flank ecchymosis (retroperitoneal hemorrhage)
- **Cullen's sign** — periumbilical ecchymosis
- **Shock** in severe cases (third-spacing, hemorrhage)
- **Fever, tachycardia**

## Diagnosis
- **Serum amylase:** Rises within 6-12 hrs, peaks 24 hrs; non-specific
- **Serum lipase:** More specific and stays elevated longer (preferred)
- **CT abdomen with contrast:** Gold standard for severity assessment; Balthazar scoring
- **USG:** Identify gallstones as etiology
- **CRP:** >150 mg/L at 48 hrs = severe
- **Ranson's criteria:** ≥3 = severe pancreatitis (at admission and 48 hrs)

## Management (Brief)
- **NPO (nil per os)**, IV fluids, analgesics (avoid morphine — sphincter of Oddi spasm)
- **Supportive care:** ICU for severe cases
- **ERCP** for gallstone pancreatitis with biliary obstruction
- **Antibiotics** if infected necrosis
- **Necrosectomy** for infected pancreatic necrosis
- **Cholecystectomy** after recovery to prevent recurrence (if gallstone etiology)

## Complications
1. **Pancreatic pseudocyst** — encapsulated necrotic collection; NO epithelial lining
2. **Pancreatic abscess** — infected necrosis/pseudocyst
3. **ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome)** — phospholipase destroys surfactant
4. **DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)**
5. **Hypocalcemia** — calcium consumed in saponification
6. **Shock** — hypovolemic/septic
7. **Pseudoaneurysm** — splenic artery most common
8. **Multi-organ failure**
9. **Chronic pancreatitis** (if recurrent episodes)
