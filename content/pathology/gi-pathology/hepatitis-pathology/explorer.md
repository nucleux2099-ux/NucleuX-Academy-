# Hepatitis — Pathology

## Overview
Hepatitis refers to inflammation of the liver. Viral hepatitis (A, B, C, D, E) is the most common cause worldwide. Other causes include alcohol, drugs, autoimmune conditions, and metabolic disorders. Chronic hepatitis (B, C) can progress to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.

## Key Points
- HAV and HEV = fecal-oral; self-limiting; no chronicity
- HBV and HCV = parenteral/sexual; can become chronic → cirrhosis → HCC
- HBV = DNA virus (only DNA hepatitis virus); all others are RNA
- HCV = most common cause of chronic hepatitis and liver transplant indication
- HDV requires HBV co-infection (defective RNA virus)
- HEV dangerous in pregnancy (20% mortality in 3rd trimester)

## Definition & Classification

### Viral Hepatitis Types
| Virus | Genome | Transmission | Chronicity | Carrier State |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| HAV | RNA | Fecal-oral | No | No |
| HBV | DNA | Parenteral, sexual, vertical | Yes (5-10% adults; 90% neonates) | Yes |
| HCV | RNA | Parenteral | Yes (80%) | Yes |
| HDV | RNA (defective) | Parenteral (needs HBV) | Yes | Yes |
| HEV | RNA | Fecal-oral | No (rarely in immunocompromised) | No |

### Classification by Duration
- **Acute hepatitis:** <6 months; self-limiting in most cases
- **Chronic hepatitis:** >6 months; persistent inflammation → fibrosis → cirrhosis

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### HBV Pathogenesis
- HBV is **NOT directly cytopathic**; liver damage is **immune-mediated** (CD8+ CTL response)
- Strong immune response → acute hepatitis → clearance
- Weak immune response → chronic hepatitis → carrier state
- HBV DNA integrates into host genome → risk of HCC even without cirrhosis
- **Ground glass hepatocytes** = accumulated HBsAg in SER

### HCV Pathogenesis
- Direct cytopathic + immune-mediated damage
- High mutation rate → escapes immune response → 80% chronicity
- HCV does NOT integrate into genome (unlike HBV)
- Associated with **mixed cryoglobulinemia**, **membranoproliferative GN**

### HBV Serology — HIGH YIELD
| Marker | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| HBsAg | Active infection (acute or chronic); first to appear |
| Anti-HBs | Immunity (recovery or vaccination) |
| HBcAg | Not detectable in serum |
| Anti-HBc IgM | Acute infection; window period marker |
| Anti-HBc IgG | Past infection (lifelong) |
| HBeAg | Active replication, high infectivity |
| Anti-HBe | Low replication, low infectivity |
| **Window period** | HBsAg negative, Anti-HBs not yet positive; only Anti-HBc IgM positive |

## Morphology

### Acute Viral Hepatitis
**Gross:** Slightly enlarged, soft liver
**Micro:**
- **Lobular disarray** — hepatocyte swelling, ballooning degeneration
- **Councilman bodies** (acidophilic/apoptotic bodies) — eosinophilic shrunken hepatocytes (apoptosis)
- **Portal and lobular inflammation** — lymphocytes and macrophages
- **Kupffer cell hyperplasia**
- Regenerative changes

### Chronic Hepatitis
**Micro:**
- **Interface hepatitis** (piecemeal necrosis) — lymphocytic destruction at portal-parenchymal interface
- **Portal inflammation** — lymphocytes, plasma cells
- **Fibrosis** — portal → bridging → cirrhosis (Metavir scoring: F0-F4)
- **Ground glass hepatocytes** — HBV (HBsAg in SER; orcein stain positive)
- **Lymphoid follicles in portal tracts** — characteristic of HCV

### Specific Features
| Finding | Virus |
|---------|-------|
| Ground glass hepatocytes | HBV |
| Lymphoid follicles/aggregates | HCV |
| Sanded nuclei | HBV (HBcAg) |
| Fatty change | HCV (genotype 3) |

## Clinical Features
### Acute Hepatitis
- **Prodrome:** Malaise, fatigue, nausea, anorexia (1-2 weeks)
- **Icteric phase:** Jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, hepatomegaly, RUQ tenderness
- **Recovery phase:** Symptoms resolve; most recover in 6-8 weeks
- **Fulminant hepatitis:** Massive hepatic necrosis → liver failure (rare; HBV > others)

### Chronic Hepatitis
- May be asymptomatic for years
- Fatigue, mild hepatomegaly
- Progressive fibrosis → cirrhosis features (portal hypertension, etc.)
- Extrahepatic: HCV → cryoglobulinemia, MPGN; HBV → PAN

## Diagnosis
- **LFTs:** ↑ALT > AST (opposite of alcohol); ↑bilirubin
- **Serology:** HAV (anti-HAV IgM), HBV (panel above), HCV (anti-HCV Ab, HCV RNA PCR)
- **Liver biopsy:** Grading (inflammation) and staging (fibrosis); Metavir score
- **FibroScan:** Non-invasive fibrosis assessment
- **HBV DNA, HCV RNA:** Viral load monitoring

## Management (Brief)
- **HAV, HEV:** Supportive; self-limiting; vaccination available (HAV; HEV in some countries)
- **HBV acute:** Supportive; chronic: tenofovir/entecavir; PEG-IFN
- **HCV:** DAAs (direct-acting antivirals) — >95% cure rate (sofosbuvir-based regimens)
- **Fulminant:** ICU, liver transplant consideration
- **Prevention:** HBV vaccination (recombinant HBsAg); blood screening; safe injection practices

## Complications
1. Chronic hepatitis → cirrhosis (HBV, HCV)
2. Hepatocellular carcinoma (HBV can cause HCC even without cirrhosis)
3. Fulminant hepatic failure (massive necrosis)
4. Extrahepatic: PAN (HBV), cryoglobulinemia/MPGN (HCV)
5. Carrier state → transmission risk
6. Cholestatic hepatitis (variant)
