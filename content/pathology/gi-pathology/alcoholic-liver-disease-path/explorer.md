# Alcoholic Liver Disease — Pathology

## Overview
Alcoholic liver disease (ALD) encompasses a spectrum of hepatic injury caused by chronic alcohol consumption: hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) → alcoholic hepatitis → alcoholic cirrhosis. It is one of the most common causes of chronic liver disease worldwide.

## Key Points
- Spectrum: Steatosis → Hepatitis → Cirrhosis (progressive but stages can overlap)
- Steatosis is reversible with abstinence; cirrhosis is irreversible
- Mallory-Denk bodies (alcoholic hyaline) = damaged cytokeratin intermediate filaments
- Acetaldehyde (toxic metabolite of ethanol) is the main mediator of injury
- Women more susceptible than men at lower doses

## Definition & Classification

| Stage | Reversibility | Key Feature |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| **Hepatic steatosis** | Fully reversible | Fat accumulation (macrovesicular) |
| **Alcoholic hepatitis** | Partially reversible | Hepatocyte necrosis + Mallory bodies + neutrophils |
| **Alcoholic cirrhosis** | Irreversible | Micronodular cirrhosis → may become macronodular |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Alcohol Metabolism
1. **Ethanol → Acetaldehyde** (by alcohol dehydrogenase & CYP2E1 in SER)
2. **Acetaldehyde → Acetate** (by aldehyde dehydrogenase)
3. Both steps generate NADH → ↑ NADH/NAD⁺ ratio

### Mechanisms of Injury
- **↑ NADH/NAD⁺ ratio** → impaired fatty acid oxidation → steatosis
- **Acetaldehyde toxicity** → lipid peroxidation, mitochondrial damage, protein adducts
- **CYP2E1 induction** → ↑ ROS → oxidative stress
- **Gut permeability ↑** → endotoxin (LPS) → Kupffer cell activation → TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6
- **Stellate cell activation** → collagen deposition → fibrosis → cirrhosis
- **Mallory-Denk body formation** → damaged cytokeratin intermediate filaments (CK8, CK18)

### Risk Factors
- >60-80 g/day alcohol for 10-20 years
- Female sex (lower ADH in gastric mucosa, higher body fat)
- Concurrent hepatitis C (synergistic)
- Genetic polymorphisms (ADH, ALDH)
- Malnutrition

## Morphology

### Hepatic Steatosis (Fatty Liver)
**Gross:** Large (up to 4-6 kg), yellow, greasy, soft liver
**Micro:** **Macrovesicular steatosis** — large clear vacuoles displacing nucleus to periphery; begins centrilobular (zone 3), may become panlobular

### Alcoholic Hepatitis
**Gross:** Enlarged, mottled red-tan liver
**Micro — KEY FEATURES:**
- **Hepatocyte ballooning** and necrosis
- **Mallory-Denk bodies** — eosinophilic, ropey, cytoplasmic inclusions (damaged cytokeratins)
- **Neutrophilic infiltration** ("satellitosis" — neutrophils surrounding damaged hepatocytes)
- **Steatosis** (usually present)
- **Perivenular/perisinusoidal fibrosis** ("chicken-wire" pattern)
- **Megamitochondria**

### Alcoholic Cirrhosis
**Gross:** Initially enlarged, fatty; later shrunken, hard, nodular; **micronodular** (nodules <3 mm, uniform)
**Micro:**
- Regenerative nodules surrounded by fibrous septa
- Initially micronodular → may become macronodular/mixed over time
- Mallory bodies may or may not be present
- Steatosis may decrease as cirrhosis advances

## Clinical Features
- **Steatosis:** Often asymptomatic; hepatomegaly; mild ↑ transaminases
- **Alcoholic hepatitis:** Fever, jaundice, RUQ pain, hepatomegaly, leukocytosis; AST:ALT ratio >2:1; may be rapidly fatal
- **Cirrhosis:** Portal hypertension (ascites, varices, splenomegaly), jaundice, coagulopathy, hepatic encephalopathy, spider angiomas, palmar erythema, gynecomastia, testicular atrophy
- **AST:ALT ratio >2:1** — characteristic of alcoholic liver disease (B6 deficiency → ↓ALT)

## Diagnosis
- **LFTs:** AST:ALT >2:1; ↑GGT (most sensitive for alcohol use)
- **Biopsy:** Gold standard (Mallory bodies, neutrophils, steatosis, fibrosis pattern)
- **Maddrey discriminant function (DF):** ≥32 = severe alcoholic hepatitis (needs steroids)
- **Imaging:** USG (fatty liver, cirrhosis); FibroScan (liver stiffness)
- **Serum albumin, PT/INR:** Assess synthetic function

## Management (Brief)
- **Abstinence** — most important intervention at all stages
- **Nutritional support** (thiamine, folate, multivitamins)
- **Alcoholic hepatitis:** Prednisolone if DF ≥32; pentoxifylline as alternative
- **Cirrhosis:** Manage complications (diuretics for ascites, band ligation for varices, lactulose for encephalopathy)
- **Liver transplant** — for end-stage disease (requires 6 months abstinence)

## Complications
1. Portal hypertension → esophageal variceal bleeding
2. Hepatic encephalopathy
3. Hepatorenal syndrome
4. Hepatocellular carcinoma (alcohol is a co-carcinogen)
5. Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP)
6. Coagulopathy → bleeding
7. Pancreatitis (concurrent)
