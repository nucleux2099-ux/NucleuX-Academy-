# Peptic Ulcer Disease — Pathology

## Overview
Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) refers to mucosal ulceration of the stomach or duodenum caused by an imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori) and protective factors (mucus, bicarbonate, prostaglandins, blood flow). H. pylori infection and NSAID use are the two most common causes.

## Key Points
- Duodenal ulcers are 4× more common than gastric ulcers
- H. pylori = most common cause overall (>70% of duodenal ulcers, 60% of gastric ulcers)
- NSAIDs = second most common cause (inhibit prostaglandin → ↓ mucosal protection)
- Duodenal ulcer: ↑acid; Gastric ulcer: normal/↓ acid with ↓ mucosal defense
- Curling's ulcer (burn) vs Cushing's ulcer (CNS injury) — special types

## Definition & Classification

### By Location
| Type | Location | Acid Levels | H. pylori |
|------|----------|------------|-----------|
| Duodenal (DU) | 1st part duodenum (anterior wall) | ↑↑ | ~95% (antral gastritis) |
| Gastric (GU) | Lesser curvature, antrum | Normal/↓ | ~70% (atrophic gastritis) |

### Special Types
- **Curling's ulcer:** Duodenal; post-burn (↓ mucosal blood flow)
- **Cushing's ulcer:** Gastric; post-CNS injury (↑ vagal stimulation → ↑ acid)
- **Zollinger-Ellison:** Multiple, refractory ulcers; gastrinoma (MEN 1 association)
- **Stress ulcers:** ICU patients; multiple, shallow erosions

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### H. pylori Mechanisms
- Gram-negative, spiral, flagellated bacterium
- Lives in mucus layer (microaerophilic)
- **Urease** → converts urea to NH₃ → neutralizes acid → creates alkaline microenvironment
- **CagA** (cytotoxin-associated gene A) → inflammation, cell proliferation
- **VacA** (vacuolating cytotoxin A) → epithelial damage
- **Adhesins** (BabA) → attaches to epithelial cells
- Induces chronic gastritis → ↑ gastrin → ↑ acid (duodenal ulcer)
- OR atrophic gastritis → ↓ acid → gastric ulcer/cancer

### NSAID Mechanisms
- Inhibit COX-1 → ↓ prostaglandin E2 and I2
- → ↓ mucus secretion, ↓ bicarbonate, ↓ mucosal blood flow
- → Direct topical injury to mucosa

### Imbalance Model
**Aggressive factors:** HCl, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs, smoking, alcohol, bile reflux
**Protective factors:** Mucus-bicarbonate barrier, prostaglandins, mucosal blood flow, epithelial renewal

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Round to oval**, sharply punched-out ulcer
- **Clean base** with smooth, overhanging margins (benign) vs raised, irregular, rolled margins (malignant)
- **Size:** Usually <4 cm (DU <2 cm, GU <4 cm)
- **Duodenal:** Anterior wall of 1st part (perforation); posterior wall (bleeding — gastroduodenal artery)
- **Gastric:** Lesser curvature, antrum

### Microscopic Features — 4 Layers (from surface to depth)
1. **Necrotic fibrinoid debris** (surface)
2. **Acute inflammatory exudate** (neutrophils)
3. **Granulation tissue** (capillaries, fibroblasts)
4. **Fibrosis/scar tissue** (deepest) — with endarteritis obliterans

- Muscularis propria may be destroyed and replaced by fibrosis
- **Malignant ulcer:** Irregular margins with tumor cells infiltrating all layers

## Clinical Features
- **Epigastric pain** — burning, gnawing
  - **DU:** Pain 2-3 hrs after meals ("hunger pain"); relieved by food/antacids; night pain
  - **GU:** Pain worsened by food; weight loss
- **Nausea, vomiting, bloating**
- **Hematemesis/melena** (if bleeding)
- **Acute abdomen** (if perforation)

## Diagnosis
- **Endoscopy (EGD):** Gold standard; allows biopsy (rule out malignancy in gastric ulcers)
- **H. pylori testing:**
  - Invasive: Rapid urease test (CLO test), histology, culture
  - Non-invasive: Urea breath test (UBT), stool antigen test, serology
- **Barium swallow:** Ulcer crater niche
- **Serum gastrin:** If Zollinger-Ellison suspected

## Management (Brief)
- **H. pylori eradication:** Triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin) × 14 days
- **PPI:** Omeprazole, pantoprazole (first-line acid suppression)
- **Stop NSAIDs** or switch to COX-2 selective + PPI
- **Surgery:** For complications (perforation, uncontrollable bleeding, obstruction)
- **Confirm eradication:** UBT at 4 weeks post-treatment

## Complications — "PHOB"
1. **P**erforation — anterior DU > GU; pneumoperitoneum; acute abdomen
2. **H**emorrhage — most common complication; posterior DU erodes gastroduodenal artery
3. **O**bstruction — pyloric stenosis from chronic fibrosis
4. **B**(malignant transformation) — gastric ulcers may undergo malignant change (1-3%); DU almost never
