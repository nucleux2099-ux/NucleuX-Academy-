# Lung Tumors — Pathology (Overview)

## Overview
Lung tumors include primary bronchogenic carcinomas, carcinoid tumors, mesenchymal tumors, and metastatic deposits. The lung is also one of the most common sites for metastatic disease. This topic provides a broad overview including benign tumors and carcinoid tumors that complement the detailed lung cancer discussion.

## Key Points
- 95% of lung tumors are malignant; 90-95% of those are bronchogenic carcinoma
- Lung is the most common site for metastatic deposits ("cannonball" metastases)
- Bronchial carcinoid = low-grade neuroendocrine tumor (distinct from small cell)
- Hamartoma = most common benign lung tumor ("coin lesion" with popcorn calcification)
- Mesothelioma = pleural tumor strongly linked to asbestos

## Definition & Classification

### Primary Lung Tumors
| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Epithelial (malignant)** | Adenocarcinoma, squamous cell, small cell, large cell |
| **Neuroendocrine** | Typical carcinoid, atypical carcinoid, small cell, large cell neuroendocrine |
| **Mesenchymal (benign)** | Hamartoma |
| **Lymphoproliferative** | Primary pulmonary lymphoma (MALT) |

### Pleural Tumors
- **Malignant mesothelioma** — asbestos-related
- **Solitary fibrous tumor** — benign mesenchymal

### Metastatic Tumors
- Most common malignant lung tumors overall are metastases
- Common primaries: breast, colon, kidney, melanoma, thyroid

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Bronchial Carcinoid
- Low-grade malignant neuroendocrine tumor
- NOT related to smoking (unlike small cell)
- Arises from Kulchitsky cells (neuroendocrine cells of bronchial mucosa)
- Typical carcinoid (90%) vs Atypical carcinoid (10%) — based on mitotic rate and necrosis

### Hamartoma
- Disorganized but mature tissue: cartilage, fat, fibrous tissue, epithelium
- NOT a true neoplasm (developmental malformation)
- Most common benign lung tumor

### Malignant Mesothelioma
- **Asbestos exposure** — 70-80% of cases
- Long latency (20-40 years)
- Amphibole fibers (crocidolite) > serpentine (chrysotile)
- Pathogenesis: Chronic mesothelial injury → inflammation → genomic instability
- **BAP1** tumor suppressor gene inactivation; **NF2** mutations

## Morphology

### Bronchial Carcinoid
**Gross:** Central (80%), endobronchial polypoid mass; highly vascular (bleeds easily on biopsy)
**Micro:**
- Nests, trabeculae, rosettes of uniform cells
- "Salt and pepper" chromatin (neuroendocrine pattern)
- Low mitotic rate (<2/10 HPF for typical; 2-10/10 HPF for atypical)
- Neuroendocrine markers: chromogranin, synaptophysin positive
- No necrosis in typical; focal necrosis in atypical

### Hamartoma
**Gross:** Well-circumscribed, round, "coin lesion" on CXR
**Micro:** Lobules of mature hyaline cartilage, fat, smooth muscle, cleft-like spaces lined by epithelium
**Imaging:** "Popcorn calcification" on CT — classic finding

### Malignant Mesothelioma
**Gross:** Thick, white, encasing pleural rind; encases lung ("frozen hemithorax")
**Micro:**
- **Epithelioid type** (60%) — tubular/papillary pattern; most common; better prognosis
- **Sarcomatoid type** (20%) — spindle cells; worse prognosis
- **Biphasic** (20%) — both patterns
- **Calretinin, CK5/6, WT-1** = positive (mesothelial markers)
- **CEA, TTF-1** = negative (rules out adenocarcinoma)
- Psammoma bodies may be present

### Metastatic Lung Tumors
**Gross:** Multiple, bilateral, well-circumscribed nodules ("cannonball" metastases)
**Micro:** Varies with primary tumor; IHC to determine origin

## Clinical Features
### Bronchial Carcinoid
- Recurrent pneumonia, cough, hemoptysis (vascular tumor)
- **Carcinoid syndrome** (rare, <5%): flushing, diarrhea, wheezing, right-sided heart valve lesions
- Typically young patients (40s)

### Hamartoma
- Usually incidental finding; asymptomatic "coin lesion"

### Mesothelioma
- Chest pain, dyspnea, recurrent pleural effusion
- History of asbestos exposure 20-40 years prior
- Rapidly progressive; poor prognosis (median survival 12-18 months)

## Diagnosis
- **Carcinoid:** Bronchoscopy (polypoid vascular mass — biopsy with caution); octreotide scan; 5-HIAA in urine
- **Hamartoma:** CT (popcorn calcification); usually no biopsy needed
- **Mesothelioma:** Thoracoscopic biopsy; IHC panel (calretinin+/CEA−); distinguish from adenocarcinoma
- **Metastases:** CT-guided biopsy; IHC to identify primary

## Management (Brief)
- **Carcinoid:** Surgical resection (bronchoplastic surgery); excellent prognosis for typical
- **Hamartoma:** Observation or excision if symptomatic
- **Mesothelioma:** Multimodal — surgery (extrapleural pneumonectomy) + chemo (pemetrexed + cisplatin); largely palliative
- **Metastases:** Treat primary; metastasectomy if limited

## Complications
1. Carcinoid syndrome (bronchial carcinoid with hepatic metastases)
2. Mesothelioma — encasement of lung, contralateral spread, peritoneal involvement
3. Asbestos bodies/plaques — markers of exposure (not tumors themselves)
4. Pathological fractures from bone metastases of lung primary
