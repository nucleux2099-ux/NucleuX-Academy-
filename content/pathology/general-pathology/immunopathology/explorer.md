# Immunopathology

## Overview

Immunopathology encompasses diseases caused by dysfunction of the immune system, including hypersensitivity reactions, autoimmune diseases, immunodeficiency disorders, and transplant rejection. These conditions arise from either overactive (hypersensitivity, autoimmunity), misdirected, or deficient immune responses. Understanding immune mechanisms is fundamental to pathology.

## Key Points

- **Hypersensitivity:** Exaggerated immune responses causing tissue injury (Types I-IV)
- **Autoimmunity:** Immune attack against self-antigens
- **Immunodeficiency:** Primary (genetic) or secondary (acquired — HIV/AIDS)
- **Transplant rejection:** Immune response against allograft
- **Key autoimmune diseases:** SLE, RA, Sjögren, scleroderma, type 1 DM

## Definition & Classification

### Hypersensitivity Reactions (Gell & Coombs Classification)

| Type | Mechanism | Timing | Examples |
|------|-----------|--------|----------|
| I (Immediate/Anaphylactic) | IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation | Minutes | Anaphylaxis, asthma, hay fever, urticaria |
| II (Cytotoxic/Antibody-mediated) | IgG/IgM against cell surface/matrix antigens | Hours | Autoimmune hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture, transfusion reactions, Rh disease |
| III (Immune complex) | Antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues | Hours-days | SLE, post-streptococcal GN, serum sickness, Arthus reaction |
| IV (Delayed/Cell-mediated) | T cell-mediated | 24-72 hours | Contact dermatitis, TB skin test, transplant rejection, type 1 DM |

### Autoimmune Diseases
- **Organ-specific:** Hashimoto thyroiditis, Graves disease, type 1 DM, myasthenia gravis, pernicious anemia
- **Systemic:** SLE, RA, systemic sclerosis (scleroderma), Sjögren syndrome, polymyositis/dermatomyositis

### Immunodeficiency Disorders
- **Primary:** X-linked agammaglobulinemia (Bruton), DiGeorge, SCID, common variable immunodeficiency, Wiskott-Aldrich, Chediak-Higashi
- **Secondary:** HIV/AIDS, malnutrition, immunosuppressive therapy, malignancy

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Type I Hypersensitivity
1. First exposure → sensitization: Allergen → APC → Th2 → IL-4 → B cell class switching to IgE
2. IgE binds Fc receptors on mast cells/basophils (sensitization)
3. Re-exposure → cross-linking of IgE → mast cell degranulation
4. **Immediate phase (minutes):** Histamine, leukotrienes → vasodilation, bronchospasm, mucus
5. **Late phase (hours):** Eosinophils, Th2 cells → sustained inflammation

### Type II Hypersensitivity
- Antibodies (IgG/IgM) directed against cell surface or ECM antigens
- Mechanisms: Complement activation + opsonization, ADCC, or antibody-mediated cellular dysfunction
- Example: Anti-GBM (Goodpasture) → linear IF pattern

### Type III Hypersensitivity
- Antigen-antibody complexes → deposit in vessels/tissues → complement activation → neutrophil recruitment → tissue damage
- Arthus reaction = localized; Serum sickness = systemic
- SLE: Anti-dsDNA → immune complexes → granular IF ("lumpy bumpy")

### Type IV Hypersensitivity
- **CD4+ T cells (delayed type):** TB skin test, contact dermatitis
- **CD8+ T cells (cytotoxic):** Viral hepatitis, transplant rejection
- No antibody involvement; takes 24-72 hours

### Autoimmunity — Mechanisms
- Loss of self-tolerance (central and peripheral)
- Molecular mimicry (rheumatic fever — strep M protein mimics cardiac antigens)
- Failure of regulatory T cells (Tregs)
- Genetic susceptibility (HLA associations: HLA-B27 and ankylosing spondylitis, HLA-DR4 and RA)
- Environmental triggers (infections, UV exposure)

### SLE (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus)
- Prototype systemic autoimmune disease
- Anti-dsDNA (specific), ANA (sensitive), anti-Smith (most specific)
- Immune complex deposition → glomerulonephritis, skin, joints, serosal surfaces
- Lupus nephritis (WHO classes I-VI): Most important prognostic factor
- Wire-loop lesion (class IV) = subendothelial immune deposits

## Morphology

### Gross
- SLE: Butterfly (malar) rash, Libman-Sacks endocarditis (vegetations on both sides of valve)
- RA: Pannus formation, joint destruction
- Scleroderma: Tight, shiny skin; CREST features

### Microscopic
- Type I: Mast cell degranulation, eosinophilic infiltrate
- Type II: Complement on cell surface (direct Coombs positive)
- Type III: Granular immune complex deposits (IF), fibrinoid necrosis of vessels
- Type IV: Lymphocytic and macrophage infiltrate, granulomas
- SLE nephritis: Mesangial, subendothelial (wire-loop), subepithelial deposits; "full house" IF (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q)

## Clinical Features

- **Type I:** Urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis (hypotension, bronchospasm, laryngeal edema)
- **SLE:** Malar rash, arthritis, nephritis, serositis, oral ulcers, photosensitivity, cytopenias, neuropsychiatric
- **RA:** Symmetric polyarthritis of small joints, morning stiffness, rheumatoid nodules
- **Scleroderma:** Raynaud, skin tightening, dysphagia, pulmonary fibrosis, renal crisis
- **HIV/AIDS:** Opportunistic infections, Kaposi sarcoma, lymphomas

## Diagnosis

- **Type I:** Skin prick test, serum IgE, tryptase (anaphylaxis)
- **Autoantibodies:** ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith (SLE); RF, anti-CCP (RA); anti-centromere (limited scleroderma); anti-Scl-70 (diffuse scleroderma)
- **Complement levels:** Low C3/C4 in SLE (consumption)
- **Immunofluorescence:** Linear (type II — Goodpasture), Granular (type III — SLE)
- **HIV:** ELISA screening → Western blot/p24 antigen confirmation; CD4 count, viral load
- **Flow cytometry:** Immunodeficiency workup

## Management

- **Type I:** Epinephrine (anaphylaxis), antihistamines, corticosteroids, desensitization
- **Autoimmune:** Corticosteroids, DMARDs (methotrexate), biologics (anti-TNF, rituximab, belimumab for SLE)
- **Immunodeficiency:** IVIG (antibody deficiency), bone marrow transplant (SCID), ART for HIV
- **Transplant rejection:** Immunosuppression (cyclosporine, tacrolimus, mycophenolate, steroids)

## Complications

- Anaphylactic shock (Type I)
- End-organ damage: Lupus nephritis → ESRD
- Increased infection risk with immunosuppression
- Lymphoma risk in chronic immune stimulation
- HIV → AIDS → opportunistic infections → death
