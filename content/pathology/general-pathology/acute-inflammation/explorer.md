# Acute Inflammation

## Overview

Acute inflammation is the rapid, early response of living vascularized tissue to injury. It is characterized by the cardinal signs described by Celsus — rubor (redness), tumor (swelling), calor (heat), dolor (pain) — and functio laesa (loss of function), added later by Virchow. The primary purpose is to deliver leukocytes and plasma proteins to the site of injury for elimination of the offending agent and initiation of repair.

## Key Points

- **Duration:** Minutes to days (short-lived)
- **Primary cells:** Neutrophils (polymorphonuclear leukocytes)
- **Vascular changes:** Vasodilation → increased permeability → stasis
- **Outcome:** Resolution, abscess formation, chronic inflammation, or scarring
- **Chemical mediators:** Histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, complement, cytokines

## Definition & Classification

Acute inflammation is the immediate and early tissue response to injurious stimuli, involving vascular and cellular events mediated by chemical mediators.

### Classification by Morphologic Pattern
1. **Serous inflammation** — thin fluid (e.g., skin blister, pleural effusion)
2. **Fibrinous inflammation** — fibrin-rich exudate (e.g., fibrinous pericarditis)
3. **Suppurative (purulent) inflammation** — pus formation (e.g., abscess)
4. **Ulcerative inflammation** — surface epithelial necrosis (e.g., peptic ulcer)

### Classification by Etiology
- Infectious (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites)
- Physical (trauma, burns, radiation, frostbite)
- Chemical (acids, alkalis, toxins)
- Immunological (hypersensitivity reactions)
- Tissue necrosis (ischemia, infarction)

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes
- Infections (most common cause)
- Tissue necrosis (MI, burns)
- Foreign bodies (splinters, sutures, dirt)
- Immune reactions (hypersensitivity)
- Physical and chemical agents

### Pathogenesis — Two Main Components

#### 1. Vascular Changes
- **Transient vasoconstriction** (seconds) → **Vasodilation** (arteriolar, then capillary)
- Increased blood flow → redness and warmth
- **Increased vascular permeability** — endothelial contraction (histamine-mediated, immediate transient response), direct endothelial injury, leukocyte-dependent injury
- Fluid exudation → edema (swelling)
- Stasis → concentration of RBCs → margination of leukocytes

#### 2. Cellular Events
- **Margination** — leukocytes move to vessel periphery
- **Rolling** — loose adhesion via selectins (E-selectin, P-selectin, L-selectin)
- **Adhesion** — firm attachment via integrins (LFA-1/ICAM-1, VLA-4/VCAM-1)
- **Transmigration (diapedesis)** — passage through endothelium via PECAM-1 (CD31)
- **Chemotaxis** — directed migration toward chemokines (C5a, LTB4, IL-8, bacterial products)
- **Phagocytosis** — recognition → engulfment → killing
  - Opsonins: IgG (Fc fragment), C3b, collectins
  - Killing: Oxygen-dependent (respiratory burst — O₂⁻, H₂O₂, HOCl via MPO) and oxygen-independent (lysozyme, defensins, lactoferrin)

### Chemical Mediators
| Mediator | Source | Action |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Histamine | Mast cells, basophils, platelets | Vasodilation, increased permeability |
| Serotonin | Platelets | Vasoconstriction |
| Prostaglandins | Arachidonic acid (COX pathway) | Pain, vasodilation, fever |
| Leukotrienes | Arachidonic acid (LOX pathway) | Chemotaxis (LTB4), permeability (LTC4, D4, E4) |
| PAF | Leukocytes, endothelium | Platelet aggregation, permeability |
| Bradykinin | Plasma (kinin system) | Pain, permeability, vasodilation |
| C3a, C5a | Complement | Chemotaxis (C5a), anaphylatoxins |
| TNF, IL-1 | Macrophages | Endothelial activation, fever, acute phase response |
| IL-8 | Macrophages | Neutrophil chemotaxis |
| Nitric oxide | Endothelium, macrophages | Vasodilation, microbicidal |

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- Redness, swelling, warmth at the affected site
- Serous: clear watery fluid (e.g., blister)
- Fibrinous: rough, shaggy fibrin coating ("bread and butter" pericarditis)
- Purulent: creamy yellow pus; abscess = walled-off collection
- Ulcer: local excavation of surface

### Microscopic Appearance
- Dilated, congested blood vessels
- Edema fluid in interstitium (eosinophilic, proteinaceous)
- **Neutrophilic infiltrate** — hallmark of acute inflammation
- Fibrin strands in fibrinous type
- Necrotic debris and neutrophils in suppurative type
- Endothelial swelling and activation

## Clinical Features

- **Local:** Redness, swelling, heat, pain, loss of function
- **Systemic:**
  - Fever (IL-1, TNF, PGE2 acting on hypothalamus)
  - Leukocytosis (neutrophilia with left shift)
  - Acute phase response (↑ CRP, ↑ fibrinogen, ↑ SAA, ↑ ESR)
  - Tachycardia, rigors, malaise
- **Severe cases:** Sepsis, septic shock, DIC

## Diagnosis

- Clinical assessment (cardinal signs)
- CBC: Neutrophilic leukocytosis with left shift
- Elevated ESR, CRP, procalcitonin
- Blood cultures if sepsis suspected
- Histopathology: Neutrophilic infiltrate with edema

## Management

- **Treat underlying cause** (antibiotics for infection, debridement for necrotic tissue)
- **Anti-inflammatory agents:** NSAIDs (COX inhibitors), corticosteroids
- **Supportive:** Rest, elevation, cold/warm compresses
- **Surgical:** Incision and drainage for abscess

## Complications

- Abscess formation
- Progression to chronic inflammation
- Excessive scarring and fibrosis
- Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS)
- Sepsis, septic shock, multi-organ failure
- Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
