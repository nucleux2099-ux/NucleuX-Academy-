# Cell Injury

## Overview

Cell injury occurs when cells are stressed beyond their adaptive capacity or exposed to inherently damaging agents. It is the fundamental basis of all disease processes. Cell injury can be reversible (if the stress is removed early) or irreversible (leading to cell death via necrosis or apoptosis). Understanding cell injury mechanisms is the cornerstone of pathology.

## Key Points

- **Reversible injury:** Cell swelling, fatty change — recoverable if stimulus removed
- **Irreversible injury:** Point of no return → necrosis or apoptosis
- **Critical targets:** Cell membrane, mitochondria, DNA, protein synthesis machinery
- **Most common cause of cell injury:** Hypoxia/ischemia
- **Two key markers of irreversibility:** Mitochondrial dysfunction and membrane damage

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Cell injury = morphologic and functional changes resulting from stress that exceeds the cell's capacity to adapt.

### Classification
1. **By reversibility:**
   - Reversible cell injury (cell swelling, fatty change)
   - Irreversible cell injury (necrosis, apoptosis)
2. **By mechanism of death:**
   - Necrosis (pathologic, always pathological)
   - Apoptosis (programmed, can be physiological or pathological)
   - Necroptosis, pyroptosis, ferroptosis (newer recognized pathways)
3. **By cause:**
   - Hypoxic/ischemic
   - Toxic (chemical)
   - Infectious
   - Immunologic
   - Genetic
   - Nutritional
   - Physical agents

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes of Cell Injury
1. **Oxygen deprivation (hypoxia)** — most common and important
   - Ischemic (reduced blood flow)
   - Hypoxemic (low blood O₂ — altitude, anemia, CO poisoning)
   - Histotoxic (cyanide — blocks cytochrome oxidase)
2. **Chemical agents** — drugs, poisons, alcohol, toxins
3. **Infectious agents** — bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
4. **Immunologic reactions** — autoimmunity, hypersensitivity
5. **Genetic defects** — inborn errors, protein misfolding
6. **Nutritional imbalances** — deficiencies and excesses
7. **Physical agents** — trauma, temperature extremes, radiation, electric shock

### Mechanisms of Cell Injury
1. **ATP depletion** (most common mechanism in ischemia)
   - ↓ Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → Na⁺ influx → cell swelling
   - ↑ Anaerobic glycolysis → lactic acid → ↓ pH → chromatin clumping
   - Detachment of ribosomes → ↓ protein synthesis
2. **Mitochondrial damage**
   - ↓ Oxidative phosphorylation → ↓ ATP
   - Formation of mitochondrial permeability transition pore → loss of membrane potential
   - Release of cytochrome c → apoptosis
3. **Calcium influx**
   - ↑ Cytosolic Ca²⁺ → activates phospholipases, proteases, endonucleases, ATPases
   - Mitochondrial Ca²⁺ accumulation → further damage
4. **Oxidative stress (ROS accumulation)**
   - Superoxide (O₂⁻), hydroxyl radical (OH·), hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)
   - Sources: Mitochondria, cytochrome P450, oxidative burst, radiation
   - Damage: Lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, DNA damage
   - Defenses: SOD, catalase, glutathione peroxidase, vitamins C & E
5. **Membrane damage**
   - Phospholipase activation → phospholipid loss
   - Cytoskeletal damage
   - Lipid peroxidation by ROS
6. **DNA and protein damage**
   - If beyond repair → apoptosis via p53

### Reversible vs Irreversible Injury

| Feature | Reversible | Irreversible |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Cell swelling | Present | Present |
| Fatty change | May be present | Present |
| Membrane blebs | Small, reversible | Rupture |
| Mitochondria | Swelling | Dense bodies, Ca²⁺ deposits |
| ER | Ribosome detachment | Disruption |
| Nucleus | Chromatin clumping | Pyknosis, karyorrhexis, karyolysis |
| Key marker | Myelin figures | Membrane disruption, mitochondrial dysfunction |

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Reversible:** Organ pallor, mild swelling (e.g., cloudy swelling of kidney)
- **Fatty change:** Yellow, greasy appearance (e.g., fatty liver)
- **Necrosis:** Depends on type (coagulative → firm; liquefactive → soft/liquefied)

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Reversible injury:**
  - Cell swelling (hydropic change) — most common morphologic change
  - Fatty change — lipid vacuoles in cytoplasm (especially liver, heart)
  - Eosinophilic cytoplasm (↓ RNA)
  - Myelin figures
- **Irreversible injury/Necrosis:**
  - Increased eosinophilia (denatured proteins)
  - Nuclear changes: Pyknosis (shrunken, dark) → Karyorrhexis (fragmentation) → Karyolysis (dissolution)
  - Cell membrane discontinuities
  - Calcium deposits in mitochondria

### Electron Microscopy
- Reversible: Mitochondrial swelling, ER dilation, ribosome detachment, membrane blebs
- Irreversible: Amorphous densities in mitochondria, membrane defects, myelin figures, nuclear changes

## Clinical Features

- Depend on the organ affected and extent of injury
- **Heart:** Chest pain, elevated troponin (myocardial injury markers)
- **Liver:** Elevated AST/ALT (hepatocyte injury markers), jaundice
- **Kidney:** Elevated creatinine, oliguria (acute tubular necrosis)
- **Brain:** Neurological deficits (stroke)
- Reversible injury → functional recovery possible
- Irreversible injury → organ dysfunction/failure

## Diagnosis

- **Serum biomarkers:** Troponin (heart), AST/ALT (liver), creatinine (kidney), LDH, CK
- **Histopathology:** H&E staining showing characteristic morphologic changes
- **Imaging:** Depending on organ (echo, CT, MRI)
- **Electron microscopy:** For ultrastructural changes (research/academic)

## Management

- **Remove the cause** (restore blood flow, stop toxic exposure)
- **Supportive care:** Maintain perfusion, oxygenation
- **Antioxidants:** Vitamins C, E, N-acetylcysteine
- **Organ-specific:** Reperfusion therapy (MI), dialysis (renal failure)
- Prevention of reperfusion injury

## Complications

- Necrosis → inflammation → scarring/fibrosis
- Organ failure (heart, liver, kidney, brain)
- Reperfusion injury (paradoxical worsening after blood flow restoration — ROS burst, complement, neutrophils)
- Calcium overload
- Systemic effects (SIRS, multi-organ failure)
