# Bone Tumors

## Overview

Bone tumors are neoplasms arising from bone, cartilage, or other mesenchymal tissues of the skeletal system. They range from benign lesions (osteochondroma, giant cell tumor) to highly malignant sarcomas (osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma). Age, location, and radiologic appearance are critical for diagnosis. Primary bone tumors are relatively rare compared to metastatic bone disease.

## Key Points

- **Most common benign bone tumor:** Osteochondroma
- **Most common primary malignant bone tumor:** Osteosarcoma (in adolescents); Multiple myeloma (overall in adults)
- **Most common bone tumor overall:** Metastatic disease
- **Diagnosis triad:** Clinical + Radiological + Histopathological
- **Key associations:** Osteosarcoma — adolescence, Paget disease, retinoblastoma (RB gene)

## Definition & Classification

### Classification by Tissue of Origin

| Tissue | Benign | Malignant |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Bone-forming | Osteoid osteoma, Osteoblastoma | Osteosarcoma |
| Cartilage-forming | Osteochondroma, Enchondroma, Chondroblastoma | Chondrosarcoma |
| Fibrous | Fibrous cortical defect, Non-ossifying fibroma | Fibrosarcoma, MFH |
| Unknown origin | Giant cell tumor (locally aggressive) | — |
| Marrow | — | Ewing sarcoma, Lymphoma, Myeloma |
| Vascular | Hemangioma | Angiosarcoma |
| Notochordal | — | Chordoma |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Risk Factors
- **Genetic:** RB mutations (osteosarcoma), Li-Fraumeni (p53), EXT1/EXT2 (hereditary multiple exostoses)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Paget disease (osteosarcoma), enchondromatosis (chondrosarcoma), bone infarcts
- **Radiation:** Post-radiation sarcomas
- **Chronic irritation:** Chronic osteomyelitis (rare squamous cell carcinoma)

### Key Molecular Pathogenesis
- **Osteosarcoma:** RB and p53 mutations
- **Ewing sarcoma:** t(11;22) — EWS-FLI1 fusion (EWSR1-FLI1)
- **Giant cell tumor:** H3F3A mutations
- **Chordoma:** Brachyury (T gene) expression

## Morphology

### Osteosarcoma
- **Gross:** Large, destructive, gray-white mass; hemorrhage and necrosis; lifts periosteum
- **Micro:** Malignant osteoblasts producing osteoid (pink, lace-like); pleomorphic, hyperchromatic nuclei; frequent mitoses
- **Radiology:** Mixed lytic-scite; Codman triangle (periosteal elevation); sunburst pattern
- **Location:** Metaphysis of long bones (distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus)
- **Age:** 10–20 years; second peak >65 years (Paget-associated)

### Ewing Sarcoma
- **Gross:** Soft, gray-white, often with necrosis and hemorrhage
- **Micro:** Sheets of small, round, blue cells with scant cytoplasm; PAS-positive (glycogen); Homer-Wright rosettes (less common than neuroblastoma)
- **IHC:** CD99 (MIC2) positive; FLI-1 positive
- **Radiology:** Permeative lytic lesion; "onion-skin" periosteal reaction
- **Location:** Diaphysis of long bones; also flat bones (pelvis, ribs)
- **Age:** 5–15 years; M > F; rare in Black populations

### Osteochondroma (Exostosis)
- **Gross:** Bony projection with cartilage cap; sessile or pedunculated
- **Micro:** Normal bone with cartilaginous cap resembling growth plate; cap <2 cm (if >2 cm, suspect malignant transformation)
- **Location:** Metaphysis near growth plate; grows away from joint

### Giant Cell Tumor (Osteoclastoma)
- **Gross:** Red-brown mass (hemorrhagic); arises in epiphysis
- **Micro:** Mononuclear stromal cells + uniformly distributed multinucleated giant cells (osteoclast-like); "egg in egg" pattern
- **Radiology:** "Soap bubble" appearance; epiphyseal, eccentric, lytic
- **Location:** Epiphysis of long bones (around knee); age 20–40

### Chondrosarcoma
- **Gross:** Lobulated, translucent, gray-white mass with calcification
- **Micro:** Malignant chondrocytes with lacunae; increased cellularity; binucleation; myxoid change
- **Location:** Pelvis, shoulder, proximal femur
- **Age:** 40–60 years; resistant to chemo/radiation

## Clinical Features

- **Pain:** Most common presenting symptom; worse at night (osteoid osteoma — relieved by aspirin)
- **Swelling/Mass:** Palpable mass, tender or non-tender
- **Pathologic fracture:** Through weakened bone
- **Systemic:** Fever, weight loss (Ewing sarcoma — may mimic osteomyelitis)
- **Restriction of movement** in adjacent joint
- **Nerve compression:** Neurological deficits if nerve involvement

## Diagnosis

- **Radiography:** First investigation — lytic vs blastic, periosteal reaction, soft tissue mass
- **MRI:** Best for soft tissue extension and marrow involvement
- **CT:** Cortical detail, matrix mineralization
- **Bone scan:** Detect skip lesions and metastases
- **Biopsy:** Core needle or open — ESSENTIAL for definitive diagnosis
- **Lab:** Alkaline phosphatase (↑ in osteosarcoma), LDH (↑ in Ewing sarcoma)
- **Molecular:** FISH for t(11;22) in Ewing; IHC for CD99

## Management

- **Osteosarcoma:** Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (MAP: Methotrexate, Adriamycin, cisPlatin) → limb salvage surgery → adjuvant chemotherapy
- **Ewing sarcoma:** Multiagent chemotherapy (VDC/IE) + surgery ± radiation
- **Chondrosarcoma:** Wide surgical excision (chemo/radio-resistant)
- **Giant cell tumor:** Curettage + cementation; denosumab (RANKL inhibitor)
- **Benign tumors:** Observation or surgical excision

## Complications

- Metastasis (lung most common — especially osteosarcoma)
- Local recurrence after surgery
- Pathologic fracture
- Malignant transformation of benign lesions (osteochondroma → chondrosarcoma)
- Chemotherapy-related toxicity (cardiotoxicity, nephrotoxicity)
- Limb loss (amputation)
