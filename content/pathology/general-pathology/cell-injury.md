# Cell Injury and Cell Death

> 📚 **Sources:** Robbins & Cotran Pathologic Basis of Disease 10th Ed, Harsh Mohan Textbook of Pathology 8th Ed
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Cell injury occurs when cells are stressed beyond their adaptive capacity or exposed to inherently damaging agents. The cellular response depends on the **type of injury**, **duration**, and **severity**. Injury can be **reversible** (cell recovers) or **irreversible** (cell dies via necrosis or apoptosis).

## Key Points

- Cell injury results from **ATP depletion**, **mitochondrial damage**, **calcium influx**, **oxidative stress**, or **membrane damage**
- **Reversible injury**: Cell swelling, fatty change - recoverable if stimulus removed
- **Irreversible injury**: Point of no return → cell death (necrosis/apoptosis)
- **Necrosis** = pathological, inflammatory; **Apoptosis** = programmed, non-inflammatory
- Most common cause of cell injury: **Hypoxia/Ischemia**

### Mechanisms of Cell Injury

#### 1. ATP Depletion
- **Most common cause**: Hypoxia/ischemia
- Effects:
  - ↓ Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → cell swelling
  - ↑ Anaerobic glycolysis → lactic acidosis → protein denaturation
  - Ribosome detachment → ↓ protein synthesis
  - Chromatin clumping

#### 2. Mitochondrial Damage
- Opens **mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT) pore**
- Releases **cytochrome c** → apoptosis
- ↓ ATP production

#### 3. Calcium Influx
- Normal cytosolic Ca²⁺: 0.1 μM
- Ischemia → ↑ Ca²⁺ to 1 μM
- Activates destructive enzymes:
  - **Phospholipases** → membrane damage
  - **Proteases** → cytoskeleton damage
  - **Endonucleases** → DNA damage
  - **ATPases** → ATP depletion

#### 4. Oxidative Stress (Free Radicals)
- **Free radicals**: O₂⁻ (superoxide), H₂O₂, OH• (hydroxyl)
- Sources: Mitochondria, inflammation, radiation, drugs
- Damage: Lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, DNA damage
- Defense: SOD, catalase, glutathione peroxidase, Vitamins E/C

#### 5. Membrane Damage
- ↓ Phospholipid synthesis (ATP depletion)
- ↑ Phospholipid breakdown (Ca²⁺ → phospholipases)
- Lipid peroxidation (free radicals)

### Morphology of Cell Injury

#### Reversible Injury
| Feature | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Cell swelling** | Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase failure |
| **Fatty change** | Impaired lipid metabolism |
| **Plasma membrane blebs** | Cytoskeleton damage |
| **ER swelling** | Ribosome detachment |
| **Myelin figures** | Membrane damage |
| **Chromatin clumping** | ↓ pH |

#### Irreversible Injury (Point of No Return)
- **Severe mitochondrial damage** (vacuolization)
- **Extensive membrane damage** (Ca²⁺ influx)
- **Lysosomal rupture** (enzyme release)

### Types of Necrosis

| Type | Morphology | Causes | Examples |
|------|------------|--------|----------|
| **Coagulative** | Cell outline preserved, "tombstone" appearance | Ischemia (except brain) | MI, renal/splenic infarct |
| **Liquefactive** | Complete digestion, pus | Brain infarct, bacterial infections | Cerebral infarct, abscess |
| **Caseous** | Cheesy, amorphous | TB, fungi | Pulmonary TB |
| **Fat** | Chalky white deposits | Pancreatitis, trauma | Acute pancreatitis |
| **Fibrinoid** | Bright pink, smudgy | Immune vasculitis, malignant HTN | Polyarteritis nodosa |
| **Gangrenous** | Coagulative + liquefactive | Ischemia + infection | Diabetic foot |

### Apoptosis vs Necrosis

| Feature | Apoptosis | Necrosis |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Trigger** | Physiologic/pathologic | Always pathologic |
| **ATP required** | Yes | No |
| **Cell size** | Shrinkage | Swelling |
| **Membrane** | Intact (blebs) | Ruptured |
| **Nucleus** | Fragmentation (karyorrhexis) | Pyknosis → karyolysis |
| **Inflammation** | Absent | Present |
| **Enzymes** | Caspases | Hydrolytic enzymes |

### Apoptosis Pathways

#### Intrinsic (Mitochondrial) Pathway
- Triggered by: DNA damage, growth factor withdrawal
- **BCL-2 family** regulators:
  - Anti-apoptotic: BCL-2, BCL-XL
  - Pro-apoptotic: BAX, BAK, BIM
- BAX/BAK → cytochrome c release → **Apoptosome** (Apaf-1 + Caspase-9) → Caspase-3

#### Extrinsic (Death Receptor) Pathway
- Death receptors: **Fas (CD95)**, **TNFR1**
- Fas + FasL → FADD → **Caspase-8** → Caspase-3

### Intracellular Accumulations

| Substance | Cause | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **Lipids** | ↑ FA, ↓ oxidation, ↓ apoprotein | Fatty liver (alcohol) |
| **Proteins** | ↑ production, abnormal folding | Russell bodies, Mallory bodies |
| **Glycogen** | Diabetes, storage diseases | von Gierke disease |
| **Pigments** | Exogenous (carbon), endogenous (lipofuscin, hemosiderin) | Anthracosis, hemochromatosis |

### Pathologic Calcification

| Type | Calcium | Tissue | Examples |
|------|---------|--------|----------|
| **Dystrophic** | Normal serum Ca²⁺ | Dead/dying tissue | Atheroma, TB, heart valves |
| **Metastatic** | Elevated serum Ca²⁺ | Normal tissue | Hyperparathyroidism, bone mets |

## Clinical Applications

1. **Myocardial Infarction**: Coagulative necrosis → troponin release → inflammatory response → scar formation
2. **Stroke**: Liquefactive necrosis in brain (rich in lipids + hydrolytic enzymes)
3. **Acetaminophen toxicity**: Free radical injury → hepatic necrosis
4. **Reperfusion injury**: Paradoxical worsening after blood flow restoration (free radicals, Ca²⁺, inflammation)

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Most common cause of cell injury** → Hypoxia
2. **Earliest morphologic change** → Cell swelling
3. **Point of no return** → Mitochondrial damage + membrane damage
4. **Coagulative necrosis** → All organs EXCEPT brain
5. **Liquefactive necrosis** → Brain + bacterial infections
6. **Caseous necrosis** → TB (pathognomonic)
7. **Fat necrosis** → Pancreatitis (saponification)
8. **Apoptosis hallmark** → Caspase activation
9. **BCL-2 overexpression** → Follicular lymphoma (anti-apoptotic)
10. **p53 function** → Induces apoptosis via BAX activation
11. **Lipofuscin** → "Wear and tear" pigment (aging)
12. **Dystrophic calcification** → Normal calcium, abnormal tissue
