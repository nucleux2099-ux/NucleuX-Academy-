# Genetic Disorders

## Overview

Genetic disorders are diseases caused by abnormalities in the genome. They range from single-gene (Mendelian) disorders to chromosomal abnormalities and complex multifactorial conditions. Understanding inheritance patterns, molecular pathogenesis, and clinical manifestations is essential for diagnosis and genetic counseling. These disorders affect ~1% of all live births.

## Key Points

- **Three major categories:** Mendelian (single-gene), chromosomal, multifactorial
- **Autosomal dominant:** Marfan, familial hypercholesterolemia, neurofibromatosis, adult PCKD
- **Autosomal recessive:** Cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, PKU, lysosomal storage diseases
- **X-linked recessive:** Hemophilia A/B, Duchenne MD, G6PD deficiency
- **Chromosomal:** Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner (45,X), Klinefelter (47,XXY)

## Definition & Classification

### Mendelian (Single-Gene) Disorders
- Mutation in a single gene following Mendelian inheritance
- **Autosomal Dominant (AD):** One mutant allele sufficient; 50% transmission; often structural proteins
- **Autosomal Recessive (AR):** Both alleles mutant; 25% risk to offspring; often enzyme deficiencies
- **X-linked Recessive (XR):** Males affected, females carriers; no male-to-male transmission

### Chromosomal Disorders
- **Numerical:** Aneuploidy (trisomy, monosomy); Polyploidy
- **Structural:** Deletion, translocation, inversion, isochromosome, ring chromosome

### Multifactorial (Complex)
- Multiple genes + environmental factors
- Examples: Diabetes, hypertension, cleft lip/palate, neural tube defects

### Single-Gene Disorders with Non-Classical Inheritance
- Trinucleotide repeats: Fragile X, Huntington disease, myotonic dystrophy
- Mitochondrial: Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (maternal inheritance)
- Genomic imprinting: Prader-Willi vs Angelman syndrome
- Mosaicism: Variable expression

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Key Autosomal Dominant Disorders

**Marfan Syndrome**
- FBN1 gene (chromosome 15) → fibrillin-1 deficiency → ↑ TGF-β signaling
- Skeletal (tall, arachnodactyly, pectus), ocular (lens subluxation — upward), cardiovascular (aortic root dilation, MVP, dissection)

**Familial Hypercholesterolemia**
- LDLR gene mutations → defective LDL receptors → ↑ serum LDL
- Heterozygotes: 2-3x ↑ cholesterol; Homozygotes: 5-6x ↑ (MI in childhood)
- Xanthomas (tendon), xanthelasma, premature atherosclerosis

**Neurofibromatosis Type 1**
- NF1 gene (chromosome 17) → neurofibromin (Ras-GAP) loss
- Café-au-lait spots (≥6), neurofibromas, Lisch nodules (iris hamartomas), optic gliomas

### Key Autosomal Recessive Disorders

**Cystic Fibrosis**
- CFTR gene (chromosome 7) → defective Cl⁻ channel → thick, viscid secretions
- ΔF508 = most common mutation (70%)
- Lungs (recurrent infections, bronchiectasis), pancreas (insufficiency), GI (meconium ileus), infertility (males)
- Sweat chloride >60 mEq/L = diagnostic

**Lysosomal Storage Diseases**
- Enzyme deficiency → substrate accumulation in lysosomes
- Gaucher (glucocerebrosidase — most common), Tay-Sachs (hexosaminidase A — cherry red spot), Niemann-Pick (sphingomyelinase), Fabry (α-galactosidase A — X-linked), Hurler/Hunter (mucopolysaccharidoses)

**Glycogen Storage Diseases**
- Von Gierke (Type I): Glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency → hepatomegaly, hypoglycemia
- Pompe (Type II): Acid maltase (lysosomal) → cardiomegaly
- McArdle (Type V): Muscle phosphorylase → exercise intolerance

### Chromosomal Disorders

**Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)**
- Most common chromosomal disorder; incidence ↑ with maternal age
- 95% trisomy (nondisjunction), 4% Robertsonian translocation, 1% mosaic
- Features: Flat facies, epicanthal folds, simian crease, duodenal atresia, ASD/VSD, ALL risk, early Alzheimer

**Turner Syndrome (45,X)**
- Most common cause of primary amenorrhea
- Short stature, webbed neck, shield chest, coarctation of aorta, streak gonads, lymphedema

**Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)**
- Most common cause of male hypogonadism
- Tall, gynecomastia, small testes, infertility, ↑ FSH/LH

## Morphology

### Gross
- Marfan: Tall habitus, long limbs, aortic dilation
- CF: Thick mucus plugging airways, pancreatic fibrosis and atrophy
- Gaucher: Massive hepatosplenomegaly, Erlenmeyer flask deformity of femur
- Down syndrome: Characteristic facies, cardiac defects

### Microscopic
- Gaucher: Crumpled tissue paper macrophages (Gaucher cells)
- Tay-Sachs: Distended neurons with whorled lamellar bodies (EM)
- Niemann-Pick: Foamy macrophages (lipid-laden)
- CF: Mucus plugging, chronic inflammation, bronchiectasis in lungs; fibrosis and fat replacement in pancreas

## Clinical Features

- Vary enormously depending on the specific disorder
- **Diagnostic clues:** Family history, consanguinity (AR), ethnic predisposition (Tay-Sachs in Ashkenazi Jews, sickle cell in African descent, CF in Northern Europeans, thalassemia in Mediterranean)
- Age of onset varies: Some present at birth, others in childhood or adulthood

## Diagnosis

- **Karyotype:** Chromosomal disorders (G-banding, FISH, chromosomal microarray)
- **Molecular/genetic testing:** PCR, DNA sequencing, MLPA
- **Enzyme assays:** Lysosomal/glycogen storage diseases
- **Newborn screening:** PKU, CF, sickle cell disease
- **Prenatal:** Amniocentesis, CVS, cell-free fetal DNA, triple/quad screen

## Management

- **Enzyme replacement therapy:** Gaucher, Fabry, Pompe
- **Substrate reduction:** Miglustat (Gaucher)
- **Gene-specific:** CFTR modulators (ivacaftor, lumacaftor) for CF
- **Supportive:** Multidisciplinary care, genetic counseling
- **Bone marrow transplant:** Some storage diseases
- **Prenatal counseling and screening**

## Complications

- Progressive organ damage (storage diseases)
- Cardiovascular complications (Marfan — dissection, FH — MI)
- Intellectual disability (Down, storage diseases)
- Recurrent infections (CF, immune deficiencies)
- Malignancy risk (Down syndrome — ALL, NF1 — MPNST)
- Reduced life expectancy in many conditions
