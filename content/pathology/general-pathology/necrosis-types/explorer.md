# Types of Necrosis

## Overview

Necrosis is the morphologic pattern of cell death resulting from irreversible cell injury in living tissue. It is always pathological and elicits an inflammatory response. Different types of necrosis have characteristic morphologic appearances that are diagnostically useful and correlate with specific underlying causes and affected tissues.

## Key Points

- **Necrosis = always pathological** (vs apoptosis which can be physiological)
- **Six major types:** Coagulative, liquefactive, caseous, fat, fibrinoid, gangrenous
- **Coagulative necrosis:** Most common type; cell outlines preserved
- **Liquefactive necrosis:** Brain, bacterial infections (abscess)
- **Caseous necrosis:** TB (granulomatous diseases)
- **Necrosis always triggers inflammation** (unlike apoptosis)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Mechanism | Organs/Conditions | Key Feature |
|------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Coagulative | Protein denaturation > enzymatic digestion | Heart, kidney, spleen | Ghost outlines preserved |
| Liquefactive | Enzymatic digestion > denaturation | Brain, abscess | Pus/liquid mass |
| Caseous | Combination; incomplete digestion | TB, fungal | Cheese-like, amorphous |
| Fat necrosis | Lipase action on fat | Pancreas, breast | Chalky white (saponification) |
| Fibrinoid | Immune complex/complement in vessel walls | Vasculitis, malignant HTN | Bright pink fibrin in walls |
| Gangrenous | Clinical term; coagulative + secondary | Extremities, bowel | Dry (coagulative) or wet (+ liquefactive) |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Coagulative Necrosis
- Denaturation of structural and enzymatic proteins
- Prevents enzymatic digestion → ghost outlines persist for days
- Occurs in most organs EXCEPT brain
- Most commonly due to ischemia/infarction

### Liquefactive Necrosis
- Enzymatic digestion predominates → tissue transforms into liquid viscous mass
- **Brain:** Rich in lipids and lysosomal enzymes; ischemia → liquefaction
- **Bacterial infections:** Neutrophil enzymes digest tissue → pus (abscess)
- Abscess = focal liquefactive necrosis surrounded by neutrophils

### Caseous Necrosis
- Distinctive form seen in granulomatous inflammation (especially TB)
- Combination of coagulative and liquefactive features
- Name derives from "cheese-like" gross appearance
- Surrounded by granulomatous inflammatory border (epithelioid macrophages, giant cells)

### Fat Necrosis
- **Enzymatic (pancreatic):** Release of pancreatic lipases → digestion of peripancreatic and omental fat → free fatty acids combine with calcium → saponification (chalky white deposits)
- **Traumatic (breast):** Direct trauma → fat necrosis → fibrosis and calcification

### Fibrinoid Necrosis
- Deposition of fibrin-like proteinaceous material in vessel walls
- Seen in immune-mediated vasculitis (Type III hypersensitivity) and malignant hypertension
- Bright pink, smudgy material on H&E ("fibrinoid" = fibrin-like)
- Immune complexes + complement + plasma proteins in wall

### Gangrenous Necrosis
- Clinical (not histologic) term
- **Dry gangrene:** Coagulative necrosis of extremity (arterial occlusion, DM) → black, dry, mummified
- **Wet gangrene:** Dry gangrene + bacterial superinfection → liquefactive necrosis → foul-smelling, swollen
- **Gas gangrene:** Wet gangrene caused by Clostridium perfringens → gas in tissues (crepitus)

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Coagulative:** Firm, pale tissue; shape preserved; wedge-shaped infarcts
- **Liquefactive:** Soft, creamy (pus in abscess); cystic cavity in brain
- **Caseous:** Soft, white-yellow, "cheesy," friable material
- **Fat necrosis:** Chalky white areas (saponification) in omentum/pancreas
- **Fibrinoid:** Not grossly visible; identified on microscopy
- **Dry gangrene:** Black, dry, shrunken, well-demarcated
- **Wet gangrene:** Swollen, dark, foul-smelling, blurred margins

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Coagulative:** Ghost outlines of cells (preserved architecture), eosinophilic cytoplasm, nuclear changes (pyknosis, karyorrhexis, karyolysis), neutrophilic infiltrate at margins
- **Liquefactive:** Complete dissolution of tissue; neutrophils and macrophages in necrotic debris
- **Caseous:** Amorphous, granular, eosinophilic debris without cell outlines; surrounded by epithelioid macrophages and giant cells (granuloma)
- **Fat necrosis:** Shadowy outlines of fat cells, basophilic calcium deposits (saponification), surrounding macrophages and inflammation
- **Fibrinoid:** Bright pink, homogeneous, smudgy material in vessel walls; may see nuclear debris (leukocytoclastic)

## Clinical Features

- **MI (coagulative):** Chest pain, troponin elevation, ECG changes
- **Brain infarct (liquefactive):** Focal neurological deficits
- **TB (caseous):** Cough, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss
- **Acute pancreatitis (fat necrosis):** Severe epigastric pain, elevated lipase/amylase, Grey Turner/Cullen signs
- **Gangrene:** Pain, blackening of extremity, systemic toxicity in wet gangrene

## Diagnosis

- **Histopathology:** H&E staining — definitive identification of necrosis type
- **Special stains:** Ziehl-Neelsen for TB (caseous), Gram stain for bacteria (liquefactive)
- **Imaging:** CT/MRI for infarcts, ultrasound for abscess
- **Lab:** Biomarkers (troponin, lipase, LDH)

## Management

- **Coagulative (infarction):** Reperfusion, supportive care
- **Liquefactive (abscess):** Drainage + antibiotics
- **Caseous (TB):** Anti-tubercular therapy
- **Fat necrosis:** Supportive (pancreatitis), excision if breast lump
- **Gangrene:** Debridement/amputation, antibiotics, hyperbaric O₂ (gas gangrene)

## Complications

- Infarction → organ dysfunction/failure
- Abscess → sepsis, fistula formation
- Caseous necrosis → cavitation, dissemination
- Fat necrosis → hypocalcemia (calcium trapped in saponification)
- Wet gangrene → septicemia → death
