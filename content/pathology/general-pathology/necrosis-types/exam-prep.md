# Types of Necrosis — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

Six types: Coagulative (most common, cell outlines preserved), Liquefactive (brain, abscess), Caseous (TB), Fat (pancreas — saponification), Fibrinoid (vasculitis — pink vessel walls), Gangrenous (dry = coagulative, wet = + liquefactive, gas = Clostridium). Necrosis is always pathological and triggers inflammation.

## High Yield Points

★ Coagulative necrosis: Protein denaturation preserves ghost outlines; seen in all organs EXCEPT brain
★ Brain infarct = liquefactive necrosis (rich in lipids and lysosomal enzymes)
★ Caseous necrosis = TB hallmark; amorphous granular debris + granuloma
★ Fat necrosis: Saponification = fatty acids + calcium → chalky white deposits; causes hypocalcemia
★ Fibrinoid necrosis: Bright pink material in vessel walls; seen in malignant HTN and vasculitis
★ Gas gangrene: Clostridium perfringens → crepitus; emergency debridement needed
★ Nuclear changes: Pyknosis (shrinkage) → Karyorrhexis (fragmentation) → Karyolysis (dissolution)
★ Abscess = localized collection of pus = liquefactive necrosis
★ Dry gangrene = coagulative (well-demarcated); Wet gangrene = + bacterial infection (poorly demarcated)
★ Necrosis triggers inflammation; Apoptosis does NOT (usually)

## Mnemonics

- **Types of necrosis:** "Carefully Listen, Children, Fat Finds Gangrene" — Coagulative, Liquefactive, Caseous, Fat, Fibrinoid, Gangrenous
- **Coagulative organs:** "All organs except Brain" (Brain = Liquefactive)
- **Nuclear changes:** "PKK = Pyknosis, Karyorrhexis, Karyolysis" (shrink, fragment, dissolve)
- **Fat necrosis calcium:** "FAT + Ca = SOAP" (saponification)
