# Cellular Adaptations

## Overview

Cellular adaptations are reversible functional and structural changes by which cells respond to changes in their environment. These represent an intermediate state between the normal, unstressed cell and the injured, overstressed cell. Understanding adaptations is essential as they explain many clinical findings and can predispose to neoplasia.

## Key Points

- **Four main adaptations:** Hypertrophy, hyperplasia, atrophy, metaplasia
- **All are reversible** if the stimulus is removed
- **Hypertrophy:** Increase in cell size
- **Hyperplasia:** Increase in cell number
- **Atrophy:** Decrease in cell size
- **Metaplasia:** Change from one mature cell type to another
- **Dysplasia:** Disordered growth (pre-neoplastic, not a true adaptation)

## Definition & Classification

### Hypertrophy
- Increase in cell size → increase in organ size
- **Physiologic:** Uterine enlargement in pregnancy (hormonal), skeletal muscle in exercise (increased workload)
- **Pathologic:** Left ventricular hypertrophy in hypertension, bladder wall hypertrophy in BPH
- Mechanism: Increased protein synthesis; activation of growth factors (IGF-1), mechanical sensors, GATA4/NFAT transcription factors
- Occurs in cells incapable of division (cardiac myocytes, skeletal muscle)

### Hyperplasia
- Increase in cell number (in cells capable of division)
- **Physiologic:**
  - Hormonal: Breast at puberty/pregnancy, endometrial proliferation
  - Compensatory: Liver regeneration after partial hepatectomy
- **Pathologic:**
  - Endometrial hyperplasia (excess estrogen) — may progress to carcinoma
  - BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia)
  - Skin warts (viral — HPV-induced epidermal hyperplasia)
- Mechanism: Growth factor–driven cell proliferation
- Key difference from neoplasia: Hyperplasia is controlled and regresses when stimulus removed

### Atrophy
- Decrease in cell size and function; shrinkage of tissue/organ
- **Physiologic:** Thymus atrophy after puberty, uterine atrophy post-menopause
- **Pathologic:**
  - Disuse atrophy (immobilized limb)
  - Denervation atrophy (polio, nerve injury)
  - Pressure atrophy (hydronephrosis, tumor compression)
  - Ischemic atrophy (atherosclerosis)
  - Nutritional/starvation atrophy (marasmus, cachexia)
  - Endocrine atrophy (loss of hormonal stimulation — adrenal atrophy with exogenous steroid use)
- Mechanism: ↓ Protein synthesis + ↑ protein degradation (ubiquitin-proteasome pathway); autophagy
- Lipofuscin granules accumulate ("wear and tear" pigment) → brown atrophy

### Metaplasia
- Replacement of one differentiated cell type by another
- **Reversible** if the stimulus is removed
- **Examples:**
  - Squamous metaplasia: Respiratory epithelium in smokers (columnar → squamous)
  - Squamous metaplasia of cervix (columnar → squamous at transformation zone)
  - Barrett esophagus: Squamous → intestinal columnar (gastric acid reflux) — premalignant
  - Osseous metaplasia: Soft tissue → bone formation at injury sites
  - Apocrine metaplasia of breast
- Mechanism: Reprogramming of tissue stem cells by cytokines, growth factors, and transcription factors
- Significance: Metaplasia is **not** direct transformation of one cell into another — it's stem cell redifferentiation

### Dysplasia (Pre-neoplastic)
- Disordered, potentially pre-malignant change
- Loss of uniformity and architectural orientation
- Features: Pleomorphism, hyperchromasia, increased mitoses, loss of polarity
- Common in cervix (CIN), Barrett esophagus
- NOT a true adaptation but often discussed alongside

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

- Adaptations are driven by altered functional demand, hormonal stimulation, or chronic irritation
- Involve changes in gene expression (transcription factors, growth factors)
- Controlled by growth factor signaling (EGF, IGF, FGF), hormone receptors
- Metaplasia involves epigenetic reprogramming of stem cells

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Hypertrophy:** Enlarged organ (thick ventricular wall, large uterus)
- **Hyperplasia:** Enlarged organ with increased cellularity
- **Atrophy:** Small, shrunken organ (small kidney in ischemia, thin limb)
- **Metaplasia:** Grossly may not be visible; Barrett esophagus shows salmon-colored patches

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Hypertrophy:** Enlarged cells with increased cytoplasm, large nuclei
- **Hyperplasia:** Increased cell number, maintained architecture
- **Atrophy:** Small cells, lipofuscin (brown pigment), autophagic vacuoles
- **Metaplasia:** One mature epithelium replaced by another (e.g., pseudostratified columnar → stratified squamous)
- **Dysplasia:** Pleomorphism, loss of polarity, hyperchromatic nuclei, increased N:C ratio

## Clinical Features

- **Hypertrophy:** Heart: S4 gallop, LVH on ECG; Bladder: trabeculation
- **Hyperplasia:** Endometrial: abnormal uterine bleeding; Prostate: urinary symptoms
- **Atrophy:** Muscle wasting, organ shrinkage, functional decline
- **Metaplasia:** Often asymptomatic; Barrett esophagus: GERD symptoms + cancer risk
- **Dysplasia:** Detected on screening (Pap smear, endoscopy)

## Diagnosis

- **Histopathology:** Biopsy and microscopic examination
- **Imaging:** Echocardiography (LVH), ultrasound (organ size)
- **Endoscopy:** Barrett esophagus, cervical metaplasia
- **Cytology:** Pap smear for cervical dysplasia/metaplasia

## Management

- **Remove the inciting stimulus** — most important step
- Hypertrophy: Treat hypertension (LVH regression), relieve obstruction
- Hyperplasia: Hormonal management (progesterone for endometrial hyperplasia)
- Atrophy: Address underlying cause (nutrition, hormone replacement, mobilization)
- Metaplasia: Surveillance (Barrett — endoscopy), smoking cessation
- Dysplasia: Close follow-up, excision if high-grade

## Complications

- Hypertrophy → eventual decompensation (heart failure)
- Hyperplasia → may predispose to dysplasia → neoplasia (endometrial)
- Atrophy → functional insufficiency
- Metaplasia → dysplasia → carcinoma (Barrett → adenocarcinoma; squamous metaplasia → squamous carcinoma)
