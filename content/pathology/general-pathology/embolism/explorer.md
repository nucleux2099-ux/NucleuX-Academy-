# Embolism

## Overview

An embolus is a detached intravascular mass (solid, liquid, or gaseous) carried by the blood to a distant site where it lodges and causes vascular occlusion. Thromboembolism (from thrombi) accounts for >99% of all emboli. Pulmonary embolism (PE) is the most clinically significant form, while systemic emboli most commonly arise from intracardiac thrombi.

## Key Points

- **Most common type:** Thromboembolism (from DVT → PE)
- **Most common source of PE:** Deep veins of lower extremity (above knee)
- **Most common source of systemic emboli:** Left heart (mural thrombi, atrial fibrillation)
- **Other types:** Fat, air, amniotic fluid, tumor, septic, foreign body
- **Saddle embolus:** Straddles the pulmonary artery bifurcation → sudden death

## Definition & Classification

### Types of Emboli
1. **Pulmonary thromboembolism** — from DVT
2. **Systemic thromboembolism** — from left heart or aorta
3. **Fat embolism** — fractures of long bones/pelvis
4. **Air embolism** — surgery, trauma, IV procedures
5. **Amniotic fluid embolism** — labor and delivery
6. **Tumor embolism** — renal cell carcinoma (IVC), HCC
7. **Septic embolism** — infective endocarditis
8. **Paradoxical embolism** — venous embolus crossing to arterial via ASD/VSD/PFO

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Pulmonary Thromboembolism
- Source: >95% from deep leg veins (iliac, femoral, popliteal)
- DVT risk factors = Virchow triad: Stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability
- Thrombus detaches → travels through IVC → right heart → pulmonary arteries
- Outcome depends on size: small → asymptomatic; medium → pulmonary infarction; large/saddle → sudden death

### Fat Embolism
- Occurs 1-3 days after long bone fractures (especially femur, pelvis)
- Mechanism: Marrow fat enters ruptured veins + biochemical theory (free fatty acids cause endothelial injury)
- Triad: Respiratory distress + neurologic symptoms + petechial rash (chest, axillae, conjunctivae)

### Air Embolism
- >100 mL air needed to cause clinical effects
- Sources: Surgery (head/neck), trauma, central line insertion, criminal abortion
- **Decompression sickness (Caisson disease):** Rapid ascent → nitrogen bubbles → "the bends" (joint pain), respiratory "chokes," CNS effects

### Amniotic Fluid Embolism
- Rare but catastrophic (80% mortality)
- Occurs during labor/C-section
- Amniotic fluid enters maternal circulation → pulmonary vascular obstruction + DIC
- Histology: Squamous cells, lanugo hair, mucin in pulmonary vasculature

## Morphology

### Pulmonary Embolism
- **Gross:** Thrombus in pulmonary arteries; may straddle bifurcation (saddle embolus). Pulmonary infarct: hemorrhagic, wedge-shaped, base at pleura (Hampton hump on CXR)
- **Micro:** Coagulative necrosis with hemorrhage (red/hemorrhagic infarct due to dual blood supply); thrombus with lines of Zahn

### Fat Embolism
- **Gross:** Petechiae on skin, brain, lungs
- **Micro:** Fat globules in pulmonary capillaries (Oil Red O stain on frozen section); cerebral microhemorrhages

### Air Embolism
- **Gross:** Frothy blood in right ventricle (at autopsy)
- **Micro:** Vascular spaces with empty spaces (dissolved during processing)

## Clinical Features

### Pulmonary Embolism
- **Small (60-80%):** Silent, asymptomatic
- **Medium (10-15%):** Pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, dyspnea (pulmonary infarction)
- **Large/Saddle (5%):** Sudden death, acute right heart failure (cor pulmonale), cardiovascular collapse
- **Recurrent small:** Chronic pulmonary hypertension

### Fat Embolism Syndrome
- Onset 1-3 days post-fracture
- Classic triad: Respiratory insufficiency + cerebral involvement + petechial rash

### Amniotic Fluid Embolism
- Sudden dyspnea, cyanosis, hypotension, DIC during/after labor → often fatal

## Diagnosis

- **PE:** CT pulmonary angiography (gold standard), D-dimer (screening), V/Q scan, echocardiography
- **Fat embolism:** Clinical diagnosis (triad); fat globules in urine/sputum; Oil Red O stain
- **Air embolism:** Clinical history; frothy blood on aspiration
- **Amniotic fluid embolism:** Clinical diagnosis; fetal squamous cells in maternal pulmonary vasculature (autopsy)

## Management

- **PE:** Anticoagulation (heparin → warfarin/DOACs), thrombolysis (massive PE), embolectomy (surgical), IVC filter (recurrent/contraindication to anticoagulation)
- **Prevention:** Prophylactic anticoagulation, early mobilization, compression stockings
- **Fat embolism:** Supportive (O₂, ventilatory support); early fracture fixation for prevention
- **Air embolism:** Left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg position; aspiration of air via central catheter
- **Amniotic fluid embolism:** Aggressive supportive care; treat DIC

## Complications

- Pulmonary infarction
- Acute cor pulmonale and right heart failure
- Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH)
- DIC (amniotic fluid embolism, fat embolism)
- Death (saddle PE, amniotic fluid embolism)
- Post-phlebitic syndrome (chronic venous insufficiency)
