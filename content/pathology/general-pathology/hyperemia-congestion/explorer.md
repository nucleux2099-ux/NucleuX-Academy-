# Hyperemia & Congestion

## Overview

Hyperemia and congestion both refer to increased blood volume in tissues but differ in mechanism. Hyperemia is an active process due to arteriolar dilation increasing blood inflow, while congestion is a passive process due to impaired venous outflow. Congestion can be acute or chronic, localized or systemic, and has important clinical and pathological consequences.

## Key Points

- **Hyperemia:** Active, arteriolar dilation, red tissue (inflammation, exercise)
- **Congestion:** Passive, impaired venous drainage, blue-red cyanotic tissue
- **Chronic pulmonary congestion:** Heart failure cells (hemosiderin-laden macrophages), brown induration
- **Chronic hepatic congestion:** Nutmeg liver, centrilobular necrosis
- **Chronic splenic congestion:** Congestive splenomegaly (portal hypertension)

## Definition & Classification

### Hyperemia (Active)
- Increased blood flow due to arteriolar dilation
- Physiologic: Exercise (skeletal muscle), blushing
- Pathologic: Inflammation (acute inflammation — one of the cardinal signs)
- Tissue appears red (erythema) due to increased oxygenated blood

### Congestion (Passive)
- Reduced venous outflow → blood pools in capillaries and venules
- **Localized:** DVT, compressed vein, torsion
- **Systemic:** CHF (right-sided → systemic; left-sided → pulmonary)
- Tissue appears blue-red (cyanosis) due to deoxygenated blood accumulation
- Acute congestion → chronic congestion → fibrosis

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes of Congestion
- **Left heart failure → pulmonary congestion**
- **Right heart failure → systemic congestion** (liver, spleen, peripheral)
- **Portal hypertension (cirrhosis) → splenic congestion, esophageal varices**
- **Local venous obstruction** (DVT, tumor compression, torsion)

### Pathologic Sequence in Chronic Congestion
1. Venous stasis → capillary dilation → edema
2. RBC extravasation → hemosiderin deposition (from RBC breakdown)
3. Hypoxia → parenchymal cell atrophy and necrosis
4. Chronic hypoxia → fibrosis

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Hyperemia:** Red, warm, swollen tissue
- **Acute congestion:** Blue-red, swollen, wet tissue
- **Chronic pulmonary congestion:** Heavy, firm, brown lungs (brown induration)
- **Chronic hepatic congestion:** Nutmeg liver — alternating red-brown (congested centrilobular zones) and yellow-tan (periportal fatty change) areas on cut surface
- **Chronic splenic congestion:** Enlarged spleen (up to 1000g in portal HTN), firm, dark red

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Acute pulmonary congestion:** Distended alveolar capillaries, alveolar septal edema, focal intra-alveolar hemorrhage
- **Chronic pulmonary congestion:** Thickened fibrotic septa, hemosiderin-laden macrophages (heart failure cells), brown induration
- **Acute hepatic congestion:** Centrilobular congestion, centrilobular hepatocyte degeneration
- **Chronic hepatic congestion:** Centrilobular necrosis and hemorrhage, periportal hepatocytes spared (or fatty change), cardiac cirrhosis in severe cases
- **Chronic splenic congestion:** Dilated sinusoids, focal hemorrhages, Gamna-Gandy bodies (iron and calcium encrusted fibrotic nodules), thickened capsule

## Clinical Features

- **Pulmonary congestion:** Dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, basal crackles, pink frothy sputum (acute pulmonary edema)
- **Hepatic congestion:** Hepatomegaly, tender liver, elevated JVP, hepatojugular reflux, ascites
- **Splenic congestion:** Splenomegaly, hypersplenism (pancytopenia)
- **Peripheral congestion:** Dependent edema, cyanosis

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical:** JVP assessment, hepatomegaly, peripheral edema
- **Imaging:** CXR (pulmonary congestion, Kerley B lines), echocardiography (LV/RV function)
- **Lab:** BNP/NT-proBNP, liver function tests
- **Histopathology:** Heart failure cells (sputum cytology or lung biopsy), nutmeg pattern on liver biopsy
- **Prussian blue stain:** Confirms hemosiderin in macrophages

## Management

- **Treat underlying cause:** CHF management (diuretics, ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers)
- **Reduce volume overload:** Diuretics (furosemide), salt restriction
- **Portal hypertension:** Beta-blockers (propranolol), TIPS, band ligation for varices
- **Positioning:** Elevate head for pulmonary congestion
- **Oxygen therapy** for pulmonary congestion

## Complications

- Chronic pulmonary congestion → pulmonary fibrosis (brown induration)
- Chronic hepatic congestion → cardiac cirrhosis (rare but possible)
- Gamna-Gandy bodies in spleen
- Portal hypertension → variceal bleeding, ascites
- Hypersplenism → cytopenias
