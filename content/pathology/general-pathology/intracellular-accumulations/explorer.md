# Intracellular Accumulations

## Overview

Intracellular accumulations occur when cells store excessive amounts of various substances — normal or abnormal — in the cytoplasm or organelles. This may result from overproduction, defective metabolism, failure to degrade, or deposition of exogenous material. These accumulations can be harmless or can cause varying degrees of cell injury.

## Key Points

- **Four categories:** Lipids, proteins, glycogen, pigments (endogenous and exogenous)
- **Fatty change (steatosis):** Most common accumulation, especially in liver
- **Pigments:** Hemosiderin, lipofuscin, melanin, bilirubin (endogenous); carbon, tattoo ink (exogenous)
- **Storage diseases:** Genetic enzyme deficiencies → lysosomal/cytoplasmic accumulation
- **Pathologic calcification:** Dystrophic (dead tissue) and metastatic (hypercalcemia)

## Definition & Classification

### Categories of Accumulation
1. **Normal endogenous substance in excess:** Fat (steatosis), glycogen, proteins
2. **Abnormal endogenous substance:** Abnormal protein (α1-antitrypsin, amyloid), products of inborn errors
3. **Exogenous substance that cannot be degraded:** Carbon (anthracosis), silica, tattoo pigments
4. **Normal substance that accumulates due to enzyme deficiency:** Lysosomal storage diseases

### Lipid Accumulations
- **Steatosis (fatty change):** Triglyceride accumulation, mainly in liver
- **Cholesterol accumulation:** Atherosclerosis (foam cells), xanthomas
- **Cholesterol clefts:** In necrotic tissue

### Protein Accumulations
- Hyaline droplets in renal tubular cells (proteinuria — reabsorbed protein)
- Russell bodies (plasma cells — excess immunoglobulin)
- Mallory-Denk bodies (damaged intermediate filaments — alcoholic hepatitis)
- α1-antitrypsin globules in hepatocytes (PAS-positive, diastase-resistant)

### Glycogen Accumulation
- Diabetes mellitus: Glycogen in renal tubular cells (Armanni-Ebstein change), hepatocytes, beta cells
- Glycogen storage diseases (von Gierke, Pompe, McArdle)

### Pigment Accumulations
**Endogenous:**
- Lipofuscin: "Wear and tear" pigment; yellow-brown, granular; in atrophic cells (heart, liver); not harmful
- Melanin: Brown-black pigment; melanocytes; ↑ in Addison disease
- Hemosiderin: Golden-yellow, iron-containing; localized (bruise) or systemic (hemosiderosis, hemochromatosis)
- Bilirubin: Yellow; jaundice when serum >2 mg/dL

**Exogenous:**
- Carbon (anthracosis): Most common exogenous pigment; coal dust in lungs and lymph nodes
- Tattoo pigments: Phagocytosed by dermal macrophages; permanent
- Silica, asbestos: Inhaled particles → fibrosis

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Fatty Change (Steatosis) in Liver
- Causes: Alcohol (most common), obesity, diabetes, toxins (CCl₄), starvation, hypoxia
- Mechanism: Imbalance between fatty acid uptake/synthesis and export/oxidation
  1. ↑ Free fatty acid delivery to liver (obesity, starvation)
  2. ↑ De novo lipogenesis (alcohol, high carb diet)
  3. ↓ Fatty acid oxidation (alcohol — NADH/NAD ratio ↑, mitochondrial damage)
  4. ↓ Apolipoprotein synthesis → ↓ VLDL export (CCl₄, protein malnutrition)

### Pathologic Calcification
- **Dystrophic calcification:** Calcium deposits in DEAD/DYING tissue; serum calcium is NORMAL
  - Examples: Atherosclerotic plaques, damaged heart valves, caseous necrosis (TB), fat necrosis, psammoma bodies
- **Metastatic calcification:** Calcium deposits in NORMAL tissue; serum calcium is ELEVATED
  - Causes: Hyperparathyroidism, vitamin D excess, bone destruction (metastases, myeloma), renal failure (secondary HPT)
  - Sites: Lung, kidney, stomach, blood vessels (acid-secreting organs with alkaline microenvironment)

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Fatty liver:** Enlarged (up to 6 kg), yellow, greasy, soft
- **Anthracosis:** Black pigment in lungs and hilar lymph nodes
- **Hemosiderosis:** Brown discoloration of organs
- **Dystrophic calcification:** Hard, white, gritty deposits

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Steatosis:** Clear vacuoles in cytoplasm (lipid dissolved during processing); macrovesicular (large single droplet, nucleus displaced) vs microvesicular (multiple small droplets, central nucleus)
- **Lipofuscin:** Fine, golden-brown, perinuclear granules; PAS-positive, acid-fast
- **Hemosiderin:** Golden-brown granules; Prussian blue (Perl's) stain positive
- **Carbon:** Black granular pigment in macrophages and interstitium
- **Calcification:** Basophilic (dark blue/purple) on H&E; Von Kossa stain (black)
- **Psammoma bodies:** Laminated calcified concretions — papillary thyroid ca, meningioma, serous papillary ovarian ca

## Clinical Features

- **Steatosis:** Often asymptomatic; hepatomegaly, mild elevation of transaminases; can progress to steatohepatitis → cirrhosis
- **Hemochromatosis:** Bronze diabetes (skin pigmentation + DM + cirrhosis), cardiac failure, arthritis
- **Hemosiderosis:** Tissue damage if extensive (iron overload)
- **Anthracosis:** Usually asymptomatic; coal workers' pneumoconiosis if extensive
- **Calcification:** Depends on location (valvular → stenosis, vascular → stiffness)

## Diagnosis

- **Steatosis:** Ultrasound (bright liver), liver biopsy (gold standard), FibroScan
- **Hemosiderin:** Prussian blue (Perl's) stain; serum ferritin, transferrin saturation
- **Calcification:** X-ray, CT (radiopaque deposits), histology (Von Kossa, Alizarin red)
- **Storage diseases:** Enzyme assays, genetic testing, EM
- **α1-antitrypsin:** PAS-positive, diastase-resistant globules in hepatocytes

## Management

- **Steatosis:** Treat underlying cause (alcohol cessation, weight loss, diabetic control)
- **Hemochromatosis:** Phlebotomy, iron chelation (deferoxamine)
- **Storage diseases:** Enzyme replacement therapy (Gaucher, Fabry)
- **Calcification:** Treat underlying cause; surgical if symptomatic

## Complications

- Steatosis → steatohepatitis → cirrhosis → HCC
- Iron overload → organ failure (heart, liver, pancreas)
- Calcification → organ dysfunction (valvular stenosis, vascular stiffness)
- Storage diseases → progressive organ damage
