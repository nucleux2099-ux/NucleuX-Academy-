# Infarction — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

Infarction = ischemic necrosis from vascular occlusion. White infarcts in solid end-artery organs (heart, kidney, spleen). Red infarcts in dual supply/loose/venous occlusion (lung, intestine). Brain = liquefactive necrosis (exception). MI temporal changes are high-yield. Most vulnerable to rupture at 1-3 weeks (granulation tissue stage).

## High Yield Points

★ White infarcts: Heart, kidney, spleen (solid, end-artery)
★ Red infarcts: Lung, intestine, testis (torsion), brain (can be either)
★ ALL venous infarcts are hemorrhagic (red)
★ Brain infarcts → liquefactive necrosis (exception to coagulative rule)
★ MI rupture risk highest at 3-7 days to 3 weeks (soft, granulation tissue)
★ Earliest histologic change in MI: Wavy fibers (0-4 hrs)
★ Neurons die in 3-4 minutes; cardiac myocytes in 20-30 minutes
★ Pulmonary infarct: Hemorrhagic, wedge-shaped, pleura-based (dual supply usually protects)
★ Intestinal infarction: "Pain out of proportion to examination" = mesenteric ischemia
★ Tetrazolium stain: Detects MI within 2-3 hours (infarcted area stays pale, viable = brick red)

## Mnemonics

- **White infarcts:** "HSK" — Heart, Spleen, Kidney (solid, single supply)
- **Red infarcts:** "LIT" — Lung, Intestine, Testis (loose/dual/venous)
- **MI timeline:** "0-Wavy, 1day-Neutro, 3day-Macro, 1wk-Granulation, 1month-Scar"
- **Factors affecting infarction:** "FACTOR" — Flow (collaterals), Artery type (end vs dual), Content of O₂, Tissue vulnerability, Occlusion speed, Rate of development
