# Infarction

## Overview

Infarction is an area of ischemic necrosis caused by occlusion of the arterial supply or venous drainage of a tissue. It is one of the most common and clinically important pathologic processes. Myocardial infarction, cerebral infarction (stroke), pulmonary infarction, and intestinal infarction are major causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide.

## Key Points

- **Definition:** Area of coagulative necrosis due to ischemia (except brain — liquefactive)
- **Most common cause:** Thrombosis/thromboembolism (arterial occlusion)
- **Two types:** White (pale/anemic) and Red (hemorrhagic)
- **Organ vulnerability:** Heart, brain, kidney, spleen (end arteries); Lung, intestine, liver (dual supply)
- **Most infarcts are wedge-shaped** with the occluded vessel at the apex

## Definition & Classification

### White (Pale/Anemic) Infarcts
- Occur in **solid organs with single (end-artery) blood supply**
- Organs: Heart, kidney, spleen, brain
- Mechanism: Arterial occlusion → no alternative blood supply → pale coagulative necrosis

### Red (Hemorrhagic) Infarcts
- Occur in: (a) organs with dual blood supply, (b) loose tissues, (c) venous occlusion, (d) reperfusion
- Organs: Lung, intestine, brain (can be either), testis (torsion — venous)
- Mechanism: Blood seeps into necrotic area from collateral/dual supply or venous drainage
- **All venous infarcts are hemorrhagic**

### Septic Infarcts
- Caused by infected emboli (infective endocarditis)
- Convert into abscesses

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Causes of Infarction
1. **Thrombosis** — most common (coronary, cerebral, mesenteric)
2. **Thromboembolism** — PE, systemic emboli from heart
3. **Vasospasm** — Prinzmetal angina, cocaine
4. **Atherosclerosis** with superimposed thrombosis
5. **External compression** — tumor, herniation, torsion (testicular, ovarian)
6. **Vasculitis** — polyarteritis nodosa

### Factors Determining Infarct Severity
1. **Nature of blood supply:** End-artery (kidney, spleen) → infarction; Dual supply (lung, liver) → usually spared
2. **Rate of occlusion:** Sudden = worse; Gradual allows collateral development
3. **Tissue vulnerability to hypoxia:** Neurons (3-4 min), cardiac myocytes (20-30 min), fibroblasts (hours)
4. **Oxygen content of blood:** Anemia, cyanosis worsen outcome
5. **Collateral circulation:** Presence of anastomoses protects

### Coagulative vs Liquefactive Necrosis in Infarction
- Most infarcts → coagulative necrosis (cell outlines preserved)
- **Brain infarct → liquefactive necrosis** (rich in lipid, lysosomal enzymes)
- Septic infarct → suppurative necrosis (abscess)

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **White infarct:** Wedge-shaped, pale, firm area with the base at organ surface and apex at occluded vessel; surrounded by hyperemic rim
  - Kidney: Well-defined pale wedge
  - Spleen: Well-defined pale wedge
  - Heart: Mottled (initially), then pale and depressed
- **Red infarct:** Wedge-shaped but hemorrhagic (dark red/purple)
  - Lung: Hemorrhagic, wedge-shaped, base at pleura (Hampton hump)
  - Intestine: Dark, hemorrhagic, edematous bowel wall

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Early (hours):** Coagulative necrosis — ghost outlines of cells, eosinophilic cytoplasm, nuclear pyknosis/karyorrhexis/karyolysis
- **24-72 hours:** Neutrophilic infiltration at margins (acute inflammation)
- **Days-weeks:** Granulation tissue at periphery, macrophages clear debris
- **Weeks-months:** Fibrous scar formation (organization)
- **Brain:** Liquefactive necrosis → cystic cavity (gliosis)
- **Heart (MI):** Coagulative necrosis → granulation tissue (1-2 weeks) → fibrous scar (6-8 weeks)

### Myocardial Infarction — Temporal Changes
| Time | Gross | Microscopy |
|------|-------|------------|
| 0-4 hrs | None (dark on tetrazolium stain) | Wavy fibers (earliest) |
| 4-12 hrs | Dark mottling | Coagulative necrosis begins, edema |
| 12-24 hrs | Dark mottling | Coagulative necrosis, neutrophil infiltration begins |
| 1-3 days | Yellow center, hyperemic border | Extensive coagulative necrosis + neutrophils |
| 3-7 days | Yellow, softening | Macrophage ingestion, granulation tissue begins at margins |
| 1-3 weeks | Red-gray borders | Granulation tissue (MOST SUSCEPTIBLE to rupture) |
| Weeks-months | White scar | Dense collagenous scar |

## Clinical Features

- **MI:** Chest pain, diaphoresis, elevated troponin/CK-MB, ECG changes
- **Cerebral infarct:** Focal neurological deficits (hemiplegia, aphasia)
- **Pulmonary infarct:** Pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, dyspnea
- **Renal infarct:** Flank pain, hematuria, elevated LDH
- **Intestinal infarct:** Severe abdominal pain ("pain out of proportion to exam"), bloody diarrhea, peritonitis
- **Splenic infarct:** LUQ pain, friction rub

## Diagnosis

- **Biomarkers:** Troponin (heart), LDH (kidney, general)
- **Imaging:** CT/MRI (brain, kidney), CT angiography (PE, mesenteric ischemia), echocardiography
- **ECG:** ST elevation (STEMI), ST depression, T wave changes
- **Histopathology:** Coagulative necrosis with inflammatory infiltrate

## Management

- **MI:** Reperfusion (PCI/thrombolysis), antiplatelet, anticoagulation, statins, ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers
- **Stroke:** tPA within window, thrombectomy, secondary prevention
- **PE:** Anticoagulation, thrombolysis (massive), embolectomy
- **Mesenteric infarction:** Emergency surgery, embolectomy, bowel resection
- **Supportive care** for all types

## Complications

- MI: Arrhythmia, heart failure, rupture (free wall, septum, papillary muscle), pericarditis, mural thrombus, aneurysm
- Stroke: Cerebral edema, herniation, hemorrhagic transformation
- Intestinal infarction: Peritonitis, sepsis, short bowel syndrome
- Pulmonary infarction: Pleural effusion, chronic pulmonary hypertension
- Gangrene (dry or wet) in extremity infarction
