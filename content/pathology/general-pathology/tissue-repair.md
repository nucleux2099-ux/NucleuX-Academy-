# Tissue Repair and Wound Healing

> 📚 **Sources:** Robbins & Cotran Pathologic Basis of Disease 10th Ed, Harsh Mohan Textbook of Pathology 8th Ed
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Tissue repair restores tissue architecture and function after injury through two processes: **regeneration** (replacement with same cell type) and **scar formation/fibrosis** (replacement with connective tissue). The outcome depends on the **proliferative capacity of cells**, **integrity of extracellular matrix (ECM)**, and **resolution of inflammation**.

## Key Points

- **Regeneration** = parenchymal cell replacement (best outcome)
- **Scar formation** = connective tissue replacement (when regeneration not possible)
- Cell proliferative capacity: **Labile** > **Stable** > **Permanent**
- Growth factors (EGF, PDGF, VEGF, TGF-β) drive repair
- Wound healing: **Primary intention** (clean, sutured) vs **Secondary intention** (large defect, granulation tissue)

## Detailed Content

### Cell Proliferative Capacity

| Category | Definition | Examples | Regeneration |
|----------|------------|----------|--------------|
| **Labile** | Continuously dividing | Skin, GI mucosa, bone marrow, respiratory epithelium | Excellent |
| **Stable (Quiescent)** | G₀ but can divide when stimulated | Hepatocytes, renal tubules, fibroblasts, endothelium | Good |
| **Permanent** | Cannot divide (post-mitotic) | Neurons, cardiac myocytes, skeletal muscle | None (scar) |

### Stem Cells in Regeneration

| Type | Potency | Location |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Embryonic stem cells** | Pluripotent | Inner cell mass |
| **Adult tissue stem cells** | Multipotent | Tissue-specific niches |
| **Bone marrow stem cells** | HSC, MSC | Bone marrow |
| **Induced pluripotent (iPSC)** | Pluripotent | Reprogrammed somatic cells |

### Growth Factors in Repair

| Growth Factor | Source | Target | Function |
|---------------|--------|--------|----------|
| **EGF** | Platelets, macrophages | Epithelial, mesenchymal | Epithelial proliferation, migration |
| **TGF-α** | Macrophages, keratinocytes | Epithelial | Similar to EGF |
| **HGF** | Mesenchymal cells | Hepatocytes, epithelial | Liver regeneration |
| **VEGF** | Mesenchymal cells | Endothelial | **Angiogenesis** |
| **PDGF** | Platelets, macrophages | Fibroblasts, smooth muscle | Fibroblast proliferation, chemotaxis |
| **FGF** | Macrophages, mast cells | Fibroblasts, endothelial | Angiogenesis, fibroblast proliferation |
| **TGF-β** | Platelets, macrophages | Fibroblasts | **Fibrosis**, ECM synthesis, anti-inflammatory |
| **KGF (FGF-7)** | Fibroblasts | Keratinocytes | Re-epithelialization |

### Extracellular Matrix (ECM)

#### Components

| Type | Examples | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Fibrous proteins** | Collagen, elastin | Structural strength, elasticity |
| **Adhesive glycoproteins** | Fibronectin, laminin | Cell-ECM attachment |
| **Proteoglycans** | Heparan sulfate, chondroitin | Hydration, growth factor reservoir |

#### Collagen Types (Mnemonic: "**Be 2 Cool, Read Books**")

| Type | Location | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| **I** | Bone, skin, tendon | Most abundant (90%), scar tissue |
| **II** | Cartilage, vitreous | "Cartwo-lage" |
| **III** | Blood vessels, uterus, GI | Reticulin, early wound healing |
| **IV** | Basement membrane | Non-fibrillar, "4 under the floor" |
| **V** | Interstitial | Minor component |

### Angiogenesis

**Process:** New vessel formation from existing vessels

**Steps:**
1. Vasodilation and ↑ permeability (VEGF, NO)
2. Basement membrane degradation (MMPs)
3. Endothelial migration and proliferation
4. Tube formation (lumen creation)
5. Pericyte recruitment (PDGF)
6. Vessel maturation

**Key regulators:**
- **Pro-angiogenic:** VEGF, FGF, angiopoietins
- **Anti-angiogenic:** Thrombospondin, angiostatin, endostatin

**Clinical:** Anti-VEGF drugs (bevacizumab) for cancer, macular degeneration

### Wound Healing

#### Cutaneous Wound Healing Phases

| Phase | Time | Events | Key Cells |
|-------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Hemostasis** | Minutes | Vasoconstriction, platelet plug, fibrin clot | Platelets |
| **Inflammation** | 1-3 days | Neutrophils (day 1), macrophages (day 2-3) | Neutrophils, macrophages |
| **Proliferation** | 3-21 days | Granulation tissue, angiogenesis, re-epithelialization | Fibroblasts, endothelium, keratinocytes |
| **Remodeling** | Weeks-months | Collagen maturation (III→I), scar contraction | Fibroblasts, myofibroblasts |

#### Primary Intention vs Secondary Intention

| Feature | Primary Intention | Secondary Intention |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Wound** | Clean, approximated edges | Large defect, edges apart |
| **Granulation tissue** | Minimal | Abundant |
| **Contraction** | Minimal | Significant (myofibroblasts) |
| **Scar** | Thin, linear | Large, irregular |
| **Time** | Faster | Slower |
| **Example** | Surgical incision | Ulcer, abscess cavity |

#### Wound Strength

| Time | Tensile Strength | Events |
|------|------------------|--------|
| 1 week | 10% | Fibrin → collagen III |
| 1 month | 50% | Collagen cross-linking |
| 3 months | 70-80% | Collagen remodeling (max) |
| Never | 100% | Scar never equals normal skin |

### Granulation Tissue

**Definition:** Pink, soft, granular tissue filling wound defects

**Composition:**
- **New capillaries** (angiogenesis)
- **Fibroblasts** (collagen synthesis)
- **Myofibroblasts** (wound contraction)
- **Inflammatory cells** (macrophages)
- **Loose ECM** (collagen III, fibronectin)

**Histology:** "Red granular" due to numerous capillaries

### Scar Formation and Remodeling

**Fibrosis process:**
1. Macrophage activation → TGF-β release
2. Fibroblast proliferation and migration
3. Collagen synthesis (initially type III)
4. Collagen cross-linking (strength)
5. Remodeling: Type III → Type I (by MMPs)
6. Wound contraction (myofibroblasts - α-SMA positive)

### Abnormal Wound Healing

#### Deficient Healing

| Factor | Mechanism | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Infection** | Prolongs inflammation | Surgical site infection |
| **Diabetes** | Microangiopathy, neuropathy, immune defect | Diabetic foot ulcers |
| **Malnutrition** | ↓ Protein, Vitamin C (collagen), Zinc | Scurvy (poor wound healing) |
| **Steroids** | ↓ Inflammation, ↓ collagen synthesis | Chronic steroid use |
| **Poor perfusion** | Ischemia | Peripheral vascular disease |
| **Foreign bodies** | Persistent inflammation | Sutures, debris |
| **Mechanical factors** | Wound tension, movement | Over joints |

#### Excessive Healing

| Type | Definition | Location | Recurrence after excision |
|------|------------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Hypertrophic scar** | Raised scar within wound margins | Any wound | Rare |
| **Keloid** | Scar extending beyond wound margins | Earlobes, shoulders, chest | Common |

**Keloid features:**
- More common in dark-skinned individuals
- Genetic predisposition
- Excessive TGF-β, collagen I/III
- Treatment: Steroid injection, silicone, radiation

#### Contracture

- Excessive contraction by myofibroblasts
- Common in burns
- Can cause joint deformity

### Liver Regeneration

**Model:** Partial hepatectomy (2/3 resection)

**Process:**
1. **Priming phase:** TNF-α, IL-6 from Kupffer cells
2. **Growth phase:** HGF, TGF-α from stellate cells and hepatocytes
3. **Termination:** TGF-β limits proliferation

**Key points:**
- Hepatocytes are **stable cells** (normally quiescent)
- Can regenerate up to 60-70% loss
- Regeneration stops when original mass restored ("hepatostat")

### Fibrosis in Chronic Disease

**Pathogenesis:**
1. Persistent injury → chronic inflammation
2. Macrophage activation (M2) → TGF-β
3. Fibroblast activation → myofibroblasts
4. Excessive collagen deposition
5. Tissue architecture disruption → organ dysfunction

**Examples:**
- **Liver cirrhosis:** Chronic hepatitis → stellate cell activation → fibrosis
- **Pulmonary fibrosis:** Idiopathic or post-inflammatory
- **Cardiac fibrosis:** Post-MI remodeling

## Clinical Applications

1. **Diabetic wound care:** Optimize glucose, debridement, growth factor dressings
2. **Keloid treatment:** Intralesional steroids, silicone sheets, radiation
3. **Anti-fibrotic therapy:** Pirfenidone, nintedanib (for IPF)
4. **Liver transplant:** When regeneration capacity exceeded (cirrhosis)
5. **Vitamin C deficiency (Scurvy):** Impaired collagen hydroxylation → poor wound healing

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Labile cells** → Continuously dividing (skin, GI, bone marrow)
2. **Permanent cells** → Neurons, cardiac myocytes (no regeneration)
3. **Most important growth factor for angiogenesis** → VEGF
4. **Most important growth factor for fibrosis** → TGF-β
5. **Key cell in wound contraction** → Myofibroblast (α-SMA positive)
6. **Granulation tissue composition** → New vessels + fibroblasts + macrophages
7. **First collagen in wound** → Type III (replaced by Type I)
8. **Maximum wound strength** → 70-80% of normal (never 100%)
9. **Keloid vs Hypertrophic scar** → Keloid extends beyond wound margins
10. **Vitamin C role** → Hydroxylation of proline/lysine in collagen
11. **Zinc role** → Cofactor for collagenases (MMPs)
12. **Factors delaying wound healing** → Infection, diabetes, steroids, malnutrition
