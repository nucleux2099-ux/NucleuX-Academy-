# Granulomatous Inflammation

## Overview

Granulomatous inflammation is a distinctive pattern of chronic inflammation characterized by aggregates of activated macrophages (epithelioid cells) with or without multinucleated giant cells. It represents an attempt to wall off agents that are difficult to eradicate. It is the hallmark of tuberculosis and several other important diseases.

## Key Points

- **Granuloma:** Collection of epithelioid macrophages, often surrounded by lymphocytes
- **Two types:** Caseating (TB) and non-caseating (sarcoidosis)
- **Epithelioid cells:** Activated macrophages with abundant pink cytoplasm resembling epithelial cells
- **Giant cells:** Langhans type (horseshoe nuclei), foreign body type (scattered nuclei)
- **Key diseases:** TB, leprosy, sarcoidosis, fungal infections, foreign body reactions, Crohn disease

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
A granuloma is a focal aggregate of epithelioid macrophages (activated macrophages) surrounded by a collar of lymphocytes and occasionally rimmed by fibroblasts and connective tissue.

### Types
1. **Immune granulomas (hypersensitivity type):**
   - Caseating: TB, fungal infections
   - Non-caseating: Sarcoidosis, berylliosis, Crohn disease
   - T cell–mediated (Th1 → IFN-γ → macrophage activation)
2. **Foreign body granulomas:**
   - Around inert material (sutures, talc, silicone, beryllium)
   - Foreign body giant cells (nuclei scattered randomly)
   - No T cell–mediated response

### Diseases Causing Granulomatous Inflammation

| Disease | Caseation | Special Features |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Tuberculosis | Yes (caseous necrosis) | Caseating granuloma, AFB positive |
| Sarcoidosis | No | Non-caseating, Schaumann bodies, asteroid bodies |
| Leprosy | Variable | Tuberculoid (well-formed) vs Lepromatous (ill-formed, foamy macrophages) |
| Fungal infections | Variable | Histoplasma, Coccidioides, Blastomyces |
| Crohn disease | No | Non-caseating, skip lesions, transmural |
| Cat-scratch disease | Yes (stellate) | Bartonella henselae |
| Foreign body | No | Foreign material visible, no T cells |
| Wegener (GPA) | Yes | Necrotizing granulomas + vasculitis |
| Syphilis | Yes (gumma) | Tertiary syphilis |

## Etiology & Pathogenesis

### Immune-Mediated Granuloma Formation
1. Macrophages ingest persistent agent (e.g., M. tuberculosis)
2. Antigen presentation to CD4+ T cells
3. Th1 response → IL-2, IFN-γ secretion
4. **IFN-γ activates macrophages** → epithelioid transformation
5. TNF → macrophage recruitment and maintenance of granuloma
6. Macrophages fuse → multinucleated giant cells
7. Peripheral T lymphocyte cuff and fibroblast rim
8. Central caseous necrosis may develop (TB)

### Key Cytokines
- **IFN-γ:** Most important for macrophage activation
- **TNF:** Essential for granuloma formation and maintenance
  - Anti-TNF therapy (infliximab) → reactivation of TB
- **IL-12:** Drives Th1 differentiation
- **IL-2:** T cell proliferation

## Morphology

### Gross Appearance
- **Caseating:** Yellow-white, cheese-like necrotic centers (caseous necrosis)
- **Non-caseating:** Firm, gray-white nodules without necrosis
- **TB:** Ghon complex (lung), miliary pattern, cavitation
- **Sarcoidosis:** Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, skin nodules

### Microscopic Appearance
- **Central zone:** Epithelioid macrophages ± caseous necrosis
- **Epithelioid cells:** Elongated macrophages with pale pink, granular cytoplasm, vesicular nuclei
- **Giant cells:**
  - Langhans giant cells: Nuclei arranged in horseshoe/peripheral crescentic pattern (TB)
  - Foreign body giant cells: Nuclei scattered haphazardly
  - Touton giant cells: Lipid-laden, ring of nuclei (fat necrosis, xanthomas)
- **Peripheral zone:** Collar of lymphocytes, plasma cells, fibroblasts
- **Special bodies (sarcoidosis):** Schaumann bodies (laminated calcium), asteroid bodies (star-shaped inclusions) — not pathognomonic
- **TB:** Central caseous necrosis (amorphous, eosinophilic, granular debris)

## Clinical Features

- Depend on the underlying cause and organ involved
- **TB:** Chronic cough, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss
- **Sarcoidosis:** Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, erythema nodosum, uveitis, hypercalcemia, ↑ ACE
- **Crohn disease:** Diarrhea, abdominal pain, fistulae, skip lesions
- **Leprosy:** Skin lesions, nerve thickening, sensory loss
- **Foreign body:** Local mass/swelling at site of foreign material

## Diagnosis

- **Histopathology:** Biopsy showing granulomas (core needle or excisional)
- **Special stains:** Ziehl-Neelsen/AFB stain (TB), GMS/PAS (fungi), polarized light (foreign body)
- **Culture:** M. tuberculosis (Löwenstein-Jensen media), fungal cultures
- **Tuberculin test/IGRA:** TB exposure
- **Serum ACE, calcium:** Sarcoidosis
- **Kveim test:** Sarcoidosis (historical, rarely used)

## Management

- **TB:** Anti-tubercular therapy (HRZE regimen)
- **Sarcoidosis:** Corticosteroids for symptomatic/progressive disease
- **Crohn:** 5-ASA, steroids, immunomodulators, anti-TNF
- **Fungal:** Antifungals (amphotericin B, itraconazole)
- **Foreign body:** Surgical removal
- **Leprosy:** MDT (dapsone + rifampicin ± clofazimine)

## Complications

- Fibrosis and scarring (end-stage granulomatous disease)
- Cavitation (TB → hemoptysis, secondary infection)
- Organ dysfunction (pulmonary fibrosis, renal failure in sarcoidosis)
- Dissemination (miliary TB, disseminated fungal infection)
- Amyloidosis (AA) in chronic granulomatous diseases
