# Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) / Hyaline Membrane Disease

## Overview
Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS), also known as hyaline membrane disease (HMD), is the most common cause of respiratory distress in preterm neonates. It results from surfactant deficiency leading to alveolar collapse, decreased compliance, and progressive respiratory failure. Incidence is inversely proportional to gestational age (60-80% at <28 weeks, 15-30% at 32-36 weeks). Antenatal corticosteroids and postnatal surfactant therapy have dramatically improved outcomes.

## Key Points
- Most common respiratory cause of death in preterm neonates
- Due to surfactant deficiency (produced by Type II pneumocytes from 24 weeks, mature by 35 weeks)
- Incidence inversely proportional to gestational age
- CXR: diffuse reticulogranular (ground glass) pattern + air bronchograms
- Treatment: CPAP + surfactant replacement therapy
- Prevention: antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone/dexamethasone)

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Progressive respiratory failure in preterm neonates due to quantitative and qualitative deficiency of pulmonary surfactant, presenting within the first 6 hours of life.

### Surfactant
- **Produced by**: Type II alveolar pneumocytes (from ~24 weeks, mature by 35 weeks)
- **Composition**: 80% phospholipids (DPPC/lecithin is major component), 10% protein (SP-A, B, C, D), 10% neutral lipids
- **Function**: Reduces alveolar surface tension → prevents alveolar collapse at end-expiration
- **L/S ratio** ≥2 indicates lung maturity (amniotic fluid test)
- **Phosphatidylglycerol (PG)** presence = lung maturity (most reliable)

### Risk Factors
**Increased risk**: Prematurity (most important), male sex, cesarean section (no labor), maternal diabetes, second twin, perinatal asphyxia, hypothermia
**Decreased risk**: IUGR/SGA (chronic stress → accelerated maturation), PROM, maternal hypertension, antenatal steroids

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Pathophysiology
Surfactant deficiency → increased surface tension → alveolar collapse (atelectasis) → decreased functional residual capacity → ventilation-perfusion mismatch → hypoxia + hypercarbia + acidosis → pulmonary vasoconstriction → decreased pulmonary blood flow → ischemic damage to alveolar epithelium → plasma protein leak into alveoli → hyaline membranes (fibrin + necrotic cells)

**Vicious cycle**: Atelectasis → hypoxia → vasoconstriction → more ischemia → more surfactant dysfunction

### Hyaline Membranes
Eosinophilic membranes lining the alveolar ducts and respiratory bronchioles, composed of fibrin, cellular debris, and plasma proteins. Pathognomonic finding on histology.

## Clinical Features
- **Onset**: Within minutes to 6 hours of birth (typically within first hour)
- **Progressive respiratory distress**:
  - Tachypnea (>60/min)
  - Nasal flaring
  - Grunting (characteristic — maintaining PEEP)
  - Intercostal/subcostal retractions
  - Cyanosis
- **Diminished breath sounds**
- **Worsens over 48-72 hours** (surfactant reaches nadir), then improves if infant survives
- **Silverman-Anderson score**: Assesses severity of respiratory distress (0-10; higher = worse)

### Silverman-Anderson Score Parameters
1. Upper chest movement
2. Lower chest retractions
3. Xiphoid retractions
4. Nasal flaring
5. Expiratory grunt

## Diagnosis
1. **Clinical**: Preterm + respiratory distress within 6 hours
2. **Chest X-ray**:
   - Diffuse reticulogranular (ground-glass) pattern bilaterally
   - Air bronchograms
   - Decreased lung volume
   - "White-out" lungs in severe cases (Grade 4)
   - **Grading**: I (mild reticulogranular) → IV (complete white-out, no cardiac border)
3. **ABG**: Hypoxemia, hypercarbia, metabolic acidosis
4. **L/S ratio**: <2 (if assessed prenatally in amniotic fluid)
5. **Shake test (foam stability test)**: Negative in RDS

### CXR Grading
| Grade | Findings |
|-------|----------|
| I | Fine reticulogranular pattern |
| II | Air bronchograms visible |
| III | Cardiac border obscured |
| IV | Complete "white-out" |

## Management

### Prevention (Most Important)
- **Antenatal corticosteroids**: Betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses (24 hours apart) OR Dexamethasone 6 mg IM × 4 doses (12 hours apart)
- Given to mothers at risk of preterm delivery between 24-34 weeks
- Maximum benefit if delivered 24 hours to 7 days after completion
- Accelerates Type II pneumocyte maturation → increased surfactant production

### Respiratory Support
- **CPAP** (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure): First-line for mild-moderate RDS
  - Maintains FRC, prevents alveolar collapse
  - INSURE technique: INtubate → SURfactant → Extubate to CPAP
  - LISA: Less Invasive Surfactant Administration (thin catheter, spontaneous breathing)
- **Mechanical ventilation**: For severe RDS, apnea, respiratory failure

### Surfactant Replacement Therapy
- **Natural surfactants**: Beractant (Survanta), Poractant alfa (Curosurf), Calfactant (Infasurf)
- **Dose**: Poractant 200 mg/kg (initial), 100 mg/kg (subsequent); given via ET tube
- **Timing**: Early (within 2 hours) better than late rescue
- **Effect**: Rapid improvement in oxygenation and compliance within minutes

### Supportive
- Thermoregulation (incubator)
- Fluid management (restrictive initially, 60-80 mL/kg/day)
- Nutrition (TPN initially, trophic feeds when stable)
- Monitoring: continuous SpO₂, ABG, vitals
- Antibiotics (rule out sepsis/pneumonia)

## Complications
- **Acute**: Pneumothorax, pulmonary interstitial emphysema, pulmonary hemorrhage, PDA, NEC, IVH
- **Chronic**: Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) — O₂ dependence at 36 weeks PMA
- **Retinopathy of prematurity** (excessive O₂)
