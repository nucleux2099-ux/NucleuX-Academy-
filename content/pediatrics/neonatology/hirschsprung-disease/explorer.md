# Hirschsprung Disease

## Overview
Hirschsprung disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon) is characterized by the absence of ganglion cells in the myenteric (Auerbach's) and submucosal (Meissner's) plexuses of the distal bowel, resulting in functional obstruction. It is the most common cause of neonatal intestinal obstruction of the large bowel. The aganglionic segment is always distal, starting from the internal anal sphincter and extending proximally for a variable distance.

## Key Points
- Absence of ganglion cells in distal bowel → functional obstruction
- Rectosigmoid involved in 80% cases (short-segment)
- Failure to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth is the hallmark
- Diagnosis: rectal suction biopsy (gold standard)
- Barium enema shows transition zone
- Treatment: Pull-through surgery (Swenson/Duhamel/Soave)

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Congenital absence of parasympathetic ganglion cells in the distal intestine due to failure of cranio-caudal migration of neural crest cells during 5th-12th weeks of gestation.

### Classification by Length
| Type | Extent | Frequency |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Short-segment (classical) | Rectosigmoid | 80% |
| Long-segment | Beyond sigmoid | 15% |
| Total colonic | Entire colon ± terminal ileum | 5% |
| Ultra-short segment | Only internal anal sphincter | Rare |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- Failure of migration of neural crest cells (vagal crest) in craniocaudal direction
- Arrest of migration between 5th-12th week of gestation
- The earlier the arrest, the longer the aganglionic segment
- **Genetics**: RET proto-oncogene mutations (most common), EDNRB, EDN3
- Male:Female = 4:1 (short segment); equal in long segment
- Associated with Down syndrome (2-10%), MEN 2A, Waardenburg syndrome

### Pathophysiology
Absent ganglion cells → absent peristalsis in affected segment → functional obstruction → proximal bowel dilates (megacolon) → The AGANGLIONIC segment is NARROW, proximal normal bowel is DILATED

**Key**: The dilated segment is NORMAL bowel; the narrow segment is DISEASED

## Clinical Features

### Neonatal Presentation (most common)
- **Failure to pass meconium within 24-48 hours** (present in 90%)
- Abdominal distension (progressive)
- Bilious vomiting
- Reluctance to feed
- Digital rectal exam → "explosive gush" of stool and gas (blast sign)

### Older Infants/Children
- Chronic constipation (not responding to dietary measures)
- Failure to thrive, malnutrition
- Abdominal distension
- Ribbon-like/pellet stools
- Visible peristalsis
- Empty rectum on digital examination (key differentiator from functional constipation)

### Hirschsprung-Associated Enterocolitis (HAEC)
- Most feared complication (can be fatal)
- Explosive foul-smelling diarrhea, abdominal distension, fever
- Can progress to toxic megacolon, perforation, sepsis
- Most common cause of death in Hirschsprung disease

## Diagnosis
1. **Plain X-ray abdomen**: Dilated loops of bowel, absence of rectal gas
2. **Barium enema** (unprepared): 
   - Transition zone (narrow distal → dilated proximal)
   - Delayed evacuation of barium at 24 hours (retained barium)
   - Irregular contractions in aganglionic segment
3. **Anorectal manometry**: Absent recto-anal inhibitory reflex (RAIR) — normal: internal sphincter relaxes on rectal distension
4. **Rectal suction biopsy** (GOLD STANDARD):
   - Absence of ganglion cells
   - Hypertrophied nerve trunks
   - Increased acetylcholinesterase (AChE) activity
5. **Full-thickness biopsy**: If suction biopsy inconclusive

## Management

### Initial Management
- NPO, nasogastric decompression
- IV fluids, antibiotics if enterocolitis
- Rectal washouts/irrigations (decompress bowel, prevent enterocolitis)

### Definitive Surgery (Pull-Through Procedures)
| Procedure | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Swenson** | Original; resect aganglionic segment, anastomose normal bowel to anus |
| **Duhamel** | Retrorectal pull-through; normal bowel brought behind aganglionic rectum |
| **Soave** | Endorectal pull-through; normal bowel pulled through muscular cuff of rectum |

- **Transanal pull-through (TEPT)**: Preferred modern approach; entirely transanal, minimal invasive
- **Timing**: Can be done as single-stage procedure in neonates or with initial colostomy → definitive surgery later
- **Frozen section**: Intraoperative to confirm ganglion cells at the margin

### HAEC Management
- Emergency: NPO, IV antibiotics (metronidazole + gentamicin), rectal irrigations
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation
- May need emergency colostomy

## Complications
- Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC) — 20-30%
- Anastomotic leak/stricture post-surgery
- Chronic constipation post-surgery
- Fecal soiling/incontinence
- Recurrent enterocolitis
