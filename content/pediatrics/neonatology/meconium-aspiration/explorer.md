# Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)

## Overview
Meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS) occurs when a neonate aspirates meconium-stained amniotic fluid (MSAF) into the airways, causing respiratory distress. Meconium passage in utero is a sign of fetal distress, most commonly in term/post-term babies. MAS is a significant cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality, with complications including air leaks and persistent pulmonary hypertension (PPHN).

## Key Points
- Occurs almost exclusively in term/post-term babies
- Meconium passage is rare before 34 weeks gestation
- MSAF occurs in 10-15% of deliveries; MAS develops in 5% of those
- Ball-valve mechanism causes air trapping
- Chemical pneumonitis from bile salts and pancreatic enzymes in meconium
- PPHN is the most feared complication
- Current guidelines: do NOT routinely intubate and suction even in non-vigorous babies

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
Respiratory distress in a neonate born through meconium-stained amniotic fluid (MSAF) with characteristic radiographic findings, when symptoms cannot be otherwise explained.

### MSAF Grading
- **Thin/light**: Yellow-green tinge
- **Moderate**: Green, opaque
- **Thick/pea soup**: Dark green, thick, particulate

### MAS Severity
| Severity | Features |
|----------|----------|
| Mild | Requires <40% O₂ for <48 hours |
| Moderate | Requires >40% O₂ for >48 hours |
| Severe | Requires mechanical ventilation, PPHN |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Why Meconium is Passed
- Fetal hypoxia → vagal stimulation → relaxation of anal sphincter + increased gut peristalsis
- Maturation of GI tract (rare <34 weeks; incidence increases with GA)
- Post-term pregnancy (highest risk)

### Pathophysiology of Lung Injury
1. **Mechanical obstruction**: Ball-valve mechanism
   - Complete obstruction → atelectasis
   - Partial obstruction → air trapping → air leak (pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum)
2. **Chemical pneumonitis**: Bile salts, pancreatic enzymes → inflammation, edema
3. **Surfactant inactivation**: Meconium inhibits surfactant function
4. **Infection**: Meconium promotes bacterial growth
5. **PPHN**: Hypoxia + acidosis → pulmonary vasoconstriction → right-to-left shunting

### Gasping
Fetal distress → gasping in utero → aspiration of MSAF into distal airways

## Clinical Features
- Born through thick MSAF (skin, nails, umbilical cord stained)
- **Respiratory distress**: Tachypnea, grunting, nasal flaring, retractions within hours of birth
- **Barrel-shaped chest** (air trapping/hyperinflation)
- Coarse crackles and rhonchi on auscultation
- Cyanosis
- Meconium staining of skin, nails, cord (yellow-green)
- Post-maturity features may be present (peeling skin, long nails)

### Signs of PPHN
- Severe cyanosis disproportionate to respiratory distress
- Pre-ductal vs post-ductal SpO₂ difference >10%
- Loud S2, tricuspid regurgitation murmur

## Diagnosis
1. **Clinical**: Respiratory distress + MSAF + characteristic findings
2. **Chest X-ray**:
   - Coarse, patchy, asymmetric opacities
   - Hyperinflation (flattened diaphragms, >9 ribs visible)
   - Air leaks (pneumothorax in 15-30%)
   - Areas of atelectasis interspersed with hyperinflation
3. **ABG**: Hypoxemia, hypercarbia, mixed acidosis
4. **Pre/post-ductal SpO₂**: Difference >10% suggests PPHN
5. **Echocardiography**: PPHN assessment, rule out structural heart disease
6. **Blood culture**: Rule out sepsis

## Management

### Delivery Room Management (Updated NRP 2020)
- **Vigorous baby** (good tone, crying, HR >100): Routine care, no special suctioning
- **Non-vigorous baby**: PPV with initial steps; **routine intubation and tracheal suctioning is NO LONGER recommended** (NRP 2020 update)
- Intubate only if airway obstruction prevents effective ventilation

### Respiratory Support
- **Supplemental O₂**: To maintain SpO₂ >90%
- **CPAP**: For mild-moderate cases
- **Mechanical ventilation**: For severe MAS; avoid high PEEP (air leak risk)
- **High-frequency ventilation**: For refractory cases
- **Surfactant therapy**: May be beneficial (meconium inactivates surfactant)

### PPHN Management
- **Inhaled nitric oxide (iNO)**: Selective pulmonary vasodilator (20 ppm)
- **Sildenafil**: PDE5 inhibitor (oral/IV alternative)
- **ECMO**: Last resort for refractory PPHN
- Correct acidosis, maintain adequate oxygenation

### Supportive Care
- Minimal handling (stimulation worsens PPHN)
- NPO initially, IV fluids
- Antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin) — meconium promotes infection
- Correct metabolic derangements

## Complications
- **Pneumothorax/pneumomediastinum** (15-30%)
- **PPHN** (most feared, high mortality)
- **Chemical pneumonitis** → secondary infection
- **Air leak syndromes**
- **Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy** (if co-existing asphyxia)
- **Long-term**: Reactive airway disease, neurodevelopmental impairment
