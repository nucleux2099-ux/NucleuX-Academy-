# Neonatal Sepsis

## Overview
Neonatal sepsis is a systemic infection occurring in the first 28 days of life. It remains a leading cause of neonatal mortality in India and developing countries. Classified as early-onset (EOS, <72 hours) from vertical transmission and late-onset (LOS, >72 hours) from nosocomial/community sources. Nonspecific presentation makes diagnosis challenging. Blood culture is the gold standard.

## Key Points
- Leading cause of neonatal mortality in developing countries
- EOS (<72h): GBS, E. coli; LOS (>72h): Staph, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas
- In India: Klebsiella, E. coli, Staph aureus dominate
- Sepsis screen: TLC, band:neutrophil ratio, CRP, micro-ESR
- Blood culture = gold standard
- Empirical: Ampicillin + Gentamicin (EOS); Piperacillin-tazobactam/Cefotaxime + Amikacin (LOS)

## Definition & Classification

### By Onset
| Feature | Early-Onset (<72h) | Late-Onset (>72h) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Source | Maternal/vertical | Nosocomial/community |
| Organisms | GBS, E. coli, Listeria | Staph, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas |
| Presentation | Pneumonia, multiorgan | Meningitis common |
| Mortality | Higher | Lower (but high with MDR) |

### Indian Scenario
- GBS is LESS common; gram-negative organisms predominate
- Klebsiella pneumoniae is the most common organism overall
- Rising antimicrobial resistance (ESBL, MRSA) is a major concern

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
**Maternal**: PROM >18 hours, fever/chorioamnionitis, UTI, GBS colonization, unclean delivery
**Neonatal**: Prematurity, LBW, male sex, invasive procedures, NICU stay, poor hygiene
**Environmental**: Unclean cord care, bottle feeding, overcrowding

### Pathophysiology
Immature neonatal immune system: deficient complement (especially alternate pathway), low IgG/IgM (except transplacental IgG), poor opsonization, decreased neutrophil function, low NK cell activity → susceptibility to infections → bacteremia → inflammatory cascade (cytokine storm) → SIRS → septic shock → multiorgan dysfunction

## Clinical Features
**The hallmark is NONSPECIFIC presentation** — "not doing well":
- Lethargy, poor feeding, weak/absent cry
- Temperature instability (hypothermia more common than fever in neonates)
- Respiratory distress, apnea
- Tachycardia or bradycardia
- Poor perfusion, mottling, prolonged CRT
- Abdominal distension, vomiting, diarrhea
- Jaundice (especially if late-onset, direct)
- Seizures (suggests meningitis)
- Bleeding (DIC)
- Sclerema neonatorum (hardening of skin — ominous sign)

**Sclerema** = diffuse hardening of subcutaneous tissue → board-like skin → very poor prognosis

## Diagnosis

### Sepsis Screen (2 or more positive = probable sepsis)
1. TLC <5000/mm³ or >20000/mm³
2. Immature:Total neutrophil ratio (I:T ratio) >0.2
3. CRP >1 mg/dL (6 mg/L)
4. Micro-ESR >15 mm in 1st hour
5. Platelet count <100,000/mm³

### Definitive
1. **Blood culture** (gold standard): Minimum 1 mL blood; positive in ~30-60%
2. **CSF analysis**: LP if meningitis suspected (all LOS, any with seizures)
3. **Urine culture**: Suprapubic aspiration or catheter (LOS)
4. **Other cultures**: Tracheal aspirate, wound swab, catheter tip

### Biomarkers
- **CRP**: Rises 6-8 hours after infection; better for monitoring than diagnosis
- **Procalcitonin (PCT)**: More sensitive and specific; rises earlier (2-4 hours)
- **IL-6**: Earliest marker but falls rapidly
- **Presepsin**: Newer marker

## Management

### Empirical Antibiotics
**Early-Onset Sepsis:**
- First-line: Ampicillin + Gentamicin
- Duration: 7-10 days (14 days if culture positive)

**Late-Onset Sepsis:**
- First-line: Piperacillin-tazobactam + Amikacin (or Cefotaxime + Amikacin)
- Vancomycin if MRSA suspected (NICU-acquired, CVC-related)
- Duration: 10-14 days

**Meningitis**: Ampicillin + Cefotaxime × 21 days minimum

### Supportive Care
- IV fluids, NPO initially
- Thermoregulation
- Respiratory support (O₂, CPAP, ventilation as needed)
- Inotropes for shock (Dopamine → Dobutamine → Epinephrine)
- Correct DIC (FFP, platelets)
- Blood glucose monitoring and correction

### Adjunctive Therapies
- IVIG: NOT routinely recommended (INIS trial showed no benefit)
- Granulocyte transfusion: Not proven beneficial
- Probiotics: May reduce NEC and LOS in preterm (ongoing research)

## Complications
- Septic shock, DIC, multiorgan failure
- Meningitis, brain abscess
- NEC
- Pneumonia, empyema
- Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis
- Death (15-40% in developing countries)
