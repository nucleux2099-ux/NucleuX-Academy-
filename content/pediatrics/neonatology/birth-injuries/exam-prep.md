# Birth Injuries — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary
Birth injuries from mechanical trauma during delivery. Caput (crosses sutures, present at birth) vs Cephalhematoma (doesn't cross, subperiosteal) vs Subgaleal (dangerous, can cause shock). Erb's palsy (C5-C6) = waiter's tip. Clavicle = most fractured bone.

## High Yield Points
- ★ Caput succedaneum: crosses sutures, present at birth, pitting edema, resolves 24-48h
- ★ Cephalhematoma: does NOT cross sutures, subperiosteal, appears after hours, can cause jaundice
- ★ Subgaleal hemorrhage: MOST DANGEROUS, crosses sutures, can hold entire blood volume → shock
- ★ Erb's palsy (C5-C6): "Waiter's tip" — adduction, internal rotation, pronation; grasp PRESENT
- ★ Klumpke's palsy (C8-T1): claw hand, absent grasp, ± Horner syndrome
- ★ Clavicle = most commonly fractured bone in birth injury
- ★ Facial nerve palsy = most common nerve injury (forceps → pressure on mastoid)
- ★ Erb's recovery: 80-90% by 3-6 months; no recovery by 3 months → surgery
- ★ NEVER aspirate cephalhematoma (infection risk)
- ★ Vacuum extraction → subgaleal hemorrhage risk
- ★ Cephalhematoma may calcify and persist for months

## Mnemonics
**"Caput Crosses, Cephal Can't"** — Caput crosses suture lines, Cephalhematoma cannot

**Erb's palsy — "WAITER" (C5-C6):**
- Wrist flexed
- Arm adducted & internally rotated
- Inability to supinate
- Triceps intact (C7)
- Extension at elbow
- Reflex (Moro) absent, grasp present

**Klumpke's = "Klaw" hand** (C8-T1)
