# Birth Injuries

## Overview
Birth injuries (birth trauma) are injuries sustained by the neonate during the process of labor and delivery. They range from minor (caput succedaneum, cephalhematoma) to severe (brachial plexus injuries, fractures, intracranial hemorrhage). Risk factors include macrosomia, instrumental delivery, malpresentation, and prolonged labor. Most are self-limiting but some cause permanent disability.

## Key Points
- Caput succedaneum crosses suture lines; cephalhematoma does NOT
- Subgaleal hemorrhage is the most dangerous scalp swelling (can cause shock)
- Erb's palsy (C5-C6) is the most common brachial plexus injury
- Clavicle is the most commonly fractured bone during delivery
- Facial nerve palsy is the most common nerve injury

## Definition & Classification

### By Type
1. **Soft tissue**: Caput succedaneum, cephalhematoma, subgaleal hemorrhage, subcutaneous fat necrosis, petechiae/bruising
2. **Skeletal**: Clavicle fracture, humerus fracture, femur fracture, skull fracture
3. **Nerve injuries**: Brachial plexus (Erb's, Klumpke's), facial nerve palsy, phrenic nerve palsy
4. **Intracranial**: Subdural hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage, epidural hemorrhage
5. **Visceral**: Liver/spleen rupture, adrenal hemorrhage

### Scalp Swellings — Key Differentiators
| Feature | Caput Succedaneum | Cephalhematoma | Subgaleal Hemorrhage |
|---------|------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Location | Subcutaneous | Subperiosteal | Subaponeurotic |
| Crosses sutures | YES | NO | YES |
| Present at birth | YES | After hours | Hours after birth |
| Pitting edema | YES | NO | Fluctuant |
| Limits | Diffuse, edematous | Limited to one bone | Entire scalp |
| Complications | None (self-resolving) | Jaundice, calcification | SHOCK, DIC, death |
| Resolution | 24-48 hours | 2-3 weeks to months | Emergency |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Fetal**: Macrosomia (>4 kg), malpresentation (breech), prematurity
- **Maternal**: Primiparity, cephalopelvic disproportion, prolonged labor
- **Delivery**: Instrumental (forceps, vacuum), precipitate delivery, shoulder dystocia
- **Iatrogenic**: Difficult cesarean extraction

### Pathophysiology
Mechanical forces during labor (compression, traction, rotation) → tissue damage at the site of maximum stress. Breach deliveries → higher risk of spinal cord and brachial plexus injuries. Vacuum extraction → subgaleal hemorrhage. Forceps → facial nerve palsy.

## Clinical Features

### Brachial Plexus Injuries
**Erb-Duchenne Palsy (C5-C6)** — Most common (90%)
- "Waiter's tip" position: adduction + internal rotation of shoulder, extension + pronation of forearm
- Absent Moro, biceps, and radial reflexes on affected side
- Grasp reflex PRESENT (C8-T1 intact)

**Klumpke's Palsy (C8-T1)** — Rare
- Claw hand deformity, absent grasp reflex
- If T1 involved → ipsilateral Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis)

**Total Plexus Palsy (C5-T1)**
- Flail arm, absent all reflexes

### Facial Nerve Palsy
- Peripheral type (LMN): affects entire half of face
- Asymmetric crying facies
- Cannot close eye on affected side
- Usually due to forceps pressure over mastoid

### Fractures
- **Clavicle** (most common): Crepitus, asymmetric Moro, pseudoparalysis
- **Humerus**: Spiral/mid-shaft fracture, swelling, Moro absent
- **Skull**: Linear (most common, benign) vs depressed (needs evaluation)

## Diagnosis
1. **Clinical examination**: Detailed neurological exam, asymmetry of movements
2. **X-ray**: Fractures (clavicle, humerus, skull)
3. **Ultrasound**: Cranial USS for intracranial hemorrhage, abdominal USS for visceral injuries
4. **MRI**: Brachial plexus, spinal cord injuries
5. **CT head**: For intracranial bleeding assessment
6. **Nerve conduction studies**: For persistent brachial plexus palsy (after 3 months)

## Management

### General Principles
- Most birth injuries are self-limiting
- Supportive care: pain management, gentle handling
- Parental reassurance and counseling

### Specific Management
**Caput succedaneum**: No treatment needed, resolves in 24-48 hours
**Cephalhematoma**: Observation (NO aspiration — infection risk), monitor for jaundice
**Subgaleal hemorrhage**: EMERGENCY — IV fluids, blood transfusion, correct coagulopathy

**Erb's palsy**:
- Immobilization in "Statue of Liberty" position (abduction + external rotation) for 1-2 weeks
- Physiotherapy after 7-10 days
- 80-90% recover by 3-6 months
- If no recovery by 3 months → MRI + surgical exploration (nerve grafting)

**Facial nerve palsy**: Most recover spontaneously in 2-3 weeks; protect eye with artificial tears

**Clavicle fracture**: Figure-of-eight bandage or pin sleeve to body; heals in 7-10 days (palpable callus)

## Complications
- Subgaleal hemorrhage → hypovolemic shock, DIC, death
- Cephalhematoma → jaundice (hemolysis), rarely calcification/infection
- Erb's palsy → permanent disability if not recovered by 3-6 months
- Intracranial hemorrhage → seizures, hydrocephalus, neurological deficit
- Visceral injury (liver/spleen) → intraperitoneal hemorrhage, shock
