# Neonatal Jaundice

## Overview
Neonatal jaundice (hyperbilirubinemia) is the most common condition requiring medical evaluation in newborns. It is visible when serum bilirubin exceeds 5 mg/dL. About 60% of term and 80% of preterm neonates develop jaundice in the first week. Most is physiological and benign, but pathological jaundice can lead to acute bilirubin encephalopathy and kernicterus if untreated.

## Key Points
- Most common neonatal condition requiring evaluation
- Physiological jaundice: appears after 24 hours, peaks day 3-5, resolves by 2 weeks
- Pathological: appears within 24 hours, rapid rise (>5 mg/dL/day), or prolonged
- Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia → risk of kernicterus
- Treatment: phototherapy and exchange transfusion
- Kramer's rule estimates bilirubin by zone of jaundice progression

## Definition & Classification

### By Type
| Feature | Physiological | Pathological |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Onset | After 24 hours | Within 24 hours |
| Peak (term) | Day 3-5 (12-15 mg/dL) | Variable |
| Duration | <2 weeks (term), <3 weeks (preterm) | Prolonged |
| Rate of rise | <5 mg/dL/day | >5 mg/dL/day |
| Direct bilirubin | <2 mg/dL | >2 mg/dL or >20% of TSB |

### By Bilirubin Type
- **Unconjugated (indirect)**: Hemolysis, physiological, breast milk jaundice, Gilbert, Crigler-Najjar
- **Conjugated (direct)**: Biliary atresia, neonatal hepatitis, choledochal cyst, metabolic (>2 mg/dL or >20% of TSB = always pathological)

### Kramer's Rule (Clinical Estimation)
| Zone | Area | Approximate TSB |
|------|------|----------------|
| 1 | Face | 5-6 mg/dL |
| 2 | Chest + upper arms | 6-8 mg/dL |
| 3 | Abdomen + thighs | 8-10 mg/dL |
| 4 | Forearms + legs | 10-12 mg/dL |
| 5 | Palms + soles | >12 mg/dL |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes of Unconjugated Hyperbilirubinemia
**Increased production (hemolysis):**
- Rh/ABO incompatibility, G6PD deficiency, spherocytosis
- Cephalhematoma, polycythemia, sepsis

**Decreased conjugation:**
- Physiological immaturity of UGT enzyme
- Crigler-Najjar syndrome (Type I: absent UGT; Type II: reduced)
- Gilbert syndrome
- Hypothyroidism

**Increased enterohepatic circulation:**
- Breast milk jaundice
- Intestinal obstruction, pyloric stenosis
- Poor feeding/starvation

### Causes of Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia
- Biliary atresia (most important surgical cause)
- Neonatal hepatitis (TORCH, metabolic)
- Choledochal cyst
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
- Galactosemia, tyrosinemia

### Pathophysiology of Kernicterus
Unconjugated bilirubin (lipid-soluble) → crosses blood-brain barrier → deposits in basal ganglia (globus pallidus), hippocampus, subthalamic nuclei, cranial nerve nuclei → neuronal necrosis

**Risk factors for kernicterus**: Prematurity, acidosis, hypoalbuminemia, sepsis, hypothermia, drugs displacing bilirubin (sulfonamides, ceftriaxone)

## Clinical Features

### Acute Bilirubin Encephalopathy (ABE)
**Phase 1 (Early)**: Lethargy, hypotonia, poor suck
**Phase 2 (Intermediate)**: Hypertonia (retrocollis, opisthotonus), high-pitched cry, fever
**Phase 3 (Advanced)**: Pronounced opisthotonus, seizures, apnea, coma

### Chronic Bilirubin Encephalopathy (Kernicterus)
- Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy
- Upward gaze palsy
- Sensorineural hearing loss (auditory neuropathy)
- Dental enamel dysplasia
- Intellectual disability (variable)

## Diagnosis
1. **Serum total bilirubin (TSB)**: Gold standard
2. **Direct and indirect bilirubin fractionation**
3. **Transcutaneous bilirubinometry (TcB)**: Screening tool
4. **Blood group (mother + baby) + Coombs test**: Rh/ABO incompatibility
5. **Reticulocyte count, peripheral smear**: Hemolysis
6. **G6PD levels**: If indicated
7. **LFT, conjugated bilirubin**: If conjugated hyperbilirubinemia suspected
8. **Thyroid function**: Prolonged jaundice
9. **HIDA scan/USS**: Biliary atresia evaluation

## Management

### Phototherapy
- **Mechanism**: Converts unconjugated bilirubin to water-soluble photoisomers (lumirubin) excreted in bile and urine without conjugation
- **Intensive phototherapy**: Irradiance ≥30 μW/cm²/nm
- **Indications**: Based on hour-specific Bhutani nomogram / AAP guidelines
- **Side effects**: Dehydration, temperature instability, skin rash, bronze baby syndrome (conjugated), retinal damage (eye shields mandatory)

### Exchange Transfusion
- **Double volume exchange** (160-180 mL/kg): Removes ~85% of circulating bilirubin
- **Indications**: TSB rising despite intensive phototherapy, TSB approaching exchange level, signs of ABE
- **Blood used**: O Rh-negative or compatible cross-matched blood
- **Complications**: Electrolyte imbalance (hypocalcemia, hyperkalemia), thrombocytopenia, NEC, infection, air embolism, cardiac arrhythmia

### Other
- **IV immunoglobulin (IVIG)**: For immune hemolytic jaundice (Rh/ABO) to reduce need for exchange
- **Treat underlying cause**: Sepsis, G6PD crisis, hypothyroidism
- **Breast milk jaundice**: Continue breastfeeding (temporary interruption controversial)

## Complications
- Acute bilirubin encephalopathy
- Kernicterus (chronic bilirubin encephalopathy)
- Exchange transfusion complications
- Biliary atresia if conjugated jaundice missed → liver failure
