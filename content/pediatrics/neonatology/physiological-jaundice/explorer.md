# Physiological Jaundice

## Overview
Physiological jaundice is a benign, self-limiting unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia that occurs in most healthy newborns. It results from the normal physiological processes of increased bilirubin production (higher RBC mass, shorter RBC lifespan) and immature hepatic conjugation (low UGT activity). It affects ~60% of term and ~80% of preterm neonates.

## Key Points
- Affects 60% term, 80% preterm neonates
- Appears AFTER 24 hours of life
- Peaks day 3-5 (term) at 12-15 mg/dL; day 5-7 (preterm) at higher levels
- Resolves by 2 weeks (term), 3 weeks (preterm)
- Rate of rise <5 mg/dL/day
- Self-limiting, requires no treatment in most cases
- Must differentiate from pathological jaundice

## Definition & Classification

### Diagnostic Criteria for Physiological Jaundice
1. Appears after 24 hours of life
2. TSB <15 mg/dL in term (peaks day 3-5)
3. Rate of rise <5 mg/dL/day (<0.5 mg/dL/hour)
4. Direct bilirubin <2 mg/dL
5. Duration <14 days (term), <21 days (preterm)
6. No hemolysis, no illness

### Physiological vs Pathological — Quick Comparison
| Feature | Physiological | Pathological |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Onset | >24 hours | <24 hours |
| Peak (term) | Day 3-5, <15 mg/dL | Higher, earlier |
| Rate of rise | <5 mg/dL/day | >5 mg/dL/day |
| Duration | <2 weeks (term) | >2 weeks |
| Direct bilirubin | <2 mg/dL | >2 mg/dL |
| Baby | Well | Ill / hemolysis signs |

### Breast Milk Jaundice vs Breastfeeding Jaundice
| Feature | Breastfeeding Jaundice | Breast Milk Jaundice |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Onset | Day 2-4 | Day 4-7 (peaks 2-3 weeks) |
| Cause | Inadequate intake, dehydration | β-glucuronidase in milk |
| Mechanism | ↑ Enterohepatic circulation | ↑ Enterohepatic circulation |
| Treatment | Increase breastfeeding | Continue breastfeeding |
| Duration | Resolves with adequate feeding | May persist 3-12 weeks |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Why Neonates Get Jaundice (Physiological Reasons)
1. **Increased bilirubin production**:
   - Higher RBC mass (Hb 18-20 g/dL at birth)
   - Shorter RBC lifespan (70-90 days vs 120 days in adults)
   - Higher turnover of non-erythrocyte heme sources

2. **Immature hepatic conjugation**:
   - UGT1A1 (UDP-glucuronosyltransferase) activity only 1% of adult at birth
   - Reaches adult levels by 6-14 weeks of age

3. **Increased enterohepatic circulation**:
   - Sterile gut (no bacteria to convert bilirubin to urobilinogen)
   - High β-glucuronidase activity in intestinal mucosa
   - Deconjugates bilirubin → reabsorbed

4. **Decreased hepatic uptake**:
   - Low ligandin (Y protein) levels in hepatocytes

### Bilirubin Metabolism (Normal)
Heme (from RBC breakdown) → Biliverdin → Unconjugated bilirubin (UCB, indirect, lipid-soluble) → bound to albumin → transported to liver → conjugated by UGT1A1 with glucuronic acid → Conjugated bilirubin (CB, direct, water-soluble) → excreted in bile → intestine → urobilinogen → stercobilin (feces)

## Clinical Features
- Yellow discoloration of skin and sclera (visible at TSB >5 mg/dL)
- Progresses cephalocaudal (Kramer's rule: face → trunk → extremities → palms/soles)
- Baby is otherwise WELL — active, feeding well, good weight gain
- No hepatosplenomegaly
- No pallor
- Normal urine and stool color

## Diagnosis
1. **Clinical assessment**: Kramer's zones (pressing skin to blanch and observe color)
2. **Transcutaneous bilirubinometry (TcB)**: Non-invasive screening
3. **Total serum bilirubin (TSB)**: If TcB elevated or clinical concern
4. **Plot on hour-specific nomogram** (Bhutani): Determine risk zone
5. **Exclude pathological causes**: Blood group, Coombs test if indicated

### Bhutani Nomogram Risk Zones
- Low risk: <40th percentile
- Low-intermediate: 40th-75th percentile
- High-intermediate: 75th-95th percentile
- High risk: >95th percentile → closer monitoring, consider phototherapy

## Management

### Observation Only (Most Cases)
- Supportive: adequate breastfeeding (8-12 feeds/day)
- Monitor for progression
- Parental education on danger signs
- Follow-up within 48-72 hours of discharge

### When to Treat
- Based on hour-specific nomogram and risk factors
- Phototherapy threshold is lower for: preterm, hemolysis, asphyxia, sepsis, acidosis
- Most physiological jaundice does NOT require phototherapy

### Promoting Bilirubin Clearance
- Frequent breastfeeding (reduces enterohepatic circulation)
- Avoid water/glucose water supplementation (no benefit)
- Sun exposure: NOT recommended (ineffective, risk of sunburn, hypothermia/hyperthermia)

## Complications
- Physiological jaundice itself is BENIGN
- Risk only if misdiagnosed (actually pathological) or if risk factors present
- Exaggerated physiological jaundice may require phototherapy
