# Pathological Jaundice in Neonates

## Overview
Pathological jaundice is hyperbilirubinemia that deviates from the normal physiological pattern and requires investigation and treatment. It is defined by onset within 24 hours of birth, rapid rise in bilirubin (>5 mg/dL/day), total serum bilirubin >95th percentile for age on hour-specific nomogram, conjugated bilirubin >2 mg/dL, or persistence beyond 2 weeks in term infants. The most common cause is hemolytic disease (Rh/ABO incompatibility).

## Key Points
- Jaundice within 24 hours = ALWAYS pathological
- Most common cause: hemolytic disease (Rh > ABO incompatibility)
- Rh incompatibility: severe, hydrops fetalis possible
- ABO incompatibility: milder, may present in first pregnancy
- Conjugated bilirubin >2 mg/dL = always pathological
- Coombs test differentiates immune from non-immune hemolysis

## Definition & Classification

### Criteria for Pathological Jaundice
1. Onset within first 24 hours
2. Rate of rise >5 mg/dL/day (>0.5 mg/dL/hour)
3. TSB >95th percentile on Bhutani nomogram
4. TSB >15 mg/dL in term, >12 in preterm
5. Direct bilirubin >2 mg/dL or >20% of TSB
6. Persistence beyond 14 days (term) or 21 days (preterm)
7. Dark urine or pale/clay colored stools (conjugated)

### Causes — Organized by Mechanism
**Increased Production (Hemolysis):**
- Immune: Rh incompatibility, ABO incompatibility, minor blood group
- Non-immune: G6PD deficiency, pyruvate kinase deficiency, spherocytosis, elliptocytosis
- Non-hemolytic: Cephalhematoma, polycythemia, swallowed blood, sepsis

**Decreased Conjugation:**
- Crigler-Najjar Type I (absent UGT — severe, kernicterus)
- Crigler-Najjar Type II (reduced UGT — responds to phenobarbitone)
- Gilbert syndrome (mild, benign)
- Hypothyroidism
- Breast milk jaundice

**Increased Enterohepatic Circulation:**
- Intestinal obstruction, pyloric stenosis
- Breast milk jaundice (beta-glucuronidase)
- Poor feeding / starvation

**Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia:**
- Biliary atresia, choledochal cyst
- Neonatal hepatitis (TORCH, metabolic)
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
- Galactosemia, tyrosinemia

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Rh Hemolytic Disease
- Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive fetus → maternal anti-D IgG antibodies → cross placenta → fetal RBC hemolysis
- Requires prior sensitization (previous pregnancy, transfusion) — rare in first pregnancy
- Severity increases with each pregnancy
- Direct Coombs test (DCT) POSITIVE
- Can cause hydrops fetalis (severe anemia → heart failure → generalized edema)
- Prevention: Anti-D immunoglobulin (300 μg) at 28 weeks and within 72 hours of delivery

### ABO Hemolytic Disease
- Mother O + Baby A or B
- Naturally occurring anti-A/anti-B IgG antibodies → can affect FIRST pregnancy
- Usually milder than Rh disease
- DCT may be weakly positive or negative
- Spherocytes on peripheral smear

### G6PD Deficiency
- X-linked recessive (males affected, females carriers)
- Oxidative stress (drugs, infections, fava beans) → Heinz bodies → hemolysis
- Bite cells, blister cells on smear
- G6PD levels may be falsely normal during acute hemolysis (test after recovery)

## Clinical Features
- Jaundice appearing within 24 hours (hemolytic)
- Rapidly progressing jaundice (deepening over hours)
- Pallor (anemia from hemolysis)
- Hepatosplenomegaly (extramedullary hematopoiesis)
- Hydrops fetalis (Rh disease): generalized edema, ascites, pleural effusion
- Dark urine, pale stools (conjugated hyperbilirubinemia)
- Signs of kernicterus if untreated

## Diagnosis
1. **Total and direct serum bilirubin**
2. **Blood group** (mother + baby) + **Coombs test** (direct + indirect)
3. **Reticulocyte count**: Elevated in hemolysis
4. **Peripheral blood smear**: Spherocytes (ABO), bite cells (G6PD)
5. **G6PD levels**
6. **Hemoglobin/hematocrit**: Anemia assessment
7. **Bhutani nomogram**: Plot TSB against age in hours → risk zone
8. **TORCH screen**: If conjugated hyperbilirubinemia
9. **Thyroid function**: Prolonged unconjugated jaundice
10. **USG abdomen + HIDA scan**: If biliary atresia suspected

## Management

### Phototherapy
- Based on hour-specific nomogram (AAP/IAP guidelines)
- More aggressive thresholds for: prematurity, hemolysis, sepsis, acidosis
- Intensive phototherapy: irradiance ≥30 μW/cm²/nm
- Fiber-optic blankets for double phototherapy

### Exchange Transfusion
- Double volume (160-180 mL/kg)
- Indications: TSB at exchange level despite intensive phototherapy, signs of ABE, cord bilirubin >5 in Rh disease
- For Rh disease: O Rh-negative blood cross-matched with mother's serum
- For ABO: O cells with AB plasma (or same group as baby)

### Specific Treatment
- **Rh disease prevention**: Anti-D immunoglobulin to Rh-negative mother
- **IVIG**: 0.5-1 g/kg for immune hemolytic jaundice (reduces exchange need)
- **G6PD**: Avoid oxidant drugs, supportive care
- **Crigler-Najjar I**: Phototherapy (lifelong), liver transplant (definitive)
- **Crigler-Najjar II**: Phenobarbitone (induces residual UGT)
- **Biliary atresia**: Kasai portoenterostomy before 60 days of age

## Complications
- Acute bilirubin encephalopathy → kernicterus
- Hydrops fetalis (Rh disease) → intrauterine/neonatal death
- Anemia (severe hemolysis)
- Liver failure (biliary atresia if untreated)
- Exchange transfusion complications
