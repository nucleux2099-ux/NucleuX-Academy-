# Neonatal Meningitis

## Overview
Neonatal meningitis is a serious infection of the meninges occurring in the first 28 days of life. It is associated with high mortality (20-30%) and significant morbidity (30-50% of survivors have neurological sequelae). Group B Streptococcus (GBS) and E. coli are the most common causative organisms. Early diagnosis and aggressive antibiotic therapy are essential.

## Key Points
- Most common organisms: GBS (early-onset), E. coli K1 (late-onset)
- Often accompanies neonatal sepsis
- CSF findings: ↑WBC (>30), ↑protein (>150 mg/dL), ↓glucose (<40 mg/dL or <50% of blood glucose)
- Subtle/nonspecific presentation in neonates
- High mortality and morbidity (hydrocephalus, hearing loss, developmental delay)
- Empirical: Ampicillin + Cefotaxime (or Ampicillin + Gentamicin)

## Definition & Classification

### By Onset
- **Early-onset** (<72 hours): Vertical transmission from mother; GBS, E. coli, Listeria
- **Late-onset** (>72 hours to 28 days): Nosocomial or community; GBS, E. coli, Klebsiella, Staph

### Common Organisms
| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Group B Streptococcus (GBS) | Most common overall in developed countries |
| E. coli (K1 antigen) | Most common gram-negative; K1 capsule = virulence |
| Listeria monocytogenes | Transplacental; associated with meconium staining in preterm |
| Klebsiella, Enterobacter | Nosocomial, NICU-associated |
| Staphylococcus aureus | Associated with VP shunts, skin breaks |
| In India | Klebsiella, E. coli, Staph aureus most common |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- Preterm birth, LBW
- Prolonged rupture of membranes (>18 hours)
- Maternal GBS colonization, UTI, chorioamnionitis
- NICU stay, invasive procedures
- Male sex (higher susceptibility)

### Pathophysiology
Bacteremia → crossing of blood-brain barrier (immature BBB in neonates) → meningeal inflammation → cerebral edema → increased ICP → vasculitis/infarction → neuronal damage

The neonatal immune system has deficient complement, low IgG/IgM, poor opsonization → increased susceptibility to encapsulated organisms.

## Clinical Features
Neonatal meningitis presents with **nonspecific signs** (similar to sepsis):
- Lethargy, irritability, poor feeding, weak cry
- Temperature instability (fever or hypothermia)
- Apnea, respiratory distress
- Seizures (present in 40-50%)
- Bulging fontanelle (late sign, may be absent initially)
- Vomiting, abdominal distension
- High-pitched cry
- Hypotonia or hypertonia
- Focal neurological signs (rare in neonates)

**Key**: Neck stiffness (Kernig/Brudzinski signs) is often ABSENT in neonates

## Diagnosis
1. **CSF analysis** (lumbar puncture):
   | Parameter | Normal Neonate | Meningitis |
   |-----------|---------------|-----------|
   | WBC | 0-30/mm³ | >30 (often >100); PMN predominant |
   | Protein | 60-150 mg/dL (term) | >150 mg/dL |
   | Glucose | 40-80 mg/dL | <40 mg/dL or <50% of blood glucose |
   | Gram stain | Negative | May show organisms |
2. **CSF culture**: Gold standard for organism identification
3. **Blood culture**: Positive in 75-80% of meningitis cases
4. **CRP, PCT**: Elevated (nonspecific)
5. **CBC**: Leukocytosis or leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
6. **Cranial ultrasound**: Ventriculitis, abscess, hydrocephalus
7. **MRI brain**: Infarction, abscess, effusion, ventriculitis

## Management

### Empirical Antibiotics
- **First-line**: Ampicillin + Cefotaxime (preferred for meningitis due to CSF penetration)
- Alternative: Ampicillin + Gentamicin (for sepsis without confirmed meningitis)
- **Duration**: Minimum 21 days for GBS/Listeria; 21-28 days for gram-negative meningitis
- **Cefotaxime** preferred over ceftriaxone in neonates (ceftriaxone displaces bilirubin from albumin)

### Specific Therapy (based on culture)
- GBS: Penicillin G or Ampicillin (14-21 days)
- E. coli: Cefotaxime + Aminoglycoside (21 days minimum)
- Listeria: Ampicillin + Gentamicin (21 days)
- Staph aureus: Vancomycin (if MRSA) or Cloxacillin

### Supportive Care
- Fluid restriction (SIADH risk)
- Anticonvulsants (Phenobarbitone)
- Dexamethasone: NOT recommended in neonatal meningitis (unlike older children)
- Monitor head circumference (hydrocephalus)
- Repeat LP at 48-72 hours to document sterilization

### Surgical
- VP shunt for post-meningitis hydrocephalus
- Drainage of brain abscess

## Complications
- **Acute**: Seizures, SIADH, ventriculitis, brain abscess, subdural empyema, cerebral infarction, DIC
- **Chronic**: Hydrocephalus (most common), sensorineural hearing loss, intellectual disability, cerebral palsy, epilepsy, cortical blindness
