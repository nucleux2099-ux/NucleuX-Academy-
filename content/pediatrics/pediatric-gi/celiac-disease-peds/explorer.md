# Celiac Disease in Children

## Overview
Celiac disease is a chronic immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by dietary gluten in genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2/DQ8). It causes villous atrophy of the small intestinal mucosa, leading to malabsorption. It is one of the most common causes of chronic diarrhea and malabsorption in children after introduction of wheat-based complementary foods. Lifelong gluten-free diet is the only effective treatment.

## Key Points
- **Autoimmune enteropathy** triggered by gluten (wheat, barley, rye)
- **HLA-DQ2** (90-95%) or **HLA-DQ8** — necessary but not sufficient
- **Anti-tTG IgA** — best screening test
- **Gold standard:** Small intestinal biopsy showing villous atrophy (Marsh classification)
- **Typical presentation:** Chronic diarrhea, abdominal distension, failure to thrive (1-3 years)
- **Treatment:** Lifelong strict gluten-free diet (GFD)
- **Associated:** Type 1 DM, Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, autoimmune thyroiditis, IgA deficiency
- **Dermatitis herpetiformis** — skin manifestation of celiac disease

## Definition & Classification

### Types of Celiac Disease
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Classical** | GI symptoms: diarrhea, malabsorption, FTT |
| **Non-classical** | Extra-intestinal: anemia, short stature, dental enamel defects |
| **Silent** | Positive serology + biopsy changes, but asymptomatic |
| **Latent** | Positive serology, normal biopsy (may develop later) |
| **Refractory** | No response to GFD after 6-12 months (rare in children) |

### Marsh Classification (Biopsy)
| Grade | Findings |
|-------|----------|
| 0 | Normal |
| 1 | Increased IELs (>25/100 enterocytes) |
| 2 | IELs + crypt hyperplasia |
| 3a | Partial villous atrophy |
| 3b | Subtotal villous atrophy |
| 3c | Total villous atrophy |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Genetics
- **HLA-DQ2** present in 90-95% (encoded by DQA1*05, DQB1*02)
- **HLA-DQ8** in remaining 5-10%
- Absence of both virtually excludes celiac disease
- First-degree relatives: 10-15% risk

### Trigger
- **Gluten** — storage protein in wheat (gliadin), barley (hordein), rye (secalin)
- **Oats** — generally safe (may be contaminated)
- **Rice, maize, millet** — safe (no gluten)

### Pathophysiology
1. Gliadin peptides resist complete digestion → reach lamina propria
2. **Tissue transglutaminase (tTG)** deamidates gliadin → enhanced binding to HLA-DQ2/DQ8
3. Antigen presentation to CD4+ T-cells → Th1 immune response
4. Release of IFN-γ, TNF-α → crypt hyperplasia, villous atrophy
5. **Intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs)** — early marker of mucosal inflammation
6. Villous atrophy → ↓absorptive surface → malabsorption
7. Anti-tTG and anti-endomysial antibodies generated (autoimmune component)

## Clinical Features

### Classical (Typical) — Age 6 months to 3 years
- **Chronic diarrhea** — bulky, pale, foul-smelling, steatorrhea
- **Abdominal distension** — protuberant abdomen
- **Failure to thrive** — weight loss/poor gain, muscle wasting
- **Anorexia, irritability**
- **Buttock wasting** — "wasted buttocks" with distended abdomen
- Onset: typically 3-6 months after gluten introduction

### Non-Classical (Atypical)
- **Iron deficiency anemia** (refractory to oral iron)
- **Short stature** — may be sole presentation
- **Dental enamel defects** (permanent teeth)
- **Delayed puberty**
- **Recurrent aphthous ulcers**
- **Osteopenia/osteoporosis** (Vitamin D malabsorption)
- **Elevated transaminases** (cryptogenic)
- **Arthritis, arthralgia**
- **Neurological:** Peripheral neuropathy, ataxia, epilepsy

### Dermatitis Herpetiformis
- Intensely pruritic papulovesicular rash
- Elbows, knees, buttocks, scalp
- Skin manifestation of celiac disease (IgA deposits in dermal papillae)
- Responds to GFD + dapsone

### High-Risk Groups to Screen
- Type 1 diabetes mellitus
- Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, Williams syndrome
- Autoimmune thyroiditis
- First-degree relatives of celiac patients
- Selective IgA deficiency
- Unexplained iron deficiency anemia, short stature

## Diagnosis

### Serology (Screening)
| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| **Anti-tTG IgA** | 95-98% | 95% | Best screening test |
| **Anti-endomysial Ab (EMA) IgA** | 95-98% | 99% | Most specific; observer-dependent |
| **Anti-deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP) IgG** | 90% | 95% | Use if IgA deficient |
| **Total serum IgA** | — | — | Must check (2-3% celiacs have IgA deficiency) |

### Small Intestinal Biopsy
- **Gold standard** — duodenal biopsy via upper GI endoscopy
- Multiple biopsies (4-6 from D2, 1-2 from D1 bulb)
- Findings: villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, increased IELs
- **Must be done while on gluten-containing diet**

### ESPGHAN 2020 — Biopsy May Be Avoided If:
- Anti-tTG IgA >10× upper limit of normal
- Positive EMA on separate blood sample
- Symptomatic child
- HLA-DQ2/DQ8 positive (optional confirmation)

### Response to GFD
- Clinical improvement within 2-4 weeks
- Serological normalization in 6-12 months
- Histological recovery in 1-2 years

## Management

### Lifelong Gluten-Free Diet (ONLY Treatment)
**Avoid:** Wheat, barley, rye (and their derivatives)
**Safe grains:** Rice, maize (corn), millet (jowar, bajra, ragi), buckwheat, quinoa, amaranth

### Nutritional Supplementation
- Iron, folic acid, calcium, Vitamin D — during initial correction
- Multivitamins
- Calorie-dense diet for catch-up growth

### Monitoring
- Serology (anti-tTG) at 6 months, then annually
- Growth monitoring
- Bone density (if deficient)
- Adherence to GFD (dietary history, serology)

### Refractory Celiac Disease
- Persistent symptoms despite strict GFD for >12 months
- Rare in children
- Rule out: dietary non-compliance (most common cause), other diagnoses
- Type I (normal IEL phenotype) vs Type II (aberrant IEL — premalignant)

## Complications
- **Growth failure, short stature** (if untreated)
- **Iron deficiency anemia** (refractory)
- **Osteoporosis** — Vitamin D and calcium malabsorption
- **Delayed puberty, infertility**
- **Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL)** — rare, adults
- **Small bowel adenocarcinoma** — rare
- **Associated autoimmune diseases** — thyroiditis, Type 1 DM, Addison's
- **Hyposplenism** — increased infection risk
