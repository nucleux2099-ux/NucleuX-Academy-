# Chronic Diarrhea in Children

## Overview
Chronic diarrhea is defined as diarrhea lasting >4 weeks (some use >14 days as persistent, >30 days as chronic). It affects 3-20% of children in developing countries and is a major cause of malnutrition and growth failure. Causes vary by age — cow's milk protein allergy and post-infectious in infants, celiac disease and giardiasis in toddlers, and IBD in older children. A systematic approach based on stool characteristics (watery vs fatty vs bloody) guides diagnosis.

## Key Points
- **Duration:** >4 weeks (>14 days = persistent diarrhea)
- **Most common cause in developing countries:** Post-infectious/persistent diarrhea
- **Most common cause in toddlers:** Toddler's diarrhea (functional)
- **Stool character guides workup:** Watery, fatty (steatorrhea), bloody
- **Celiac disease** — most common cause of malabsorption in children
- **Cow's milk protein allergy** — common in infants <1 year
- **Growth failure** — key differentiator between organic and functional causes
- **Toddler's diarrhea** — normal growth, no malabsorption; benign

## Definition & Classification

### By Duration
| Term | Duration |
|------|----------|
| Acute | <14 days |
| Persistent | 14-28 days |
| Chronic | >28 days (>4 weeks) |

### By Stool Type
| Type | Stool Character | Causes |
|------|----------------|--------|
| **Watery (secretory/osmotic)** | Large volume, watery | Lactose intolerance, toddler's diarrhea, VIPoma |
| **Fatty (steatorrhea)** | Bulky, pale, foul-smelling, oily | Celiac, CF, pancreatic insufficiency |
| **Bloody (inflammatory)** | Blood, mucus, pus | IBD, allergic colitis, infection |

### By Mechanism
| Mechanism | Features | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Osmotic** | Stops with fasting, stool osmotic gap >50 | Lactose intolerance |
| **Secretory** | Continues despite fasting, stool osmotic gap <50 | Congenital chloride diarrhea |
| **Inflammatory** | Blood, mucus, WBCs in stool | IBD, infection |
| **Motility** | Postprandial, mucus, undigested food | Toddler's diarrhea |
| **Malabsorptive** | Steatorrhea, weight loss | Celiac, CF |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes by Age
| Age | Common Causes |
|-----|---------------|
| **<6 months** | CMPA, post-infectious, congenital (microvillous inclusion, tufting) |
| **6 months-3 years** | Post-infectious, toddler's diarrhea, celiac disease, CMPA, giardiasis |
| **3-7 years** | Celiac, giardiasis, lactose intolerance |
| **>7 years** | IBD (Crohn's, UC), celiac, lactose intolerance, IBS |

### Common Causes in Detail
- **Toddler's diarrhea (chronic nonspecific diarrhea):** Functional; normal growth, undigested food in stool, excess juice intake
- **Celiac disease:** Gluten-triggered; villous atrophy → malabsorption
- **CMPA:** Non-IgE mediated (most common); bloody stools, eczema in infants
- **Giardiasis:** Fatty, foul-smelling stools; abdominal distension
- **Post-infectious:** Persistent lactose intolerance, small bowel bacterial overgrowth
- **IBD:** Crohn's (anywhere, transmural, skip lesions) vs UC (colon only, continuous, superficial)
- **Cystic fibrosis:** Steatorrhea, recurrent pneumonia, failure to thrive

### Pathophysiology Framework
1. **Mucosal damage** → ↓absorption (celiac, infections)
2. **Enzyme deficiency** → undigested substrate retains water osmotically (lactose intolerance)
3. **Active secretion** → electrolyte and water secretion (secretory diarrhea)
4. **Inflammation** → mucosal disruption, blood/mucus loss (IBD, infection)
5. **Motility disorder** → rapid transit, incomplete absorption (toddler's diarrhea)

## Clinical Features

### History Clues
- **Onset timing:** After gluten introduction (celiac), after formula (CMPA), post-gastroenteritis
- **Stool character:** Watery, fatty, bloody — directs workup
- **Growth pattern:** Normal (functional) vs faltering (organic)
- **Diet history:** Excess juice, cow's milk introduction, gluten timing
- **Family history:** Celiac, IBD, CF, atopy
- **Associated symptoms:** Rash (celiac/CMPA), joint pain (IBD), respiratory (CF)

### Examination
- **Growth parameters** — most important differentiator
- Abdominal distension (celiac, malabsorption)
- Perianal disease (Crohn's — fissures, fistulae, skin tags)
- Clubbing (CF, IBD, celiac)
- Pallor (anemia — celiac, IBD)
- Edema (hypoalbuminemia)
- Skin: eczema (CMPA), dermatitis herpetiformis (celiac)
- Mouth ulcers (Crohn's, celiac)

## Diagnosis

### Step-wise Approach
1. **Stool examination:** Microscopy (fat globules, ova/cysts, WBCs/RBCs), culture, pH
2. **Stool reducing substances** — lactose intolerance (pH <5.5)
3. **Celiac serology:** Anti-tTG IgA + total IgA
4. **Stool for Giardia** antigen or microscopy
5. **CBC, iron studies, albumin, electrolytes**
6. **Sweat chloride test** — CF (if steatorrhea + respiratory symptoms)
7. **Endoscopy + biopsy** — if celiac suspected (duodenal) or IBD (colonoscopy)
8. **Hydrogen breath test** — lactose/sucrose malabsorption

### Key Tests by Diagnosis
| Condition | Key Test |
|-----------|----------|
| Celiac disease | Anti-tTG IgA + duodenal biopsy |
| Giardiasis | Stool for Giardia antigen/cysts |
| CMPA | Elimination diet → challenge |
| CF | Sweat chloride test (>60 mEq/L) |
| IBD | Fecal calprotectin, colonoscopy + biopsy |
| Lactose intolerance | Stool pH <5.5, hydrogen breath test |

## Management

### General Principles
- **Continue feeding** — never starve the child
- **Nutritional rehabilitation** — catch-up growth
- **Correct micronutrient deficiencies** — iron, zinc, vitamins
- **Zinc supplementation** — 20 mg/day × 14 days (WHO)

### Specific Treatment
| Condition | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Celiac disease** | Lifelong gluten-free diet |
| **CMPA** | Cow's milk elimination; extensively hydrolyzed formula; maternal elimination if breastfed |
| **Giardiasis** | Metronidazole 15 mg/kg/day × 5-7 days |
| **Lactose intolerance** | Lactose-free diet; lactase enzyme supplements |
| **Toddler's diarrhea** | Reassurance; ↑fat, ↓juice, ↑fiber; no treatment needed |
| **IBD** | Mesalamine (UC), steroids, immunomodulators, biologics |
| **CF** | Pancreatic enzyme replacement, fat-soluble vitamins |

## Complications
- **Malnutrition / growth failure** — most common and important
- **Micronutrient deficiencies** — iron, zinc, vitamin A, D, B12
- **Dehydration** (less common than acute diarrhea)
- **Developmental delay** — if prolonged malnutrition in early life
- **Anemia**
- **Osteopenia/rickets** — calcium, vitamin D malabsorption
- **Perianal complications** (Crohn's disease)
