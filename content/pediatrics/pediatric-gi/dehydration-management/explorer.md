# Dehydration Management in Children

## Overview
Dehydration is the most dangerous consequence of acute diarrheal disease and a leading cause of under-5 mortality. It results from excessive loss of water and electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻). Accurate assessment of dehydration severity and appropriate fluid resuscitation using ORS or IV fluids following WHO Plan A/B/C is life-saving. Understanding the types (isotonic, hypotonic, hypertonic) is crucial for safe correction.

## Key Points
- **Most common cause of dehydration in children:** Acute diarrhea
- **WHO Plan A/B/C** based on severity — no/some/severe dehydration
- **ORS is the mainstay** — corrects dehydration in >90% of cases
- **Isotonic (isonatremic)** dehydration is most common (80%)
- **Hypernatremic dehydration** — most dangerous; correct slowly over 48-72 hours
- **Ringer's Lactate** preferred IV fluid for severe dehydration
- **Skin pinch** — most reliable clinical sign for dehydration assessment
- **Fluid deficit (mL)** = % dehydration × body weight (kg) × 10

## Definition & Classification

### Types of Dehydration by Serum Sodium
| Type | Serum Na⁺ | Frequency | Fluid Lost | Clinical Features |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Isotonic (Isonatremic)** | 130-150 mEq/L | 80% | Na and water proportional | Classic signs of dehydration |
| **Hypotonic (Hyponatremic)** | <130 mEq/L | 5-10% | More Na than water | Severe signs early, doughy skin, seizures |
| **Hypertonic (Hypernatremic)** | >150 mEq/L | 10-15% | More water than Na | Doughy skin, irritability, seizures, less visible signs |

### Severity Assessment
| Parameter | Mild (3-5%) | Moderate (5-10%) | Severe (>10%) |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Mental status | Alert | Restless, irritable | Lethargic, unconscious |
| Eyes | Normal | Sunken | Deeply sunken |
| Tears | Present | Reduced | Absent |
| Mouth | Moist | Dry | Parched |
| Skin pinch | Quick return | Slow (<2 sec) | Very slow (>2 sec) |
| Pulse | Normal | Rapid | Rapid, weak/absent |
| CRT | Normal | 2-3 seconds | >3 seconds |
| Urine output | Slightly reduced | Oliguria | Anuria |
| Fontanelle | Normal | Sunken | Very sunken |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Common Causes
- **Diarrhea** (most common) — acute gastroenteritis
- **Vomiting** — pyloric stenosis, gastroenteritis
- **Decreased intake** — pharyngitis, stomatitis, anorexia
- **Increased losses** — fever, burns, DKA, diabetes insipidus
- **Third-spacing** — peritonitis, intestinal obstruction

### Pathophysiology
1. Fluid loss → ↓intravascular volume → ↓cardiac output → ↓tissue perfusion
2. **Compensatory mechanisms:** ↑HR, ↑SVR (peripheral vasoconstriction), ↑ADH, ↑aldosterone
3. Electrolyte losses with diarrhea: Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻
4. **Metabolic acidosis** — bicarbonate loss in stool + lactic acidosis from poor perfusion
5. **Hypokalemia** — K⁺ loss in stool; worsened by acidosis correction

### Hypernatremic Dehydration — Special Concerns
- Water loss > sodium loss (high-solute feeds, diabetes insipidus, inadequate water intake)
- Brain cells generate idiogenic osmoles to prevent shrinkage
- **Rapid correction → cerebral edema → seizures, death**
- Must correct slowly over 48-72 hours (Na drop <0.5 mEq/L/h, <10-12 mEq/L/day)

## Clinical Features

### General Signs (All Types)
- Thirst (earliest symptom)
- Dry mucous membranes, cracked lips
- Sunken eyes, absent tears
- Decreased skin turgor (skin pinch test)
- Tachycardia, weak pulses
- Decreased urine output
- Sunken anterior fontanelle (infants)
- Cool extremities, prolonged CRT
- Altered sensorium (severe)

### Type-Specific Features
| Feature | Isotonic | Hypotonic | Hypertonic |
|---------|----------|-----------|------------|
| Skin turgor | Reduced | Very reduced (doughy) | Less obviously reduced (doughy) |
| Clinical signs | Proportional to deficit | Appear MORE dehydrated | Appear LESS dehydrated |
| Sensorium | Lethargy | Seizures, coma (early) | Irritability → seizures (late) |
| Skin feel | Dry | Doughy, cold | Doughy, thick ("velvety") |
| Shock | Late | Early | Late (intravascular volume preserved) |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment
- WHO dehydration classification (no/some/severe)
- Weight change is the GOLD STANDARD (if pre-illness weight known)
  - % dehydration = (pre-illness wt − current wt) / pre-illness wt × 100

### Laboratory
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Serum Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ | Type of dehydration, guide replacement |
| Blood gas (ABG/VBG) | Metabolic acidosis (↓HCO₃, ↓pH) |
| BUN, creatinine | Prerenal azotemia |
| Blood glucose | Hypoglycemia risk (malnourished) |
| Urine specific gravity | >1.020 suggests dehydration |
| Serum osmolality | Type classification |

## Management

### WHO Plan A — No Dehydration (Home)
- Extra fluids after each stool: <2y: 50-100 mL; >2y: 100-200 mL
- Continue breastfeeding and age-appropriate diet
- ORS, rice water, dal water, lemon water
- Zinc supplementation
- Danger signs to watch for

### WHO Plan B — Some Dehydration (Health Facility)
- **ORS 75 mL/kg over 4 hours** (in small, frequent sips)
- Reassess after 4 hours
- If improved → Plan A; if worse → Plan C
- Continue breastfeeding between ORS

### WHO Plan C — Severe Dehydration (Emergency IV)
**IV Fluid Protocol:**
| Age | Phase 1 (Rapid) | Phase 2 (Slower) |
|-----|-----------------|------------------|
| Infants (<12 months) | 30 mL/kg in 1 hour | 70 mL/kg in 5 hours |
| Children (>12 months) | 30 mL/kg in 30 min | 70 mL/kg in 2.5 hours |

- **Fluid:** Ringer's Lactate (preferred) or Normal Saline
- Reassess every 1-2 hours
- Switch to ORS as soon as child can drink
- If IV not available: NG tube ORS at 20 mL/kg/hour

### Hypernatremic Dehydration Correction
1. **Initial:** If shocked: NS bolus 20 mL/kg (repeat if needed)
2. **Replacement:** 0.45% NS + 5% dextrose
3. **Rate:** Correct over 48-72 hours
4. **Target:** Na⁺ drop ≤0.5 mEq/L/hour (max 10-12 mEq/day)
5. **Monitor:** Serum Na every 4-6 hours
6. **Watch for:** Seizures (cerebral edema from too-rapid correction)

### Hyponatremic Dehydration
- If Na⁺ <120 with seizures: **3% NaCl** 3-5 mL/kg over 15-20 min
- Otherwise: NS for initial correction
- Goal: raise Na by 8-10 mEq/L in first 24 hours (not faster)

### Potassium Replacement
- Add K⁺ to IV fluids ONLY after urine output established
- 20-40 mEq/L of KCl in maintenance fluids
- Never give IV bolus potassium
- Oral K⁺ preferred when possible (ORS contains 20 mEq/L K⁺)

### Maintenance Fluid Calculation (Holliday-Segar)
| Weight | Rate |
|--------|------|
| First 10 kg | 100 mL/kg/day |
| 10-20 kg | 50 mL/kg/day |
| >20 kg | 20 mL/kg/day |

## Complications
- **Hypovolemic shock** — most dangerous acute complication
- **Metabolic acidosis** — from HCO₃ loss and poor perfusion
- **Hypokalemia** — cardiac arrhythmias, ileus
- **Cerebral edema** — from too-rapid correction of hypernatremia
- **Central pontine myelinolysis** — from too-rapid correction of hyponatremia
- **Renal failure** — acute tubular necrosis from prolonged hypoperfusion
- **Seizures** — from electrolyte imbalance or cerebral edema
- **Death** — if untreated severe dehydration
