# Acute Diarrhea in Children

## Overview
Acute diarrhea is defined as passage of 3 or more loose/watery stools per day lasting <14 days. It is the second leading cause of under-5 mortality globally, causing ~525,000 deaths annually. Rotavirus is the most common cause worldwide. The cornerstone of management is prevention and treatment of dehydration using ORS, with zinc supplementation. Most cases are self-limiting and do not require antibiotics.

## Key Points
- **Second leading cause** of under-5 mortality globally
- **Rotavirus** — most common cause of severe dehydrating diarrhea
- **ORS** is the single most important intervention (has saved millions of lives)
- **Low-osmolarity ORS (245 mOsm/L)** — current WHO recommendation
- **Zinc supplementation** — 20 mg/day × 14 days (reduces duration and recurrence)
- **Continue breastfeeding** during diarrhea
- **Antibiotics only for:** Cholera, Shigella (bloody diarrhea), severe cases
- **Plan A/B/C** — WHO treatment plans based on dehydration severity

## Definition & Classification

### By Duration
| Type | Duration |
|------|----------|
| Acute | <14 days |
| Persistent | 14-30 days |
| Chronic | >30 days |

### By Mechanism
| Type | Features | Common Causes |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Secretory** | Large volume, watery, no blood | Cholera, ETEC, rotavirus |
| **Osmotic** | Reduces with fasting | Lactose intolerance, ORS misuse |
| **Invasive/Inflammatory** | Blood/mucus, fever, tenesmus | Shigella, Salmonella, E. histolytica |

### Dehydration Assessment (WHO)
| Feature | No Dehydration | Some Dehydration | Severe Dehydration |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **General** | Well, alert | Restless, irritable | Lethargic/unconscious |
| **Eyes** | Normal | Sunken | Very sunken, dry |
| **Tears** | Present | Absent | Absent |
| **Mouth/tongue** | Moist | Dry | Very dry |
| **Thirst** | Normal | Thirsty, drinks eagerly | Unable to drink |
| **Skin pinch** | Goes back quickly | Goes back slowly | Goes back very slowly (>2 sec) |
| **Fluid deficit** | <5% | 5-10% | >10% |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Common Causes by Age
| Age | Common Pathogens |
|-----|-----------------|
| **<6 months** | EPEC, rotavirus |
| **6 months-2 years** | Rotavirus (most common), ETEC, Shigella |
| **>2 years** | Shigella, Salmonella, E. histolytica, Giardia |

### Viral (Most Common — 70%)
- **Rotavirus** — most common cause of severe dehydration; winter peak
- Norovirus — most common cause in >5 years
- Adenovirus (type 40, 41)
- Astrovirus

### Bacterial
- **ETEC** — traveler's diarrhea, watery
- **Shigella** — bloody diarrhea (dysentery), seizures
- **Cholera** — rice-water stools, severe dehydration
- **Salmonella** — bloody diarrhea, bacteremia risk in infants
- **Campylobacter** — bloody diarrhea, Guillain-Barré association

### Parasitic
- **E. histolytica** — amoebic dysentery
- **Giardia** — chronic watery diarrhea, malabsorption
- **Cryptosporidium** — immunocompromised

### Pathophysiology
1. **Secretory:** Toxins activate adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion → water follows
2. **Osmotic:** Unabsorbed solutes retain water in lumen
3. **Invasive:** Mucosal invasion → inflammation → bloody diarrhea
4. **Dehydration:** Loss of water and electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, HCO₃⁻) → metabolic acidosis

## Clinical Features

### Diarrhea Characteristics
- **Watery diarrhea:** Rotavirus, cholera, ETEC — dehydration is main risk
- **Bloody diarrhea (dysentery):** Shigella, EIEC, Salmonella, E. histolytica — systemic toxicity
- **Rice-water stools:** Cholera
- **Mucoid, foul-smelling:** Giardia

### Signs of Dehydration
- Dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, absent tears
- Decreased skin turgor (>2 sec = severe)
- Tachycardia, weak pulses
- Decreased urine output
- Sunken fontanelle (infants)
- Altered sensorium (severe)

### Danger Signs (Refer Immediately)
- Unable to drink or breastfeed
- Vomiting everything
- Convulsions
- Lethargic or unconscious
- Blood in stool with high fever

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment
- Dehydration severity (WHO classification)
- Duration, frequency, presence of blood
- Feeding history, recent travel, contacts

### Investigations (Not Routine)
- **Stool examination:** Microscopy (WBCs, RBCs, parasites), culture
- **Stool for rotavirus antigen** (ELISA)
- **Electrolytes, blood gas** — if severe dehydration or IV fluids needed
- **Blood glucose** — hypoglycemia risk in malnourished
- Most mild-moderate cases need NO investigations

## Management

### WHO Treatment Plans

#### Plan A — No Dehydration (Treat at Home)
- Continue breastfeeding/feeding
- Increase fluids (ORS after each loose stool)
- ORS amount: <2 years: 50-100 mL; >2 years: 100-200 mL per stool
- Zinc: 20 mg/day × 14 days (10 mg if <6 months)
- Return if: unable to drink, blood in stool, high fever, not improving

#### Plan B — Some Dehydration (ORS Under Supervision)
- ORS 75 mL/kg over 4 hours
- Reassess after 4 hours
- Continue breastfeeding between ORS
- If improving → Plan A; if worsening → Plan C

#### Plan C — Severe Dehydration (IV Fluids)
- **Ringer's Lactate** (preferred) or Normal Saline
- 30 mL/kg in 1 hour, then 70 mL/kg in 5 hours (infants)
- 30 mL/kg in 30 min, then 70 mL/kg in 2.5 hours (>1 year)
- Reassess frequently; switch to ORS when able to drink
- If IV not possible: NG tube ORS 20 mL/kg/h

### Low-Osmolarity ORS (Current WHO Formula)
| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Sodium | 75 mEq/L |
| Potassium | 20 mEq/L |
| Chloride | 65 mEq/L |
| Glucose | 75 mmol/L |
| Citrate | 10 mmol/L |
| Osmolarity | 245 mOsm/L |

### Antibiotics — Only Specific Indications
| Indication | Antibiotic |
|------------|-----------|
| **Cholera** | Doxycycline (single dose) or Azithromycin |
| **Shigella dysentery** | Ciprofloxacin or Azithromycin |
| **Amoebic dysentery** | Metronidazole |
| **Giardiasis** | Metronidazole |

### What NOT to Do
- NO antidiarrheals (loperamide) in children
- NO antiemetics routinely
- NO unnecessary antibiotics
- NO withholding feeds ("starving the gut")
- NO undiluted fruit juices or carbonated drinks

## Complications
- **Dehydration** — hypovolemic shock, death (most common cause of mortality)
- **Electrolyte imbalance** — hyponatremia/hypernatremia, hypokalemia
- **Metabolic acidosis** — bicarbonate loss
- **Hypoglycemia** — especially malnourished children
- **Secondary lactose intolerance** — transient villous damage
- **Persistent diarrhea** — if >14 days
- **Malnutrition** — diarrhea-malnutrition vicious cycle
- **Seizures** — Shigella (neurotoxin), electrolyte imbalance
- **HUS** — Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC O157:H7)
