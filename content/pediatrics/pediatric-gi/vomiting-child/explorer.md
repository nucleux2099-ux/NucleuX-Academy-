# Vomiting in Children

## Overview
Vomiting is one of the most common symptoms in pediatric practice. It is a protective reflex but when persistent can lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and nutritional compromise. Causes vary significantly by age — GERD and pyloric stenosis in neonates/infants, gastroenteritis in toddlers, and raised ICP or appendicitis in older children. A systematic approach to differentiate benign from surgical/dangerous causes is essential.

## Key Points
- **Most common cause overall:** Acute gastroenteritis
- **Bilious vomiting in a neonate** = surgical emergency until proven otherwise (malrotation/volvulus)
- **Pyloric stenosis:** Projectile non-bilious vomiting at 3-6 weeks; palpable olive
- **Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis:** Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
- **Raised ICP:** Morning vomiting, headache, papilledema
- **Cyclical vomiting syndrome:** Recurrent stereotypical episodes with well intervals
- **Key distinction:** Bilious vs non-bilious; projectile vs non-projectile

## Definition & Classification

### By Character
| Type | Suggests |
|------|----------|
| **Bilious (green/yellow)** | Obstruction distal to ampulla of Vater — SURGICAL |
| **Non-bilious** | Gastritis, GERD, pyloric stenosis, metabolic |
| **Projectile** | Pyloric stenosis, raised ICP |
| **Bloody (hematemesis)** | Mallory-Weiss, esophagitis, peptic ulcer |
| **Feculent** | Distal bowel obstruction |

### By Age
| Age | Common Causes |
|-----|---------------|
| **Neonate** | GERD, pyloric stenosis, malrotation/volvulus, atresia, NEC, sepsis, inborn errors |
| **Infant** | GERD, gastroenteritis, intussusception, CMPA, UTI |
| **Toddler/Child** | AGE, appendicitis, UTI, DKA, raised ICP, foreign body |
| **Adolescent** | AGE, appendicitis, pregnancy, eating disorders, cyclical vomiting |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes — Systematic Approach
**GI:** GERD, gastroenteritis, pyloric stenosis, malrotation, intussusception, appendicitis, Hirschsprung's
**CNS:** Raised ICP (tumor, meningitis, hydrocephalus), migraine, cyclical vomiting
**Metabolic:** DKA, inborn errors of metabolism (urea cycle, organic acidemias), Addisonian crisis
**Renal:** UTI (common cause of unexplained vomiting in infants), renal failure
**Drugs/Toxins:** Chemotherapy, digoxin, theophylline, accidental ingestion
**Surgical:** Intestinal obstruction, appendicitis, testicular torsion
**Other:** Food allergy, motion sickness, psychogenic

### Pathophysiology of Vomiting
1. **Vomiting center** in lateral reticular formation of medulla
2. Inputs from: chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ, area postrema), vagal afferents (GI), vestibular system, higher cortical centers
3. CTZ lies outside blood-brain barrier — detects circulating toxins, drugs
4. Coordinated motor response: diaphragm contraction, abdominal muscle contraction, glottis closure, pylorus closure, gastric retroperistalsis

## Clinical Features

### Red Flags in Vomiting Child
- **Bilious vomiting** — intestinal obstruction (surgical emergency)
- **Projectile vomiting at 3-6 weeks** — pyloric stenosis
- **Blood in vomit** — esophagitis, Mallory-Weiss, ulcer
- **Early morning vomiting + headache** — raised ICP
- **Abdominal distension + absent bowel sounds** — obstruction/ileus
- **Bulging fontanelle** — raised ICP, meningitis
- **Signs of dehydration** — prolonged vomiting
- **Failure to thrive** — chronic vomiting cause
- **Altered sensorium** — metabolic (DKA, IEM), CNS

### Pyloric Stenosis (Key Topic)
- **Age:** 3-6 weeks; male:female 4:1
- **Projectile non-bilious vomiting** after feeds
- **Hungry baby** — feeds immediately after vomiting
- **Visible peristalsis** left to right across abdomen
- **Palpable "olive"** in right upper quadrant
- **Metabolic:** Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis (loss of HCl)
- **Diagnosis:** Ultrasound — pyloric muscle thickness >3 mm, length >15 mm
- **Treatment:** Ramstedt pyloromyotomy (after correcting electrolytes)

## Diagnosis

### History
- Timing (relation to feeds, morning), character (bilious/bloody/projectile), frequency
- Associated symptoms: fever, diarrhea, headache, weight loss
- Diet changes, drug exposure, family history

### Examination
- Dehydration assessment
- Abdominal exam: distension, tenderness, masses (olive), visible peristalsis
- Neurological: fontanelle, papilledema, meningism

### Investigations
| Indication | Tests |
|-----------|-------|
| Dehydration | Electrolytes, blood gas, BUN/creatinine |
| Suspected obstruction | Abdominal X-ray (air-fluid levels), USS |
| Pyloric stenosis | Ultrasound (pyloric dimensions) |
| Raised ICP | CT/MRI brain |
| Metabolic | Blood glucose, ammonia, lactate, urine ketones |
| Infection | Urine analysis/culture, blood culture |

## Management

### General Principles
- **Correct dehydration** — ORS or IV fluids
- **Correct electrolyte abnormalities** — especially K⁺, Cl⁻
- **Treat underlying cause**
- **Antiemetics** — use judiciously in children

### Antiemetics in Children
| Drug | Mechanism | Use |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Ondansetron** | 5-HT3 antagonist | Gastroenteritis vomiting (safe, effective) |
| **Domperidone** | D2 antagonist (peripheral) | GERD-related vomiting |
| **Promethazine** | H1/anticholinergic | Motion sickness (avoid <2 years) |
| **Metoclopramide** | D2 antagonist (central) | Avoid in children (extrapyramidal side effects) |

### Surgical Causes — Urgent Referral
- Bilious vomiting + abdominal distension → X-ray → surgical consult
- Pyloric stenosis → USS → correct electrolytes → Ramstedt pyloromyotomy
- Intussusception → USS (target/donut sign) → air/barium enema reduction

## Complications
- **Dehydration and electrolyte imbalance**
- **Metabolic alkalosis** (loss of gastric acid — pyloric stenosis)
- **Aspiration pneumonia** — especially in altered sensorium
- **Mallory-Weiss tear** — forceful vomiting → hematemesis
- **Malnutrition / failure to thrive** — chronic vomiting
- **Esophagitis** — from chronic GERD
- **Dental erosion** — chronic vomiting (bulimia in adolescents)
