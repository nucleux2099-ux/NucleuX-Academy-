# Wheezing in Children

## Overview
Wheezing is a continuous, high-pitched musical sound produced by turbulent airflow through narrowed airways. It is extremely common in young children — up to 50% of children wheeze at least once before age 6. The differential diagnosis is broad, ranging from viral-induced wheeze (most common) to asthma, foreign body aspiration, and structural airway anomalies. Distinguishing transient viral wheezers from true asthmatics guides management and prognosis.

## Key Points
- **Wheeze = expiratory** high-pitched sound from lower airway obstruction
- **50% of children** wheeze by age 6; most are transient viral wheezers
- **RSV bronchiolitis** — most common cause of wheezing in infants <1 year
- **Viral-induced wheeze** — triggered by viral infections only; no atopy
- **Asthma** — recurrent wheeze + atopy + response to bronchodilators
- **Foreign body** — sudden onset unilateral wheeze in previously well child
- **Modified Asthma Predictive Index (mAPI)** — predicts persistence of wheezing as asthma
- **Bronchiolitis:** Supportive care only; no bronchodilators, no steroids

## Definition & Classification

### Wheezing Phenotypes in Children
| Phenotype | Features | Prognosis |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Transient early wheezers** | <3 years; viral URTI trigger; no atopy; reduced lung function at birth | Resolve by age 3-6 |
| **Non-atopic wheezers** | Viral trigger; normal IgE; no family history | Improve by school age |
| **Atopic/persistent wheezers** | Atopy (eczema, allergic rhinitis), family history of asthma, elevated IgE | Persist → asthma |
| **Late-onset wheezers** | Start after age 6; allergic sensitization | Persist into adulthood |

### Modified Asthma Predictive Index (mAPI)
**Recurrent wheeze in <3 years + 1 major OR 2 minor criteria:**
- **Major:** Parent with asthma, physician-diagnosed atopic dermatitis, allergic sensitization to aeroallergen
- **Minor:** Allergic sensitization to milk/egg, wheezing apart from colds, eosinophilia ≥4%

Positive mAPI → ~80% chance of asthma at school age

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes of Wheezing by Age
| Age | Common Causes |
|-----|---------------|
| **<1 year** | Bronchiolitis (RSV), congenital anomalies (vascular ring, tracheomalacia), GERD, CF |
| **1-3 years** | Viral-induced wheeze, foreign body aspiration, asthma beginning |
| **>3 years** | Asthma, persistent viral wheeze, allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis |

### Differential Diagnosis of Recurrent Wheeze
- **Asthma** (most common cause of recurrent wheeze after infancy)
- **Post-viral/viral wheeze** (single trigger, episodic)
- **Foreign body aspiration** (sudden onset, unilateral)
- **GERD** (nocturnal cough, feeding-related symptoms)
- **Cystic fibrosis** (steatorrhea, recurrent pneumonia, failure to thrive)
- **Primary ciliary dyskinesia** (chronic rhinosinusitis, situs inversus)
- **Congenital anomalies** (tracheomalacia, vascular ring, bronchogenic cyst)
- **Cardiac causes** (large L→R shunt with pulmonary overcirculation)
- **Tuberculosis**
- **Immune deficiency** (recurrent infections)

### Bronchiolitis (Most Common Cause in <1 Year)
- **RSV** — most common cause (60-80%)
- Peak 2-6 months; winter epidemics
- Inflammation and necrosis of bronchiolar epithelium → mucus plugging → air trapping
- Treatment: SUPPORTIVE ONLY (O₂, fluids, nasal suctioning)
- NO bronchodilators, NO steroids, NO antibiotics (routinely)
- **Palivizumab** — RSV prophylaxis for high-risk infants (preterm, BPD, CHD)

## Clinical Features

### History Clues
| Finding | Suggests |
|---------|----------|
| First episode, infant, winter, URTI prodrome | Bronchiolitis |
| Recurrent episodes, atopy, family history | Asthma |
| Sudden onset, choking episode | Foreign body aspiration |
| Since birth, stridor + wheeze | Congenital anomaly |
| Steatorrhea, recurrent infections, FTT | Cystic fibrosis |
| Feeding-related, nocturnal | GERD |
| Response to bronchodilators | Asthma |

### Examination
- **Wheeze:** Expiratory > inspiratory; bilateral = bronchiolitis/asthma; unilateral = FB
- **Tachypnea, chest retractions, nasal flaring** (respiratory distress)
- **Hyperinflation** (barrel chest) — chronic air trapping
- **Clubbing** — CF, bronchiectasis (NOT asthma)
- **Eczema** — atopic wheeze/asthma

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment
- Episode pattern (episodic viral vs multi-trigger)
- Trigger identification
- Atopic status (personal/family history)
- Response to bronchodilator trial (diagnostic for asthma)

### Investigations
| Test | When |
|------|------|
| **Trial of SABA** | Reversibility suggests asthma |
| **CXR** | Atypical presentation, first wheeze, suspected FB |
| **SpO₂** | All wheezing children |
| **Spirometry** | >6 years (FEV1 reversibility for asthma) |
| **Allergy testing** | Recurrent wheeze (IgE, skin prick test) |
| **Sweat chloride** | If CF suspected |
| **CT chest** | Structural anomaly suspected |
| **Bronchoscopy** | Foreign body, structural assessment |

## Management

### Acute Wheezing Episode
1. **Assess severity:** Respiratory rate, SpO₂, air entry, ability to talk/feed
2. **Oxygen** if SpO₂ <92%
3. **Salbutamol trial** (MDI via spacer or nebulized)
   - Good response → likely asthma; continue with asthma management
   - No response → consider bronchiolitis, FB, other causes
4. **Add ipratropium** for moderate-severe
5. **Systemic steroids** (prednisolone) for moderate-severe asthma exacerbation
6. **DO NOT give bronchodilators/steroids** routinely for bronchiolitis

### Bronchiolitis Management
- **Supportive:** O₂, nasal suctioning, NG/IV fluids if unable to feed
- **Monitoring:** SpO₂, respiratory effort, hydration
- **Discharge criteria:** SpO₂ >92% on room air, adequate feeding
- **NO evidence for:** Bronchodilators, steroids, antibiotics, hypertonic saline (routine)

### Long-term Management of Recurrent Wheezing
- If likely asthma (positive mAPI) → start ICS trial (4-8 weeks)
- Good response confirms asthma → continue as per GINA steps
- Poor response → reconsider diagnosis, investigate further

## Complications
- **Respiratory failure** — severe bronchiolitis or status asthmaticus
- **Apnea** — bronchiolitis in young infants (especially ex-preterm)
- **Recurrent wheezing episodes** — impaired quality of life
- **Chronic lung disease** — if underlying cause untreated
- **Growth impact** — from chronic respiratory disease
