# Upper Respiratory Infections in Children

## Overview
Upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) are the most common infections in children, accounting for 50% of all pediatric outpatient visits. They include common cold (acute nasopharyngitis), pharyngitis/tonsillitis, acute otitis media, sinusitis, and croup. Most are viral and self-limiting, requiring only symptomatic treatment. The key clinical skill is identifying the few cases requiring antibiotics (GAS pharyngitis, AOM, bacterial sinusitis) from the vast majority that don't.

## Key Points
- **Most common infection in children** — average 6-8 URTIs per year in young children
- **Rhinovirus** — most common cause of common cold
- **Most URTIs are viral** — antibiotics NOT indicated
- **GAS pharyngitis** — only pharyngitis requiring antibiotics (prevent rheumatic fever)
- **Centor/McIsaac criteria** — guide decision for throat swab/RADT in pharyngitis
- **Croup:** Barking cough + inspiratory stridor + hoarseness; steeple sign on X-ray
- **Parainfluenza virus** — most common cause of croup
- **Epiglottitis** — H. influenzae type b; drooling, tripod position; medical emergency
- **AOM** — most common bacterial complication of URTI; amoxicillin first-line

## Definition & Classification

### Types of URTIs
| Condition | Site | Common Cause | Key Feature |
|-----------|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Common cold** | Nasopharynx | Rhinovirus | Rhinorrhea, sneezing, mild fever |
| **Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis** | Pharynx/tonsils | Viral (70%); GAS (30%) | Sore throat, exudates |
| **Acute Otitis Media** | Middle ear | S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Moraxella | Ear pain, fever, bulging TM |
| **Sinusitis** | Paranasal sinuses | Same as AOM | Nasal discharge >10 days, facial pain |
| **Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis)** | Larynx/trachea | Parainfluenza | Barking cough, stridor, steeple sign |
| **Epiglottitis** | Epiglottis | H. influenzae type b | Drooling, tripod, thumb sign; EMERGENCY |
| **Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy)** | Peritonsillar space | GAS, anaerobes | Trismus, "hot potato" voice, unilateral |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Common Cold
- **Rhinovirus** (30-50%), coronavirus, RSV, adenovirus, parainfluenza, enterovirus
- Spread by droplets and fomites; incubation 1-3 days
- Self-limiting: 7-10 days

### Pharyngitis
- **Viral (70%):** Adenovirus, EBV (infectious mononucleosis), enterovirus, influenza
- **Bacterial (30%):** Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes) — only one requiring antibiotics
- GAS pharyngitis: Age 5-15 years; sudden onset sore throat, fever, tonsillar exudate, anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, NO cough/rhinorrhea

### Croup
- **Parainfluenza virus types 1 & 3** (75%)
- Age 6 months-3 years; autumn/winter peak
- Subglottic inflammation → airway narrowing → stridor, barking cough

### AOM
- S. pneumoniae (35%), H. influenzae (25%), Moraxella catarrhalis (15%)
- Usually follows viral URTI → eustachian tube dysfunction → middle ear effusion → bacterial superinfection

## Clinical Features

### Common Cold
- Rhinorrhea (clear → mucopurulent), nasal congestion, sneezing
- Mild sore throat, cough
- Low-grade fever
- Self-resolving in 7-10 days
- Yellow/green nasal discharge does NOT mean bacterial infection

### GAS Pharyngitis vs Viral Pharyngitis
| Feature | GAS (Bacterial) | Viral |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| Age | 5-15 years | Any age |
| Onset | Sudden | Gradual |
| Sore throat | Severe | Mild-moderate |
| Fever | High (>38.5°C) | Low-grade |
| Tonsillar exudates | Common | Uncommon |
| Cervical lymphadenopathy | Tender, anterior | Less prominent |
| Cough/rhinorrhea | **Absent** | Present |
| Palatal petechiae | Present (specific for GAS) | Absent |
| Scarlatiniform rash | May be present | Absent |

### Croup — Westley Croup Score
- **Barking cough** (seal-like) — hallmark
- **Inspiratory stridor**
- **Hoarseness**
- Worse at night; aggravated by agitation
- **Steeple sign** on AP neck X-ray (subglottic narrowing)

### Epiglottitis (EMERGENCY)
- Sudden onset high fever, severe sore throat
- **Drooling** (unable to swallow)
- **Tripod/sniffing position** (sitting forward, chin extended)
- **Muffled "hot potato" voice**
- **Thumb sign** on lateral neck X-ray (enlarged epiglottis)
- **DO NOT examine throat** (may precipitate complete obstruction)
- Rare now due to Hib vaccination

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis for Most URTIs
- Common cold, croup — diagnosed clinically

### GAS Pharyngitis
- **Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT)** — high specificity; if positive, treat
- **Throat culture** — gold standard (if RADT negative)
- **McIsaac Score:** Fever, no cough, tonsillar exudates, tender anterior cervical nodes, age <15 → score ≥4: empirical treatment or RADT

### AOM
- **Otoscopy:** Bulging, erythematous, immobile tympanic membrane; middle ear effusion
- Pneumatic otoscopy: Decreased TM mobility

### Imaging
- **AP neck X-ray (croup):** Steeple sign (subglottic narrowing)
- **Lateral neck X-ray (epiglottitis):** Thumb sign (swollen epiglottis)
- **Sinus X-ray/CT:** Opacification, air-fluid level (sinusitis)

## Management

### Common Cold
- **Symptomatic only:** Saline nasal drops, adequate fluids, paracetamol for fever
- **NO antibiotics, NO antihistamines** in <2 years, NO cough suppressants in <6 years
- Honey (>1 year) may help cough

### GAS Pharyngitis
- **Penicillin V** (oral) × 10 days — drug of choice
- **Amoxicillin** × 10 days — alternative (better taste for children)
- **Benzathine Penicillin G** IM single dose — if compliance concerns
- **Penicillin allergy:** Azithromycin × 5 days
- **Why treat:** Prevent rheumatic fever (treatment effective up to 9 days after symptom onset)

### Croup
| Severity | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| Mild (barking cough, no stridor at rest) | Mist/cool air, oral dexamethasone 0.15-0.6 mg/kg single dose |
| Moderate (stridor at rest) | Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg + nebulized epinephrine |
| Severe (severe distress, decreased air entry) | Nebulized epinephrine (0.5 mL/kg of 1:1000, max 5 mL) + dexamethasone + observe ≥4h |

### AOM
- **Amoxicillin** 80-90 mg/kg/day × 10 days (first-line)
- **Watch and wait** option for mild AOM in >2 years (72-hour observation)
- **Failure:** Amoxicillin-clavulanate
- Pain management: Paracetamol/ibuprofen

### Epiglottitis
- **AIRWAY EMERGENCY** — secure airway first (intubation/tracheostomy)
- IV Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime
- Do NOT examine throat, do NOT agitate child

## Complications
- **AOM → hearing loss** (most common complication of AOM; conductive)
- **Mastoiditis** — extension from AOM (tenderness behind ear, displaced pinna)
- **Peritonsillar abscess** — from pharyngitis (trismus, uvula deviation)
- **Rheumatic fever** — from untreated GAS pharyngitis
- **Post-streptococcal GN** — from GAS (not prevented by antibiotics)
- **Sinusitis** → orbital cellulitis → cavernous sinus thrombosis
- **Croup** → respiratory failure (rare)
- **Epiglottitis** → complete airway obstruction → death
