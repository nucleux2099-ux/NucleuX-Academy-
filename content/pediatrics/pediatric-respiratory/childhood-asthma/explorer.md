# Childhood Asthma

## Overview
Asthma is the most common chronic respiratory disease in children, affecting 5-15% globally. It is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by reversible airway obstruction, bronchial hyperresponsiveness, and airway inflammation. It presents with recurrent episodes of wheezing, cough, chest tightness, and breathlessness. Inhaled corticosteroids are the cornerstone of long-term control, while inhaled SABA is the rescue medication.

## Key Points
- **Most common chronic disease of childhood**
- **Triad:** Airway inflammation + reversible obstruction + bronchial hyperresponsiveness
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical + spirometry showing reversibility (≥12% improvement with SABA)
- **ICS (Inhaled Corticosteroids)** — mainstay of controller therapy
- **SABA (Salbutamol)** — first-line rescue/reliever
- **Spacer with MDI** is as effective as nebulizer (preferred for maintenance)
- **Exercise-induced bronchospasm** is common in children
- **Viral infections** are the most common trigger in young children
- **GINA guidelines** — stepwise approach to management

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Atopy** (strongest risk factor) — personal/family history of eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy
- **Family history** of asthma
- **Viral respiratory infections** (RSV, rhinovirus) — triggers and risk factor
- **Allergens:** House dust mite (most common), cockroach, animal dander, molds, pollen
- **Environmental:** Tobacco smoke exposure, air pollution, indoor biomass fuel
- **Obesity**
- **Male sex** (pre-pubertal); Female sex (post-pubertal)

### Pathophysiology
1. **Allergic sensitization:** Allergen → IgE production → mast cell sensitization
2. **Early phase (minutes):** Allergen cross-links IgE → mast cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction, edema, mucus
3. **Late phase (4-8 hours):** Eosinophils, Th2 lymphocytes infiltrate → sustained inflammation
4. **Chronic inflammation:** Airway remodeling — basement membrane thickening, smooth muscle hypertrophy, goblet cell hyperplasia
5. **Bronchial hyperresponsiveness:** Exaggerated bronchoconstriction to triggers
6. **Key cells:** Eosinophils, mast cells, Th2 lymphocytes
7. **Key mediators:** IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, leukotrienes, histamine

## Clinical Features

### Typical Symptoms
- **Recurrent wheezing** — expiratory > inspiratory
- **Chronic cough** — worse at night and early morning
- **Chest tightness**
- **Breathlessness** — episodic
- **Triggered by:** Exercise, cold air, viral infections, allergens, smoke
- **Symptom variability** — worse at night, seasonal variation, trigger-related

### Acute Exacerbation Assessment
| Feature | Mild | Moderate | Severe | Life-threatening |
|---------|------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Speech | Full sentences | Phrases | Words | Unable to speak |
| SpO₂ | >95% | 92-95% | <92% | <90% |
| Peak flow | >75% | 50-75% | 33-50% | <33% |
| Heart rate | Normal | Increased | >120 | Bradycardia |
| Accessory muscles | No | Some | Yes | Exhaustion |
| Wheeze | End-expiratory | Throughout | Loud | Silent chest ⚠️ |

### Features Suggesting Asthma in <5 Years
- ≥3 episodes of wheezing
- Wheeze with viral infections + between episodes
- Personal history of atopy
- Family history of asthma/atopy
- Response to bronchodilator trial

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis (Primarily)
- Pattern of symptoms: recurrent, variable, triggered, worse at night
- Family/personal atopic history
- Physical exam: wheeze (may be normal between episodes)

### Spirometry (>6 Years)
- **FEV1/FVC <80%** (obstructive pattern)
- **Reversibility:** ≥12% increase in FEV1 after SABA (200 μg salbutamol)
- **Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF):** >20% variability = suggestive

### Other Investigations
- **Chest X-ray:** Usually normal; hyperinflation during acute attack
- **Allergy testing:** Skin prick test, specific IgE (identify triggers)
- **Fractional exhaled NO (FeNO):** Elevated in eosinophilic airway inflammation
- **Blood eosinophil count:** May be elevated
- **Bronchial provocation (methacholine):** For doubtful cases (positive if PC20 <8 mg/mL)

## Management

### GINA Stepwise Approach (Children 6-11 Years)
| Step | Preferred Controller | Alternative |
|------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1 | Low-dose ICS as needed (with SABA) | — |
| 2 | Daily low-dose ICS | LTRA (montelukast) |
| 3 | Low-dose ICS + LABA or Medium-dose ICS | Low-dose ICS + LTRA |
| 4 | Medium-dose ICS + LABA | Refer specialist |
| 5 | High-dose ICS + LABA ± add-on (anti-IgE) | Specialist referral |

**Reliever (all steps):** SABA as needed (salbutamol/albuterol)

### Inhaler Devices by Age
| Age | Device |
|-----|--------|
| <4 years | MDI + spacer + face mask |
| 4-6 years | MDI + spacer with mouthpiece |
| >6 years | MDI + spacer or DPI (dry powder inhaler) |

### Acute Exacerbation Management
1. **Oxygen** to maintain SpO₂ 94-98%
2. **Salbutamol (SABA):** Nebulized 2.5-5 mg or MDI 4-8 puffs via spacer, every 20 min × 3
3. **Ipratropium bromide:** Add for moderate-severe (250-500 μg nebulized)
4. **Systemic corticosteroids:** Prednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day × 3-5 days (oral) or IV methylprednisolone
5. **If severe/life-threatening:** IV magnesium sulfate 50 mg/kg over 20 min, consider IV aminophylline
6. **Monitor:** SpO₂, respiratory rate, peak flow, clinical assessment

### Important Management Points
- **Spacer with MDI** as effective as nebulizer — preferred for home use
- **ICS does not cure** asthma; controls inflammation
- **Step up** if uncontrolled after 2-3 months; **Step down** after 3 months well-controlled
- **Montelukast** (LTRA) — add-on or alternative in mild persistent
- **Allergen avoidance** — dust mite covers, no smoking, pet avoidance
- **Written asthma action plan** — essential for every patient
- **Annual influenza vaccination** recommended

## Complications
- **Status asthmaticus** — severe, prolonged attack not responding to bronchodilators
- **Pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum** — air leak from hyperinflation
- **Atelectasis** — mucus plugging
- **Growth suppression** — from systemic steroid use (ICS effect minimal at low-medium doses)
- **Oral candidiasis** — ICS side effect (prevent with spacer and mouth rinse)
- **Airway remodeling** — irreversible if poorly controlled
- **Respiratory failure** — in severe uncontrolled asthma
- **Death** — rare but preventable with proper management
