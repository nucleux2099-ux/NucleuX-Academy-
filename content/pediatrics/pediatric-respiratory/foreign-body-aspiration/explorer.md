# Foreign Body Aspiration in Children

## Overview
Foreign body aspiration (FBA) is a common pediatric emergency, predominantly affecting children aged 1-3 years. It accounts for significant morbidity and mortality, being a leading cause of accidental death in children under 3. Peanuts and other food items are the most common aspirated objects. The right main bronchus is the most common site of lodgment. Rigid bronchoscopy is both diagnostic and therapeutic (gold standard). A high index of suspicion is needed as initial CXR may be normal in 30-50% of cases.

## Key Points
- **Peak age:** 1-3 years (exploring phase, mouthing behavior, incomplete dentition)
- **Most common foreign body:** Peanuts/nuts (organic — cause more inflammation)
- **Most common site:** Right main bronchus (wider, shorter, more vertical)
- **Rigid bronchoscopy** — gold standard for diagnosis AND removal
- **CXR may be normal** in 30-50% of cases
- **Obstructive emphysema** — most common CXR finding (air trapping)
- **Choking episode** in history is the most important diagnostic clue
- **Heimlich maneuver** — for complete obstruction in conscious child >1 year

## Definition & Classification

### By Location
| Site | Frequency | Features |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Laryngeal** | 2-12% | Stridor, hoarseness, complete obstruction (life-threatening) |
| **Tracheal** | 5-15% | Audible slap/palpatory thud, stridor, biphasic wheeze |
| **Right main bronchus** | 50-60% | Unilateral wheeze, air trapping |
| **Left main bronchus** | 25-35% | Similar to right but less common |

### By Type
| Type | Examples | Characteristics |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Organic** | Peanuts, seeds, beans | Cause intense inflammation, swell when wet, harder to remove |
| **Inorganic** | Coins, buttons, pins, plastic toys | Less inflammation; metallic objects visible on X-ray |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- Age 1-3 years (mouthing behavior, running/playing while eating)
- Incomplete dentition (no molars to grind nuts)
- Older siblings offering inappropriate foods
- Unsupervised eating
- Neurodevelopmental delay (any age)
- Playing with small objects (coins, beads, marbles)

### Pathophysiology
1. Aspiration occurs during inspiration (laughing, crying, running while eating)
2. **Laryngeal FB:** Can cause complete airway obstruction → death within minutes
3. **Bronchial FB — 3 phases:**
   - **Phase 1:** Acute choking, coughing, gagging (immediate)
   - **Phase 2:** Asymptomatic interval (hours to weeks) — FB settles, reflexes subside
   - **Phase 3:** Complications — pneumonia, atelectasis, bronchiectasis (delayed)
4. **Ball-valve mechanism:** FB allows air entry on inspiration but traps it on expiration → obstructive emphysema (hyperinflation)
5. **Complete obstruction:** Atelectasis (lung collapse distal to FB)
6. **Organic FBs:** Swell with moisture → worsening obstruction; lipoid pneumonia from nut oils

## Clinical Features

### Acute Phase
- **Sudden onset choking episode** — most important historical clue
- Paroxysmal coughing
- Gagging, stridor
- Cyanosis (if severe obstruction)
- Aphonia (laryngeal FB)

### After Lodgment
- **Unilateral wheeze** (most common finding on auscultation)
- Decreased air entry on affected side
- **Persistent cough** not responding to treatment
- **Recurrent/non-resolving pneumonia** (same lobe)
- Hemoptysis (mucosal erosion)
- **Asymptomatic interval** can be misleading

### Red Flags
- Sudden respiratory distress in a previously well child
- Unilateral wheeze (especially right-sided)
- Recurrent pneumonia in same location
- Non-resolving pneumonia despite antibiotics
- History of choking episode (even if now asymptomatic)

## Diagnosis

### Chest X-ray (Inspiratory + Expiratory)
| Finding | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Obstructive emphysema (hyperinflation)** | Ball-valve effect — most common finding |
| **Atelectasis** | Complete bronchial obstruction |
| **Mediastinal shift** | Away from hyperinflated side; toward atelectatic side |
| **Radio-opaque FB** | Only metallic/dense objects visible |
| **Normal CXR** | 30-50% of cases! |

- **Expiratory film** or **lateral decubitus** more sensitive than inspiratory film
- Air trapping: Affected side fails to deflate on expiration

### Fluoroscopy
- Dynamic assessment of air trapping
- Mediastinal shift on expiration

### CT Chest
- Useful when CXR inconclusive
- Better sensitivity for radiolucent FBs

### Rigid Bronchoscopy
- **Gold standard** — both diagnostic and therapeutic
- Indicated whenever FBA is suspected clinically, even with normal CXR

## Management

### Emergency — Complete Airway Obstruction
**Conscious child (choking):**
| Age | Technique |
|-----|-----------|
| **<1 year** | 5 back blows (between scapulae, head down) + 5 chest thrusts |
| **>1 year** | **Heimlich maneuver** (abdominal thrusts) |

- **If unconscious:** Start CPR; look for FB during rescue breaths
- **DO NOT** do blind finger sweeps (may push FB deeper)

### Definitive Treatment — Rigid Bronchoscopy
- Under general anesthesia
- Removal using optical forceps through rigid bronchoscope
- **Rigid bronchoscopy preferred** over flexible (better FB removal, better airway control)
- Timing: Semi-urgent (within hours); immediate if complete obstruction

### Post-Removal
- Chest X-ray post-procedure
- Short course antibiotics if secondary infection
- Follow-up bronchoscopy if symptoms persist (retained fragments)
- Steroids may be used for laryngeal edema

### Prevention
- **Avoid nuts, hard foods** in children <3 years
- Supervise eating (no running/playing while eating)
- Keep small objects out of reach
- Age-appropriate toys (no small parts for <3 years)
- Awareness and first-aid training for caregivers

## Complications
- **Asphyxia and death** (laryngeal FB — complete obstruction)
- **Recurrent pneumonia** (same lobe/segment)
- **Lung abscess**
- **Bronchiectasis** (chronic inflammation from retained FB)
- **Pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum**
- **Atelectasis**
- **Granulation tissue** formation (around long-standing FB)
- **Erosion into blood vessels** — hemoptysis (rare, dangerous)
