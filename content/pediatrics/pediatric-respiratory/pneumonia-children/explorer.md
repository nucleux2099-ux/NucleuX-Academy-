# Pneumonia in Children

## Overview
Pneumonia is the leading infectious cause of death in children under 5 globally, causing ~740,000 deaths annually. It is an acute infection of the lung parenchyma presenting with cough, fever, and respiratory distress. WHO classifies pneumonia by respiratory rate and chest indrawing for resource-limited settings. Streptococcus pneumoniae is the most common bacterial cause, while RSV is the most common viral cause.

## Key Points
- **Leading infectious cause of under-5 mortality** worldwide
- **S. pneumoniae** — most common bacterial cause at all ages
- **RSV** — most common viral cause and most common overall in <2 years
- **WHO classification:** Pneumonia vs Severe Pneumonia (based on tachypnea + danger signs)
- **Tachypnea** — most sensitive and specific single sign for pneumonia (WHO)
- **Amoxicillin** — first-line antibiotic for non-severe pneumonia
- **Chest X-ray** not required for non-severe cases (clinical diagnosis)
- **PCV and Hib vaccines** have significantly reduced pneumonia mortality

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Age-Specific Tachypnea Thresholds
| Age | Respiratory Rate |
|-----|-----------------|
| <2 months | ≥60/min |
| 2-12 months | ≥50/min |
| 1-5 years | ≥40/min |

### WHO Classification (Revised)
| Category | Features | Management |
|----------|----------|------------|
| **Pneumonia** | Cough/difficulty breathing + tachypnea | Oral amoxicillin, home |
| **Severe Pneumonia** | Pneumonia + any danger sign (chest indrawing, unable to feed, convulsions, lethargy, stridor) | Hospitalize, IV/IM antibiotics |

### Anatomical Classification
- **Lobar pneumonia:** Consolidation of entire lobe (S. pneumoniae)
- **Bronchopneumonia:** Patchy bilateral consolidation (S. aureus, Klebsiella)
- **Interstitial pneumonia:** Diffuse interstitial involvement (viral, Mycoplasma)

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes by Age
| Age | Bacterial | Viral | Atypical |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------|
| **Neonates** | GBS, E. coli, Listeria | CMV, HSV | — |
| **1-3 months** | S. pneumoniae, Chlamydia trachomatis | RSV | C. trachomatis |
| **3 months-5 years** | S. pneumoniae (#1), H. influenzae, S. aureus | RSV (#1), influenza, parainfluenza | — |
| **>5 years** | S. pneumoniae, Mycoplasma | Influenza | Mycoplasma pneumoniae (#1), Chlamydophila |

### Risk Factors
- Age <2 years, malnutrition, low birth weight
- Non-breastfed, incomplete immunization
- Indoor air pollution, overcrowding
- HIV, immunodeficiency
- GERD, aspiration risk
- Congenital heart disease

### Pathophysiology
1. Pathogen reaches lower airways (aspiration, inhalation, hematogenous)
2. Alveolar inflammation → exudate (neutrophils, fibrin) fills alveoli
3. Consolidation → impaired gas exchange → hypoxemia
4. May spread to pleura (empyema) or bloodstream (sepsis)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Cough** — productive or dry
- **Fever** — high-grade in bacterial; low-grade in viral/atypical
- **Tachypnea** — most reliable sign (WHO threshold)
- **Difficulty breathing** — grunting, nasal flaring
- **Chest pain** (pleuritic — older children)
- **Poor feeding, lethargy** (infants)

### Signs
- **Tachypnea** + **chest indrawing** (subcostal retractions)
- **Nasal flaring, grunting** (severe)
- **Decreased air entry** on affected side
- **Crackles/crepitations** — characteristic
- **Bronchial breathing** — consolidation
- **Dull percussion** — effusion/consolidation
- **Hypoxemia** (SpO₂ <90%)

### Special Presentations
- **Staphylococcal pneumonia:** Rapid progression, pneumatoceles, empyema, pneumothorax
- **Mycoplasma:** Insidious onset, dry cough, headache, "walking pneumonia," cold agglutinins
- **Chlamydial pneumonia (infant):** Afebrile, staccato cough, eosinophilia, conjunctivitis history
- **PCP (Pneumocystis):** HIV-infected infants, bilateral diffuse infiltrates, severe hypoxemia

## Diagnosis

### Clinical (WHO Approach)
- Cough/difficulty breathing + tachypnea = pneumonia
- No routine investigations needed for non-severe cases in community

### Investigations (When Indicated)
| Test | Indication/Finding |
|------|-------------------|
| **Chest X-ray** | Severe/complicated cases; lobar consolidation, bilateral infiltrates, effusion |
| **SpO₂** | Essential; <90% = severe |
| **CBC** | Leukocytosis (bacterial); lymphocytosis (viral) |
| **Blood culture** | Severe pneumonia, poor response |
| **CRP/PCT** | Procalcitonin more specific for bacterial |
| **Nasopharyngeal swab** | Viral PCR (RSV, influenza) |
| **Pleural fluid** | If effusion — culture, biochemistry |

### CXR Patterns
- **Lobar consolidation:** S. pneumoniae
- **Bilateral patchy infiltrates:** Bronchopneumonia, viral
- **Pneumatoceles:** S. aureus
- **Hyperinflation + perihilar infiltrates:** Viral (RSV)
- **Round pneumonia:** S. pneumoniae (children — mimics mass)

## Management

### Non-Severe Pneumonia (Home Treatment)
- **Oral Amoxicillin** 40-50 mg/kg/day (high dose: 80-90 mg/kg/day) × 5 days
- Reassess at 48-72 hours
- If no improvement: switch to amoxicillin-clavulanate or add macrolide

### Severe Pneumonia (Hospital)
- **Oxygen:** If SpO₂ <90% (target 92-96%)
- **IV Ampicillin + Gentamicin** (WHO first-line)
- OR IV Ceftriaxone (if available)
- Switch to oral after 48-72 hours improvement
- Total course: 7-10 days

### Special Situations
| Situation | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Staphylococcal** | IV Cloxacillin + Gentamicin (or Vancomycin if MRSA) |
| **Mycoplasma** | Azithromycin 10 mg/kg Day 1, then 5 mg/kg Days 2-5 |
| **Neonatal** | IV Ampicillin + Gentamicin |
| **PCP** | High-dose Cotrimoxazole + steroids |
| **Empyema** | IV antibiotics + chest drain |

### Prevention
- **PCV (Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine)** — most effective intervention
- **Hib vaccine**
- **Influenza vaccine** (annually)
- **Breastfeeding** — protective
- **Zinc supplementation**
- **Reduce indoor air pollution**
- **Hand hygiene**

## Complications
- **Pleural effusion / Empyema** — most common complication
- **Lung abscess**
- **Pneumatoceles** (S. aureus)
- **Pneumothorax**
- **Septicemia / Meningitis** — hematogenous spread
- **Respiratory failure**
- **ARDS**
- **Bronchiectasis** — from recurrent/severe infections
