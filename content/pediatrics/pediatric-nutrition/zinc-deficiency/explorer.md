# Zinc Deficiency in Children

## Overview
Zinc is an essential trace element involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, immune function, growth, and wound healing. Zinc deficiency is widespread in developing countries, particularly in children consuming cereal-based diets high in phytates. WHO recommends zinc supplementation as adjunct therapy in acute diarrhea, reducing duration and severity. Acrodermatitis enteropathica is the classical inherited form of severe zinc deficiency.

## Key Points
- **Essential for over 300 metalloenzymes** including DNA/RNA polymerase
- **Zinc supplementation reduces diarrhea** duration by ~25% (WHO recommendation)
- **Acrodermatitis enteropathica** — autosomal recessive, severe zinc deficiency triad
- **Triad:** Acral dermatitis + Alopecia + Diarrhea
- **Phytates** (in cereals) are the main dietary inhibitor of zinc absorption
- **Serum zinc <65 μg/dL** suggests deficiency
- **WHO diarrhea protocol:** ORS + Zinc (20 mg/day × 14 days for >6 months)
- **Growth faltering** is an early sign of mild zinc deficiency

## Definition & Classification

### Forms of Zinc Deficiency
| Type | Cause |
|------|-------|
| **Acquired (nutritional)** | Cereal-based diet, PEM, chronic diarrhea, malabsorption |
| **Inherited (Acrodermatitis enteropathica)** | SLC39A4 gene mutation → impaired zinc absorption |
| **Conditional** | Prematurity, parenteral nutrition without zinc, chronic liver disease |

### Acrodermatitis Enteropathica
- Autosomal recessive
- Mutation in SLC39A4 gene (ZIP4 transporter)
- Presents after weaning (breast milk has zinc-binding ligand that enhances absorption)
- Classic triad: **Acral dermatitis + Alopecia + Diarrhea**

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Dietary Sources
- **Animal sources (best bioavailability):** Red meat, shellfish (oysters — richest), liver, eggs
- **Plant sources (lower bioavailability):** Legumes, nuts, whole grains (phytate inhibits absorption)
- **Breast milk:** Adequate zinc with high bioavailability (zinc-binding ligand)

### Requirements
| Age | RDA (mg/day) |
|-----|-------------|
| 0-6 months | 2 |
| 7-12 months | 3 |
| 1-3 years | 3 |
| 4-8 years | 5 |
| 9-13 years | 8 |

### Risk Factors
- Cereal-based diet with high phytate content
- Protein-energy malnutrition
- Chronic diarrhea / malabsorption
- Exclusive cow's milk feeding (poor zinc bioavailability)
- Prematurity (low stores)
- Chronic liver/kidney disease
- Sickle cell disease
- Vegetarian diet

### Pathophysiology
1. Zinc is cofactor for >300 enzymes (including DNA polymerase, carbonic anhydrase, alkaline phosphatase)
2. Essential for: cell division, immune function (T-cell function), wound healing, taste/smell
3. Deficiency → impaired DNA synthesis → poor growth
4. ↓T-cell function → increased infection susceptibility
5. ↓Intestinal mucosal integrity → diarrhea (vicious cycle)
6. Zinc also regulates vitamin A metabolism (↓zinc → ↓retinol-binding protein)

## Clinical Features

### Mild-Moderate Deficiency
- **Growth retardation** (earliest and most common sign)
- **Anorexia** and decreased food intake
- **Impaired taste (hypogeusia)** and smell
- **Delayed wound healing**
- **Increased susceptibility to infections** (diarrhea, pneumonia)
- **Delayed sexual maturation**
- **Behavioral changes:** Irritability, lethargy

### Severe Deficiency / Acrodermatitis Enteropathica
- **Skin:** Erythematous, vesiculobullous, pustular lesions → crusted plaques
  - Distribution: Periorificial (mouth, nose, eyes, perineum) and acral (hands, feet, knees, elbows)
- **Alopecia:** Scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes
- **Diarrhea:** Chronic, watery
- **Nail dystrophy:** Paronychia, ridging
- **Angular stomatitis, glossitis**
- **Photophobia, blepharitis**
- **Immune deficiency:** Recurrent infections (candida, bacterial)
- **Growth failure**
- **Irritability, mood changes**

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory
- **Serum zinc:** <65 μg/dL (deficient); normal 70-120 μg/dL
  - Limitations: Affected by inflammation, albumin levels, diurnal variation
- **Alkaline phosphatase:** ↓ (zinc-dependent enzyme — sensitive marker)
- **Serum albumin:** May be low (confounding factor)
- **Hair zinc levels:** Low (reflects chronic deficiency)
- **Therapeutic trial:** Response to zinc supplementation confirms diagnosis

### Diagnosis of Acrodermatitis Enteropathica
- Clinical triad + low serum zinc
- Dramatic response to oral zinc supplementation
- Genetic testing: SLC39A4 mutation

## Management

### Treatment of Zinc Deficiency
- **Oral zinc supplementation:**
  - Mild-moderate: Elemental zinc 1-2 mg/kg/day
  - Severe/AE: Elemental zinc 3 mg/kg/day
- **Forms:** Zinc sulfate, zinc gluconate, zinc acetate
- **Duration:** Until clinical improvement + normalize levels; lifelong in AE

### WHO Zinc Supplementation for Diarrhea
| Age | Dose | Duration |
|-----|------|----------|
| <6 months | 10 mg/day | 14 days |
| ≥6 months | 20 mg/day | 14 days |

- Given with ORS for ALL episodes of acute diarrhea
- Reduces duration by ~25%, severity, and recurrence for 2-3 months

### Prevention
- Dietary diversification (animal-source foods)
- Reduce phytate content: soaking, fermentation, germination of cereals
- Zinc-fortified foods
- Zinc supplementation in high-risk groups
- Continued breastfeeding

## Complications
- **Recurrent severe infections** (especially diarrhea, pneumonia)
- **Severe growth failure** / stunting
- **Chronic diarrhea** → malabsorption → worsening deficiency (vicious cycle)
- **Skin superinfection** (candida, staphylococcal)
- **Death** in untreated acrodermatitis enteropathica
- **Zinc toxicity** (overdose): Nausea, vomiting, copper deficiency, sideroblastic anemia
