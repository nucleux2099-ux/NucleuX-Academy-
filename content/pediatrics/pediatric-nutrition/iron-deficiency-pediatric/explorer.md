# Iron Deficiency Anemia in Children

## Overview
Iron deficiency anemia (IDA) is the most common nutritional anemia and the most prevalent nutritional deficiency worldwide, affecting approximately 50-60% of Indian children under 5 years. Iron is essential for hemoglobin synthesis, myoglobin function, and neurodevelopment. Even without anemia, iron deficiency impairs cognitive function, behavior, and motor development in children.

## Key Points
- **Most common cause of anemia in children** worldwide
- **Most common nutritional deficiency** globally
- **Microcytic hypochromic** anemia on peripheral smear
- **Serum ferritin** is the best single test for iron stores (earliest to fall)
- **TIBC elevated, serum iron low, transferrin saturation <16%**
- **Peak incidence:** 6 months to 2 years (exhaustion of fetal stores)
- **Pica (pagophagia)** — craving for ice; geophagia — eating clay/dirt
- **Treatment:** Elemental iron 3-6 mg/kg/day for 3 months after Hb normalization
- **Reticulocyte response** peaks at 5-7 days (earliest sign of response)

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Definition of Anemia in Children
| Age | Hb cutoff (g/dL) |
|-----|-------------------|
| 6 months - 5 years | <11.0 |
| 5-11 years | <11.5 |
| 12-14 years | <12.0 |

### Severity Classification
| Severity | Hb (g/dL) |
|----------|-----------|
| Mild | 10-10.9 |
| Moderate | 7-9.9 |
| Severe | <7 |

### Stages of Iron Deficiency
| Stage | Iron Stores | Serum Iron | TIBC | Hb | RBC Morphology |
|-------|-------------|------------|------|-----|---------------|
| **1. Storage depletion** | ↓Ferritin | Normal | Normal | Normal | Normal |
| **2. Iron-deficient erythropoiesis** | ↓↓Ferritin | ↓ | ↑ | Normal | Normal/early changes |
| **3. Iron deficiency anemia** | ↓↓↓ | ↓↓ | ↑↑ | ↓ | Microcytic hypochromic |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes by Age Group
| Age | Common Causes |
|-----|---------------|
| **0-6 months** | Prematurity, LBW, maternal IDA, twin pregnancy |
| **6-24 months** | Inadequate complementary feeding, exclusive milk diet, rapid growth |
| **2-5 years** | Poor diet, hookworm (rural), chronic infections |
| **>5 years** | Hookworm infestation, celiac disease, chronic blood loss, menorrhagia (adolescent girls) |

### Iron Metabolism Highlights
- **Total body iron in neonate:** ~75 mg/kg (full-term) — maternal stores last ~4-6 months
- **Daily iron requirement:** 1 mg/kg/day (term infant), 2 mg/kg/day (preterm)
- **Absorption:** Duodenum and upper jejunum; heme iron (meat) better absorbed than non-heme (vegetables)
- **Enhancers:** Vitamin C, citric acid, meat
- **Inhibitors:** Phytates (cereals), tannins (tea), calcium (milk), phosphates
- **Regulation:** Hepcidin (liver) — master regulator; ↑hepcidin → ↓iron absorption

### Pathophysiology
1. Depleted iron stores → impaired hemoglobin synthesis
2. ↓Hb → microcytic hypochromic RBCs → tissue hypoxia
3. Iron also needed for: myoglobin, cytochromes, brain myelination, neurotransmitter synthesis
4. Non-hematologic effects: cognitive impairment, behavioral changes, impaired immunity

## Clinical Features

### Hematologic
- **Pallor** — most common sign (conjunctival, palmar, nail bed)
- **Easy fatigability, irritability, poor feeding**
- **Tachycardia, systolic flow murmur** (high-output state)
- **Hepatosplenomegaly** (extramedullary hematopoiesis — severe cases)

### Non-Hematologic (Iron Deficiency Effects)
- **Pica:** Pagophagia (ice), geophagia (dirt/clay) — characteristic
- **Koilonychia:** Spoon-shaped nails
- **Angular stomatitis, glossitis** (smooth tongue)
- **Plummer-Vinson syndrome:** Dysphagia + esophageal web + IDA (rare in children)
- **Cognitive impairment:** Poor attention, decreased scholastic performance, lower IQ
- **Behavioral:** Irritability, poor concentration, ADHD-like symptoms
- **Growth retardation**
- **Increased susceptibility to infections** (impaired cell-mediated immunity)
- **Blue sclera** (thinning of sclera)

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory
| Test | Finding | Significance |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **CBC** | ↓Hb, ↓MCV, ↓MCH, ↓MCHC | Microcytic hypochromic anemia |
| **Peripheral smear** | Microcytic hypochromic, target cells, pencil cells | Characteristic |
| **RDW** | ↑ (>14.5%) | Anisocytosis; differentiates from thalassemia trait (normal RDW) |
| **Serum ferritin** | ↓ (<12 ng/mL) | Best single test for iron stores; earliest to decrease |
| **Serum iron** | ↓ | Low |
| **TIBC** | ↑ (>400 μg/dL) | Increased transferrin production |
| **Transferrin saturation** | <16% | Diagnostic |
| **Reticulocyte count** | Low (before treatment) | Inadequate erythropoiesis |
| **Mentzer index** | >13 | MCV/RBC >13 = IDA; <13 = thalassemia trait |

### Differentiating IDA from Thalassemia Trait
| Feature | IDA | β-Thalassemia Trait |
|---------|-----|---------------------|
| RDW | ↑ | Normal |
| Mentzer index | >13 | <13 |
| Serum ferritin | ↓ | Normal/↑ |
| TIBC | ↑ | Normal |
| HbA2 | Normal | ↑ (>3.5%) |
| Response to iron | Yes | No |

## Management

### Oral Iron Therapy (First-Line)
- **Elemental iron:** 3-6 mg/kg/day in 2-3 divided doses
- **Preferred form:** Ferrous sulfate (20% elemental iron)
- **Give between meals** with Vitamin C (enhances absorption)
- **Avoid** with milk, tea, calcium, antacids

### Duration
- Continue for **3 months after Hb normalization** to replenish stores
- Total duration usually 4-6 months

### Monitoring Response
| Time | Expected Response |
|------|-------------------|
| 3-4 days | Subjective improvement, ↑reticulocytes begin |
| 5-7 days | **Peak reticulocyte response** (earliest objective sign) |
| 2-4 weeks | Hb rises ~1 g/dL per week |
| 1-2 months | Hb normalizes |
| 3 months post | Ferritin normalizes (stores replenished) |

### Parenteral Iron (If Oral Fails/Not Tolerated)
- **IV iron sucrose** or **iron dextran**
- Dose: Total iron deficit = Weight(kg) × (Target Hb – Actual Hb) × 2.4 + 500
- Indications: malabsorption, intolerance, non-compliance, chronic blood loss

### Blood Transfusion
- Only if Hb <4 g/dL or <7 with cardiac failure
- Packed RBCs 10 mL/kg slowly + furosemide
- Risk of transfusion-related cardiac overload in severe anemia

### Prevention
- **Exclusive breastfeeding** for 6 months
- **Iron-rich complementary foods** from 6 months
- **IAP recommendation:** Elemental iron 2 mg/kg/day from 6 months to 2 years
- **Weekly Iron-Folic Acid Supplementation (WIFS)** — government program for adolescents
- **Deworming** every 6 months (albendazole 400 mg)
- **Delayed cord clamping** (≥60 seconds) at birth

## Complications
- **Cognitive impairment** — may be irreversible if prolonged IDA in first 2 years
- **Growth failure**
- **Cardiac failure** (severe anemia — high-output failure)
- **Increased infection susceptibility**
- **Poor scholastic performance**
- **Iron overload** from inappropriate parenteral therapy or transfusions
