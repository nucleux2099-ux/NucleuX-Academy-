# Vitamin A Deficiency

## Overview
Vitamin A deficiency (VAD) is the leading cause of preventable childhood blindness worldwide and a major contributor to child mortality in developing countries. It affects approximately 190 million preschool children globally, with India having one of the highest burdens. VAD increases susceptibility to infections (measles, diarrhea, pneumonia) and is closely linked with protein-energy malnutrition.

## Key Points
- **Leading cause of preventable blindness** in children worldwide
- **Fat-soluble vitamin** — requires dietary fat for absorption
- **Night blindness (XN)** is the earliest clinical manifestation
- **Bitot's spots (X1B)** — most common clinical finding in community surveys
- **Corneal xerosis → keratomalacia** — irreversible blindness
- **National Vitamin A Prophylaxis Programme** — 9 months to 5 years
- **Measles** is the most important precipitating infection
- **Dose:** 1 lakh IU (6-12 months), 2 lakh IU (>12 months) every 6 months

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Classification of Xerophthalmia
| Stage | Description | Significance |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **XN** | Night blindness | Earliest symptom |
| **X1A** | Conjunctival xerosis | Dry, non-wettable conjunctiva |
| **X1B** | Bitot's spots | Foamy, cheesy patches on conjunctiva |
| **X2** | Corneal xerosis | Dry, hazy cornea — reversible |
| **X3A** | Corneal ulceration (<1/3 cornea) | Irreversible damage begins |
| **X3B** | Corneal ulceration (≥1/3 cornea) / Keratomalacia | Liquefaction of cornea — blindness |
| **XS** | Corneal scar | End-stage |
| **XF** | Xerophthalmic fundus | Retinal changes |

### Public Health Significance Criteria (WHO)
VAD is a public health problem if prevalence in children 6-71 months exceeds:
- Night blindness >1%
- Bitot's spots >0.5%
- Corneal xerosis/ulceration >0.01%
- Corneal scar >0.05%
- Serum retinol <0.35 μmol/L in >5%

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Sources of Vitamin A
- **Preformed Vitamin A (Retinol):** Liver, fish liver oil, egg yolk, milk, butter
- **Provitamin A (β-carotene):** Dark green leafy vegetables (spinach, amaranth), yellow-orange fruits/vegetables (carrot, papaya, mango, pumpkin)
- **Richest source:** Liver (cod liver oil)
- **Cheapest source in India:** Dark green leafy vegetables

### Daily Requirements
| Age | RDA (μg retinol/day) |
|-----|---------------------|
| Infants (0-12 months) | 350 |
| Children (1-6 years) | 400 |
| Children (7-12 years) | 600 |
| Adolescents/Adults | 600-800 |
| Pregnancy | 800 |
| Lactation | 950 |

### Risk Factors
- Protein-energy malnutrition (most important)
- Measles infection (precipitates xerophthalmia)
- Diarrheal diseases (malabsorption)
- Respiratory infections
- Exclusive cereal-based diet (no fruits/vegetables)
- Low breastfeeding rates
- Fat malabsorption (celiac, cystic fibrosis, biliary atresia)

### Pathophysiology
1. Vitamin A (retinol) → retinal (vision) + retinoic acid (cell differentiation)
2. **Visual cycle:** Retinal combines with opsin → rhodopsin (rod cells) — essential for dim-light vision
3. **Epithelial integrity:** Retinoic acid maintains normal differentiation of epithelial cells
4. Deficiency → **squamous metaplasia** of epithelial surfaces (conjunctiva, cornea, respiratory, GI, urinary tract)
5. **Keratinization** of conjunctiva → xerosis → Bitot's spots
6. Loss of goblet cells → reduced mucin → dry eye
7. Corneal stromal edema → ulceration → keratomalacia (collagenase-mediated liquefaction)
8. **Immune dysfunction:** ↓T-cell function, ↓mucosal immunity → increased infection susceptibility

## Clinical Features

### Ocular Manifestations (Xerophthalmia)
- **Night blindness (XN):** Earliest symptom; child stumbles in dim light
- **Conjunctival xerosis (X1A):** Dry, thickened, non-wettable conjunctiva; loss of goblet cells
- **Bitot's spots (X1B):** Triangular, foamy, cheesy white patches on temporal bulbar conjunctiva; contain *Corynebacterium xerosis*
- **Corneal xerosis (X2):** Cornea appears dull, dry, hazy; stippled with fluorescein staining
- **Corneal ulceration (X3A/X3B):** Punched-out ulcer → keratomalacia (melting of cornea)
- **Corneal scar (XS):** End result; permanent visual impairment
- **Xerophthalmic fundus (XF):** White/yellow dots on retina

### Extra-Ocular Manifestations
- **Skin:** Follicular hyperkeratosis (phrynoderma — "toad skin"), dry rough skin
- **Respiratory:** Increased susceptibility to pneumonia and bronchitis
- **GI:** Diarrhea, malabsorption (mucosal metaplasia)
- **Urinary:** Increased UTI risk (urinary tract keratinization)
- **Immune:** Increased severity and mortality from measles, diarrhea
- **Growth:** Growth retardation
- **Anemia:** Vitamin A deficiency can cause anemia (impaired iron mobilization)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
- Based on characteristic ocular findings + risk factors
- History of night blindness (ask mother: "Does the child stumble in dim light?")
- Slit lamp examination for corneal changes

### Laboratory
- **Serum retinol:** <20 μg/dL (0.70 μmol/L) = deficient; <10 μg/dL = severe
- **Relative dose response (RDR):** >20% increase after vitamin A dose suggests deficiency
- **Conjunctival impression cytology (CIC):** Loss of goblet cells, keratinization — non-invasive, field-applicable
- **Dark adaptation test:** Impaired in night blindness
- **Serum retinol-binding protein:** Correlates with retinol levels

## Management

### Treatment of Xerophthalmia (WHO Schedule)
| Day | Age <6 months | Age 6-12 months | Age >12 months |
|-----|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Day 1 | 50,000 IU | 1,00,000 IU | 2,00,000 IU |
| Day 2 | 50,000 IU | 1,00,000 IU | 2,00,000 IU |
| 2 weeks | 50,000 IU | 1,00,000 IU | 2,00,000 IU |

- **Route:** Oral (preferred); IM if vomiting/malabsorption
- **For corneal lesions:** Add topical antibiotics (prevent secondary infection), atropine eye drops, eye padding
- **Never give topical steroids** (worsen corneal melting)

### Prophylaxis — National Vitamin A Prophylaxis Programme (India)
| Age | Dose | Frequency |
|-----|------|-----------|
| 9 months (with measles vaccine) | 1,00,000 IU | Once |
| 16 months to 5 years | 2,00,000 IU | Every 6 months |

### Dietary Counseling
- Promote breastfeeding (colostrum is rich in Vitamin A)
- Include dark green leafy vegetables, yellow-orange fruits
- Food fortification (sugar, oil, flour)
- Kitchen gardens

## Complications
- **Irreversible blindness** (keratomalacia — X3B)
- **Corneal perforation** and secondary endophthalmitis
- **Increased mortality** from measles, diarrhea, pneumonia (30-50% higher)
- **Growth failure**
- **Hypervitaminosis A** (from overtreatment): headache, vomiting, papilledema (pseudotumor cerebri), hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity
- **Corneal scarring** with permanent visual impairment
