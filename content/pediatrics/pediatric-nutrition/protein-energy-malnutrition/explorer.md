# Protein Energy Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor & Marasmus)

## Overview
Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM) is the most important nutritional disorder in developing countries, contributing to nearly half of all under-5 deaths globally. It encompasses a spectrum from mild undernutrition to severe acute malnutrition (SAM) including marasmus (caloric deficiency), kwashiorkor (protein deficiency), and marasmic-kwashiorkor. India bears the highest burden worldwide with ~35% of children under 5 being underweight (NFHS-5).

## Key Points
- **Leading cause of under-5 mortality** in developing nations
- **SAM definition:** Weight-for-height < -3 SD, or MUAC <11.5 cm, or bilateral pedal edema
- **Marasmus:** Total calorie deficiency — emaciated, no edema, old man's face
- **Kwashiorkor:** Protein deficiency with adequate calories — edema, flaky-paint dermatosis, fatty liver
- **WHO 10-Step Protocol** is the standard for inpatient management
- **Refeeding syndrome** is the most dangerous complication of treatment
- **F-75 → F-100** feeding regimen is critical
- **IAP/WHO classification** by weight-for-age, weight-for-height, MUAC

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Classification of Malnutrition
| Grade | Weight-for-Age (Gomez) | Weight-for-Height (Waterlow Wasting) | Height-for-Age (Waterlow Stunting) |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Normal | >90% | >90% | >95% |
| Mild (Grade I) | 75-90% | 80-90% | 90-95% |
| Moderate (Grade II) | 60-75% | 70-80% | 85-90% |
| Severe (Grade III) | <60% | <70% | <85% |

### IAP Classification (Indian Academy of Pediatrics)
| Grade | Weight-for-Age (% of median) |
|-------|------------------------------|
| Grade I (Mild) | 71-80% |
| Grade II (Moderate) | 61-70% |
| Grade III (Severe) | 51-60% |
| Grade IV (Very Severe) | ≤50% |

### Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) — WHO Criteria (any one)
- Weight-for-height < -3 SD (Z-score)
- MUAC < 11.5 cm (age 6-59 months)
- Bilateral pitting pedal edema (nutritional)
- Visible severe wasting

### Types of Severe PEM
| Feature | Marasmus | Kwashiorkor | Marasmic-Kwashiorkor |
|---------|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Deficiency** | Total calorie | Protein (relative) | Both |
| **Age** | <1 year (common) | 1-3 years | Any |
| **Edema** | Absent | Present (hallmark) | Present |
| **Weight** | <60% expected | 60-80% (masked by edema) | <60% + edema |
| **Muscle wasting** | Severe | Moderate | Severe |
| **Subcutaneous fat** | Absent | Present/reduced | Absent |
| **Hair changes** | Thin, sparse | Flag sign, depigmented | Both |
| **Skin changes** | Loose, wrinkled | Flaky-paint dermatosis | Both |
| **Appetite** | Good (ravenous) | Anorexia | Poor |
| **Fatty liver** | Absent | Present | Variable |
| **Mental status** | Alert, irritable | Apathetic, miserable | Apathetic |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Primary Causes
- **Inadequate dietary intake** — poverty, food insecurity
- **Faulty feeding practices** — early weaning, dilute formula, delayed complementary feeding
- **Frequent infections** — diarrhea, pneumonia, measles, TB, HIV
- **Maternal factors** — malnutrition, low birth weight, poor breastfeeding

### Secondary Causes
- Chronic diseases (celiac, IBD, CF, congenital heart disease)
- Malabsorption syndromes
- Malignancy
- Chronic kidney/liver disease
- HIV/AIDS

### Pathophysiology of Marasmus
1. Total caloric deprivation → body uses fat and muscle stores
2. Hormonal adaptation: ↑cortisol, ↑growth hormone, ↓insulin
3. Lean body mass catabolism → muscle wasting
4. Subcutaneous fat loss → wrinkled, loose skin
5. Visceral proteins relatively preserved (albumin near normal)
6. Immune function impaired but relatively maintained

### Pathophysiology of Kwashiorkor
1. Protein deficiency with relatively adequate carbohydrate intake
2. ↓Albumin synthesis → ↓oncotic pressure → **edema**
3. ↓Lipoprotein synthesis → fatty liver (hepatomegaly)
4. Free radical theory: ↓antioxidants (glutathione) → oxidative damage → skin/hair changes
5. Aflatoxin theory: mycotoxin exposure may contribute
6. Immune dysfunction more severe than marasmus
7. ↓Transferrin → functional iron deficiency

### Adaptations in PEM
- ↓Basal metabolic rate
- ↓Body temperature (hypothermia risk)
- ↓Cardiac output and renal concentrating ability
- Impaired glucose homeostasis (hypoglycemia risk)
- Electrolyte derangements: ↓K+, ↓Mg2+, ↑Na+ (intracellular)

## Clinical Features

### Marasmus
- **General:** Severely emaciated, "skin and bones," alert but irritable
- **Face:** "Old man's face" (loss of buccal fat pad — last fat depot to go)
- **Skin:** Loose, wrinkled, baggy pants appearance
- **Hair:** Thin, sparse, dry
- **Abdomen:** Distended (due to gas, weak muscles), no hepatomegaly
- **Limbs:** Severe muscle wasting, ribs visible
- **Growth:** Weight <60% of expected, severe stunting
- **Appetite:** Usually preserved (hungry, ravenous)
- **No edema**

### Kwashiorkor
- **General:** Miserable, apathetic, irritable when handled
- **Edema:** Bilateral, pitting, starts in feet → legs → hands → face (moon face)
- **Skin:** **Flaky-paint dermatosis** (enamel paint appearance), **crazy pavement dermatosis**, hyperpigmented patches that peel off
- **Hair:** **Flag sign** (alternating bands of depigmented/normal hair), sparse, easily pluckable, silky
- **Face:** Moon face (edema), depigmented hair
- **Abdomen:** Distended, **hepatomegaly** (fatty liver)
- **Muscles:** Wasting (masked by edema)
- **Appetite:** Anorexia (poor appetite)
- **Mental:** Apathetic, withdrawn, miserable

### Common Features in Both
- Growth failure (weight, height, head circumference)
- Susceptibility to infections (measles, diarrhea, pneumonia, sepsis)
- Vitamin deficiencies (A, D, B-complex, C)
- Anemia (iron, folate deficiency)
- Hypothermia, hypoglycemia
- Electrolyte imbalance

## Diagnosis

### Anthropometry (Most Important)
- **Weight-for-height/length** — Z-scores (WHO growth standards)
- **MUAC** — Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (6-59 months)
  - Green: ≥13.5 cm (normal)
  - Yellow: 12.5-13.4 cm (moderate malnutrition)
  - Red: <11.5 cm (SAM)
- **Weight-for-age** — Gomez / IAP classification
- **Height-for-age** — chronic malnutrition (stunting)

### Laboratory Investigations
| Test | Marasmus | Kwashiorkor |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Serum albumin | Near normal / mildly ↓ | Markedly ↓ (<2.5 g/dL) |
| Blood sugar | ↓ (hypoglycemia common) | ↓ |
| Serum electrolytes | ↓K+, ↓Mg2+ | ↓K+, ↓Mg2+, ↓Na+ |
| Hemoglobin | ↓ (anemia) | ↓ (anemia) |
| Transferrin | Normal/↓ | Markedly ↓ |
| Total protein | Near normal | ↓ |
| Liver function | Normal | ↑ transaminases |

### Additional Investigations
- Blood glucose monitoring (hypoglycemia)
- Serum electrolytes (especially K+, Mg2+)
- CBC — anemia, infection
- Blood culture — sepsis screening
- Chest X-ray — pneumonia, TB
- Stool examination — parasites
- HIV testing
- Mantoux test — TB screening

## Management

### WHO 10-Step Protocol for SAM (⭐ High Yield)

#### Phase 1: Stabilization (Day 1-7)
| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1. Treat/prevent hypoglycemia** | Oral/NG 10% dextrose; breastfeed; F-75 feeds 2-hourly |
| **2. Treat/prevent hypothermia** | Warm clothing, kangaroo care, keep room warm (25-30°C) |
| **3. Treat/prevent dehydration** | **ReSoMal** (NOT standard ORS — lower Na, higher K) |
| **4. Correct electrolyte imbalance** | K+ (3-4 mEq/kg/day), Mg2+ supplementation; NO IV fluids unless shock |
| **5. Treat infection** | Broad-spectrum antibiotics empirically (amoxicillin + gentamicin) |
| **6. Correct micronutrient deficiency** | Vitamin A, folic acid, zinc, multivitamins; NO iron in Phase 1 |
| **7. Start cautious feeding** | **F-75** (75 kcal/100 mL), small frequent feeds (8-12 times/day) |

#### Phase 2: Rehabilitation (Week 2-6)
| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **8. Achieve catch-up growth** | Transition to **F-100** (100 kcal/100 mL), increase gradually |
| **9. Sensory stimulation** | Play therapy, emotional support, structured activities |
| **10. Prepare for discharge** | Nutrition education, follow-up plan, growth monitoring |

### Key Formula Details
| Feature | F-75 | F-100 |
|---------|------|-------|
| Energy | 75 kcal/100 mL | 100 kcal/100 mL |
| Protein | 0.9 g/100 mL | 2.9 g/100 mL |
| Phase | Stabilization | Rehabilitation |
| Purpose | Prevent metabolic crisis | Catch-up growth |

### ReSoMal vs Standard ORS
| Feature | ReSoMal | Standard ORS |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Sodium | 45 mEq/L | 75 mEq/L |
| Potassium | 40 mEq/L | 20 mEq/L |
| Osmolarity | 300 | 245 |
| Use | SAM with diarrhea | Normal children |

### Important Management Points
- **NO IV fluids** unless in shock (risk of heart failure)
- **NO iron** in Phase 1 (worsens infection; start only in Phase 2)
- **NO diuretics** for edema (resolves with nutrition)
- **Empirical antibiotics** even without overt infection signs
- **Blood transfusion** only if Hb <4 g/dL or <6 g/dL with respiratory distress (use packed cells 10 mL/kg slowly)
- **Vitamin A:** Single dose (Day 1 and Day 2 and at 2 weeks)

### Community-Based Management (CMAM)
- For SAM without complications (appetite present, no medical complications)
- Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) — e.g., Plumpy'Nut
- Weekly follow-up at nutrition rehabilitation center

## Complications
- **Hypoglycemia** — most common cause of death in first 48 hours
- **Hypothermia** — impaired thermoregulation
- **Sepsis/infections** — leading cause of mortality; impaired immunity
- **Refeeding syndrome** — rapid feeding → hypophosphatemia, cardiac failure, death
- **Heart failure** — due to fluid overload (avoid IV fluids!)
- **Electrolyte imbalance** — hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmias
- **Micronutrient deficiencies** — Vitamin A (xerophthalmia), iron (anemia)
- **Persistent diarrhea** — villous atrophy, secondary lactose intolerance
- **Growth failure** — stunting may be permanent if prolonged
- **Neurodevelopmental impairment** — reduced IQ, cognitive deficits (especially <2 years)
- **Mortality:** 20-30% in SAM with complications (can be reduced to <5% with WHO protocol)
