# Fluid and Electrolyte Requirements in Children

## Overview
Fluid and electrolyte management is fundamental to pediatric care. Children have higher water requirements per kilogram than adults due to higher metabolic rate, larger body surface area relative to weight, and immature renal concentrating ability. The Holliday-Segar formula is the cornerstone for calculating maintenance fluid requirements. Understanding sodium, potassium, and acid-base balance is essential for managing dehydration, post-operative states, and critically ill children.

## Key Points
- **Holliday-Segar formula:** 100/50/20 mL/kg/day (or 4/2/1 mL/kg/hour)
- **Maintenance fluid:** 0.45% NS + 5% dextrose (commonly used) or DNS
- **Neonates have ~80% TBW** vs adults ~60%; higher daily water turnover
- **Sodium requirement:** 2-3 mEq/kg/day; **Potassium:** 1-2 mEq/kg/day
- **Insensible water loss:** Skin (2/3) and lungs (1/3); ~30-40 mL/kg/day in infants
- **Isotonic dehydration** — most common (80%); Na 130-150 mEq/L
- **Hyponatremia** — most common electrolyte abnormality in hospitalized children
- **SIADH** — common cause of hyponatremia in CNS diseases, pneumonia
- **IV potassium** — NEVER bolus; max rate 0.5 mEq/kg/hour; max concentration 40 mEq/L peripheral

## Definition & Classification

### Total Body Water by Age
| Age | TBW (% body weight) | ECF | ICF |
|-----|---------------------|-----|-----|
| Premature neonate | 80-85% | 50% | 30% |
| Term neonate | 75% | 40% | 35% |
| 1 year | 65% | 25% | 40% |
| Adult | 60% | 20% | 40% |

### Holliday-Segar Maintenance Formula
| Body Weight | Daily Rate | Hourly Rate |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| First 10 kg | 100 mL/kg/day | 4 mL/kg/h |
| 10-20 kg | 50 mL/kg/day | 2 mL/kg/h |
| >20 kg | 20 mL/kg/day | 1 mL/kg/h |

**Example:** 25 kg child: (10×100) + (10×50) + (5×20) = 1000+500+100 = 1600 mL/day

### Maintenance Electrolyte Requirements
| Electrolyte | Daily Requirement |
|-------------|------------------|
| Sodium (Na⁺) | 2-3 mEq/kg/day |
| Potassium (K⁺) | 1-2 mEq/kg/day |
| Chloride (Cl⁻) | 2-3 mEq/kg/day |
| Calcium (Ca²⁺) | 0.5-1 mEq/kg/day |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Why Children Need More Fluid Per Kg
1. Higher metabolic rate → more metabolic water production needed
2. Larger BSA:weight ratio → higher insensible losses
3. Immature renal concentrating ability (especially neonates — max ~700 mOsm/kg vs adult 1200)
4. Higher proportion of TBW as ECF → more vulnerable to dehydration

### Common Electrolyte Disturbances
| Disturbance | Causes | Key Features |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| **Hyponatremia (<130)** | SIADH, excessive hypotonic fluids, water intoxication | Seizures, lethargy, cerebral edema |
| **Hypernatremia (>150)** | Dehydration (water > Na loss), diabetes insipidus | Irritability, doughy skin, seizures |
| **Hypokalemia (<3.5)** | Diarrhea, vomiting, diuretics | Weakness, ileus, U-waves, arrhythmias |
| **Hyperkalemia (>5.5)** | Renal failure, acidosis, hemolysis | Peaked T-waves, bradycardia, cardiac arrest |
| **Hypocalcemia** | Rickets, DiGeorge, post-thyroidectomy | Tetany, seizures, Chvostek/Trousseau sign |

### Acid-Base Basics
| Disorder | pH | pCO₂ | HCO₃⁻ | Common Causes |
|----------|-----|------|--------|---------------|
| **Metabolic acidosis** | ↓ | ↓ (compensation) | ↓ | Diarrhea, DKA, RTA, lactic acidosis |
| **Metabolic alkalosis** | ↑ | ↑ (compensation) | ↑ | Vomiting (pyloric stenosis), diuretics |
| **Respiratory acidosis** | ↓ | ↑ | ↑ (compensation) | Hypoventilation, pneumonia |
| **Respiratory alkalosis** | ↑ | ↓ | ↓ (compensation) | Hyperventilation, anxiety, salicylate |

## Clinical Features

### Signs of Fluid Imbalance
- **Dehydration:** Dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes/fontanelle, tachycardia, decreased skin turgor, oliguria
- **Overhydration:** Edema (periorbital, peripheral), weight gain, crackles, hepatomegaly
- **Electrolyte disturbance:** Seizures (Na, Ca), weakness/arrhythmias (K), tetany (Ca, Mg)

## Diagnosis

### Assessment
- **Weight:** Best measure of fluid status (acute weight change = fluid change)
- **Input-output charting:** Strict fluid balance
- **Serum electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, blood gas**
- **Urine:** Specific gravity, osmolality, electrolytes
- **Clinical signs:** Dehydration assessment (WHO classification)

### Key Lab Interpretation
- **Anion gap** = Na⁺ - (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻); normal 8-12; elevated in DKA, lactic acidosis
- **Urine specific gravity:** >1.020 = dehydration; <1.005 = diabetes insipidus
- **Fractional excretion of Na (FENa):** <1% = prerenal; >2% = intrinsic renal

## Management

### IV Fluid Selection
| Fluid | Na (mEq/L) | Use |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| Normal Saline (0.9%) | 154 | Resuscitation, boluses |
| Ringer's Lactate | 130 | Resuscitation (preferred) |
| 0.45% NS + 5% dextrose | 77 | Maintenance (traditional) |
| Isolyte-P | 25 | Neonatal maintenance |
| 5% Dextrose | 0 | NOT for resuscitation; hypoglycemia treatment |

### Fluid Management Approach
1. **Deficit replacement** (% dehydration × weight × 10 = mL deficit)
2. **Maintenance** (Holliday-Segar)
3. **Ongoing losses** (replace volume-for-volume)
4. Give deficit + maintenance over 24 hours (unless hypernatremia — 48-72 hours)

### Key Rules
- Resuscitation bolus: NS or RL **20 mL/kg** over 20 minutes; repeat up to 3 times
- Add K⁺ to fluids ONLY after urine output confirmed
- Never give K⁺ as bolus (cardiac arrest risk)
- Correct hyponatremia slowly (<10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours)
- Correct hypernatremia slowly (Na drop <0.5 mEq/L/hour)

### SIADH Management
- **Fluid restriction** (60-75% of maintenance)
- Treat underlying cause
- Severe/symptomatic: 3% NaCl (3-5 mL/kg over 20 min)

## Complications
- **Cerebral edema** — rapid correction of hypernatremia
- **Central pontine myelinolysis** — rapid correction of hyponatremia
- **Cardiac arrhythmias** — hypo/hyperkalemia
- **Seizures** — electrolyte disturbances
- **Iatrogenic fluid overload** — excessive IV fluids
- **Hospital-acquired hyponatremia** — from hypotonic maintenance fluids (reason for isotonic maintenance trend)
