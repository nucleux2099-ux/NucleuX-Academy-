# Nephrotic Syndrome in Children

## Overview
Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by massive proteinuria (>40 mg/m²/hr or urine protein/creatinine ratio >2), hypoalbuminemia (<2.5 g/dL), generalized edema, and hyperlipidemia. It is the most common glomerular disease in children. Minimal Change Disease (MCD) accounts for 80% of cases in children and responds excellently to corticosteroids. The peak age of onset is 2-6 years with a male predominance.

## Key Points
- **Tetrad:** Massive proteinuria + hypoalbuminemia + edema + hyperlipidemia
- **MCD (Minimal Change Disease)** — 80% of childhood nephrotic syndrome
- **Peak age:** 2-6 years; Male:Female 2:1
- **Steroid-sensitive** in 85-90% of cases (favorable prognosis)
- **Prednisolone** 2 mg/kg/day × 6 weeks, then 1.5 mg/kg/alternate day × 6 weeks (ISKDC)
- **Peritonitis (SBP)** — most common serious complication; Streptococcus pneumoniae
- **No biopsy needed** for typical presentation (age 1-8, no hematuria, normal C3, steroid-responsive)
- **Steroid-resistant:** Consider FSGS; needs biopsy + alternative immunosuppression

## Definition & Classification

### Diagnostic Criteria
| Feature | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Proteinuria | >40 mg/m²/hr or spot urine protein/creatinine >2 or 3+ on dipstick |
| Serum albumin | <2.5 g/dL |
| Edema | Generalized (periorbital → pedal → ascites → anasarca) |
| Hyperlipidemia | ↑Cholesterol, ↑triglycerides, ↑LDL |

### Classification
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Steroid-sensitive (SSNS)** | Remission within 4 weeks of standard prednisolone (85-90%) |
| **Steroid-resistant (SRNS)** | No remission after 4 weeks of daily prednisolone |
| **Frequent relapsers (FRNS)** | ≥2 relapses in first 6 months or ≥4 in any 12 months |
| **Steroid-dependent (SDNS)** | 2 consecutive relapses during steroid taper or within 14 days of stopping |

### Histological Types in Children
| Type | Frequency | Steroid Response |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Minimal Change Disease** | 80% | 95% respond |
| **FSGS** | 10% | 20% respond |
| **Mesangial proliferative** | 5% | Variable |
| **Membranous** | 2% | Poor |
| **MPGN** | 3% | Poor |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Primary (idiopathic):** 90% — MCD, FSGS, membranous
- **Secondary:** SLE, HSP, HBV, HCV, HIV, malaria, drugs (NSAIDs, gold), malignancy

### Pathophysiology
1. **Glomerular permeability defect** → massive protein loss in urine
2. ↓Serum albumin → ↓oncotic pressure → fluid shifts to interstitium → **edema** (underfill theory)
3. Also: Primary Na⁺ retention by kidneys → volume overload (overfill theory)
4. ↓Albumin → ↑hepatic lipoprotein synthesis → **hyperlipidemia**
5. Loss of immunoglobulins (IgG) and complement → **infection susceptibility**
6. Loss of antithrombin III, protein C/S → **hypercoagulability** → thrombosis risk
7. Loss of thyroxine-binding globulin → functional hypothyroidism
8. MCD pathogenesis: T-cell dysfunction → circulating permeability factor → podocyte foot process effacement

## Clinical Features

### Presentation
- **Periorbital edema** — earliest sign, worse in morning (pathognomonic presentation)
- **Pedal edema** — worsens through the day
- **Ascites, pleural effusion** — progressive
- **Anasarca** — generalized severe edema
- **Scrotal/labial edema**
- **Decreased urine output**
- **Frothy urine** — due to proteinuria
- **Weight gain** — from fluid retention
- **No hypertension** (typically in MCD)
- **No gross hematuria** (typically in MCD)

### Features Suggesting Non-MCD (Need Biopsy)
- Age <1 year or >8 years
- Persistent hypertension
- Gross hematuria
- Low serum C3/C4
- Renal failure at presentation
- Steroid resistance
- Extrarenal features (rash, joint pain — SLE)

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| Urine protein | 3-4+ dipstick; spot PCR >2; 24h >40 mg/m²/hr |
| Serum albumin | <2.5 g/dL (often <1.5 in severe) |
| Serum cholesterol | ↑↑ (>200 mg/dL) |
| Serum triglycerides | ↑ |
| Serum C3, C4 | Normal (in MCD, FSGS) |
| Urine microscopy | Oval fat bodies, fatty casts; no RBC casts in MCD |
| BUN, creatinine | Usually normal |
| CBC | Hemoconcentration; ↑platelets |

### When to Do Renal Biopsy
- Age <1 year or >12 years at onset
- Steroid resistance (most common indication)
- Persistent hematuria or hypertension
- Low C3/C4
- Renal impairment
- Features of secondary nephrotic syndrome

## Management

### Initial Episode — ISKDC Protocol
| Phase | Regimen | Duration |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Induction** | Prednisolone 2 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) in single morning dose | 6 weeks |
| **Consolidation** | Prednisolone 1.5 mg/kg/alternate day (max 40 mg) | 6 weeks |
| **Total** | | 12 weeks |

**Remission defined as:** Urine protein nil/trace for 3 consecutive days

### Relapse Treatment
- Prednisolone 2 mg/kg/day until remission (protein nil × 3 days)
- Then 1.5 mg/kg/alternate day × 4 weeks, taper

### Frequent Relapsers / Steroid-Dependent
| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Levamisole** | 2.5 mg/kg/alternate day | Steroid-sparing; first-line add-on |
| **Cyclophosphamide** | 2 mg/kg/day × 12 weeks | Cumulative dose ≤168 mg/kg |
| **Mycophenolate (MMF)** | 25-30 mg/kg/day | Maintenance steroid-sparer |
| **Calcineurin inhibitors** | Cyclosporine/Tacrolimus | For SRNS or refractory SDNS |
| **Rituximab** | 375 mg/m² × 1-4 doses | Refractory cases |

### Supportive Care
- **Salt restriction** (no-added-salt diet) during edema
- **Fluid restriction** only if severe hyponatremia
- **Diuretics:** Furosemide 1-2 mg/kg (cautious — risk of hypovolemia)
- **Albumin infusion** (20% 1 g/kg) ONLY if: intravascular depletion (↑Hct, ↓BP) or tense edema/ascites — followed by furosemide
- **Pneumococcal vaccine** — due to infection risk
- **Calcium + Vitamin D** — steroid-induced bone loss
- **No routine albumin infusion**

### Infection Management
- **SBP** (most common serious infection) — Ceftriaxone or Ampicillin
- Causative organism: S. pneumoniae (most common), E. coli
- Suspect SBP if: fever + abdominal pain + peritoneal signs in NS
- Skin infections (cellulitis), UTI, septicemia also common
- **Avoid live vaccines** while on high-dose immunosuppression

## Complications
- **Infections:** SBP (most common serious), cellulitis, sepsis (loss of IgG, complement)
- **Thromboembolism:** Renal vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism (loss of AT-III)
- **Hypovolemic crisis:** During diuretic overuse or rapid albumin loss
- **AKI:** Prerenal (hypovolemia) or intrinsic (severe disease)
- **Steroid toxicity:** Cushingoid, growth suppression, cataracts, osteoporosis, DM
- **Hyperlipidemia:** Long-term cardiovascular risk
- **Peritonitis**
- **Cyclophosphamide toxicity:** Hemorrhagic cystitis, gonadal toxicity, leukopenia

## Prognosis
- **SSNS/MCD:** Excellent long-term prognosis; 80% relapse, but >95% resolve by adulthood
- **SRNS/FSGS:** Guarded; 30-50% progress to ESRD over 5-10 years
- Frequent relapses common but most children outgrow the disease
