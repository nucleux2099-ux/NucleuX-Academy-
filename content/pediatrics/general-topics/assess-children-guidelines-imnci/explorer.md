# IMNCI — Integrated Management of Neonatal and Childhood Illness

## Overview
IMNCI (Integrated Management of Neonatal and Childhood Illness) is the Indian adaptation of WHO's IMCI strategy, expanded to include neonatal care (0-2 months). It provides a systematic clinical algorithm for health workers at primary care level to assess, classify, and manage the most common causes of childhood illness and mortality. IMNCI addresses the five major killers: pneumonia, diarrhea, malaria, measles, and malnutrition, using simple clinical signs without requiring laboratory tests.

## Key Points
- **IMNCI** = Indian adaptation of WHO IMCI + neonatal component
- **Target:** Children 0 days to 5 years at primary care level
- **No lab tests needed** — uses clinical signs for classification
- **Color-coded classification:** Pink (urgent referral), Yellow (treat at facility), Green (home management)
- **Five major killers:** Pneumonia, diarrhea, malaria, measles, malnutrition
- **Young infant (0-2 months)** assessed separately using specific danger signs
- **Assess → Classify → Identify Treatment → Treat → Counsel → Follow-up**
- **Integrated approach** — addresses multiple conditions simultaneously

## Definition & Classification

### IMNCI Components
1. **Improving health worker skills** — training in assessment algorithms
2. **Strengthening health systems** — drug supply, referral systems
3. **Improving community practices** — health education, care-seeking behavior

### Assessment of Sick Child (2 months–5 years)
**Check for general danger signs FIRST:**
- Unable to drink or breastfeed
- Vomits everything
- Convulsions (current or recent)
- Lethargic or unconscious

**Then assess for main symptoms:**
1. Cough or difficulty breathing → Classify: Pneumonia/Severe pneumonia/No pneumonia
2. Diarrhea → Classify: Severe dehydration/Some dehydration/No dehydration
3. Fever → Classify: Very severe febrile disease/Malaria/Fever-no malaria
4. Ear problem → Classify: Mastoiditis/Acute ear infection/Chronic ear infection
5. Malnutrition and anemia → Classify: Severe malnutrition/Anemia/Not malnourished
6. Immunization status → Update if needed
7. Feeding assessment

### Young Infant (0-2 months) — Separate Algorithm
**Check for possible serious bacterial infection:**
- Convulsions
- Fast breathing (≥60/min)
- Severe chest indrawing
- Nasal flaring, grunting
- Bulging fontanelle
- Temperature >37.5°C or <35.5°C
- Many/severe skin pustules
- Lethargy, reduced movements
- Umbilical redness extending to skin

### Color-Coded Classification
| Color | Meaning | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Pink** | Severe classification; needs urgent referral | Pre-referral treatment + REFER |
| **Yellow** | Moderate; can be managed at facility | Treat at health facility |
| **Green** | Mild; can be managed at home | Home care + follow-up advice |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Five Major Killers of Under-5 Children
| Cause | % of Under-5 Deaths | IMNCI Approach |
|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Pneumonia** | 15% | Tachypnea + danger signs → classify → antibiotics |
| **Diarrhea** | 9% | Dehydration assessment → ORS/IV fluids + zinc |
| **Malaria** | Variable | Fever classification → antimalarials |
| **Measles** | Declining | Rash + fever → classify complications |
| **Malnutrition** | 45% (underlying) | Weight-for-age → nutritional rehabilitation |

### Why Integrated Approach?
- Children often present with multiple overlapping conditions
- Malnutrition underlies 45% of under-5 deaths (increases risk of pneumonia, diarrhea severity)
- Single-disease approach misses coexisting conditions
- Primary care workers need simple, effective algorithms

## Clinical Features

### Key Assessment Parameters

#### Pneumonia Assessment
| Age | Tachypnea | Classification |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| <2 months | ≥60/min | Possible serious bacterial infection |
| 2-12 months | ≥50/min | Pneumonia |
| 12 months-5 years | ≥40/min | Pneumonia |

**Severe pneumonia:** Tachypnea + chest indrawing OR any danger sign

#### Dehydration Assessment
- **Severe:** Lethargic/unconscious + sunken eyes + skin pinch very slow + unable to drink
- **Some:** Restless/irritable + sunken eyes + skin pinch slow + thirsty/drinks eagerly
- **No dehydration:** None of the above signs

#### Nutritional Assessment
- **Severe malnutrition:** Visible severe wasting or edema of both feet
- **Moderate:** Weight-for-age very low (below -3SD line)
- **Anemia:** Palmar pallor — severe or some

### Danger Signs Requiring Urgent Referral
- Unable to drink/breastfeed
- Vomits everything
- Convulsions
- Lethargic/unconscious
- Severe chest indrawing
- Stridor in calm child

## Diagnosis
IMNCI is a **clinical classification system**, not a diagnostic system. It does not provide specific diagnoses but classifies children into severity categories requiring specific treatment actions. No laboratory tests are used in the basic algorithm.

## Management

### Pre-Referral Treatments (Pink Classification)
- **Severe pneumonia:** First dose IM ampicillin/gentamicin
- **Severe dehydration:** IV RL/NS if possible; or NG ORS
- **Severe malnutrition:** Prevent hypoglycemia (oral sugar/breastfeed); keep warm
- **Convulsions:** Rectal diazepam
- **Very severe febrile disease:** IM chloroquine/artesunate + first-dose antibiotic

### Treatments at Facility (Yellow Classification)
- **Pneumonia:** Oral amoxicillin × 5 days
- **Some dehydration:** ORS 75 mL/kg over 4 hours
- **Acute ear infection:** Oral amoxicillin × 5 days + dry wicking
- **Dysentery:** Oral ciprofloxacin × 3 days
- **Malaria:** Oral antimalarial as per national protocol

### Home Management (Green Classification)
- **No pneumonia (cough/cold):** Home care, soothe throat, watch for danger signs
- **No dehydration:** Extra fluids + zinc + continued feeding
- **Feeding advice:** Age-appropriate feeding counseling

### Counseling (IMNCI Key Component)
- When to return immediately (danger signs)
- Follow-up dates
- Feeding and fluids advice
- Immunization update
- Family planning (mother)

## Complications
- **Missed diagnosis** if algorithm not followed properly
- **Delayed referral** — if danger signs not recognized
- **Over-treatment** — inappropriate antibiotics for viral infections
- **Under-treatment** — if malnutrition not assessed alongside acute illness
- **Program challenges:** Training quality, drug availability, referral transport
