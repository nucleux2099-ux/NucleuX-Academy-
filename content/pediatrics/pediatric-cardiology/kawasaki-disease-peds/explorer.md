# Kawasaki Disease

## Overview
Kawasaki Disease (KD) is an acute, self-limited febrile vasculitis of medium-sized arteries primarily affecting children under 5 years. It is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries, surpassing rheumatic fever. The most feared complication is coronary artery aneurysm, which can lead to myocardial infarction and sudden death. Early treatment with IVIG within 10 days dramatically reduces coronary complications.

## Key Points
- **Leading cause of acquired heart disease** in children in developed countries
- **Age:** 80% are <5 years; peak 1-2 years
- **Etiology unknown** — likely infectious trigger in genetically susceptible
- **Diagnosis is CLINICAL** — no specific diagnostic test
- **Fever ≥5 days + 4/5 principal criteria** (or fewer with echo findings)
- **Coronary artery aneurysm** — most serious complication (25% untreated, <5% with IVIG)
- **IVIG 2 g/kg single infusion** within 10 days = standard treatment
- **Aspirin** used in KD (one of the few pediatric indications)

## Definition & Classification

### Diagnostic Criteria (AHA)
**Fever ≥5 days PLUS ≥4 of 5 principal features:**
1. **Bilateral non-exudative conjunctival injection** (limbal sparing)
2. **Oral changes:** Strawberry tongue, red cracked lips, oropharyngeal erythema
3. **Extremity changes:** Erythema/edema of hands/feet (acute); periungual desquamation (subacute)
4. **Polymorphous rash** (not vesicular)
5. **Cervical lymphadenopathy** ≥1.5 cm (usually unilateral) — least common criterion

### Incomplete (Atypical) Kawasaki Disease
- Fever ≥5 days + 2-3 criteria + laboratory/echo findings
- More common in infants <6 months (higher risk of coronary aneurysms)
- Must maintain high index of suspicion

### Phases
| Phase | Duration | Features |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **Acute** | Week 1-2 | Fever, all clinical features, myocarditis, irritability |
| **Subacute** | Week 2-4 | Fever resolves, desquamation, thrombocytosis, highest risk for aneurysm |
| **Convalescent** | Week 4-8 | Resolution, Beau's lines on nails |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Unknown** — likely infectious trigger (viral/bacterial) in genetically predisposed
- Genetic associations: ITPKC, CASP3, FCGR2A polymorphisms
- More common in Japanese/East Asian descent
- Seasonal variation (winter-spring)
- NOT directly contagious

### Pathophysiology
1. Immune activation → vasculitis of medium-sized arteries
2. **Coronary arteries** preferentially affected
3. Inflammatory infiltrate → destruction of media and internal elastic lamina
4. Weakened vessel wall → aneurysm formation
5. Aneurysm → thrombus formation → MI risk
6. Giant aneurysms (>8 mm) rarely regress

## Clinical Features

### Principal Features (Detail)
- **Conjunctivitis:** Bilateral, non-exudative, painless, **limbal sparing** (differentiates from adenovirus)
- **Oral:** Erythema of lips (red, cracked, bleeding), strawberry tongue, diffuse oropharyngeal erythema
- **Extremities:** Edema/erythema of palms and soles (acute); **periungual peeling** (subacute — hallmark)
- **Rash:** Polymorphous (macular, maculopapular, erythema multiforme-like); perineal desquamation early
- **Lymphadenopathy:** Unilateral cervical ≥1.5 cm; least common feature

### Associated Features
- **Extreme irritability** (very characteristic — "unhappy child")
- **Sterile pyuria** (urethritis)
- **Arthritis/arthralgia**
- **Hydrops of gallbladder**
- **Aseptic meningitis**
- **Hepatitis, diarrhea**
- **Anterior uveitis**
- **BCG scar reactivation** (erythema/induration — useful clue in vaccinated children)

### Cardiac Manifestations
- **Coronary artery aneurysm:** 25% untreated → <5% with timely IVIG
- **Myocarditis:** Common in acute phase; tachycardia, decreased LV function
- **Pericardial effusion**
- **Valvular regurgitation** (usually MR)
- **Giant aneurysms** (>8 mm) — highest risk for MI, rupture

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory (Supportive — No Specific Test)
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **ESR/CRP** | Markedly elevated |
| **WBC** | Leukocytosis with left shift |
| **Platelets** | ↑↑ Thrombocytosis (Week 2-3, may reach >1 million) |
| **Albumin** | ↓ (hypoalbuminemia) |
| **Transaminases** | ↑ (mild) |
| **Sterile pyuria** | WBCs in urine without bacteria |
| **Anemia** | Normocytic |
| **Na** | ↓ (hyponatremia) |

### Echocardiography
- **ESSENTIAL** at diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6-8 weeks
- Coronary artery Z-scores (adjusted for body surface area)
- Classification of aneurysms: Small (<5 mm), Medium (5-8 mm), Giant (>8 mm)
- Also assess: LV function, MR, pericardial effusion

### Differential Diagnosis
- Measles, scarlet fever, toxic shock syndrome, drug reaction (Stevens-Johnson), JIA, adenovirus

## Management

### Standard Treatment
1. **IVIG 2 g/kg** single infusion over 10-12 hours — within 10 days of fever onset
   - Reduces coronary aneurysm risk from 25% to <5%
   - Can give after day 10 if ongoing inflammation
2. **Aspirin:**
   - High-dose: 80-100 mg/kg/day (anti-inflammatory) during acute febrile phase
   - Low-dose: 3-5 mg/kg/day (antiplatelet) for 6-8 weeks OR until echo normal
   - Continue indefinitely if coronary abnormalities

### IVIG-Resistant KD (~10-20%)
- Persistent/recurrent fever >36 hours after IVIG
- **Second dose IVIG** 2 g/kg
- **IV methylprednisolone** pulse
- **Infliximab** (anti-TNF) — rescue therapy
- **Cyclosporine** — refractory cases

### Long-term Management (Based on Coronary Status)
| Risk | Coronary Status | Management |
|------|----------------|------------|
| Level I | No abnormality | No long-term therapy; follow-up at 1 year |
| Level II | Transient dilation | Low-dose aspirin 6-8 weeks |
| Level III | Small-medium aneurysm | Low-dose aspirin until regression |
| Level IV-V | Giant aneurysm | Aspirin + anticoagulation (warfarin/LMWH); avoid contact sports |

## Complications
- **Coronary artery aneurysm** — most feared (giant aneurysms rarely regress)
- **Myocardial infarction** — from coronary thrombosis
- **Sudden death** — coronary rupture or MI
- **Coronary artery stenosis** — from intimal thickening of aneurysm
- **Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)** — rare but life-threatening
- **IVIG side effects:** Aseptic meningitis, hemolytic anemia
- **Aspirin-related:** Reye syndrome risk with concurrent influenza/varicella (switch to clopidogrel)
