# Pneumonia in Children (Infections Perspective)

## Overview
Pneumonia is the leading infectious cause of death in children under 5 globally. From an infectious diseases perspective, understanding the microbiology by age group, the role of vaccines (PCV, Hib) in prevention, IMNCI-based classification, and appropriate antibiotic selection is critical. S. pneumoniae remains the most common bacterial cause across all age groups, while RSV dominates viral etiology in infants. The WHO approach using respiratory rate and danger signs enables management even in resource-limited settings.

## Key Points
- **Leading infectious killer** of children under 5 globally (~740,000 deaths/year)
- **S. pneumoniae** — #1 bacterial cause; **RSV** — #1 viral cause (<2 years)
- **WHO tachypnea thresholds:** <2m: ≥60, 2-12m: ≥50, 1-5y: ≥40 breaths/min
- **Amoxicillin** — first-line for non-severe community-acquired pneumonia
- **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** — WHO first-line for severe pneumonia
- **PCV + Hib vaccines** — most effective preventive interventions
- **Pneumonia-diarrhea-malnutrition** — deadly triad in developing countries
- **IMNCI** algorithm for classification and management in primary care

## Definition & Classification

### WHO/IMNCI Classification
| Category | Criteria | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| **Cough/cold (no pneumonia)** | No tachypnea, no danger signs | Home care, supportive |
| **Pneumonia** | Tachypnea (age-specific) | Oral amoxicillin, home |
| **Severe pneumonia** | Any danger sign: chest indrawing, stridor in calm child, inability to drink, convulsions, lethargy, severe malnutrition | Hospitalize, injectable antibiotics |

### Tachypnea Thresholds (Age-Specific)
| Age Group | Rate (breaths/min) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| <2 months | ≥60 |
| 2-12 months | ≥50 |
| 12 months-5 years | ≥40 |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Microbiology by Age
| Age | Bacteria | Virus | Atypical |
|-----|----------|-------|----------|
| **<20 days** | GBS, gram-negatives, Listeria | CMV, HSV | — |
| **3 weeks-3 months** | S. pneumoniae, C. trachomatis, Pertussis | RSV, parainfluenza | C. trachomatis |
| **4 months-5 years** | **S. pneumoniae**, H. influenzae, S. aureus | **RSV**, parainfluenza, influenza | — |
| **>5 years** | S. pneumoniae, **Mycoplasma** | Influenza | **M. pneumoniae**, Chlamydophila |

### Key Organism Features
- **S. pneumoniae:** Lobar consolidation, empyema; gram-positive diplococcus; PCV reduces
- **S. aureus:** Rapid progression, pneumatoceles, empyema, pneumothorax; consider in malnourished
- **Mycoplasma:** "Walking pneumonia"; >5 years; insidious onset; cold agglutinins; Rx: macrolides
- **C. trachomatis (infant):** Afebrile, staccato cough, eosinophilia, history of conjunctivitis
- **PCP (Pneumocystis):** HIV-infected infants 3-6 months; bilateral diffuse infiltrates; Rx: high-dose cotrimoxazole

### Pathogenesis
1. Colonization of nasopharynx → aspiration/inhalation to lower airways
2. Overwhelms local defenses → alveolar inflammation → exudate
3. Consolidation → impaired gas exchange → hypoxemia
4. May extend: empyema (pleural), bacteremia, meningitis

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Cough (dry → productive), fever, tachypnea
- Difficulty breathing, chest pain (older children)
- Poor feeding, lethargy (infants)
- Grunting (severe — maintains PEEP)

### Signs
- Tachypnea (most sensitive single sign — WHO)
- Chest indrawing (severe)
- Nasal flaring, grunting
- Crackles, bronchial breathing, decreased air entry
- Dullness to percussion (consolidation/effusion)

### IMNCI Danger Signs
- Inability to drink/breastfeed
- Persistent vomiting
- Convulsions
- Lethargy/unconsciousness
- Stridor in calm child
- Severe malnutrition

## Diagnosis

### Clinical (IMNCI Approach)
- Cough/difficulty breathing + tachypnea = pneumonia
- Suitable for primary care; investigations not required for non-severe

### Investigations (Severe/Hospital)
- **SpO₂** — most important; <90% needs oxygen
- **CXR:** Consolidation, infiltrates, effusion, pneumatoceles
- **Blood culture:** If severe; positive in 10-30%
- **CBC:** Leukocytosis suggests bacterial
- **Procalcitonin/CRP:** Help differentiate bacterial vs viral
- **Nasopharyngeal swab:** Viral PCR (RSV, influenza)

## Management

### Outpatient (Non-Severe Pneumonia)
- **Amoxicillin** 40-50 mg/kg/day (or 80-90 mg/kg/day high-dose) × 5 days
- Follow up at 48-72 hours
- Counsel parents on danger signs

### Inpatient (Severe Pneumonia)
- **Oxygen** if SpO₂ <90%
- **IV Ampicillin** (50 mg/kg Q6h) **+ Gentamicin** (7.5 mg/kg/day) — WHO first-line
- OR **Ceftriaxone** 50-80 mg/kg/day
- Step-down to oral after 48-72 hours improvement
- Total duration: 7-10 days

### Special Situations
- **Staphylococcal:** Cloxacillin/Vancomycin + Gentamicin
- **Atypical (Mycoplasma):** Azithromycin
- **PCP:** High-dose Cotrimoxazole ± steroids
- **Empyema:** IV antibiotics + intercostal drainage

### Prevention
- **PCV (Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine)** — most impactful
- **Hib vaccine** — reduced H. influenzae meningitis and pneumonia
- **Breastfeeding** — reduces pneumonia risk by 15-23%
- **Zinc supplementation** — reduces incidence
- **Reduce indoor air pollution** — biomass fuel avoidance
- **Hand hygiene**
- **Measles vaccination** — measles predisposes to pneumonia

## Complications
- **Pleural effusion/Empyema** — most common complication
- **Lung abscess**
- **Pneumatoceles** (S. aureus)
- **Pneumothorax**
- **Bacteremia → meningitis**
- **Septic shock**
- **ARDS**
- **Bronchiectasis** (recurrent pneumonia)
- **Death** — 5-15% case fatality in severe pneumonia in developing countries
