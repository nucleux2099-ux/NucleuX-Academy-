# HIV in Children

## Overview
Pediatric HIV primarily results from mother-to-child transmission (MTCT) which can occur during pregnancy, labor/delivery, or breastfeeding. Without intervention, MTCT rate is 25-40%; with comprehensive PMTCT (Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission), it reduces to <2%. India has ~70,000 children living with HIV. Early infant diagnosis (EID) using DNA PCR at 6 weeks is essential. ART should be initiated in ALL HIV-infected children regardless of CD4 count or clinical stage.

## Key Points
- **MTCT** accounts for >90% of pediatric HIV; can occur in utero, intrapartum, or via breastfeeding
- **Without PMTCT:** 25-40% transmission; **with PMTCT:** <2%
- **DNA PCR at 6 weeks** — gold standard for early infant diagnosis
- **Antibody tests unreliable <18 months** (maternal IgG crosses placenta)
- **ART for ALL** HIV-positive children (treat regardless of CD4/stage)
- **PCP prophylaxis** — Cotrimoxazole from 6 weeks for all HIV-exposed infants
- **WHO Clinical Staging** — Stage 1-4 (4 = AIDS)
- **Most common opportunistic infection:** PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii) in infants

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Clinical Staging (Pediatric)
| Stage | Features |
|-------|----------|
| **1** | Asymptomatic; persistent generalized lymphadenopathy |
| **2** | Hepatosplenomegaly, papular pruritic eruptions, recurrent URTIs, herpes zoster |
| **3** | Moderate malnutrition, persistent diarrhea, persistent fever, oral candidiasis, pulmonary TB, severe bacterial infections |
| **4 (AIDS)** | Severe wasting, PCP, cryptococcal meningitis, CMV, Kaposi sarcoma, HIV encephalopathy, chronic cryptosporidiosis |

### Immunological Classification (CD4)
| Age | Severe Immunodeficiency |
|-----|------------------------|
| <1 year | CD4 <25% or <1500 |
| 1-5 years | CD4 <20% or <750 |
| >5 years | CD4 <15% or <200 |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Transmission
- **Vertical (MTCT):** >90% of pediatric cases
  - In utero: 5-10%
  - Intrapartum: 10-20% (most common)
  - Breastfeeding: 10-15% (cumulative over breastfeeding period)
- **Horizontal:** Blood products, sexual abuse (rare in children)

### Risk Factors for MTCT
- High maternal viral load (most important)
- Low CD4 count
- Prolonged rupture of membranes (>4 hours)
- Vaginal delivery (vs elective C-section)
- Breastfeeding (especially mixed feeding)
- Prematurity, chorioamnionitis

### Pathophysiology
1. HIV (retrovirus) infects CD4+ T-cells, macrophages, dendritic cells
2. Viral replication → CD4 cell destruction → immunodeficiency
3. Children have higher viral loads and faster disease progression than adults
4. **Without ART:** 30-50% die by age 2 years; 50% by age 5

## Clinical Features

### Early Signs (Infants)
- Failure to thrive, weight loss
- Recurrent oral thrush (persistent/recurrent after neonatal period)
- Chronic diarrhea
- Recurrent pneumonia
- Persistent generalized lymphadenopathy
- Hepatosplenomegaly
- Chronic parotitis (bilateral)
- Developmental delay

### Late/Severe Manifestations (AIDS)
- **PCP (Pneumocystis pneumonia):** Severe hypoxemia, bilateral diffuse infiltrates; peak 3-6 months
- **CMV retinitis, pneumonitis**
- **Cryptococcal meningitis**
- **TB** (pulmonary and extrapulmonary)
- **HIV encephalopathy:** Developmental regression, microcephaly, spasticity
- **Lymphoid interstitial pneumonitis (LIP):** Bilateral reticulonodular infiltrates; chronic; clubbing
- **Kaposi sarcoma** (rare in children)
- **Severe wasting (HIV wasting syndrome)**

## Diagnosis

### Early Infant Diagnosis (EID)
| Age | Test |
|-----|------|
| **6 weeks** | HIV DNA PCR (DBS — dried blood spot) — first test |
| **6 months** | Repeat HIV DNA PCR if first negative (continues breastfeeding) |
| **18 months** | Antibody test (ELISA/rapid) — confirmatory |

- DNA PCR at 6 weeks has >95% sensitivity
- **Antibody tests unreliable <18 months** (maternal antibodies persist)
- If symptomatic earlier: test at any age with DNA PCR

### Diagnosis >18 Months
- Same as adults: Rapid antibody tests (3-test strategy in India)

## Management

### PMTCT Strategy
1. **Maternal ART:** Start/continue ART in all HIV-positive pregnant women (TDF + 3TC + EFV or DTG)
2. **Infant prophylaxis:**
   - NVP (Nevirapine) syrup for 6-12 weeks
   - High-risk: NVP + AZT for 6 weeks
3. **Safe feeding:** Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months (WHO); avoid mixed feeding
4. **Elective C-section** if viral load >1000 at delivery
5. **Avoid invasive procedures** during labor (amniotomy, episiotomy if possible)

### ART in Children
- **Treat ALL** HIV-infected children regardless of age, CD4, or clinical stage
- **First-line regimen:**
  - <4 weeks: AZT + 3TC + NVP
  - 4 weeks-3 years: ABC + 3TC + LPV/r (or DTG if >4 weeks and >3 kg)
  - 3-10 years: ABC + 3TC + DTG (or EFV)
  - >10 years: TDF + 3TC + DTG
- **DTG (Dolutegravir)** — preferred integrase inhibitor; potent, well-tolerated

### Cotrimoxazole Prophylaxis (CPT)
- ALL HIV-exposed infants from 6 weeks until HIV excluded
- ALL HIV-infected children (regardless of CD4)
- Dose: TMP 5 mg/kg/day
- Prevents: PCP, toxoplasma, bacterial infections, malaria

### Monitoring
- Viral load (best marker for treatment response): at 6 months, then every 12 months
- CD4 count (immunological monitoring)
- Growth monitoring
- Developmental assessment
- Drug toxicity monitoring

## Complications
- **Opportunistic infections:** PCP, TB, CMV, Cryptococcus
- **HIV encephalopathy:** Developmental regression, cognitive decline
- **Growth failure** and severe malnutrition
- **Malignancies:** Lymphoma, Kaposi sarcoma
- **IRIS** — paradoxical worsening after starting ART
- **ART side effects:** Anemia (AZT), lipodystrophy, hepatotoxicity (NVP), renal toxicity (TDF)
- **Drug resistance** from non-adherence
- **Psychosocial:** Stigma, disclosure challenges, orphanhood
