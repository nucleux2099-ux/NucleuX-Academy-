# Acute Diarrheal Disease and ORS

## Overview
Acute diarrheal disease (ADD) is among the top causes of under-5 mortality globally, responsible for ~525,000 deaths annually. ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) is considered the most important medical discovery of the 20th century, having saved an estimated 70+ million lives since its introduction. This topic focuses on the epidemiology, ORS formulation and science, and the WHO/UNICEF integrated approach to diarrhea management including ORS + Zinc.

## Key Points
- **Second leading cause** of under-5 mortality worldwide
- **ORS** — "potentially the most important medical advance this century" (The Lancet, 1978)
- **Low-osmolarity ORS** (245 mOsm/L) = current WHO standard — reduces stool output and vomiting
- **Rotavirus** — #1 cause of severe dehydrating diarrhea in children <5
- **ORS + Zinc** = WHO/UNICEF recommended dual therapy
- **Zinc 20 mg/day × 14 days** reduces duration, severity, and recurrence
- **Continue breastfeeding and feeding** during diarrhea — NEVER starve
- **Antibiotics rarely needed** — only for cholera, shigella, amoebic dysentery
- **WASH (Water, Sanitation, Hygiene)** = key preventive strategy

## Definition & Classification

### ORS Composition (Low-Osmolarity — Current WHO)
| Component | Amount per Liter |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Sodium chloride | 2.6 g (Na⁺ 75 mEq/L) |
| Trisodium citrate | 2.9 g (Citrate 10 mmol/L) |
| Potassium chloride | 1.5 g (K⁺ 20 mEq/L) |
| Glucose (anhydrous) | 13.5 g (Glucose 75 mmol/L) |
| **Total osmolarity** | **245 mOsm/L** |

### Previous vs Current ORS
| Feature | Old ORS (1975) | Low-Osmolarity ORS |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Na⁺ | 90 mEq/L | 75 mEq/L |
| Glucose | 111 mmol/L | 75 mmol/L |
| Osmolarity | 311 mOsm/L | 245 mOsm/L |
| Stool output | Higher | ↓25% less |
| Vomiting | More | ↓30% less |
| IV fluid need | More | Reduced by 33% |

### Science of ORS
- **Sodium-glucose co-transport (SGLT-1):** Na⁺ and glucose are absorbed together in a 1:1 ratio across intestinal brush border
- Water follows osmotically
- This co-transport is preserved even in secretory diarrhea (cholera)
- Optimal when glucose:Na ratio ≈ 1:1 (low-osmolarity achieves this)

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Epidemiology
- 1.7 billion episodes of diarrhea per year in children <5 globally
- ~525,000 deaths per year (declining due to ORS, vaccines, WASH)
- India: ~100,000 diarrhea deaths per year in children
- Peak age: 6-24 months (weaning period)
- Peak season: Summer (bacterial), winter (rotavirus)

### Major Etiological Agents
| Agent | Type | Stool | Mechanism |
|-------|------|-------|-----------|
| **Rotavirus** | Virus | Watery | Villous destruction + secretory (NSP4 toxin) |
| **ETEC** | Bacteria | Watery | Heat-labile/heat-stable enterotoxin → secretory |
| **V. cholerae** | Bacteria | Rice-water | Cholera toxin → cAMP → massive secretion |
| **Shigella** | Bacteria | Bloody | Mucosal invasion + Shiga toxin |
| **E. histolytica** | Parasite | Bloody mucoid | Mucosal invasion, flask-shaped ulcers |
| **Giardia** | Parasite | Fatty, foul | Villous flattening, malabsorption |

### Pathophysiology of Dehydration
1. Diarrhea → loss of water + electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻)
2. ↓Intravascular volume → ↓cardiac output → shock
3. HCO₃⁻ loss → metabolic acidosis
4. K⁺ loss → hypokalemia → weakness, ileus, arrhythmias
5. **Zinc deficiency** worsened by diarrhea → impaired mucosal repair

## Clinical Features

### Assessment — IMNCI Approach
| Sign | No Dehydration | Some | Severe |
|------|---------------|------|--------|
| Condition | Well, alert | Restless, irritable | Lethargic/unconscious |
| Eyes | Normal | Sunken | Very sunken |
| Thirst | Drinks normally | Thirsty, eager | Unable to drink |
| Skin pinch | Immediate return | Slow return | Very slow (>2 sec) |

### Danger Signs
- Unable to drink or breastfeed
- Vomiting everything
- Convulsions
- Lethargic/unconscious
- Blood in stool with high fever

## Diagnosis

### Clinical
- History: Duration, frequency, stool character (watery/bloody), feeding, contacts
- Examination: Dehydration assessment (WHO criteria)
- Nutritional status assessment

### Investigations (Not Routine)
- Stool microscopy only if bloody diarrhea or persistent
- Electrolytes, blood gas if severe dehydration or IV fluids needed
- Stool for rotavirus antigen (ELISA) in hospital settings

## Management

### WHO/UNICEF Recommended Management (4 Pillars)
1. **ORS for rehydration** (Plan A/B/C)
2. **Zinc supplementation** (20 mg/day × 14 days; 10 mg if <6 months)
3. **Continued feeding** (breastfeeding + age-appropriate diet)
4. **Appropriate antibiotics** ONLY when indicated

### ORS Administration
- **Plan A (No dehydration):** Home fluids + ORS after each stool (50-100 mL if <2y; 100-200 mL if >2y)
- **Plan B (Some dehydration):** ORS 75 mL/kg over 4 hours under supervision
- **Plan C (Severe):** IV Ringer's Lactate (30 mL/kg rapid + 70 mL/kg slower) → ORS when able

### ReSoMal (Rehydration Solution for Malnutrition)
- For SAM children ONLY (NOT standard children)
- Lower Na (45 mEq/L), higher K (40 mEq/L) — prevents heart failure in SAM

### Antibiotics — Limited Indications
| Condition | Antibiotic |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cholera | Doxycycline or azithromycin |
| Shigella (dysentery) | Ciprofloxacin or azithromycin |
| Amoebiasis | Metronidazole |
| Giardiasis | Metronidazole |

### Prevention — WASH
- Safe drinking water
- Handwashing with soap (most effective behavioral intervention)
- Sanitary disposal of feces
- Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months
- Rotavirus vaccination
- Zinc supplementation
- Vitamin A supplementation

## Complications
- **Dehydration → hypovolemic shock → death** (leading cause of diarrhea mortality)
- **Metabolic acidosis** (HCO₃⁻ loss)
- **Hypokalemia** (cardiac, GI)
- **Hypoglycemia** (malnourished children)
- **Secondary lactose intolerance** (transient)
- **Persistent/chronic diarrhea** (>14 days)
- **HUS** (Shiga toxin — E. coli O157:H7)
- **Malnutrition** (diarrhea-malnutrition vicious cycle)
