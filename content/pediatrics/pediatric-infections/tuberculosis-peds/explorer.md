# Tuberculosis in Children

## Overview
Pediatric tuberculosis accounts for 10-15% of all TB cases globally. Children usually acquire TB from adult contacts (source case) through inhalation of M. tuberculosis. Diagnosis is challenging due to paucibacillary nature (low organism load) — smear is often negative. Mantoux test and chest X-ray are the primary diagnostic tools. The 4-drug regimen (HRZE) for 2 months followed by HR for 4 months is standard. BCG vaccination provides protection against severe forms (TB meningitis, miliary TB).

## Key Points
- **Source case:** Almost always an adult with active pulmonary TB
- **Paucibacillary** — gastric aspirate/sputum often smear-negative
- **Mantoux test (TST):** ≥10 mm induration = positive in most children; ≥5 mm if immunocompromised
- **Primary complex:** Ghon focus (parenchymal) + lymph node involvement + lymphangitis = Ranke complex (calcified)
- **TB meningitis** — most serious form; presents with basal meningitis, cranial nerve palsies, hydrocephalus
- **Treatment:** 2HRZE + 4HR (6 months total for pulmonary); 2HRZE + 10HR for TB meningitis (12 months)
- **BCG** — protects against disseminated TB and TB meningitis (not pulmonary TB reliably)
- **RNTCP/NTEP** — revised national TB programme; weight-based dosing

## Definition & Classification

### Types in Children
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **TB infection (latent)** | Positive Mantoux, no symptoms, normal CXR |
| **Pulmonary TB** | Primary complex, progressive primary, reactivation |
| **TB lymphadenitis** | Most common extrapulmonary TB in children |
| **TB meningitis** | Most serious form; basal meningitis |
| **Miliary TB** | Hematogenous dissemination; millet seed nodules |
| **Bone/joint TB** | Spine (Pott's spine), hip, knee |
| **Abdominal TB** | Peritoneal, intestinal (ileocecal) |

### Stages of Primary TB
1. **Ghon focus:** Subpleural parenchymal lesion (usually mid/lower zone)
2. **Primary complex (Ghon complex):** Ghon focus + draining lymphadenopathy + lymphangitis
3. **Ranke complex:** Healed, calcified primary complex

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Mycobacterium tuberculosis** (acid-fast bacillus)
- Transmission: Airborne droplet nuclei from adult source case
- Children are rarely infectious (paucibacillary, poor cough force)

### Pathophysiology
1. Inhalation → alveolar macrophage phagocytosis → intracellular survival
2. Granuloma formation (caseating): epithelioid cells, Langhans giant cells, lymphocytes
3. Cell-mediated immunity (Th1, CD4+ T-cells) → containment (latent TB)
4. **If immunity fails:** Progressive primary TB, miliary dissemination, meningitis
5. **High-risk for progression:** Age <5 years (especially <2), HIV, malnutrition, immunosuppression
6. Mantoux positivity reflects delayed-type hypersensitivity (Type IV)

## Clinical Features

### Pulmonary TB
- **Persistent cough >2 weeks** not responding to antibiotics
- Low-grade fever, night sweats
- Weight loss / failure to thrive
- Lymphadenopathy (hilar on CXR; cervical palpable)
- **CXR:** Hilar/paratracheal lymphadenopathy (most common finding), parenchymal lesion, miliary pattern, pleural effusion

### TB Meningitis (Most Serious)
- **Stage I:** Prodrome — fever, headache, vomiting, irritability (1-2 weeks)
- **Stage II:** Meningism, cranial nerve palsies (VI, VII), altered sensorium
- **Stage III:** Coma, seizures, decerebrate posturing
- **CSF:** Lymphocytic pleocytosis, ↑↑protein, ↓↓glucose, cobweb appearance
- **Complications:** Hydrocephalus, cranial nerve palsies, tuberculomas, vasculitic infarcts

### Miliary TB
- Hematogenous dissemination — multiple organs involved
- Fever, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy
- **CXR:** Miliary (millet seed) pattern — uniform small nodules throughout both lungs
- **Choroidal tubercles** on fundoscopy — pathognomonic

### TB Lymphadenitis
- Most common extrapulmonary TB in children
- Cervical (posterior triangle) — matted, non-tender, sinus formation
- Cold abscess (no warmth/redness)

## Diagnosis

### Mantoux Test (Tuberculin Skin Test)
- **Intradermal injection** of 0.1 mL (5 TU) PPD on volar forearm
- Read at **48-72 hours** — measure induration (NOT erythema)
- **Positive:** ≥10 mm (most children); ≥5 mm (HIV, immunocompromised, close contacts)
- False negative: Miliary TB, TB meningitis, severe malnutrition, HIV, measles, early infection
- False positive: Previous BCG (usually <10 mm, wanes over 3-5 years), NTM

### Other Investigations
| Test | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **CXR** | Hilar lymphadenopathy (most common), parenchymal lesion |
| **Gastric aspirate** | Early morning × 3 days; AFB smear + culture + CBNAAT |
| **CBNAAT/GeneXpert** | Rapid molecular test; detects MTB + rifampicin resistance (2 hours) |
| **IGRA** | Blood test; not affected by BCG; expensive; limited availability |
| **AFB culture (MGIT)** | Gold standard; takes 2-8 weeks |
| **CSF** | For TB meningitis — lymphocytes, ↓↓glucose, ↑↑protein |
| **Histopathology** | Caseating granulomas — lymph node biopsy |

### Diagnosis in Practice
- Often **clinical diagnosis** based on: contact history + symptoms + positive Mantoux + suggestive CXR
- Microbiological confirmation ideal but not always possible

## Management

### NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme) — Weight-Based Daily Regimen
| Phase | Drugs | Duration |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **Intensive** | HRZE (Isoniazid, Rifampicin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol) | 2 months |
| **Continuation** | HR | 4 months |

### Duration by Type
| Type | Total Duration |
|------|---------------|
| Pulmonary TB | 6 months (2HRZE + 4HR) |
| TB meningitis | 12 months (2HRZE + 10HR) |
| Bone/joint TB | 12 months |
| TB lymphadenitis | 6 months |
| Miliary TB | 6-12 months |

### Drug Doses (Pediatric — Daily)
| Drug | Dose | Max |
|------|------|-----|
| Isoniazid (H) | 10 mg/kg | 300 mg |
| Rifampicin (R) | 15 mg/kg | 600 mg |
| Pyrazinamide (Z) | 35 mg/kg | 1500 mg |
| Ethambutol (E) | 20 mg/kg | 1000 mg |

### Adjunctive Steroids
- **TB meningitis** (Stage II/III): Prednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day × 4 weeks, taper
- **Pericardial TB, airway compression** by lymph nodes

### Latent TB Treatment (Preventive Therapy)
- **Isoniazid** 10 mg/kg/day × 6 months
- Indicated: <5 years contacts of smear-positive cases, HIV-positive contacts

### BCG Vaccination
- Live attenuated vaccine; given at birth
- Protects against severe forms: TB meningitis (64-80%), miliary TB
- Does NOT reliably prevent pulmonary TB

## Complications
- **TB meningitis:** Hydrocephalus, cranial nerve palsies, cognitive deficit, death
- **Drug toxicity:** Hepatotoxicity (H, R, Z), peripheral neuropathy (H — give pyridoxine), optic neuritis (E)
- **IRIS** (Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome) — worsening after starting treatment
- **MDR-TB:** Resistance to at least H + R; difficult to treat
- **Spinal TB:** Kyphosis, paraplegia (Pott's spine)
- **Mortality:** 20-30% in TB meningitis; 1-3% in pulmonary TB with treatment
