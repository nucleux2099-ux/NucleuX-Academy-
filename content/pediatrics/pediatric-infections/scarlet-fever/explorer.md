# Scarlet Fever

## Overview
Scarlet fever is an exotoxin-mediated disease caused by Group A β-hemolytic Streptococcus (GAS/S. pyogenes) producing erythrogenic toxin. It typically presents in children aged 5-15 years with pharyngitis followed by a characteristic sandpaper-like erythematous rash, strawberry tongue, and circumoral pallor. It is treated with penicillin and can be complicated by rheumatic fever and post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis if untreated.

## Key Points
- **Caused by:** GAS producing erythrogenic (pyrogenic) exotoxin (SPE A, B, C)
- **Age:** 5-15 years (same as GAS pharyngitis)
- **Sandpaper rash** — fine, erythematous, blanching; starts neck/chest → generalizes
- **Pastia lines** — linear petechiae in skin folds (antecubital, inguinal)
- **Strawberry tongue** — white coating → red papillae (white then red strawberry tongue)
- **Circumoral pallor** — pallor around mouth with flushed cheeks
- **Desquamation** — peeling of skin (especially fingertips) during recovery (1-3 weeks)
- **Treatment:** Penicillin V × 10 days (same as GAS pharyngitis)
- **Complications:** Rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal GN

## Definition & Classification

### Type
- **Scarlet fever** = GAS pharyngitis + erythrogenic toxin-mediated rash
- Requires both: GAS infection AND production of erythrogenic toxin
- Patient must lack immunity to the specific toxin type

### Phases
1. **Prodrome (1-2 days):** Fever, sore throat, headache, vomiting
2. **Eruptive phase (Day 2-4):** Rash appears, strawberry tongue
3. **Desquamation (Week 1-3):** Skin peeling, especially hands and feet

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes)** — gram-positive cocci in chains
- **Erythrogenic toxins:** SPE-A, SPE-B, SPE-C (superantigens)
- Transmitted by respiratory droplets; incubation 2-5 days

### Pathophysiology
1. GAS pharyngitis → production of erythrogenic exotoxin
2. Toxin enters bloodstream → widespread vasodilation and capillary damage → rash
3. **Superantigen effect:** Massive non-specific T-cell activation → cytokine release → systemic effects
4. Direct endothelial damage by toxin → petechiae (Pastia lines)
5. Delayed hypersensitivity reactions → desquamation
6. Rash requires lack of antitoxin antibodies (first-time exposure to that toxin type)

## Clinical Features

### Typical Presentation
- **Sudden onset high fever** (38.5-40°C), sore throat
- **Tonsillar exudates,** tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy
- **Sandpaper rash** (12-48 hours after fever):
  - Fine, erythematous, blanching papules
  - Rough texture ("sandpaper" feel)
  - Starts: neck, chest → trunk → extremities
  - Spares: Palms, soles
  - Accentuated in skin folds
- **Pastia lines:** Linear petechiae in antecubital fossa, axillary, and inguinal folds (persist even after rash fades)
- **Strawberry tongue:**
  - Day 1-2: White strawberry tongue (white coating with red papillae)
  - Day 4-5: Red strawberry tongue (coating peels off, leaving red glistening surface)
- **Circumoral pallor** — flushed cheeks with pale area around mouth
- **Desquamation:** Starts face (fine), then trunk, then hands/feet (sheet-like peeling of fingertips) — 1-3 weeks after rash

### Absence of Cough/Rhinorrhea
- GAS pharyngitis typically has NO cough, NO rhinorrhea (helps distinguish from viral)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
- Characteristic triad: Sandpaper rash + strawberry tongue + circumoral pallor in a child with pharyngitis

### Laboratory
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT)** | Positive for GAS (quick, specific) |
| **Throat culture** | Gold standard for GAS detection |
| **ASO titer** | Elevated (indicates recent strep infection) — useful retrospectively |
| **CBC** | Leukocytosis with neutrophilia, eosinophilia during recovery |
| **Schultz-Charlton reaction** | Historical — blanching of rash after injection of antitoxin (not used now) |

## Management

### Antibiotic Treatment (Same as GAS Pharyngitis)
- **Penicillin V** (Phenoxymethyl penicillin) 250-500 mg PO BD-TDS × 10 days
- **Amoxicillin** 50 mg/kg/day × 10 days (preferred in children — better taste)
- **Benzathine Penicillin G** 6 lakh IU (<27 kg) or 12 lakh IU (>27 kg) IM single dose
- **Penicillin allergy:** Azithromycin 12 mg/kg/day × 5 days

### Supportive
- Paracetamol/ibuprofen for fever and pain
- Adequate hydration
- Soft diet during throat pain
- School exclusion until 24 hours after starting antibiotics

### Why Treat?
- Prevents **rheumatic fever** (antibiotics effective up to 9 days after symptom onset)
- Reduces duration and severity of symptoms
- Reduces spread to contacts
- Does NOT prevent post-streptococcal GN (this is not preventable by antibiotics)

## Complications
- **Suppurative:** Peritonsillar abscess, retropharyngeal abscess, cervical lymphadenitis, otitis media, sinusitis
- **Non-suppurative (immune-mediated):**
  - **Rheumatic fever** (2-4 weeks after pharyngitis) — PREVENTABLE with antibiotics
  - **Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis** (1-3 weeks) — NOT preventable with antibiotics
- **Toxic:** Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (rare)
- **Invasive disease:** Necrotizing fasciitis (rare)
