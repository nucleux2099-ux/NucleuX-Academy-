# Epilepsy in Children

## Overview
Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by recurrent unprovoked seizures (≥2 separated by >24 hours) or one unprovoked seizure with high recurrence risk (≥60%). It affects 0.5-1% of children. Classification by seizure type (focal, generalized, unknown) and epilepsy syndrome is essential for management. Sodium valproate and carbamazepine are traditional first-line AEDs, while levetiracetam is increasingly used for its favorable side-effect profile.

## Key Points
- **Definition:** ≥2 unprovoked seizures >24 hours apart, or 1 seizure + high recurrence risk
- **Prevalence:** 0.5-1% of children
- **ILAE 2017 classification:** Focal → Generalized → Unknown
- **Absence epilepsy:** 3 Hz spike-and-wave on EEG; ethosuximide first-line
- **West syndrome (Infantile spasms):** Hypsarrhythmia on EEG; ACTH/vigabatrin
- **Lennox-Gastaut:** Slow spike-wave; multiple seizure types; refractory
- **Valproate** — broad-spectrum; first-line for generalized epilepsy
- **Carbamazepine** — first-line for focal epilepsy
- **Treat for 2 seizure-free years** before considering AED withdrawal

## Definition & Classification

### ILAE 2017 Classification of Seizures
| Level | Types |
|-------|-------|
| **Focal** | Aware / Impaired awareness; Motor / Non-motor onset |
| **Generalized** | Motor: Tonic-clonic, tonic, clonic, myoclonic, atonic; Non-motor: Absence (typical, atypical) |
| **Unknown** | Epileptic spasms, unclassified |

### Common Epilepsy Syndromes in Children
| Syndrome | Age | Seizure Type | EEG | Treatment |
|----------|-----|-------------|-----|-----------|
| **Childhood Absence (CAE)** | 4-10 years | Typical absence (staring, 10-30 sec) | 3 Hz spike-and-wave | Ethosuximide/Valproate |
| **Juvenile Myoclonic (JME)** | 12-18 years | Myoclonic jerks (morning) + GTCS | 4-6 Hz polyspike-wave | Valproate (lifelong) |
| **Benign Rolandic (BECTS)** | 3-13 years | Focal, face/arm, sleep-related | Centrotemporal spikes | Often no treatment; self-limiting |
| **West Syndrome** | 3-12 months | Infantile spasms (clusters) | Hypsarrhythmia | ACTH/Vigabatrin |
| **Lennox-Gastaut** | 1-8 years | Multiple (tonic, atonic, absence) | Slow (<2.5 Hz) spike-wave | Valproate, lamotrigine; refractory |
| **Dravet Syndrome** | <1 year | Prolonged febrile → afebrile seizures | Normal initially | Valproate + clobazam; avoid Na⁺ channel blockers |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Genetic:** Channelopathies (SCN1A — Dravet), CAE, JME, BECTS
- **Structural:** Cortical dysplasia, tuberous sclerosis, tumors, stroke, trauma
- **Infectious:** Post-meningitis/encephalitis, neurocysticercosis
- **Metabolic:** Pyridoxine-dependent seizures, GLUT-1 deficiency
- **Immune:** Autoimmune encephalitis (NMDA-R antibody)
- **Unknown:** No identifiable cause (~50%)

### Pathophysiology
1. Imbalance between excitatory (glutamate) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmission
2. Neuronal hyperexcitability and hypersynchrony
3. Kindling phenomenon: repeated subthreshold stimulation → eventually spontaneous seizures
4. Ion channel dysfunction (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺) → abnormal neuronal firing

## Clinical Features

### Generalized Seizures
- **Tonic-clonic (GTCS):** Tonic stiffening → clonic jerking → post-ictal state
- **Absence:** Brief staring (5-30 sec), abrupt onset/offset, may have automatisms; provoked by hyperventilation
- **Myoclonic:** Brief, shock-like jerks; morning predominance in JME
- **Atonic (drop attacks):** Sudden loss of tone → falls → injury
- **Tonic:** Sustained stiffening; sleep-predominant in Lennox-Gastaut

### Focal Seizures
- Symptoms depend on cortical area involved
- **With awareness:** Motor (clonic jerking), sensory (tingling), autonomic
- **With impaired awareness:** Staring, automatisms (lip-smacking, picking)
- **Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic:** Starts focal, spreads to GTCS

### Infantile Spasms (West Syndrome)
- Brief flexor/extensor spasms in clusters (often on waking)
- Developmental regression
- Triad: Spasms + hypsarrhythmia + developmental regression

## Diagnosis

### EEG (Essential)
| Syndrome | EEG Pattern |
|----------|------------|
| Childhood Absence | 3 Hz generalized spike-and-wave |
| JME | 4-6 Hz polyspike-and-wave |
| BECTS | Centrotemporal spikes |
| West Syndrome | Hypsarrhythmia (chaotic high-amplitude) |
| Lennox-Gastaut | Slow (<2.5 Hz) spike-and-wave |

### Neuroimaging
- **MRI brain** — indicated for all focal seizures, infantile spasms, abnormal neuro exam
- Not mandatory for typical generalized epilepsy syndromes (CAE, JME, BECTS)
- **Findings:** Mesial temporal sclerosis, cortical dysplasia, tumors, neurocysticercosis

### Other Investigations
- Blood glucose, electrolytes, calcium, magnesium (rule out metabolic)
- Metabolic workup if suspected (ammonia, lactate, amino acids)
- Genetic testing for suspected genetic epilepsy syndromes
- CSF analysis if infectious etiology suspected

## Management

### AED Selection
| Seizure Type | First-Line | Alternatives |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Focal** | Carbamazepine, Oxcarbazepine | Levetiracetam, Lamotrigine |
| **Generalized tonic-clonic** | Valproate | Levetiracetam, Lamotrigine |
| **Absence** | Ethosuximide, Valproate | Lamotrigine |
| **Myoclonic** | Valproate | Levetiracetam, Clobazam |
| **Infantile spasms** | ACTH, Vigabatrin | Prednisolone |
| **Lennox-Gastaut** | Valproate, Lamotrigine | Rufinamide, Clobazam |

### AED Principles
- Start monotherapy; lowest effective dose
- Increase dose gradually
- If first drug fails at adequate dose → try second monotherapy before combination
- **Avoid in specific situations:**
  - Carbamazepine → worsens absence, myoclonic seizures
  - Valproate → avoid in girls of childbearing age (teratogenic — neural tube defects)
  - Phenobarbital → cognitive/behavioral side effects in children
- **Duration:** Minimum 2 seizure-free years before considering withdrawal
- **Taper gradually** over 3-6 months

### Non-Pharmacological
- **Ketogenic diet** — effective for refractory epilepsy (especially GLUT-1 deficiency)
- **Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS)** — adjunctive in drug-resistant epilepsy
- **Epilepsy surgery** — for drug-resistant focal epilepsy with identifiable lesion

### Counseling
- Seizure first aid education
- Activity restrictions (swimming supervision, avoid heights)
- Regular school attendance
- Drug compliance
- Avoid triggers (sleep deprivation, stress, flashing lights)

## Complications
- **Status epilepticus** — most serious acute complication
- **Injury** — falls, burns, drowning
- **SUDEP** (Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy) — rare in children
- **Cognitive/behavioral:** AED side effects, seizure impact on developing brain
- **Psychosocial:** Stigma, school difficulties, depression
- **AED toxicity:** Hepatotoxicity (valproate), SJS (lamotrigine, carbamazepine), teratogenicity
