# Meningitis in Children

## Overview
Meningitis is inflammation of the meninges, most commonly caused by bacterial or viral infections. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency with mortality of 5-15% even with treatment and significant morbidity (deafness, developmental delay). Neonatal meningitis is caused by GBS and E. coli, while S. pneumoniae and N. meningitidis are the leading causes in older children. CSF analysis is the cornerstone of diagnosis. Empirical antibiotics must be started immediately.

## Key Points
- **Medical emergency** — treat first, investigate second
- **S. pneumoniae** — most common bacterial cause overall (post-Hib vaccination era)
- **N. meningitidis** — epidemic meningitis; petechial rash (meningococcemia)
- **Neonatal:** GBS + E. coli + Listeria
- **Viral (aseptic)** — most common overall; enterovirus most common cause
- **CSF analysis** — essential for diagnosis
- **Empirical:** Ceftriaxone ± vancomycin (or ampicillin in neonates)
- **Dexamethasone** — given before/with first antibiotic dose (reduces hearing loss in Hib)
- **Most common sequela:** Sensorineural hearing loss

## Definition & Classification

### Types
| Type | CSF Profile | Common Causes |
|------|------------|---------------|
| **Bacterial (pyogenic)** | ↑WBC (neutrophils), ↑protein, ↓glucose, ↑pressure | S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae |
| **Viral (aseptic)** | ↑WBC (lymphocytes), normal/↑protein, normal glucose | Enterovirus, HSV, mumps |
| **Tubercular** | ↑WBC (lymphocytes), ↑↑protein, ↓↓glucose, cobweb appearance | M. tuberculosis |
| **Fungal** | ↑WBC (lymphocytes), ↑protein, ↓glucose | Cryptococcus (immunocompromised) |

### CSF Normal Values
| Parameter | Neonate | Older Child |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| WBC | <30/mm³ | <5/mm³ |
| Protein | <150 mg/dL | <40 mg/dL |
| Glucose | 50-80% of blood | 60-70% of blood |
| Pressure | 5-8 cmH₂O | 8-18 cmH₂O |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes by Age
| Age | Common Organisms |
|-----|-----------------|
| **<1 month** | GBS, E. coli, Listeria, Klebsiella |
| **1-3 months** | GBS, E. coli, S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis |
| **3 months-5 years** | S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae type b |
| **>5 years** | S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis |

### Pathophysiology
1. Organisms reach meninges via: hematogenous spread (most common), direct extension (sinusitis, otitis), or post-surgical/trauma
2. Bacterial multiplication in CSF (poor immune defenses in CSF)
3. Release of bacterial components → cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1β)
4. Inflammation → ↑permeability of blood-brain barrier → cerebral edema
5. ↑Intracranial pressure → ↓cerebral perfusion → brain injury
6. Vasculitis → cerebral infarction
7. Exudate → hydrocephalus (blocks CSF flow)

## Clinical Features

### Infants (<12 months) — Nonspecific
- Fever or hypothermia
- Poor feeding, vomiting
- Irritability, high-pitched cry
- Lethargy, floppiness
- **Bulging fontanelle** (late sign, very specific)
- Seizures
- Neck stiffness may be ABSENT

### Older Children — Classic Triad
- **Fever**
- **Headache**
- **Neck stiffness (meningism)**
- Also: vomiting, photophobia, altered sensorium, seizures

### Meningeal Signs
- **Kernig sign:** Resistance/pain on knee extension with hip flexed
- **Brudzinski sign:** Involuntary hip flexion when neck is flexed
- **Neck stiffness:** Resistance to passive neck flexion

### Specific Features
- **Petechial/purpuric rash** — N. meningitidis (meningococcemia) — may progress to DIC, shock (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome — adrenal hemorrhage)
- **Cranial nerve palsies** — VI, VII, VIII (hearing loss)
- **Opisthotonus** — severe meningism

## Diagnosis

### CSF Analysis (Gold Standard)
| Parameter | Bacterial | Viral | TB |
|-----------|-----------|-------|-----|
| **Appearance** | Turbid/purulent | Clear | Clear/slightly turbid |
| **WBC** | >1000 (neutrophils) | 10-500 (lymphocytes) | 100-500 (lymphocytes) |
| **Protein** | >100 mg/dL | Normal/↑ | ↑↑ (100-500) |
| **Glucose** | <40 mg/dL (<50% blood) | Normal | ↓↓ (<30% blood) |
| **Gram stain** | Positive 60-90% | Negative | AFB rarely positive |
| **Culture** | Gold standard | Viral PCR | Culture (4-8 weeks) |

### Additional Tests
- **Blood culture** — positive in 50-80% of bacterial meningitis
- **CRP, procalcitonin** — elevated in bacterial
- **Latex agglutination** — rapid bacterial antigen detection
- **CSF PCR** — for viral (enterovirus, HSV), TB
- **CT brain** — BEFORE LP if: papilledema, focal neurological signs, GCS <12, immunocompromised

### When NOT to Delay Antibiotics for LP
- Give antibiotics IMMEDIATELY if: unstable patient, signs of raised ICP, DIC
- LP can be done later; antibiotics should NEVER be delayed

## Management

### Empirical Antibiotic Therapy
| Age | Empirical Regimen | Duration |
|-----|------------------|----------|
| **<1 month** | Ampicillin + Cefotaxime (or + Gentamicin) | 14-21 days |
| **1-3 months** | Ceftriaxone + Ampicillin | 10-14 days |
| **>3 months** | Ceftriaxone ± Vancomycin | 10-14 days |

### Dexamethasone
- **0.15 mg/kg/dose Q6h × 2-4 days** — start BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose
- Proven benefit in Hib meningitis (reduces hearing loss)
- Controversial in pneumococcal meningitis; still recommended by many guidelines
- NOT recommended in neonatal meningitis

### Supportive Care
- Fluid management: Avoid overhydration (risk of SIADH → hyponatremia)
- Monitor: Neuro status, head circumference, seizures
- Seizure management: IV phenobarbitone or levetiracetam
- Raised ICP: Head elevation 30°, mannitol, avoid fluid overload

### Duration of Treatment
| Organism | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| N. meningitidis | 7 days |
| H. influenzae | 7-10 days |
| S. pneumoniae | 10-14 days |
| GBS/E. coli (neonates) | 14-21 days |
| Listeria | 21 days |

### Chemoprophylaxis
- **Meningococcal contacts:** Rifampicin 10 mg/kg BD × 2 days OR single dose ciprofloxacin/ceftriaxone
- **H. influenzae contacts:** Rifampicin 20 mg/kg/day × 4 days (if unvaccinated children in household)

## Complications
- **Sensorineural hearing loss** — most common long-term sequela (check BERA at discharge)
- **Hydrocephalus** — communicating (exudate blocks absorption) or non-communicating
- **Subdural effusion/empyema** — increasing head circumference, persistent fever
- **Brain abscess**
- **Cerebral infarction** — vasculitis
- **SIADH** — hyponatremia, fluid restriction needed
- **Seizures** — acute and late
- **Intellectual disability, cerebral palsy**
- **Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome** — adrenal hemorrhage with meningococcemia
- **Mortality:** 5-15% (bacterial); <1% (viral)
