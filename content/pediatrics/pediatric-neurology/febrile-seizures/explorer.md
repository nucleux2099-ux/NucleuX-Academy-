# Febrile Seizures

## Overview
Febrile seizures are the most common seizure disorder in childhood, occurring in 2-5% of children aged 6 months to 5 years. They are provoked by fever (≥38°C / 100.4°F) without evidence of intracranial infection or other defined cause. Though alarming to parents, they are generally benign with an excellent prognosis. Understanding classification into simple and complex types is crucial for management decisions.

## Key Points
- **Age group:** 6 months to 5 years (peak: 12-18 months)
- **Prevalence:** 2-5% of children
- **Strong genetic predisposition** — positive family history in 25-40%
- **Simple febrile seizures** are benign and do not require long-term treatment
- **Complex febrile seizures** need further evaluation
- **Risk of epilepsy:** 1-2% (simple) vs 4-6% (complex)
- **Recurrence risk:** ~30% after first episode
- **No evidence** that febrile seizures cause brain damage or cognitive impairment
- **Antipyretics do NOT prevent** febrile seizure recurrence
- **Lumbar puncture** recommended in children <12 months or if meningitis suspected

## Definition & Classification

### Definition
A febrile seizure is a seizure occurring in a child aged 6 months to 5 years, associated with a febrile illness not caused by an infection of the CNS, without previous neonatal seizures or a previous unprovoked seizure.

### Classification

| Feature | Simple Febrile Seizure | Complex Febrile Seizure |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Duration** | <15 minutes | ≥15 minutes |
| **Type** | Generalized tonic-clonic | Focal features |
| **Recurrence in 24h** | No | Yes (≥2 seizures) |
| **Post-ictal paresis** | No | May be present (Todd's paresis) |
| **Frequency** | 80% of febrile seizures | 20% of febrile seizures |

### Febrile Status Epilepticus
- Febrile seizure lasting >30 minutes
- Accounts for ~5% of febrile seizures
- Requires emergency management

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Etiology
- **Viral infections** (most common trigger):
  - Human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6) — roseola (most common specific cause)
  - Influenza A
  - Adenovirus
  - Parainfluenza
- **Bacterial infections:** UTI, otitis media, pharyngitis, pneumonia
- **Post-vaccination:** DPT, MMR (rare, benign)
- **Genetic factors:**
  - Autosomal dominant with reduced penetrance
  - FEB1 (8q13-q21), FEB2 (19p), FEB3 (2q23-q24)
  - SCN1A, SCN1B mutations (also linked to Dravet syndrome)
  - Polygenic inheritance most common

### Pathophysiology
1. **Immature thermoregulatory system** — developing brain has lower seizure threshold
2. **Rapid rise in temperature** more important than absolute temperature
3. **Cytokine-mediated mechanisms:**
   - IL-1β increases neuronal excitability
   - Fever enhances glutamate release and reduces GABA inhibition
4. **Age-dependent susceptibility** — myelination incomplete, ion channel expression evolving
5. **Genetic susceptibility** — sodium channel mutations lower seizure threshold

### Risk Factors for First Febrile Seizure
- Family history of febrile seizures (first-degree relative)
- Developmental delay
- Neonatal nursery stay >30 days
- Day care attendance
- High peak temperature

### Risk Factors for Recurrence (~30% overall)
- Age <18 months at first seizure (strongest predictor)
- Lower temperature at time of seizure
- Shorter duration of fever before seizure
- Family history of febrile seizures
- Complex febrile seizure (modest increase)

## Clinical Features

### Presentation
- **Onset:** Usually within first 24 hours of febrile illness
- **Seizure type:** Generalized tonic-clonic (most common in simple FS)
- **Duration:** Usually <5 minutes (simple), may be prolonged (complex)
- **Post-ictal:** Brief drowsiness, rapid return to baseline
- **No focal neurological deficits** (in simple FS)

### History Points to Elicit
- Exact description of seizure (type, duration, focality)
- Temperature at time of seizure
- Number of episodes in 24 hours
- Source of fever / preceding illness
- Immunization history (recent vaccines)
- Family history of febrile seizures or epilepsy
- Developmental milestones
- Previous seizure episodes

### Red Flags (Suggest Complex FS or Other Diagnosis)
- Focal onset or focal features during seizure
- Duration >15 minutes
- Multiple seizures within 24 hours
- Prolonged post-ictal drowsiness (>1 hour)
- Post-ictal focal deficit (Todd's paresis)
- Age <6 months or >5 years
- Signs of meningism (neck stiffness, bulging fontanelle)
- Pre-existing neurological abnormality

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
Febrile seizures are primarily a **clinical diagnosis**. The key is to:
1. Confirm the seizure was associated with fever
2. Exclude CNS infection
3. Classify as simple or complex

### Investigations

#### When to Perform Lumbar Puncture (AAP Guidelines)
| Age | Recommendation |
|-----|---------------|
| **6-12 months** | Strongly consider if Hib/PCV status incomplete or unknown |
| **12-18 months** | Consider if signs of meningitis present |
| **>18 months** | Only if clinical signs of meningitis |
| **Any age** | If pretreated with antibiotics (may mask meningitis signs) |

#### Laboratory Tests
- **Routine labs NOT recommended** for simple febrile seizures
- Blood glucose, electrolytes — if clinically indicated
- CBC, blood culture — to identify source of fever
- Urinalysis — especially in girls <2 years

#### EEG
- **NOT recommended** for simple febrile seizures
- Does not predict recurrence or epilepsy
- May be considered for complex febrile seizures (controversial)

#### Neuroimaging (CT/MRI)
- **NOT recommended** for simple febrile seizures
- Consider MRI for:
  - Focal seizures
  - Prolonged febrile seizures (febrile status epilepticus)
  - Persistent neurological abnormality

## Management

### Acute Management (During Seizure)
1. **First Aid:**
   - Place child on side (recovery position)
   - Protect from injury, do not restrain
   - Do not put anything in mouth
   - Note time of onset
2. **If seizure >5 minutes — Benzodiazepines:**
   - **Rectal diazepam:** 0.5 mg/kg (max 10 mg)
   - **Buccal/intranasal midazolam:** 0.2-0.5 mg/kg
   - **IV lorazepam:** 0.1 mg/kg (hospital setting)
3. **If seizure continues >15-20 minutes:** Treat as status epilepticus

### Post-Seizure Management
- **Identify and treat the source of fever**
- **Antipyretics:**
  - Paracetamol 15 mg/kg/dose Q4-6h
  - Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg/dose Q6-8h
  - Note: Antipyretics provide comfort but do NOT prevent recurrence
- **Parental education and reassurance** (most important intervention)

### Long-Term Prophylaxis

#### Intermittent Prophylaxis
- **Oral diazepam** 0.33 mg/kg Q8h during febrile illness
- **Rectal diazepam** 0.5 mg/kg at onset of fever
- Indicated if parental anxiety is high or recurrences are frequent
- Side effects: sedation, ataxia (may mask meningitis signs)

#### Continuous Prophylaxis (Rarely Indicated)
- **NOT recommended** for simple febrile seizures (AAP, IAP)
- Consider ONLY if:
  - Febrile status epilepticus
  - Very frequent recurrences causing significant parental anxiety
- **Drugs:** Sodium valproate (20-30 mg/kg/day) or Phenobarbital (3-5 mg/kg/day)
- Risks outweigh benefits in most cases

### When to Hospitalize
- First febrile seizure in child <12 months
- Complex febrile seizure
- Febrile status epilepticus
- Suspected meningitis/encephalitis
- Incomplete immunization status
- Diagnostic uncertainty

### Parent Counseling (Essential)
- Febrile seizures are common and generally harmless
- No evidence of brain damage or intellectual impairment
- ~30% chance of recurrence, decreasing with age
- Risk of epilepsy only slightly higher than general population
- Demonstrate seizure first aid
- When to seek emergency care
- Antipyretics do not prevent seizures but can be used for comfort

## Complications
- **Recurrence:** ~30% after first episode; 50% after second
- **Febrile status epilepticus:** ~5%, requires aggressive treatment
- **Risk of subsequent epilepsy:**
  - Simple FS: 1-2% (vs 1% general population)
  - Complex FS: 4-6%
  - Risk factors: complex FS, family history of epilepsy, neurodevelopmental abnormality
- **Mesial temporal sclerosis:** Controversial association with prolonged febrile seizures
- **Parental anxiety:** Significant — address with education and reassurance
- **No evidence** of cognitive decline, academic difficulties, or behavioral problems

## Prognosis
- **Excellent** for simple febrile seizures
- Most children outgrow febrile seizures by age 5-6 years
- Normal development and cognition expected
- Long-term AED therapy not justified for most children
