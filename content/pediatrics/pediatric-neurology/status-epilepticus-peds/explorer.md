# Status Epilepticus in Children

## Overview
Status epilepticus (SE) is a neurological emergency defined as continuous seizure activity lasting ≥5 minutes or recurrent seizures without return to baseline consciousness. It occurs in 10-60 per 100,000 children per year and carries mortality of 3-5%. Convulsive SE (tonic-clonic) is the most common and dangerous form. Rapid, protocolized management with benzodiazepines as first-line, followed by second-line AEDs, is critical to prevent brain injury.

## Key Points
- **Definition:** Seizure ≥5 minutes (operational) or ≥30 minutes (traditional)
- **Refractory SE:** Continues despite 2 appropriate AEDs
- **Super-refractory:** Continues >24 hours despite anesthesia
- **Benzodiazepines** — first-line (lorazepam IV, midazolam IM/buccal, diazepam rectal)
- **Second-line:** Phenytoin/fosphenytoin, valproate, levetiracetam
- **Most common cause in children:** Febrile SE, acute symptomatic (meningitis, encephalitis)
- **Time is brain** — neuronal damage begins after 30 minutes of continuous seizure
- **ABC first** — airway, breathing, circulation before drugs

## Definition & Classification

### Time-Based Definitions
| Time | Term | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| 0-5 min | Prolonged seizure | First-line treatment |
| 5-20 min | Early SE | Second-line if benzos fail |
| 20-40 min | Established SE | Third-line agents |
| >40-60 min | Refractory SE | ICU, anesthesia |
| >24 h despite anesthesia | Super-refractory SE | Specialized care |

### Types
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Convulsive SE (CSE)** | Tonic-clonic activity; most common, most dangerous |
| **Non-convulsive SE (NCSE)** | Altered consciousness without motor activity; EEG needed |
| **Focal SE (Epilepsia partialis continua)** | Continuous focal seizure; often refractory |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Febrile SE** — most common in young children
- **Acute symptomatic:** CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis), metabolic (hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hypocalcemia), trauma, stroke, intoxication
- **Remote symptomatic:** Previous CNS injury, cerebral palsy
- **Epilepsy-related:** Breakthrough seizures, AED non-compliance, AED withdrawal
- **Cryptogenic/Unknown**

### Pathophysiology
1. **Phase 1 (0-30 min):** Compensated — ↑BP, ↑HR, ↑glucose, ↑catecholamines maintain cerebral perfusion
2. **Phase 2 (>30 min):** Decompensated — ↓BP, ↓glucose, ↑lactate, cerebral edema, hyperthermia
3. **Excitotoxicity:** Sustained glutamate release → Ca²⁺ influx → neuronal death
4. **GABA receptor internalization:** Benzodiazepines become less effective with time (pharmacoresistance)
5. **Systemic complications:** Rhabdomyolysis, DIC, multi-organ failure

## Clinical Features

### Presentation
- Continuous tonic-clonic seizure activity
- May evolve from bilateral convulsive → subtle (eye deviation, nystagmus, minimal twitching)
- **Subtle SE** — dangerous because may be missed; EEG needed
- Post-ictal: Prolonged obtundation if seizure terminates

### Assess During SE
- Airway patency and oxygenation
- Hemodynamic status
- Blood glucose (bedside)
- Temperature
- Signs of infection (meningitis), trauma, poisoning
- Pupil size and reaction

## Diagnosis

### Investigations (Concurrent with Treatment)
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Blood glucose | Hypoglycemia (most important rapid test) |
| Electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg) | Metabolic causes |
| CBC, blood culture | Infection |
| ABG/VBG | Acidosis, oxygenation |
| AED levels | Sub-therapeutic in known epileptics |
| Toxicology screen | Ingestion/poisoning |
| EEG | Confirm SE, detect NCSE |
| CT/MRI | After stabilization — structural cause |
| LP | After stabilization — if infection suspected |

## Management

### Step-wise Protocol (⭐ High Yield)

#### Step 1: Stabilize (0-5 minutes)
- **ABC:** Airway (recovery position, suction), Breathing (O₂), Circulation
- **Blood glucose:** If <60 mg/dL → 25% dextrose 2 mL/kg IV bolus
- **IV access** (two large bore if possible)
- **Monitor:** SpO₂, ECG, BP

#### Step 2: First-Line — Benzodiazepines (5-15 minutes)
| Route | Drug | Dose |
|-------|------|------|
| **IV** | Lorazepam | 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg); repeat once at 5 min |
| **IM** | Midazolam | 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) |
| **Buccal** | Midazolam | 0.2-0.5 mg/kg |
| **Rectal** | Diazepam | 0.5 mg/kg (max 10 mg) |
| **IN** | Midazolam | 0.2 mg/kg |

- **Maximum 2 doses** of benzodiazepines before moving to second-line
- IM midazolam is as effective as IV lorazepam (RAMPART trial)

#### Step 3: Second-Line (15-30 minutes)
| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Phenytoin/Fosphenytoin** | 20 mg PE/kg IV over 20 min | Monitor ECG; risk of arrhythmia |
| **Valproate** | 20-40 mg/kg IV over 5-10 min | Avoid in <2 years, liver disease |
| **Levetiracetam** | 40-60 mg/kg IV over 15 min | Fewer side effects; increasingly preferred |
| **Phenobarbital** | 20 mg/kg IV over 20 min | May cause respiratory depression |

#### Step 4: Refractory SE (>30-40 minutes) — ICU
- **Midazolam infusion:** 0.1-0.4 mg/kg/h
- **Thiopentone:** 3-5 mg/kg bolus, then infusion
- **Propofol:** (generally avoided in children — propofol infusion syndrome risk)
- **Continuous EEG monitoring** — target: burst suppression
- **Intubation and ventilation** usually required

### Key Management Points
- **Time is critical** — every minute of delay reduces efficacy
- **Benzodiazepines become less effective after 20-30 min** (GABA receptor internalization)
- **Correct reversible causes:** Hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia
- **Pyridoxine 100 mg IV** if <18 months and cause unknown (pyridoxine-dependent seizures)
- **Fever control** — antipyretics, cooling

## Complications
- **Neuronal injury** — excitotoxic damage (especially hippocampi)
- **Aspiration pneumonia**
- **Respiratory depression** — from benzodiazepines and AEDs
- **Cardiac arrhythmias** — from phenytoin, prolonged seizure
- **Rhabdomyolysis** → acute kidney injury
- **Metabolic acidosis** — lactic acidosis
- **Cerebral edema** → herniation
- **DIC** — in prolonged SE
- **Death** — 3-5% in children; higher if refractory
- **Long-term:** Epilepsy development, cognitive deficits, mesial temporal sclerosis
