# Bacterial Meningitis in Children

> 📚 **Sources:** Nelson's Pediatrics 21st Ed, OP Ghai 9th Ed, IDSA Guidelines
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Bacterial meningitis is an **acute purulent infection of the meninges and CSF**. It's a medical emergency with high mortality and morbidity if untreated.

**Pathophysiology:**
Nasopharyngeal colonization → Bacteremia → Crossing blood-brain barrier → Subarachnoid space invasion → Inflammatory response → Cerebral edema, ↑ ICP → Brain injury

## Key Points

- **Medical emergency** - Treatment within 1 hour of presentation
- Age determines likely organism
- Classic triad: Fever + Headache + Neck stiffness (only 50% of children)
- **Bulging fontanelle** in infants is highly suggestive
- LP is essential but don't delay antibiotics if LP cannot be done immediately
- Dexamethasone before/with first antibiotic dose (especially for Hib)
- Vaccines have dramatically reduced incidence (Hib, Pneumococcal, Meningococcal)

## Etiology by Age

| Age Group | Common Organisms |
|-----------|------------------|
| **<1 month (Neonatal)** | Group B Streptococcus, E. coli (K1), Listeria monocytogenes |
| **1-3 months** | GBS, E. coli, Listeria, S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis |
| **3 months - 5 years** | **S. pneumoniae (#1)**, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae type b |
| **>5 years** | S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis |

**Special Situations:**
- VP shunt: Staph epidermidis, Staph aureus
- Immunocompromised: Listeria, Gram-negatives, fungi
- Basilar skull fracture: S. pneumoniae (CSF leak)
- Penetrating trauma: Staph aureus, Gram-negatives

## Clinical Features

### Classic Triad (Older Children/Adults)
1. **Fever** (90-95%)
2. **Headache** (severe)
3. **Neck stiffness** (meningismus)

*Only 50% of children present with complete triad*

### Symptoms by Age

**Neonates/Infants (<12 months):**
- Non-specific: Irritability, lethargy, poor feeding
- Fever or hypothermia
- **Bulging fontanelle** (late sign, very specific)
- High-pitched cry
- Seizures
- **Neck stiffness often ABSENT**

**Older Children:**
- Fever, headache, vomiting
- Neck stiffness, photophobia
- Altered mental status
- Seizures
- Focal neurological deficits

### Meningeal Signs
| Sign | Technique | Positive Finding |
|------|-----------|------------------|
| **Nuchal rigidity** | Flex neck forward | Resistance/pain |
| **Kernig's sign** | Extend knee with hip flexed 90° | Pain/resistance |
| **Brudzinski's sign** | Flex neck | Involuntary hip/knee flexion |

*Sensitivity: 60-80% in children >12 months; Often absent in infants*

### Signs Suggesting Etiology

**N. meningitidis (Meningococcemia):**
- Petechial/purpuric rash (may rapidly progress)
- Hypotension, shock (Waterhouse-Friderichsen)
- Occurs in epidemics

**S. pneumoniae:**
- Often preceded by otitis media, sinusitis, pneumonia
- Higher mortality
- CSF leak history

**H. influenzae type b:**
- Age 6 months - 5 years (unvaccinated)
- Preceding URI common
- Rare in vaccinated populations

## Complications

### Acute
- Cerebral edema, raised ICP
- Seizures (20-30%)
- Shock, DIC
- SIADH (hyponatremia)
- Subdural effusion (especially Hib, pneumococcus)
- Cerebral venous thrombosis
- Death (5-10% with treatment)

### Long-term Sequelae (10-30%)
- **Hearing loss** (most common neurological sequela) - 10-30%
- Cognitive impairment
- Motor deficits
- Seizure disorder
- Hydrocephalus

## Diagnosis

### Lumbar Puncture (Essential)

**CSF Findings in Bacterial Meningitis:**
| Parameter | Normal | Bacterial |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Appearance | Clear | Turbid/purulent |
| WBC | <5/mm³ | >1000/mm³ (often >5000) |
| Differential | Lymphocytes | **Neutrophil predominant** (>80%) |
| Protein | <40 mg/dL | **↑↑ (100-500 mg/dL)** |
| Glucose | 50-80 mg/dL | **↓↓ (<40 or <50% of blood glucose)** |
| CSF:Blood glucose | >0.6 | **<0.4** |
| Gram stain | Negative | Positive (60-90%) |
| Culture | Negative | Positive (gold standard) |

**CSF Comparison:**
| Parameter | Bacterial | Viral | TB |
|-----------|-----------|-------|-----|
| Cells | ↑↑↑ (>1000) | ↑ (50-500) | ↑↑ (100-500) |
| Type | Neutrophils | Lymphocytes | Lymphocytes |
| Protein | ↑↑↑ | ↑ | ↑↑↑ |
| Glucose | ↓↓ | Normal | ↓↓ |

### Contraindications to LP
**Do CT before LP if:**
- Papilledema or signs of raised ICP
- Focal neurological deficits
- New onset seizures
- Altered consciousness (GCS <13)
- Immunocompromised with focal signs

**Other contraindications:**
- Hemodynamic instability (resuscitate first)
- Skin infection at LP site
- Coagulopathy (relative)

**Rule: Never delay antibiotics waiting for LP or CT**

### Other Investigations
- Blood culture (positive in 50-80%)
- CBC, CRP, Procalcitonin
- Blood glucose (for CSF ratio)
- Electrolytes (SIADH)
- Coagulation studies (DIC)
- Latex agglutination (rapid antigen detection)
- PCR (especially if prior antibiotics)

### Rapid Tests
- **Gram stain:** Immediate, guides initial therapy
- **Latex agglutination:** Detects antigens (Hib, Pneumococcus, Meningococcus)
- **PCR:** Most sensitive, especially after antibiotics

## Management

### Principles
1. **Empirical antibiotics immediately** (within 1 hour)
2. Dexamethasone before/with first antibiotic dose
3. Supportive care
4. Modify therapy based on culture/sensitivity

### Empirical Antibiotic Therapy

**Neonates (<1 month):**
```
Ampicillin + Cefotaxime
OR
Ampicillin + Gentamicin

Doses:
- Ampicillin: 100 mg/kg/dose q8h (meningitis dose)
- Cefotaxime: 50 mg/kg/dose q6h
- Gentamicin: 5 mg/kg/dose q24h
```

**Infants and Children (1 month - 18 years):**
```
Ceftriaxone OR Cefotaxime
+ Vancomycin (for resistant pneumococcus)

Doses:
- Ceftriaxone: 100 mg/kg/day ÷ q12h (max 4g/day)
- Cefotaxime: 200-300 mg/kg/day ÷ q6h
- Vancomycin: 60 mg/kg/day ÷ q6h (meningitis dose)
```

### Directed Therapy (Once Organism Known)

| Organism | First Choice | Alternative | Duration |
|----------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| S. pneumoniae (sensitive) | Ceftriaxone | Penicillin G | 10-14 days |
| S. pneumoniae (resistant) | Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin | Meropenem | 10-14 days |
| N. meningitidis | Ceftriaxone | Penicillin G | 7 days |
| H. influenzae type b | Ceftriaxone | Ampicillin | 7-10 days |
| GBS (neonatal) | Penicillin G + Gentamicin | Ampicillin | 14-21 days |
| E. coli (neonatal) | Cefotaxime + Gentamicin | Meropenem | 21 days |
| Listeria | Ampicillin + Gentamicin | TMP-SMX | 21 days |

### Dexamethasone

**Indication:** All suspected bacterial meningitis in children >6 weeks

**Dose:** 0.15 mg/kg q6h × 4 days (or 0.4 mg/kg q12h × 2 days)

**Timing:** Give 15-30 minutes BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose

**Benefits:**
- Reduces hearing loss (especially Hib)
- May reduce mortality (pneumococcal)
- Reduces neurological sequelae

**Discontinue if:** Viral/tubercular meningitis confirmed

### Supportive Care
- Monitor neuro status frequently
- Manage raised ICP (head elevation, avoid hyperthermia)
- Fluid management: Avoid both dehydration and overhydration
- Seizure control (phenytoin, phenobarbital)
- Correct electrolytes (watch for SIADH)
- Nutritional support

### Repeat LP Indications
- No clinical improvement after 48-72 hours
- Gram-negative meningitis (confirm sterilization)
- Recurrent meningitis
- Resistant organisms

## Prevention

### Chemoprophylaxis

**N. meningitidis (Close contacts):**
| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|------|------|----------|
| Rifampicin | 10 mg/kg BD (max 600mg) | 2 days |
| Ceftriaxone | 125mg (<15y) or 250mg (>15y) IM | Single dose |
| Ciprofloxacin | 500mg (adults only) | Single dose |

**H. influenzae type b:**
- Rifampicin: 20 mg/kg/day × 4 days (max 600mg)
- For household contacts with <4 year old unvaccinated

### Vaccination
| Organism | Vaccine | Schedule |
|----------|---------|----------|
| H. influenzae type b | Hib conjugate | 6, 10, 14 weeks + booster |
| S. pneumoniae | PCV13 | 6, 10, 14 weeks + booster |
| N. meningitidis | MenACWY, MenB | Risk groups, outbreaks |

## High-Yield for Exams

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Classic triad | Fever + Headache + Neck stiffness |
| #1 cause (>3 months) | S. pneumoniae |
| #1 cause (neonatal) | Group B Streptococcus |
| CSF glucose | <40 mg/dL or <50% of blood |
| CSF cells | Neutrophil predominant, >1000/mm³ |
| Empirical Rx (child) | Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin |
| Empirical Rx (neonate) | Ampicillin + Cefotaxime |
| Dexamethasone timing | Before or with first antibiotic |
| Most common sequela | Hearing loss |
| Prophylaxis for contacts | Rifampicin (Meningococcus, Hib) |

**MCQ Pearls:**
- ❗ Bulging fontanelle in febrile infant = Meningitis until proven otherwise
- ❗ Petechial rash + meningitis = N. meningitidis
- ❗ Low CSF glucose is CHARACTERISTIC of bacterial meningitis
- ❗ Dexamethasone reduces hearing loss (especially Hib)
- ❗ Never delay antibiotics for LP or CT
- ❗ Waterhouse-Friderichsen = Adrenal hemorrhage in meningococcemia
- ❗ Listeria needs Ampicillin (not covered by cephalosporins)
- ❗ Duration: Pneumococcus 10-14 days, Meningococcus 7 days
