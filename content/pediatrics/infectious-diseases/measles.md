# Measles (Rubeola)

> 📚 **Sources:** Nelson's Pediatrics 21st Ed, OP Ghai 9th Ed, IAP Guidelines, WHO
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Measles is a highly contagious **viral exanthematous disease** caused by the **Measles virus** (Paramyxoviridae family, Morbillivirus genus). It remains a leading cause of vaccine-preventable deaths in children globally.

**Basic Reproduction Number (R₀):** 12-18 (one of the most infectious diseases known)
- One infected person infects 12-18 susceptible contacts
- Requires >95% herd immunity for elimination

## Key Points

- **RNA virus**, single serotype → lifelong immunity after infection
- Transmission: **Respiratory droplets and airborne** (aerosol)
- Infectious: 4 days before to 4 days after rash onset
- **Koplik spots** = Pathognomonic (appear before rash)
- Vitamin A deficiency increases severity and mortality
- Vaccine-preventable with MMR/MR vaccine

## Epidemiology

- Endemic in many developing countries
- Peak age: 9 months - 5 years (unvaccinated)
- Peak season: Late winter/spring
- Case fatality: 1-2% (developing countries: up to 10%)
- Maternal antibodies protect until ~6 months

## Clinical Features

### Incubation Period
- 10-14 days (rash appears ~14 days after exposure)

### Prodromal Phase (3-5 days)
**"3 Cs":**
1. **Cough** (brassy, non-productive)
2. **Coryza** (runny nose)
3. **Conjunctivitis** (red, watery eyes, photophobia)

**Plus:**
- High fever (stepwise increase, peaks with rash)
- Malaise, anorexia
- **Koplik spots** (Day 2-3 of prodrome)

### Koplik Spots (PATHOGNOMONIC)
- **Location:** Buccal mucosa opposite lower molars
- **Appearance:** White/blue-gray papules on erythematous base ("grains of salt on red background")
- **Timing:** Appear 2-3 days before rash, disappear 1-2 days after rash
- **Significance:** Allows early diagnosis before rash

### Exanthematous Phase (Rash)
**Characteristics:**
- **Maculopapular, erythematous** rash
- **Cephalocaudal spread:** Face (behind ears, hairline) → trunk → extremities
- Progression over 3-4 days
- Becomes confluent (especially on face)
- Rash blanches on pressure initially

**Fever Pattern:**
- Peaks on Day 2-3 of rash
- Defervescence occurs as rash reaches feet

### Recovery Phase
- Rash fades in order of appearance (face first)
- Leaves **branny desquamation** (fine flaking)
- May leave **brownish discoloration** (copper-colored staining)
- Complete recovery in 7-10 days (uncomplicated)

### Modified/Atypical Measles
- In partially immune individuals
- Milder prodrome, atypical rash
- May lack Koplik spots

## Complications

### Respiratory (Most Common)
| Complication | Features |
|--------------|----------|
| Otitis media | Most common complication (7-9%) |
| Pneumonia | Leading cause of measles death |
| Laryngotracheobronchitis (Croup) | Barking cough, stridor |

**Measles Pneumonia Types:**
1. Primary viral (giant cell pneumonia)
2. Secondary bacterial (Staph, Strep, H. influenzae)
3. Opportunistic (PCP in immunocompromised)

### Neurological (Serious)
| Complication | Timing | Features | Prognosis |
|--------------|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Acute encephalitis** | Days 2-6 of rash | Fever, seizures, altered consciousness | 15% mortality, 25% sequelae |
| **ADEM** | 1-2 weeks post-measles | Demyelination, ataxia, weakness | Usually recovers |
| **SSPE** | 7-10 YEARS later | Progressive dementia, myoclonus, death | Always fatal |

### Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE)
- Rare (1 in 10,000-100,000 cases)
- Caused by **persistent defective measles virus** in CNS
- **Progressive:** Behavioral changes → myoclonic jerks → dementia → coma → death
- **Diagnosis:** Elevated measles antibodies in CSF, EEG (periodic complexes)
- **No effective treatment** - 100% fatal

### Other Complications
- Diarrhea, dehydration
- Keratitis, corneal ulceration → blindness (especially with Vitamin A deficiency)
- Myocarditis (rare)
- Thrombocytopenic purpura
- Reactivation of latent TB

### High-Risk Groups
- Children <5 years
- Malnourished children
- Vitamin A deficiency
- Immunocompromised (HIV, leukemia)
- Pregnant women (fetal loss, preterm birth)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical
- **Classic triad:** Fever + 3 Cs + Maculopapular rash (cephalocaudal)
- Koplik spots clinch diagnosis

### Laboratory (for confirmation)
| Test | Specimen | Timing |
|------|----------|--------|
| **IgM antibody** (ELISA) | Serum | Positive from rash onset to 1 month |
| **IgG antibody** (4-fold rise) | Paired sera | Acute and convalescent |
| **RT-PCR** | Throat swab, urine, blood | Any time (best in first 3 days of rash) |
| Viral culture | Throat, nasopharynx | First 3 days (for genotyping) |

### Differential Diagnosis
- Rubella (milder, no Koplik spots, postauricular lymphadenopathy)
- Scarlet fever (sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue)
- Roseola (rash appears as fever subsides)
- Drug rash
- Kawasaki disease
- Dengue

## Management

### Supportive Care (No specific antiviral)
1. **Fever:** Paracetamol 10-15 mg/kg/dose
2. **Fluids:** Ensure adequate hydration
3. **Nutrition:** Continue feeding
4. **Isolation:** Until 4 days after rash onset

### Vitamin A Therapy (WHO Recommendation)
**Reduces mortality by 50%!**

| Age | Dose | Schedule |
|-----|------|----------|
| <6 months | 50,000 IU | Two doses, 24 hours apart |
| 6-12 months | 100,000 IU | Two doses, 24 hours apart |
| >12 months | 200,000 IU | Two doses, 24 hours apart |

**Third dose after 2-4 weeks** if clinical signs of Vitamin A deficiency

### Treatment of Complications
- **Bacterial superinfection:** Antibiotics (amoxicillin, co-amoxiclav)
- **Pneumonia:** Hospitalize, O2, antibiotics
- **Encephalitis:** Supportive care, anticonvulsants
- **Keratitis:** Vitamin A, eye care, ophthalmology referral

## Prevention

### Vaccination (Most Effective)
**Vaccines Available:**
- **MR (Measles-Rubella):** India's National Program
- **MMR (Measles-Mumps-Rubella):** IAP recommendation
- **MMRV:** Adds Varicella

**Schedule (IAP 2024):**
| Dose | Age | Vaccine |
|------|-----|---------|
| MR-1 | 9-12 months | MR/MMR |
| MR-2 | 15-18 months | MR/MMR |
| MMR-3 (optional) | 4-6 years | MMR |

**Vaccine Efficacy:**
- One dose at 9 months: 85%
- One dose at 12 months: 95%
- Two doses: >99%

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis
| Intervention | Timing | Indication |
|--------------|--------|------------|
| **Vaccine** | Within 72 hours | Healthy contacts >6 months |
| **Immunoglobulin** | Within 6 days | Infants <6 months, pregnant, immunocompromised |

**Immunoglobulin Dose:** 0.25-0.5 mL/kg IM (max 15 mL)

### Isolation
- **Airborne + Contact precautions**
- Until 4 days after rash onset
- Immunocompromised: For duration of illness
