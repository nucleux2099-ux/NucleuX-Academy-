# Upper Respiratory Tract Infections (URTI)

> 📚 **Sources:** Nelson's Pediatrics 21st Ed, OP Ghai 9th Ed, AAP Guidelines
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

URTIs are infections involving the **nose, pharynx, and larynx**. They are the most common infections in children, with most children experiencing 6-8 URTIs per year.

**Key Principle:** Most URTIs are **viral and self-limiting**. Antibiotics are rarely indicated and overuse drives resistance.

## Classification

1. **Common Cold (Nasopharyngitis)**
2. **Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis**
3. **Acute Otitis Media (AOM)**
4. **Sinusitis**
5. **Laryngotracheobronchitis (Croup)**

---

## 1. Common Cold (Acute Nasopharyngitis)

### Etiology
**Viruses (>200 types):**
- **Rhinovirus** (50%) - Most common
- Coronavirus (15-20%)
- RSV, Parainfluenza, Adenovirus
- Influenza, Enterovirus

### Clinical Features
- **Rhinorrhea** (clear → mucopurulent → clear)
- Nasal congestion, sneezing
- Sore throat, mild cough
- Low-grade fever (more in young children)
- Duration: 7-10 days (self-limiting)

### Management
**Supportive Only:**
- Adequate fluids
- Saline nasal drops
- Paracetamol/Ibuprofen for fever/discomfort
- Honey (>1 year) for cough

**NOT Recommended:**
- ❌ Antibiotics
- ❌ Antihistamines (<6 years)
- ❌ Cough suppressants (<6 years)
- ❌ Decongestants (<6 years)

### When to Suspect Complications
- Symptoms worsening after Day 5
- Persistent fever >5 days
- Ear pain (otitis media)
- Facial pain (sinusitis)

## 2. Acute Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis

### Etiology
**Viral (70-80%):**
- Rhinovirus, Adenovirus
- EBV (Infectious mononucleosis)
- Enterovirus (Herpangina, Hand-foot-mouth)

**Bacterial (20-30%):**
- **Group A Streptococcus (GAS)** - Most important bacterial cause
- Group C, G Streptococcus

### Clinical Features

**Viral Pharyngitis:**
- Gradual onset
- Cough, rhinorrhea, conjunctivitis
- Low-grade fever
- Anterior cervical lymphadenopathy (small)
- Vesicles/ulcers (enterovirus)

**Strep Pharyngitis (GAS):**
- Sudden onset
- High fever (>38.5°C)
- Severe sore throat, painful swallowing
- **Tonsillar exudates** (white patches)
- **Palatal petechiae**
- **Tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy**
- Scarlatiniform rash (Scarlet fever)
- **Absence of cough, rhinorrhea, conjunctivitis**

### Centor/Modified Centor Criteria

| Criterion | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| Fever >38°C | +1 |
| Tonsillar exudates | +1 |
| Tender anterior cervical nodes | +1 |
| Absence of cough | +1 |
| Age 3-14 years | +1 |
| Age 15-44 years | 0 |
| Age >44 years | -1 |

**Interpretation:**
- 0-1: Low risk, no testing/treatment
- 2-3: Test (Rapid Strep/Culture), treat if positive
- ≥4: Consider empirical treatment

### Diagnosis
- **Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT):** High specificity, moderate sensitivity
- **Throat culture:** Gold standard (48-hour result)

### Treatment

**GAS Pharyngitis:**
| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|------|------|----------|
| **Penicillin V** | 250mg (child) or 500mg (adult) BD-TDS | 10 days |
| **Amoxicillin** | 50 mg/kg/day (max 1g) OD or ÷ BD | 10 days |
| **Benzathine Penicillin G** | 600,000 U (<27kg) or 1.2 MU (>27kg) IM | Single dose |

**Penicillin Allergy:**
- Cephalexin, Azithromycin, Clindamycin

**Why treat GAS?**
1. Prevent **Rheumatic fever** (most important)
2. Reduce duration of symptoms
3. Prevent suppurative complications (peritonsillar abscess)
4. Reduce transmission

### Complications of GAS Pharyngitis

**Suppurative:**
- Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy)
- Retropharyngeal abscess
- Cervical lymphadenitis
- Otitis media, sinusitis

**Non-suppurative (Immune-mediated):**
- **Acute Rheumatic Fever** (2-4 weeks post)
- **Post-streptococcal Glomerulonephritis** (1-2 weeks post)

## 3. Acute Otitis Media (AOM)

### Definition
Acute infection of the **middle ear** with rapid onset of signs/symptoms and middle ear effusion.

### Etiology
- **S. pneumoniae** (35-40%)
- **H. influenzae** (30%)
- **M. catarrhalis** (10-15%)
- Viruses (25%)

### Risk Factors
- Age <2 years
- Daycare attendance
- Bottle feeding (especially lying down)
- Passive smoking
- Cleft palate
- Down syndrome
- Immunodeficiency

### Clinical Features
- Ear pain (tugging ears in infants)
- Fever
- Irritability, poor sleep
- Decreased hearing
- Otorrhea (if TM perforation)

**Otoscopic Findings:**
- **Bulging** tympanic membrane (most specific)
- Erythema
- Decreased mobility (pneumatic otoscopy)
- Opacification
- Air-fluid level (OME)

### Management

**AAP Guidelines (2013):**

**Immediate Antibiotics:**
- Age <6 months (all cases)
- Age 6 months - 2 years with bilateral AOM
- Severe symptoms (high fever >39°C, severe otalgia)
- Otorrhea

**Watchful Waiting (48-72 hours):**
- Age 6-23 months with unilateral, non-severe AOM
- Age ≥2 years with unilateral or bilateral, non-severe AOM

**First-line:** Amoxicillin 80-90 mg/kg/day ÷ BD × 10 days

**Treatment Failure (after 48-72h):** Amoxicillin-clavulanate

**Penicillin Allergy:** Cefdinir, Cefuroxime, Azithromycin

### Complications
- Tympanic membrane perforation
- Mastoiditis
- Cholesteatoma
- Hearing loss
- Intracranial (meningitis, brain abscess) - rare

## 4. Acute Sinusitis

### Definition
Bacterial infection of paranasal sinuses, usually complicating viral URI.

### Anatomy by Age
- **Ethmoid/Maxillary:** Present at birth
- **Sphenoid:** Pneumatizes by 5 years
- **Frontal:** Pneumatizes by 7-10 years

### When to Suspect (vs Viral URI)
1. **Persistent symptoms:** >10 days without improvement
2. **Worsening course:** Initial improvement then worsening (Day 6-7)
3. **Severe onset:** High fever (≥39°C) + purulent discharge ≥3 days

### Etiology
- S. pneumoniae
- H. influenzae
- M. catarrhalis

### Clinical Features
- Nasal congestion, purulent rhinorrhea
- Facial pain/pressure (older children)
- Post-nasal drip, cough (worse at night)
- Fever
- Headache

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical** (no imaging for uncomplicated)
- CT/MRI only for: Complications, recurrent, chronic

### Treatment
- **First-line:** Amoxicillin-clavulanate 45 mg/kg/day ÷ BD × 10-14 days
- Adjuncts: Saline irrigation, intranasal steroids

### Complications
- Orbital cellulitis/abscess
- Intracranial extension (epidural/subdural abscess, meningitis)
- Cavernous sinus thrombosis

## High-Yield for Exams

| Condition | Key Points |
|-----------|------------|
| Common Cold | Viral, self-limiting, no antibiotics |
| Strep Pharyngitis | Exudates, no cough, treat to prevent RF |
| AOM | Bulging TM, Amoxicillin first-line |
| Sinusitis | >10 days or biphasic course |

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Most common URTI pathogen | Rhinovirus |
| GAS pharyngitis treatment goal | Prevent Rheumatic Fever |
| Antibiotic for Strep | Penicillin V or Amoxicillin × 10 days |
| Most specific AOM sign | Bulging tympanic membrane |
| First-line for AOM | Amoxicillin 80-90 mg/kg/day |
| Sinusitis criteria | Symptoms >10 days or biphasic |
| First-line for sinusitis | Amoxicillin-clavulanate |

**MCQ Pearls:**
- ❗ Cough + Rhinorrhea = Likely viral, not Strep
- ❗ Palatal petechiae = Suggests GAS pharyngitis
- ❗ GAS: Treat even if culture pending (if high suspicion)
- ❗ Rheumatic fever can be prevented if antibiotics given within 9 days
- ❗ Antibiotic duration for GAS = 10 days (except Azithro 5 days)
- ❗ Peritonsillar abscess = "Hot potato" voice, trismus, uvula deviation
- ❗ Green nasal discharge does NOT mean bacterial infection
- ❗ Watch and wait is acceptable for non-severe AOM in older children
