# Vaccine-Preventable Diseases Overview

> 📚 **Sources:** Nelson's Pediatrics 21st Ed, IAP Immunization Guidelines 2024, WHO
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Vaccine-preventable diseases (VPDs) are infections that can be effectively prevented through immunization. Vaccines have eliminated or dramatically reduced many childhood diseases that once caused significant morbidity and mortality.

**Herd Immunity Threshold:**
- Measles: 95%
- Polio: 80-85%
- Diphtheria: 85%
- Pertussis: 92-94%

## IAP Recommended Schedule (2024)

Additional vaccines beyond UIP:
- Typhoid conjugate vaccine (TCV): 9-12 months, booster at 2 years
- Hepatitis A: 12 months, 18 months
- Varicella: 15 months, 4-6 years
- MMR instead of MR
- Influenza: Annual from 6 months
- Meningococcal: High-risk groups
- HPV: 9-14 years (girls primarily, boys optionally)

## Key Vaccine-Preventable Diseases

### 1. Diphtheria

**Agent:** Corynebacterium diphtheriae (Gram-positive bacillus)

**Transmission:** Respiratory droplets

**Clinical Features:**
- Pseudomembrane over tonsils/pharynx (grayish-white, bleeds on removal)
- "Bull neck" (cervical lymphadenopathy + edema)
- Toxin-mediated: Myocarditis, neuropathy

**Diagnosis:** Culture (Loeffler's medium, tellurite agar), Elek test for toxigenicity

**Treatment:**
- Diphtheria Antitoxin (DAT) - 40,000-120,000 units
- Penicillin or Erythromycin × 14 days
- Isolation, supportive care

**Prevention:** DTP/DTaP vaccine

---

### 2. Pertussis (Whooping Cough)

**Agent:** Bordetella pertussis (Gram-negative coccobacillus)

**Transmission:** Highly contagious, respiratory droplets

**Clinical Phases:**
1. **Catarrhal (1-2 weeks):** URI symptoms, most contagious
2. **Paroxysmal (2-8 weeks):** Severe coughing spells + inspiratory whoop + post-tussive vomiting
3. **Convalescent (weeks to months):** Gradual recovery

**Infants:** Apnea without whoop ("apneic pertussis")

**Diagnosis:**
- Nasopharyngeal swab: Culture (gold standard), PCR
- Lymphocytosis (absolute lymphocyte count >10,000)

**Treatment:**
- Macrolides: Azithromycin 10mg/kg × 5 days (preferred)
- Erythromycin, Clarithromycin alternatives
- Effective if given in catarrhal phase

**Chemoprophylaxis:** Same regimen for close contacts

**Prevention:** DTP/DTaP/Tdap vaccine

---

### 3. Tetanus

**Agent:** Clostridium tetani (anaerobic, Gram-positive, spore-forming)

**Source:** Soil, contaminated wounds

**Clinical Features:**
- **Trismus** (lockjaw) - Earliest sign
- **Risus sardonicus** (sardonic smile)
- **Opisthotonus** (severe arching)
- Painful muscle spasms
- Autonomic instability

**Neonatal Tetanus:**
- Day 3-14 after birth
- Inability to suck, rigidity, spasms
- Umbilical sepsis
- Very high mortality

**Treatment:**
- Human Tetanus Immunoglobulin (TIG) 500 IU IM
- Wound debridement
- Metronidazole or Penicillin × 10-14 days
- Muscle relaxants (diazepam, magnesium sulfate)
- Supportive care (quiet, dark room)

**Prevention:**
- DTP/Td vaccination
- Tetanus toxoid in pregnancy (TT-1, TT-2)
- Wound care and appropriate prophylaxis

---

### 4. Poliomyelitis

**Agent:** Poliovirus (Types 1, 2, 3) - Enterovirus

**Transmission:** Fecal-oral

**Clinical Forms:**
- Asymptomatic (95%)
- Abortive polio (minor illness)
- Non-paralytic (aseptic meningitis)
- **Paralytic polio:** Flaccid, asymmetric, lower limb predominant

**Diagnosis:** Stool culture, serology

**Treatment:** Supportive (no antiviral)

**Prevention:**
- OPV (live attenuated) - Mucosal immunity, easy to administer
- IPV (inactivated) - No VAPP risk, better seroconversion

**Global Eradication:** Wild poliovirus Type 2 and 3 eradicated. Type 1 endemic in Pakistan, Afghanistan.

---

### 5. Tuberculosis (BCG-Preventable)

**BCG Vaccine:**
- Live attenuated *Mycobacterium bovis*
- Given at birth
- Prevents severe forms: TB meningitis, miliary TB
- Does NOT prevent pulmonary TB reliably

**Contraindications:**
- Symptomatic HIV
- Immunodeficiency

---

### 6. Hepatitis B

**Agent:** Hepatitis B virus (DNA virus)

**Transmission:** Blood, body fluids, vertical (mother to child)

**Complications:** Chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma

**Prevention:**
- Birth dose within 24 hours (prevents vertical transmission)
- 3-dose series: 0, 6 weeks, 14 weeks (or 0, 1, 6 months)

**Post-Exposure (Infant of HBsAg+ mother):**
- HepB vaccine + HBIG within 12 hours of birth

---

### 7. Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib)

**Diseases Prevented:**
- Meningitis (most important)
- Epiglottitis
- Pneumonia
- Septic arthritis

**Vaccine:** Conjugate vaccine (PRP-T, PRP-OMP)

**Schedule:** 6, 10, 14 weeks + booster at 15-18 months

**Impact:** Near elimination of Hib meningitis in vaccinated populations

---

### 8. Pneumococcal Disease

**Diseases Prevented:**
- Pneumonia
- Meningitis
- Otitis media
- Bacteremia

**Vaccines:**
- **PCV13 (Conjugate):** For children <5 years
- **PPSV23 (Polysaccharide):** For high-risk children ≥2 years

**Schedule:** 6, 10, 14 weeks + booster at 15 months

---

### 9. Rotavirus

**Disease:** Severe gastroenteritis

**Vaccines (India):**
- **Rotavac:** 3 doses (6, 10, 14 weeks)
- **Rotasiil:** 3 doses
- **Rotarix:** 2 doses

**Administration:** Oral

**Key Point:** Complete series before 8 months (1st dose before 15 weeks)

---

### 10. Japanese Encephalitis (JE)

**Endemic Areas:** Eastern UP, Bihar, Assam, West Bengal, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh

**Vaccine:** Live attenuated (SA 14-14-2)

**Schedule:** 9-12 months, booster at 16-24 months

**Clinical:** Acute encephalitis, high mortality, neurological sequelae

## Vaccine Types Summary

| Type | Examples | Key Features |
|------|----------|--------------|
| Live Attenuated | BCG, OPV, MMR, Rotavirus, Varicella | Strong immunity, CI in immunocompromised |
| Inactivated | IPV, Hepatitis A, Influenza | Safer, may need boosters |
| Subunit/Conjugate | Hib, PCV, MenACWY | Safe, excellent for young children |
| Toxoid | DTP (D and T components) | Neutralizes toxin |
| mRNA | COVID-19 | Newer technology |

## Contraindications

**True Contraindications:**
- Anaphylaxis to previous dose or vaccine component
- Severe immunodeficiency (for live vaccines)
- Pregnancy (for live vaccines)

**NOT Contraindications:**
- Minor illness with or without fever
- Current antibiotic therapy
- Recent exposure to infectious disease
- Prematurity (vaccinate at chronological age)
- Breastfeeding
- Family history of adverse events
- Malnutrition

## High-Yield for Exams

| Disease | Key Feature | Vaccine |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Diphtheria | Pseudomembrane, bull neck | DTP |
| Pertussis | Paroxysmal cough, whoop | DTP |
| Tetanus | Trismus, opisthotonus | TT/DTP |
| Polio | Flaccid asymmetric paralysis | OPV/IPV |
| Measles | Koplik spots, 3 Cs | MMR/MR |
| Hib | Meningitis, epiglottitis | Hib conjugate |
| Rotavirus | Severe watery diarrhea | Oral vaccine |

**MCQ Pearls:**
- ❗ Birth dose vaccines: BCG, OPV-0, Hep B
- ❗ Herd immunity for measles: 95%
- ❗ Pertussis most contagious in catarrhal phase
- ❗ Neonatal tetanus = Umbilical infection + unvaccinated mother
- ❗ OPV vs IPV: OPV gives mucosal immunity, IPV has no VAPP risk
- ❗ BCG does NOT prevent pulmonary TB, only severe disseminated forms
- ❗ HepB birth dose prevents vertical transmission
- ❗ Live vaccines contraindicated in immunocompromised
- ❗ Minor illness is NOT a contraindication for vaccination
