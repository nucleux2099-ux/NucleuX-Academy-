## Overview

A lung abscess is a **circumscribed collection of pus within the lung parenchyma** resulting from necrosis of lung tissue, most commonly caused by **aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions** containing anaerobic bacteria. It presents as a cavitary lesion on imaging with an air-fluid level.

## Key Points

- **Aspiration** (alcoholism, seizures, poor dentition, impaired consciousness) is the most common cause
- **Anaerobes** (Bacteroides, Fusobacterium, Peptostreptococcus) are the most common organisms
- Most common location: **Right lower lobe posterior segment** (gravity-dependent aspiration)
- Chest X-ray: **Cavitary lesion with air-fluid level**
- Treatment: **Prolonged antibiotics** (4-6 weeks minimum)
- Clindamycin or Amoxicillin-clavulanate are first-line
- Surgical drainage rarely needed (most respond to antibiotics + postural drainage)

## Definition & Classification

| Feature | Primary | Secondary |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Aspiration, pneumonia | Obstruction (tumor), septic emboli |
| **Organisms** | Anaerobes | Mixed, may include aerobes |
| **Response** | Usually responds to antibiotics | May need intervention |

| Duration | Classification |
|----------|---------------|
| <4-6 weeks | **Acute** |
| >6 weeks | **Chronic** |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Aspiration** (most common) — alcoholism, stroke, seizures, general anesthesia, bulbar palsy
- **Necrotizing pneumonia** — S. aureus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas
- **Post-obstructive** — bronchial carcinoma (consider in elderly, non-resolving abscess)
- **Septic emboli** — right-sided endocarditis, IVDU
- **Extension** — subphrenic abscess, liver abscess (amoebic)
- **Hematogenous** — distant septic foci

### Microbiology
| Source | Common Organisms |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Aspiration** | Anaerobes (Bacteroides, Fusobacterium, Peptostreptococcus) |
| **Nosocomial** | S. aureus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas |
| **Immunocompromised** | Nocardia, fungi, Mycobacteria |
| **Amoebic** | Entamoeba histolytica (anchovy sauce pus) |

### Pathogenesis
1. Aspiration/infection → pneumonitis
2. Suppuration → tissue necrosis
3. Central liquefaction → **abscess cavity**
4. Erosion into bronchus → air entry → **air-fluid level**
5. Expectoration of necrotic material → copious foul-smelling sputum

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **High fever** with chills and rigors
- **Cough** — initially dry, then **copious foul-smelling purulent sputum** (when abscess drains into bronchus)
- Hemoptysis
- **Pleuritic chest pain**
- Weight loss, anorexia, night sweats (chronic abscess — mimics TB/malignancy)

### Signs
- Fever, toxic appearance
- Poor dental hygiene (risk factor clue)
- Clubbing (if chronic)
- Reduced breath sounds over abscess
- Amphoric breathing (over large cavity)
- Signs of consolidation

## Diagnosis

### Chest X-ray
- **Thick-walled cavitary lesion** with **air-fluid level** (pathognomonic)
- Location: Right lower lobe posterior segment (aspiration)
- Surrounding consolidation

### CT Chest
- Better delineation of cavity
- Differentiate from empyema (abscess = round; empyema = lenticular, splits pleura)
- Detect underlying malignancy or foreign body
- Guide drainage if needed

### Sputum
- Culture (aerobic + anaerobic)
- AFB (rule out TB)
- Cytology (rule out malignancy)
- Gram stain

### Other Investigations
- CBC: Leukocytosis, elevated ESR/CRP
- Blood cultures (if septic)
- Bronchoscopy: If post-obstructive abscess suspected (to exclude malignancy) or non-resolving abscess

### Differentials of Cavitary Lung Lesion
| Cause | Clues |
|-------|-------|
| Lung abscess | Air-fluid level, thick wall, foul sputum |
| TB cavity | Upper lobe, thin wall, acid-fast bacilli |
| Lung cancer | Irregular thick wall, no air-fluid level |
| Wegener (GPA) | Multiple bilateral, ANCA positive |
| Septic emboli | Multiple peripheral, IVDU |

## Management

### Antibiotics (Mainstay)
- **Clindamycin** 600mg IV TDS → oral (excellent anaerobic + lung penetration)
- OR **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (alternative)
- **Metronidazole** — NOT as monotherapy (poor coverage of microaerophilic streptococci); use with penicillin
- Duration: **4-6 weeks minimum** (until cavity resolves or stabilizes)

### Supportive
- **Postural drainage** (position patient with abscess dependent to promote drainage)
- Nutritional support
- Treat underlying risk factors (dental care, alcohol cessation)

### Interventional (if medical therapy fails)
- **CT-guided percutaneous drainage** — for large/non-resolving abscess
- **Bronchoscopic drainage**
- **Surgical resection** (lobectomy) — massive hemoptysis, suspected malignancy, failed medical therapy

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Empyema** (abscess ruptures into pleural space)
- **Bronchopleural fistula**
- **Massive hemoptysis** (erosion into vessel)
- **Brain abscess** (hematogenous spread — rare)
- **Sepsis**

### Prognosis
- **Primary abscess:** 90% resolve with antibiotics alone
- **Secondary abscess** (obstruction): Prognosis depends on underlying cause
- Mortality: ~5% (primary); higher in immunocompromised, large abscess, delayed treatment

> **Clinical Pearl:** A non-resolving lung abscess in an elderly smoker should always raise suspicion for **underlying bronchial carcinoma**. Bronchoscopy is mandatory.
