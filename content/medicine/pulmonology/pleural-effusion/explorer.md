## Overview

Pleural effusion is an **abnormal accumulation of fluid in the pleural space** between the visceral and parietal pleura. It is classified as **transudative** (systemic factors — protein-poor) or **exudative** (local/pleural factors — protein-rich) based on **Light's criteria**. It is a very common clinical finding with diverse etiologies.

## Key Points

- **Heart failure** is the most common cause of transudative effusion
- **Pneumonia (parapneumonic)** and **malignancy** are the most common exudative causes
- **Light's criteria** differentiates transudate from exudate (any ONE criterion = exudate)
- **Stony dull** percussion note is characteristic
- **Diagnostic thoracentesis** should be performed for all new unilateral effusions
- **Chest X-ray:** Blunting of costophrenic angle (>200 ml needed for detection)
- **Ultrasound** is the best bedside tool and guides safe thoracentesis
- Management: Treat underlying cause + drainage if symptomatic

## Definition & Classification

### Light's Criteria (Exudate if ANY ONE present)

| Criterion | Exudate |
|-----------|---------|
| Pleural fluid protein / Serum protein | > 0.5 |
| Pleural fluid LDH / Serum LDH | > 0.6 |
| Pleural fluid LDH | > 2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH |

### Causes

| Transudative | Exudative |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Heart failure** (most common) | **Parapneumonic / Empyema** |
| Cirrhosis (hepatic hydrothorax) | **Malignancy** (lung, breast, lymphoma) |
| Nephrotic syndrome | **Tuberculosis** (common in India) |
| Hypoalbuminemia | Pulmonary embolism |
| Hypothyroidism | Autoimmune (RA, SLE) |
| Constrictive pericarditis | Pancreatitis (left-sided, high amylase) |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Mechanisms of Fluid Accumulation
- **Increased hydrostatic pressure** (heart failure)
- **Decreased oncotic pressure** (nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis)
- **Increased capillary permeability** (infection, inflammation)
- **Impaired lymphatic drainage** (malignancy, TB)
- **Transdiaphragmatic** (ascites, pancreatitis)

### TB Pleural Effusion (Important in India)
- **Lymphocyte-predominant exudate** with high ADA (>40 U/L)
- Usually unilateral, moderate
- Biopsy shows **caseating granulomas** (most sensitive diagnostic method)
- Respond to ATT

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnea** (proportional to effusion size)
- **Pleuritic chest pain** (inflammation of parietal pleura — exudative)
- Cough (dry)
- Symptoms of underlying cause (orthopnea in HF, weight loss in malignancy)

### Signs
- **Decreased chest expansion** on affected side
- **Stony dull percussion** note
- **Decreased or absent breath sounds**
- **Decreased vocal resonance/fremitus**
- **Mediastinal shift** to opposite side (if large effusion)
- **Tracheal deviation** away from effusion (large)
- **Egophony** (E→A change) at upper border of effusion

## Diagnosis

### Imaging
- **Chest X-ray (PA):** Blunting of costophrenic angle (needs >200 ml), meniscus sign, opacification
- **Lateral decubitus X-ray:** Confirms free-flowing fluid (layers dependently)
- **Ultrasound:** Most sensitive; guides thoracentesis; detects loculations
- **CT chest:** Complex effusions, empyema, underlying malignancy, loculations

### Thoracentesis (Diagnostic)
Perform for all new, clinically significant, unilateral effusions.

**Routine Tests:**
| Test | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| **Protein, LDH** | Light's criteria (transudate vs exudate) |
| **Cell count + differential** | Neutrophils (bacterial), Lymphocytes (TB, malignancy) |
| **Glucose** | Low: Empyema, RA, malignancy, TB |
| **pH** | <7.2: Empyema, complicated parapneumonic |
| **Cytology** | Malignant cells (60-70% sensitivity) |
| **ADA** | >40 U/L: TB (in high-prevalence settings) |
| **Culture + Gram stain** | Infection |
| **Amylase** | Elevated: Pancreatitis, esophageal rupture |
| **Triglycerides** | >110 mg/dL: Chylothorax |

### Pleural Biopsy
- **Closed (Abrams) needle biopsy:** TB (granulomas + culture — most sensitive)
- **CT-guided biopsy:** Malignancy
- **Thoracoscopy (VATS):** Gold standard for unexplained exudative effusion

## Management

### General Principles
- **Treat the underlying cause** (most important)
- **Therapeutic thoracentesis** for symptomatic relief (limit to 1-1.5L at a time to avoid re-expansion pulmonary edema)

### Specific Management

| Cause | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Heart failure** | Diuretics (usually bilateral, no need for thoracentesis unless refractory) |
| **Parapneumonic (simple)** | Antibiotics alone |
| **Complicated parapneumonic / Empyema** | Chest tube drainage + antibiotics ± fibrinolytics (tPA + DNase) |
| **TB** | ATT (6-9 months); steroids controversial |
| **Malignant** | Repeated thoracentesis, indwelling pleural catheter, or **talc pleurodesis** |
| **Chylothorax** | Conservative (MCT diet, TPN), thoracic duct ligation if refractory |

### Empyema Management
- **Stage 1 (Exudative):** Antibiotics + aspiration
- **Stage 2 (Fibrinopurulent):** Chest tube + antibiotics ± intrapleural fibrinolytics
- **Stage 3 (Organized):** VATS decortication or open surgery

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Empyema** (infected parapneumonic effusion)
- **Trapped lung** (fibrous peel prevents expansion)
- **Re-expansion pulmonary edema** (rapid drainage of large effusion)
- **Fibrothorax** (chronic organized effusion)
- **Respiratory failure** (massive effusion)

### Prognosis
- Depends on underlying cause
- Parapneumonic: Excellent with timely antibiotics/drainage
- TB: Excellent with ATT
- Malignant: Poor (median survival 4-12 months); prognosis reflects primary cancer

> **Clinical Pearl:** In India, the three most common causes of exudative pleural effusion are TB, malignancy, and parapneumonic effusion. ADA >40 with lymphocytic effusion in a young patient is virtually diagnostic of TB.
