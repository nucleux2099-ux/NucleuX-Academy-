# Pneumonia — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **Streptococcus pneumoniae** = #1 cause of CAP in all age groups
- ★ **Mycoplasma pneumoniae** = #1 atypical pneumonia; common in young adults ("walking pneumonia")
- ★ **Klebsiella pneumoniae** = "currant jelly sputum"; common in alcoholics/diabetics; upper lobe cavitating
- ★ **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** = #1 cause of HAP/VAP; treat with anti-pseudomonal agents
- ★ **Legionella** = contaminated water/AC; hyponatremia + GI symptoms + high fever; urine antigen test
- ★ **CURB-65** score for severity: Confusion, Urea>40, RR≥30, BP<90/60, Age≥65 (≥3 = severe → ICU)
- ★ CAP treatment: **Amoxicillin** (outpatient mild) or **Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin** (inpatient)
- ★ HAP (>48h after admission): Broad-spectrum — **Piperacillin-tazobactam** or **Meropenem + Vancomycin** (if MRSA risk)
- ★ **PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii)** — HIV with CD4 <200; bilateral ground-glass on CT; TMP-SMX treatment
- ★ **Aspiration pneumonia** — right lower lobe (upright) or right upper lobe posterior segment (supine); anaerobes

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A chronic alcoholic presents with high fever, productive cough with "currant jelly" sputum, and CXR showing right upper lobe consolidation with bulging fissure. The most likely organism is:

a) Streptococcus pneumoniae
b) Klebsiella pneumoniae
c) Staphylococcus aureus
d) Mycobacterium tuberculosis

**Answer: b) Klebsiella pneumoniae**

*Classic triad: Alcoholic + currant jelly sputum + upper lobe cavitating pneumonia with bulging fissure sign (heavy consolidation). Klebsiella has a thick mucoid capsule. Treat: 3rd gen cephalosporin + aminoglycoside. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 126*

### MCQ 2
A 22-year-old college student has dry cough, low-grade fever, headache, and bilateral patchy infiltrates on CXR. Cold agglutinin test is positive. The most likely organism is:

a) Streptococcus pneumoniae
b) Legionella pneumophila
c) Mycoplasma pneumoniae
d) Chlamydia pneumoniae

**Answer: c) Mycoplasma pneumoniae**

*Young adult + dry cough + bilateral infiltrates + cold agglutinins = classic Mycoplasma ("walking pneumonia"). Complications: erythema multiforme, bullous myringitis, hemolytic anemia (cold agglutinins = IgM against I antigen on RBCs). Treat: Azithromycin/Doxycycline. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 185*

### MCQ 3
An HIV+ patient with CD4 count 80 presents with progressive dyspnea and bilateral ground-glass opacities on HRCT. LDH is markedly elevated. The most likely diagnosis and treatment?

a) Tuberculosis; ATT
b) PCP pneumonia; TMP-SMX
c) CMV pneumonitis; Ganciclovir
d) Cryptococcal pneumonia; Amphotericin B

**Answer: b) PCP pneumonia; TMP-SMX**

*PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii): CD4 <200, bilateral GGO, elevated LDH, hypoxemia. Silver methenamine stain shows cysts. Treatment: TMP-SMX (21 days) + steroids if PaO2 <70. Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX when CD4 <200. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 213*

### MCQ 4
A patient in ICU develops pneumonia on day 5 of mechanical ventilation. Gram stain shows gram-negative rods. The most appropriate empiric antibiotic is:

a) Amoxicillin-clavulanate
b) Azithromycin
c) Piperacillin-tazobactam
d) Amoxicillin

**Answer: c) Piperacillin-tazobactam**

*VAP (>48h of intubation): Most common organism is Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Empiric therapy needs anti-pseudomonal coverage: pip-taz, cefepime, meropenem, or imipenem. Add vancomycin/linezolid if MRSA risk. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 126*

### MCQ 5
CURB-65 score of 4 indicates:

a) Outpatient management with oral antibiotics
b) Short hospitalization with IV antibiotics
c) ICU admission and aggressive management
d) No antibiotic needed

**Answer: c) ICU admission and aggressive management**

*CURB-65: 0-1 = outpatient; 2 = consider hospitalization; ≥3 = severe CAP, consider ICU. Score ≥4 has mortality >25%. Components: Confusion, Urea >40mg/dL (>7mmol/L), RR ≥30, BP <90/60, Age ≥65. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 126*
