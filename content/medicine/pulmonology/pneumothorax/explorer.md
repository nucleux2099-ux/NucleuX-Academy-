## Overview

Pneumothorax is the presence of **air in the pleural space** causing partial or complete lung collapse. It can be **spontaneous** (primary or secondary), **traumatic**, or **iatrogenic**. **Tension pneumothorax** is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate decompression.

## Key Points

- **Primary spontaneous:** Tall, thin, young males; ruptured apical subpleural blebs
- **Secondary spontaneous:** Underlying lung disease (COPD with bullae most common)
- **Tension pneumothorax:** One-way valve mechanism → mediastinal shift → cardiovascular collapse
- Chest X-ray: **Absent lung markings** with **visible visceral pleural line**
- **Tension pneumothorax** is a **clinical diagnosis** — do NOT wait for X-ray
- Emergency: **Needle decompression** (2nd ICS MCL) → chest tube (5th ICS MAL)
- Small primary: Observation or aspiration; large/symptomatic: Chest drain
- **Avoid flying** and **diving** until resolved (risk of expansion)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Cause | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Primary spontaneous** | Rupture of apical subpleural blebs | Young, tall, thin males; no lung disease |
| **Secondary spontaneous** | Underlying lung disease | COPD, TB, CF, ILD, PCP |
| **Traumatic** | Blunt/penetrating chest injury | Rib fractures, stab wounds |
| **Iatrogenic** | Medical procedures | Central line insertion, mechanical ventilation, thoracentesis, lung biopsy |
| **Tension** | One-way valve mechanism | Any cause; rapidly fatal if untreated |
| **Catamenial** | Related to menstruation | Thoracic endometriosis; right-sided, recurrent |

### Size Classification (BTS Guidelines)
- **Small:** <2 cm between lung margin and chest wall at hilar level
- **Large:** ≥2 cm

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Primary spontaneous:** Rupture of apical blebs (tall thin males, smokers)
- **Secondary:** COPD (most common), TB (especially with cavitation), asthma, CF, PCP, ILD, lung cancer, Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos
- **Iatrogenic:** CVC insertion (subclavian), positive pressure ventilation, thoracentesis, transbronchial biopsy
- **Traumatic:** Rib fractures, penetrating injury

### Pathophysiology
1. Air enters pleural space through visceral (or parietal) pleural breach
2. Loss of negative intrapleural pressure → lung recoil → **lung collapse**
3. Ventilation-perfusion mismatch → hypoxemia
4. **Tension pneumothorax:** One-way valve → air enters on inspiration, cannot exit on expiration
   - Progressive air accumulation → increasing intrathoracic pressure
   - **Mediastinal shift** → compression of contralateral lung + great vessels
   - **Reduced venous return → decreased cardiac output → obstructive shock**

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Sudden-onset pleuritic chest pain** (sharp, ipsilateral)
- **Dyspnea** (proportional to size)
- May be asymptomatic (small primary)

### Signs
- Tachypnea, tachycardia
- **Reduced expansion** on affected side
- **Hyperresonant percussion**
- **Absent or reduced breath sounds**
- **Reduced vocal resonance/fremitus**

### Tension Pneumothorax — Emergency Signs
- Severe respiratory distress, cyanosis
- **Tracheal deviation** (away from affected side)
- **Distended neck veins** (impaired venous return)
- **Hypotension → cardiovascular collapse**
- Absent breath sounds on affected side
- **This is a clinical diagnosis — act immediately, do NOT wait for imaging**

## Diagnosis

### Chest X-ray (Erect, Inspiratory)
- **Visible visceral pleural line** with absent lung markings beyond it
- **Lung collapse** toward hilum
- **Deep sulcus sign** (in supine X-ray — air collects anteriorly)
- **Mediastinal shift** (tension pneumothorax)

### CT Chest
- Most sensitive — detects small pneumothoraces missed on X-ray
- Useful for secondary pneumothorax (identify underlying disease)
- Not needed for most primary spontaneous

### Ultrasound (Bedside)
- **Absent lung sliding** — highly sensitive
- **Barcode/stratosphere sign** on M-mode (instead of normal "seashore" sign)
- **Absent lung pulse**
- Rapid bedside diagnosis in trauma/ICU

## Management

### Tension Pneumothorax — Immediate
1. **Needle decompression:** Large-bore needle (14-16G), **2nd intercostal space, mid-clavicular line**
2. **Chest tube insertion** (definitive): **5th intercostal space, mid-axillary line** (safe triangle)
3. High-flow oxygen

### Primary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (BTS)

| Size | Symptoms | Management |
|------|----------|------------|
| Small (<2cm) | Minimal | **Observation** + repeat X-ray in 2 weeks |
| Small (<2cm) | Symptomatic | **Aspiration** → if successful, discharge |
| Large (≥2cm) | Any | **Aspiration** first → if fails, **chest drain** |

### Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax
- More aggressive approach (underlying lung disease limits reserve)
- Small (1-2cm) + minimal symptoms → aspiration
- Large or symptomatic → **chest drain** (intercostal tube with underwater seal)
- **All secondary pneumothoraces require hospital admission**

### Chest Drain Management
- **Underwater seal drainage** (bubbling = ongoing air leak)
- **Do NOT clamp** chest drain (risk of tension)
- Remove when: No air leak for 24 hrs + lung fully expanded on X-ray

### Preventing Recurrence
- **Recurrence rate:** 30-50% for primary spontaneous
- **Chemical pleurodesis** (talc) — via chest drain
- **VATS (Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery):** Bleb resection + pleurodesis — gold standard for recurrence prevention
- Indications for surgery: Second ipsilateral episode, first contralateral episode, bilateral, persistent air leak >5 days

### Advice
- **No flying** until radiological resolution confirmed (risk of expansion at altitude)
- **No diving** unless bilateral pleurectomy performed
- **Smoking cessation** — reduces recurrence by 40%

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Tension pneumothorax** (fatal if untreated)
- **Recurrence** (30-50% for primary; higher for secondary)
- **Re-expansion pulmonary edema** (after rapid re-expansion of collapsed lung)
- **Persistent air leak** (bronchopleural fistula)
- **Surgical emphysema** (subcutaneous)
- **Hemopneumothorax** (blood + air)

### Prognosis
- Primary spontaneous: Excellent; main concern is recurrence
- Secondary: Depends on underlying disease
- Tension: Fatal within minutes if not decompressed

> **Clinical Pearl:** Tension pneumothorax is a CLINICAL diagnosis. Do not send the patient for an X-ray — needle decompression should be performed immediately based on clinical findings (absent breath sounds + tracheal deviation + hypotension).
