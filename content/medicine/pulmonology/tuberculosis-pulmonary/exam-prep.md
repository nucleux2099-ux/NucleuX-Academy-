# Tuberculosis — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **Ghon focus** = primary parenchymal lesion; **Ghon complex** = Ghon focus + hilar lymphadenopathy; **Ranke complex** = calcified Ghon complex
- ★ **Reactivation TB** = upper lobe apical/posterior segments (high O2)
- ★ **CBNAAT/GeneXpert** = initial test for all presumptive TB (2-hr result + rifampicin resistance)
- ★ **Pyrazinamide** = most hepatotoxic ATT; works in acidic pH (kills intracellular bacilli in macrophages)
- ★ **Rifampicin** = most important drug; potent CYP450 inducer (↓ OCP, warfarin, HIV drugs efficacy)
- ★ **INH** → peripheral neuropathy (prevent with pyridoxine); slow acetylators at higher risk
- ★ **Ethambutol** → optic neuritis (red-green color blindness); test visual acuity before starting
- ★ **Mantoux ≥10mm** = positive in most; **≥5mm** = positive in HIV, close contacts, immunosuppressed
- ★ **TB pleural effusion:** Exudative, lymphocytic, ADA >40 U/L, low glucose
- ★ **Pott's spine:** Lower thoracic; paradiscal destruction; cold abscess; NO disc destruction (vs pyogenic)

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A patient on ATT for 2 months develops decreased visual acuity and red-green color blindness. The offending drug is:

a) Isoniazid
b) Rifampicin
c) Ethambutol
d) Pyrazinamide

**Answer: c) Ethambutol**

*Ethambutol causes retrobulbar optic neuritis (dose-dependent, usually reversible). Presents as decreased visual acuity + red-green color blindness. Must test visual acuity before starting and monthly during treatment. Stop immediately if symptoms occur. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 179*

### MCQ 2
A 30-year-old HIV+ patient (CD4 150) is started on ATT. After 2 weeks of ART initiation, he develops high fever, worsening lymphadenopathy, and new pulmonary infiltrates. The most likely diagnosis is:

a) Drug-resistant TB
b) Treatment failure
c) Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)
d) Superinfection with Pneumocystis

**Answer: c) Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)**

*Paradoxical IRIS occurs weeks after ART initiation — immune recovery unmasks/worsens TB inflammation. Management: continue ATT + ART; add corticosteroids if severe. More common with low baseline CD4. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 179*

### MCQ 3
The most common site of extrapulmonary TB is:

a) Meninges
b) Lymph nodes
c) Spine
d) Intestine

**Answer: b) Lymph nodes**

*Lymph node TB (scrofula) is the most common form of EPTB. Typically cervical nodes — matted, non-tender, cold abscess, may have sinus formation. Diagnosis: FNAC showing epithelioid granulomas + caseation. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 179*

---

### MCQ 4
Which ATT drug requires dose adjustment with the shortest intensive phase activity at acidic intracellular pH?

a) Isoniazid
b) Rifampicin
c) Pyrazinamide
d) Ethambutol

**Answer: c) Pyrazinamide**

*Pyrazinamide is uniquely active at acidic pH inside macrophages — kills semi-dormant intracellular bacilli. This is why it's used only in the intensive phase (first 2 months). It's also the most hepatotoxic ATT drug and causes hyperuricemia. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 179*

---

### MCQ 5
MDR-TB is defined as resistance to:

a) Isoniazid only
b) Isoniazid + Rifampicin
c) All first-line drugs
d) Isoniazid + Ethambutol

**Answer: b) Isoniazid + Rifampicin**

*MDR-TB = resistance to at least INH + Rifampicin (the two most potent first-line drugs). XDR-TB = MDR + resistance to fluoroquinolone + one injectable (or bedaquiline/linezolid per updated WHO definition). Pre-XDR = MDR + fluoroquinolone resistance. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 179*
