## Overview

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a **progressive, largely irreversible** airflow limitation caused by chronic inflammation of the airways and lung parenchyma, primarily due to **cigarette smoking**. It encompasses **chronic bronchitis** (airway disease) and **emphysema** (parenchymal destruction).

## Key Points

- **Smoking** is the cause in 80-90% of cases
- Defined by **post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC < 0.70** on spirometry
- **GOLD staging** based on FEV1: GOLD 1 (mild) to GOLD 4 (very severe)
- **ABCD assessment** combines symptoms (mMRC/CAT) and exacerbation history
- **Smoking cessation** is the ONLY intervention that slows FEV1 decline
- **Long-acting bronchodilators (LAMA/LABA)** are the mainstay of maintenance
- **ICS** only with frequent exacerbations + eosinophilia; increases pneumonia risk
- **LTOT (Long-term oxygen therapy)** improves survival if PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg

## Definition & Classification

### COPD Subtypes

| Feature | Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater") | Emphysema ("Pink Puffer") |
|---------|-------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Definition** | Cough + sputum ≥3 months/year × 2 years | Permanent enlargement of airspaces distal to terminal bronchioles |
| **Body type** | Obese, cyanosed | Thin, barrel chest |
| **Cough** | Productive | Minimal |
| **Dyspnea** | Mild-moderate | Severe |
| **Cor pulmonale** | Early | Late |
| **PaCO₂** | Elevated | Normal/low |

### GOLD Severity Classification (Post-BD FEV1)

| GOLD Stage | FEV1 (% predicted) |
|------------|-------------------|
| **1 — Mild** | ≥80% |
| **2 — Moderate** | 50-79% |
| **3 — Severe** | 30-49% |
| **4 — Very severe** | <30% |

### GOLD ABCD Assessment (Combined)

| Group | Symptoms | Exacerbation History |
|-------|----------|---------------------|
| **A** | Low (mMRC 0-1, CAT <10) | 0-1 (no hospitalization) |
| **B** | High (mMRC ≥2, CAT ≥10) | 0-1 (no hospitalization) |
| **C** | Low | ≥2 or ≥1 hospitalization |
| **D/E** | High | ≥2 or ≥1 hospitalization |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Cigarette smoking** (most important — 80-90%)
- Biomass fuel exposure (significant in India — cooking with wood/dung)
- Occupational exposure (coal dust, silica, grain)
- **α1-antitrypsin deficiency** (young, non-smoker, basal emphysema, liver disease)
- Air pollution
- Childhood respiratory infections

### Pathophysiology
- **Chronic bronchitis:** Mucus gland hyperplasia (Reid index >0.5), goblet cell metaplasia, mucus hypersecretion, airway wall thickening
- **Emphysema:** Protease-antiprotease imbalance → alveolar wall destruction → loss of elastic recoil → air trapping
  - **Centriacinar** (centrilobular) — upper lobes, smoking-related
  - **Panacinar** (panlobular) — lower lobes, α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  - **Paraseptal** — peripheral, subpleural → spontaneous pneumothorax

### Pathogenesis
1. Smoking/irritants → recruitment of neutrophils and macrophages
2. Release of **proteases (elastase, MMP)** > antiproteases (α1-AT)
3. Oxidative stress + protease-antiprotease imbalance
4. Alveolar destruction (emphysema) + airway remodeling (bronchitis)
5. **Air trapping** → hyperinflation → flattened diaphragm → ↓ breathing efficiency

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Progressive dyspnea** (initially exertional → at rest)
- Chronic cough (with or without sputum)
- **Wheeze**
- Reduced exercise tolerance
- **Acute exacerbations:** Increased dyspnea, sputum volume, sputum purulence

### Signs
- **Barrel chest** (hyperinflation)
- Use of accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid, scalene)
- **Pursed-lip breathing** (auto-PEEP)
- Reduced chest expansion
- Hyperresonant percussion
- **Decreased breath sounds** with prolonged expiration
- Wheeze
- **Cyanosis** (in chronic bronchitis)
- Cor pulmonale signs: Elevated JVP, pedal edema, loud P2

## Diagnosis

### Spirometry (Essential for Diagnosis)
- **Post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC < 0.70** confirms COPD
- **Poor bronchodilator reversibility** (<12% or <200ml) — differentiates from asthma
- FEV1 decline: ~30-60 ml/year in COPD (vs ~25-30 ml/year normal)

### Other Investigations
- **Chest X-ray:** Hyperinflation, flat diaphragm, increased AP diameter, bullae, tubular heart
- **HRCT:** Emphysema quantification, bronchial wall thickening, bullae
- **ABG:** Hypoxemia ± hypercapnia (Type 2 respiratory failure)
- **α1-antitrypsin level:** All patients <45 years or with basal emphysema
- **CBC:** Polycythemia (chronic hypoxia)
- **BNP:** If cor pulmonale suspected
- **Sputum culture:** During exacerbations

### Differentiating COPD from Asthma

| Feature | COPD | Asthma |
|---------|------|--------|
| Age of onset | >40 years | Childhood/young adult |
| Smoking | Usually | Variable |
| Reversibility | Minimal | Significant (>12%) |
| Diurnal variation | Minimal | Marked |
| Atopy/allergy | Uncommon | Common |
| Response to ICS | Limited | Excellent |

## Management

### Smoking Cessation (Most Important Intervention)
- Counseling + **pharmacotherapy:** Nicotine replacement, Varenicline, Bupropion
- Only intervention proven to **slow FEV1 decline**

### Stable COPD — Pharmacotherapy

| GOLD Group | Initial Therapy |
|------------|----------------|
| **A** | Bronchodilator (SABA or LAMA or LABA) |
| **B** | LABA or LAMA |
| **E** | LABA + LAMA (± ICS if eosinophils ≥300) |

### Drug Classes

| Class | Examples | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **SABA** | Salbutamol, Terbutaline | Rescue use |
| **SAMA** | Ipratropium | Rescue use |
| **LABA** | Formoterol, Salmeterol, Indacaterol | 12-24 hr duration |
| **LAMA** | Tiotropium, Glycopyrronium | **Most effective single agent** |
| **ICS** | Budesonide, Fluticasone | Only with frequent exacerbations + eosinophils ≥300 |
| **PDE4 inhibitor** | Roflumilast | FEV1 <50% + frequent exacerbations |

### Non-Pharmacological
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation** (improves exercise capacity and quality of life)
- **Vaccination:** Influenza (annual), pneumococcal, COVID-19
- **LTOT (Long-term O₂ therapy):** ≥15 hrs/day if PaO₂ ≤55 or ≤59 with cor pulmonale/polycythemia — **improves survival**
- **NIV (BiPAP):** Chronic hypercapnia
- **Lung volume reduction surgery (LVRS):** Upper-lobe predominant emphysema + low exercise capacity
- **Lung transplant:** End-stage COPD

### Acute Exacerbation Management
1. **Controlled O₂** — target SpO₂ 88-92% (risk of CO₂ retention)
2. **Nebulized SABA + SAMA** (salbutamol + ipratropium)
3. **Systemic corticosteroids** (prednisolone 40mg × 5 days)
4. **Antibiotics** if purulent sputum (amoxicillin-clavulanate or doxycycline)
5. **NIV (BiPAP)** if respiratory acidosis (pH <7.35)
6. **Intubation/ventilation** if NIV fails

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Acute exacerbations** (major cause of morbidity/mortality)
- **Cor pulmonale** (right heart failure from chronic hypoxic PH)
- **Pneumothorax** (especially in emphysema with bullae)
- **Respiratory failure** (Type 2)
- **Polycythemia** (chronic hypoxia)
- **Lung cancer** (shared risk factor — smoking)
- **Depression/anxiety** (common comorbidity)

### BODE Index (Prognostic)
- **B**MI, **O**bstruction (FEV1), **D**yspnea (mMRC), **E**xercise capacity (6MWD)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In COPD exacerbations, give controlled oxygen (target SpO₂ 88-92%). High-flow oxygen can suppress hypoxic drive and worsen CO₂ retention — "oxygen kills" in COPD is a real phenomenon.
