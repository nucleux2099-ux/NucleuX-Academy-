# COPD — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **FEV1/FVC <0.70** (post-bronchodilator) = diagnostic of COPD
- ★ **Smoking** is the #1 risk factor; **Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency** = #1 genetic cause (panacinar emphysema, lower lobes, young non-smokers + liver disease)
- ★ **Emphysema** = destruction of alveolar walls (permanent airspace enlargement distal to terminal bronchioles)
- ★ **Chronic bronchitis** = productive cough for ≥3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years (clinical definition)
- ★ **Pink puffers** (emphysema) vs **Blue bloaters** (chronic bronchitis)
- ★ **GOLD classification** by FEV1: Mild ≥80%, Moderate 50-79%, Severe 30-49%, Very severe <30%
- ★ **LAMA (tiotropium)** = cornerstone of maintenance therapy
- ★ **Oxygen therapy** (if PaO2 <55 or SpO2 <88%) — ONLY intervention besides smoking cessation that improves survival
- ★ **Acute exacerbation:** Short-acting bronchodilators + systemic steroids (prednisolone 40mg × 5 days) + antibiotics if purulent sputum
- ★ **Cor pulmonale** = most common cardiac complication of COPD

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 60-year-old male smoker with progressive dyspnea has FEV1/FVC of 0.62 and FEV1 of 45% predicted (post-bronchodilator). His GOLD stage is:

a) Mild (GOLD 1)
b) Moderate (GOLD 2)
c) Severe (GOLD 3)
d) Very severe (GOLD 4)

**Answer: c) Severe (GOLD 3)**

*GOLD spirometric classification: 1 (mild) ≥80%, 2 (moderate) 50-79%, 3 (severe) 30-49%, 4 (very severe) <30%. FEV1 45% = GOLD 3. The FEV1/FVC <0.70 confirms obstruction. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 292*

### MCQ 2
A young non-smoker presents with panacinar emphysema predominantly affecting lower lobes + liver cirrhosis. The most likely diagnosis is:

a) Cystic fibrosis
b) Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
c) Hypersensitivity pneumonitis
d) Sarcoidosis

**Answer: b) Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency**

*AAT deficiency: autosomal codominant (PiZZ phenotype). Panacinar emphysema in LOWER lobes (smoking-related = centrilobular, UPPER lobes). Liver disease from accumulated abnormal AAT protein (PAS-positive globules). Screen with serum AAT level. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 292*

### MCQ 3
Which is the ONLY pharmacological intervention that improves survival in COPD (besides smoking cessation)?

a) Inhaled corticosteroids
b) Long-term oxygen therapy
c) Tiotropium
d) Theophylline

**Answer: b) Long-term oxygen therapy**

*LTOT (>15 hrs/day) improves survival in COPD patients with chronic hypoxemia (PaO2 ≤55 mmHg or SpO2 ≤88%). Two landmark trials: NOTT and MRC. Smoking cessation is the only other intervention proven to slow FEV1 decline and improve survival. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 292*

### MCQ 4
A COPD patient in acute exacerbation is given 100% oxygen. He becomes drowsy. The mechanism is:

a) CO2 narcosis from loss of hypoxic drive
b) Oxygen toxicity
c) Bronchospasm
d) Pulmonary edema

**Answer: a) CO2 narcosis from loss of hypoxic drive**

*Chronic CO2 retainers depend on hypoxic drive for ventilation. High-flow O2 suppresses this drive → hypoventilation → CO2 retention → narcosis. Also involves Haldane effect and V/Q mismatch worsening. Target SpO2 88-92% in COPD. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 292*

### MCQ 5
The most common cardiac complication of COPD is:

a) Left ventricular failure
b) Atrial fibrillation
c) Cor pulmonale
d) Pericardial effusion

**Answer: c) Cor pulmonale**

*Chronic hypoxia → pulmonary vasoconstriction → pulmonary hypertension → RV hypertrophy/failure (cor pulmonale). Signs: loud P2, RV heave, JVP elevation, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly. ECG: P-pulmonale, RAD, RVH. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 292*
