## Overview

Bronchial asthma is a **chronic inflammatory airway disease** characterized by reversible airflow obstruction, bronchial hyperresponsiveness, and airway remodeling. It presents with episodic **wheeze, dyspnea, cough, and chest tightness**, typically worse at night and triggered by allergens, exercise, or infections.

## Key Points

- **Reversible airflow obstruction** differentiates asthma from COPD
- **Type I hypersensitivity** (IgE-mediated) in allergic asthma; **eosinophilic** inflammation
- Spirometry: **FEV1/FVC <0.70** with **≥12% and ≥200ml improvement** post-bronchodilator
- **Stepwise management** (GINA guidelines): ICS is the cornerstone
- **SABA** for rescue; **ICS** for maintenance (even in mild asthma — GINA 2019 update)
- **Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF)** variability >20% is diagnostic
- **Acute severe asthma:** Silent chest, inability to speak, PEF <33%, life-threatening
- **Status asthmaticus:** Severe attack not responding to standard bronchodilators

## Definition & Classification

### Severity (Before Treatment)

| Severity | Symptoms | Night Symptoms | FEV1/PEF |
|----------|----------|---------------|----------|
| **Intermittent** | ≤2 days/week | ≤2/month | ≥80% |
| **Mild persistent** | >2 days/week | 3-4/month | ≥80% |
| **Moderate persistent** | Daily | >1/week | 60-80% |
| **Severe persistent** | Continuous | Frequent | <60% |

### GINA Step-wise Approach (Simplified)

| Step | Preferred Controller | Reliever |
|------|---------------------|----------|
| **1** | As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol | As-needed ICS-formoterol |
| **2** | Low-dose ICS daily | As-needed SABA or ICS-formoterol |
| **3** | Low-dose ICS-LABA | As-needed SABA |
| **4** | Medium-dose ICS-LABA | As-needed SABA |
| **5** | High-dose ICS-LABA + add-on (LAMA, anti-IgE, anti-IL5) | As-needed SABA |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Atopy** (strongest risk factor) — personal/family history of eczema, rhinitis, asthma
- **Genetic:** Multiple genes involved (ADAM33, IL-4, IL-13, ORMDL3)
- **Environmental:** Allergens (dust mites, pollen, pets), air pollution, tobacco smoke
- **Triggers:** Respiratory infections, exercise, cold air, occupational agents, drugs (NSAIDs, beta-blockers)

### Pathophysiology
1. **Allergen exposure** → IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins
2. **Early phase** (minutes): Bronchospasm, mucosal edema, mucus hypersecretion
3. **Late phase** (4-8 hrs): Eosinophilic inflammation, T-helper 2 (Th2) cytokines
4. **Chronic inflammation** → airway remodeling: basement membrane thickening, smooth muscle hypertrophy, goblet cell hyperplasia, subepithelial fibrosis
5. **Bronchial hyperresponsiveness** → exaggerated bronchoconstriction to triggers

### Pathology
- **Curschmann spirals** (mucus plugs)
- **Charcot-Leyden crystals** (eosinophil breakdown products)
- **Creola bodies** (shed epithelial clusters)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Asthma is predominantly a Th2-mediated eosinophilic disease. The new biologics (anti-IgE, anti-IL5, anti-IL4/13) target this pathway specifically.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Episodic wheeze** (expiratory > inspiratory)
- **Dyspnea** (with chest tightness)
- **Cough** (may be only symptom — cough-variant asthma; worse at night)
- **Diurnal variation** — worse at night/early morning
- **Trigger-related** — allergens, exercise, cold air, emotions

### Signs (During Attack)
- Tachypnea, use of accessory muscles
- **Bilateral expiratory wheeze** (polyphonic)
- Prolonged expiration
- Hyperinflated chest
- Tachycardia
- **Pulsus paradoxus** (in severe attack)

### Signs of Severity in Acute Attack

| Feature | Moderate | Severe | Life-threatening |
|---------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Speech | Sentences | Phrases/words | Unable |
| PEF | 50-75% | 33-50% | <33% |
| RR | ↑ | >25 | Variable |
| HR | <110 | >110 | Bradycardia |
| SpO₂ | >92% | <92% | <92% |
| Other | — | Accessory muscles | **Silent chest**, cyanosis, altered consciousness |

## Diagnosis

### Spirometry
- **FEV1/FVC ratio <0.70** (obstructive pattern)
- **Bronchodilator reversibility:** ≥12% AND ≥200ml improvement in FEV1 after SABA
- If normal spirometry: **Bronchial provocation test** (methacholine/histamine) — positive if PC20 <8 mg/ml

### Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF)
- **Diurnal variation >20%** (averaged over 2 weeks) is diagnostic
- Useful for self-monitoring

### Other Investigations
- **Chest X-ray:** Usually normal; hyperinflation during attack
- **Blood:** Eosinophilia, elevated total IgE
- **Skin prick tests / specific IgE:** Identify allergens
- **FeNO (fractional exhaled NO):** Elevated in eosinophilic inflammation (>50 ppb suggestive)
- **Sputum:** Eosinophils, Curschmann spirals, Charcot-Leyden crystals

### ABG in Acute Severe Asthma
- Initial: Respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation)
- Worsening: Normal pCO₂ (ominous — tiring patient)
- Critical: **Respiratory acidosis** (impending respiratory failure)

## Management

### Chronic Asthma — GINA Guidelines
- **ICS** is the cornerstone (budesonide, fluticasone, beclomethasone)
- **LABA** (formoterol, salmeterol) — always with ICS, never alone
- **LTRA** (montelukast) — adjunct, especially exercise/aspirin-induced
- **LAMA** (tiotropium) — add-on at Steps 4-5
- **Biologics** (Step 5): Omalizumab (anti-IgE), Mepolizumab/Benralizumab (anti-IL5), Dupilumab (anti-IL4/13)

### Acute Severe Asthma — Emergency Management
1. **Oxygen** — high-flow, target SpO₂ 94-98%
2. **Nebulized salbutamol** — 5mg, repeat every 20 min × 3
3. **Nebulized ipratropium** — 0.5mg with first 3 salbutamol doses
4. **IV hydrocortisone 100mg** or oral prednisolone 40-50mg
5. If life-threatening: **IV magnesium sulfate 2g** over 20 min
6. If not responding: **IV aminophylline**, consider ICU + mechanical ventilation

### Patient Education
- Inhaler technique (critical — poor technique = poor control)
- Written asthma action plan
- Trigger avoidance
- Self-monitoring with PEF

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Status asthmaticus** — fatal if untreated
- **Pneumothorax/pneumomediastinum** (air trapping)
- **Airway remodeling** → fixed obstruction (asthma-COPD overlap)
- **Growth retardation** (chronic steroid use in children)
- **Steroid complications** (oral; ICS side effects: oral candidiasis, dysphonia)

### Prognosis
- Most patients achieve good control with appropriate therapy
- Childhood asthma: ~50% remit by adulthood
- Risk of death: Highest in patients with frequent exacerbations, poor adherence, prior ICU admission

> **Clinical Pearl:** A "silent chest" in acute asthma is NOT a good sign — it means airflow is so severely reduced that no wheeze is generated. This is life-threatening and needs immediate escalation.
