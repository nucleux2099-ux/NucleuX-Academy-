# Bronchial Asthma — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **Asthma** = chronic airway inflammation + reversible airflow obstruction + bronchial hyperresponsiveness
- ★ **FEV1/FVC reduced** but **reversible** (≥12% AND ≥200mL improvement post-bronchodilator) — key difference from COPD
- ★ **Eosinophilic inflammation** — hallmark; Charcot-Leyden crystals, Curschmann spirals, Creola bodies in sputum
- ★ **GINA stepwise approach:** Step 1-2: Low-dose ICS-formoterol PRN; Step 3: Low-dose ICS-LABA; Step 4: Medium/high ICS-LABA; Step 5: Add LAMA, biologics
- ★ **ICS (inhaled corticosteroids)** = cornerstone of long-term control; LABA alone is NEVER given without ICS
- ★ **Status asthmaticus:** Silent chest = ominous sign (severe bronchospasm); PaCO2 normalizing/rising = impending respiratory failure
- ★ **Exercise-induced asthma:** Short-acting β2 agonist 15 min before exercise; LTRA (montelukast) for prevention
- ★ **Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD):** Samter's triad = asthma + nasal polyps + aspirin sensitivity
- ★ **Omalizumab** (anti-IgE) for severe allergic asthma; **Mepolizumab** (anti-IL5) for severe eosinophilic asthma
- ★ **Peak expiratory flow (PEF)** monitoring — diurnal variation >20% suggests asthma

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 20-year-old with recurrent wheeze shows FEV1 improvement of 15% and 250mL after salbutamol nebulization. This confirms:

a) COPD
b) Bronchial asthma
c) Bronchiectasis
d) Vocal cord dysfunction

**Answer: b) Bronchial asthma**

*Positive bronchodilator reversibility: ≥12% AND ≥200mL improvement in FEV1 post-bronchodilator. This is the hallmark of asthma (reversible obstruction) vs COPD (irreversible). 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 287*

### MCQ 2
A known asthmatic develops severe bronchospasm after taking aspirin. She also has nasal polyps. This triad is known as:

a) Kartagener syndrome
b) Samter's triad
c) Churg-Strauss syndrome
d) Löffler syndrome

**Answer: b) Samter's triad**

*AERD / Samter's triad: aspirin/NSAID sensitivity + asthma + nasal polyps. Mechanism: COX-1 inhibition → shunting of arachidonic acid to leukotriene pathway → bronchoconstriction. Treatment: leukotriene inhibitors (montelukast), aspirin desensitization. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 287*

### MCQ 3
In acute severe asthma, which finding indicates impending respiratory failure?

a) Tachycardia
b) Pulsus paradoxus
c) Normal or rising PaCO2
d) Wheezing

**Answer: c) Normal or rising PaCO2**

*In acute asthma, hyperventilation → low PaCO2 (respiratory alkalosis). Normal/rising PaCO2 = respiratory muscle fatigue → impending arrest. Silent chest (no wheeze) = another ominous sign. Both warrant immediate intubation consideration. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 287*

### MCQ 4
According to GINA 2023, the preferred reliever at ALL steps of asthma management is:

a) Short-acting β2 agonist (SABA) alone
b) Low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed
c) Oral theophylline
d) Ipratropium bromide

**Answer: b) Low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed**

*GINA 2023 recommends ICS-formoterol PRN as the preferred reliever for adults/adolescents at all steps (Track 1). This ensures every reliever use delivers anti-inflammatory ICS, reducing exacerbations vs SABA-only approach. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 287*

---

### MCQ 5
Which sputum finding is pathognomonic of bronchial asthma?

a) Rusty sputum
b) Curschmann spirals
c) Dittrich's plugs
d) Sulfur granules

**Answer: b) Curschmann spirals**

*Asthma sputum findings: Curschmann spirals (mucus casts of bronchioles), Charcot-Leyden crystals (eosinophil breakdown), Creola bodies (clusters of epithelial cells). Rusty = pneumococcal pneumonia; sulfur granules = actinomycosis. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 287*
