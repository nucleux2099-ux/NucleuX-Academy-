# Thyroid Disorders — Explorer

## Overview

**Thyroid disorders** are among the most commonly tested endocrine topics in NEET-PG. **Hypothyroidism** (most common: Hashimoto's thyroiditis) and **Hyperthyroidism** (most common: Graves' disease) represent opposite ends of thyroid dysfunction. Understanding the HPT axis, autoantibodies, and treatment algorithms is essential.

📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 380-381

---

## HYPOTHYROIDISM

## Key Points

- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis** = #1 cause of hypothyroidism worldwide (in iodine-sufficient areas)
- **Anti-TPO antibodies** = most sensitive marker for Hashimoto's; Anti-thyroglobulin also elevated
- **TSH elevated + free T4 low** = overt primary hypothyroidism
- **Subclinical hypothyroidism:** TSH elevated + normal T4 (treat if TSH >10 or symptomatic)
- **Myxedema coma** = severe hypothyroidism emergency → IV levothyroxine + IV hydrocortisone
- **Levothyroxine** (T4) = treatment of choice; dose 1.6 μg/kg/day; monitor with TSH at 6-8 weeks

## Clinical Features — Hypothyroidism

### Symptoms
- Fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation
- Dry skin, hair loss, brittle nails
- Menorrhagia (paradoxically), infertility
- Depression, cognitive slowing, hoarse voice

### Signs
- **Myxedema** — non-pitting edema (periorbital, hands, feet) — GAG deposition
- Bradycardia, diastolic hypertension (↑ SVR)
- **Delayed relaxation of ankle jerk** — classic exam finding
- Pericardial effusion, pleural effusion
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- **Macroglossia**

### Lab Findings
- ↑ TSH, ↓ free T4 (primary); ↓ TSH, ↓ T4 (secondary/central)
- ↑ Cholesterol, ↑ LDL (dyslipidemia)
- ↑ CK (myopathy)
- Anemia (normocytic or macrocytic)
- Hyponatremia (↓ free water clearance)

## Etiology

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Hashimoto's** | Autoimmune; anti-TPO, anti-Tg; lymphocytic infiltration; Hürthle cells |
| **Iodine deficiency** | #1 cause worldwide; endemic goiter |
| **Post-thyroidectomy/RAI** | Iatrogenic |
| **Drugs** | Amiodarone (Type 2), lithium, interferon-α |
| **Subacute thyroiditis (De Quervain's)** | Viral; painful thyroid; transient hypothyroidism after thyrotoxic phase |
| **Congenital** | Thyroid dysgenesis; cretinism if untreated |

## Management

- **Levothyroxine** (T4): Start 1.6 μg/kg/day in young healthy; start low (25-50 μg) in elderly/CAD
- Take on empty stomach, 30-60 min before breakfast
- Monitor TSH in 6-8 weeks; target TSH 0.5-2.5 mU/L
- **Myxedema coma:** IV levothyroxine 200-400 μg bolus + IV hydrocortisone 100mg (rule out coexisting adrenal insufficiency before T4 → precipitate adrenal crisis)
- **Pregnancy:** ↑ levothyroxine dose by ~30% (TSH target: trimester-specific)

## HYPERTHYROIDISM

## Key Points

- **Graves' disease** = #1 cause of hyperthyroidism; TSH receptor stimulating antibodies (TRAb/TSI)
- **Toxic multinodular goiter** = #1 cause in elderly/iodine-deficient areas
- **TSH suppressed + free T4/T3 elevated** = overt hyperthyroidism
- **Thyroid storm** = life-threatening; Burch-Wartofsky score >45; mortality 20-30%
- **Treatment:** Anti-thyroid drugs (carbimazole/methimazole, PTU) or RAI or surgery

## Clinical Features — Hyperthyroidism

### Symptoms
- Anxiety, irritability, tremor, heat intolerance
- Weight loss despite increased appetite
- Palpitations, diarrhea, oligomenorrhea
- Proximal muscle weakness

### Signs
- Tachycardia, AF (in elderly), wide pulse pressure
- Warm, moist skin; fine tremor of outstretched hands
- **Lid lag, lid retraction** (all hyperthyroidism — sympathetic)
- **Thyroid bruit** (Graves')

### Graves'-Specific Features
- **Diffuse goiter** with thyroid bruit
- **Graves' ophthalmopathy:** Proptosis/exophthalmos, periorbital edema, EOM restriction, exposure keratitis — due to anti-TSH receptor antibodies cross-reacting with orbital fibroblasts
- **Pretibial myxedema** — localized dermal mucinosis over shins
- **Thyroid acropachy** — clubbing (rare)

### Lab Findings
- ↓ TSH, ↑ free T4/T3
- **T3 thyrotoxicosis:** T3 elevated with normal T4 (early Graves', toxic nodule)
- **RAIU (radioactive iodine uptake):**
  - Graves' → diffuse ↑ uptake
  - Toxic nodule → focal "hot" uptake with suppressed background
  - Thyroiditis/exogenous T4 → ↓ uptake (destructive/no endogenous production)

## Management — Hyperthyroidism

### Anti-thyroid Drugs
| Drug | Mechanism | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Carbimazole/Methimazole** | Blocks TPO → ↓ T4/T3 synthesis | First-line; teratogenic in 1st trimester (aplasia cutis) |
| **Propylthiouracil (PTU)** | Same + blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion | Preferred in 1st trimester pregnancy and thyroid storm |

**Side effects:** Agranulocytosis (most dangerous — 0.3%; warn about sore throat/fever); hepatotoxicity (PTU > methimazole)

### Radioactive Iodine (I-131)
- Definitive treatment for Graves' (especially relapse after ATDs)
- **Contraindicated in pregnancy** and moderate-severe ophthalmopathy
- Most patients become hypothyroid → lifelong levothyroxine

### Beta-blockers
- **Propranolol** preferred — also blocks T4→T3 conversion
- Symptomatic relief (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety)

### Thyroid Storm Management
1. **PTU** (blocks synthesis + peripheral conversion)
2. **Iodine** (Lugol's/SSKI — 1 hour AFTER PTU; Wolff-Chaikoff effect)
3. **Propranolol** (rate control)
4. **Hydrocortisone** (blocks T4→T3 + treats relative adrenal insufficiency)
5. **Cooling** + supportive care
6. Identify and treat **precipitant** (infection, surgery, trauma, DKA)

> **Exam Pearl:** In thyroid storm, give **PTU BEFORE iodine** — iodine given alone provides substrate for more T4 synthesis (Jod-Basedow effect).
