# Diabetic Ketoacidosis — Explorer

## Overview

**Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** is a life-threatening acute metabolic complication of diabetes characterized by the triad of **hyperglycemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis**. It occurs primarily in **T1DM** but can occur in T2DM under stress. Mortality is 2-5% with treatment but higher if untreated.

## Key Points

- Triad: **Hyperglycemia (>250 mg/dL) + Ketonemia + Metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3)**
- Primarily in **T1DM**; may be first presentation
- Most common precipitant: **Infection** and **insulin omission**
- **High anion gap metabolic acidosis (HAGMA)**
- Management: **IV fluids → Insulin → Potassium** (in that order)
- Monitor potassium before starting insulin
- **Cerebral edema** = most feared complication in children

## Definition & Classification

### Severity

| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|---|---|---|---|
| **pH** | 7.25-7.30 | 7.00-7.24 | <7.00 |
| **Bicarbonate** | 15-18 | 10-14 | <10 |
| **Mental status** | Alert | Alert/drowsy | Stupor/coma |
| **Anion gap** | >10 | >12 | >12 |

### DKA vs HHS

| Feature | DKA | HHS |
|---|---|---|
| Type of DM | T1DM (usually) | T2DM |
| Glucose | >250 (usually 300-600) | >600 (often >1000) |
| pH | <7.3 | >7.3 |
| Ketones | Strongly positive | Mild/negative |
| Osmolality | Variable | >320 mOsm/kg |
| Dehydration | Moderate (3-6 L) | Severe (8-10 L) |
| Mortality | 2-5% | 15-20% |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Precipitants — "5 I's"
- **I**nfection (most common — UTI, pneumonia, sepsis)
- **I**nsulin omission/inadequate dose
- **I**nfarction (MI, stroke)
- **I**ntoxication/Illicit drugs (cocaine)
- **I**nitial presentation (new-onset T1DM)

### Other Triggers
- Surgery, trauma, pregnancy
- Medications: steroids, thiazides, SGLT2 inhibitors (euglycemic DKA)
- Pancreatitis

### Pathophysiology
1. **Absolute/relative insulin deficiency** + **↑ counter-regulatory hormones** (glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, GH)
2. ↑ Hepatic glucose production + ↓ peripheral glucose uptake → **hyperglycemia**
3. ↑ Lipolysis → ↑ FFA → hepatic β-oxidation → **ketone bodies** (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, acetone)
4. Ketones → **high anion gap metabolic acidosis**
5. Osmotic diuresis → **dehydration, electrolyte losses** (Na⁺, K⁺, PO₄³⁻)
6. Total body potassium is **always depleted** (even if serum K⁺ is normal/high due to acidosis shift)

## Clinical Features

| Feature | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Polyuria, polydipsia** | Osmotic diuresis |
| **Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain** | Ketosis, gastric stasis (may mimic acute abdomen) |
| **Kussmaul breathing** | Deep, rapid — respiratory compensation for acidosis |
| **Fruity breath** | Acetone |
| **Dehydration** | Tachycardia, hypotension, dry mucous membranes |
| **Altered consciousness** | Moderate-severe DKA |
| **Hypothermia** | Vasodilation from acidosis (even if infected — may mask fever) |

## Diagnosis

| Test | Expected Finding |
|---|---|
| **Blood glucose** | >250 mg/dL (often 300-600) |
| **ABG** | pH <7.3, low HCO₃⁻ |
| **Serum ketones** | β-hydroxybutyrate >3 mmol/L (preferred) |
| **Urine ketones** | Positive (but urine dipstick detects acetoacetate, NOT β-OHB) |
| **Anion gap** | Elevated (>12); AG = Na⁺ - (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻) |
| **Serum potassium** | May be high (despite total body depletion — acidosis shifts K⁺ out of cells) |
| **Serum sodium** | Often low (dilutional + osmotic shift); correct: Na⁺ + 1.6 per 100 mg/dL glucose >100 |
| **BUN/Cr** | Elevated (prerenal AKI from dehydration) |
| **CBC** | Leukocytosis (stress response — doesn't always indicate infection) |

## Management

### DKA Protocol — "FIK" (Fluids → Insulin → Potassium)

#### 1. IV Fluids (First Priority)
- **0.9% NS**: 1-2 L/hr initially (first 1-2 hrs)
- Then 250-500 mL/hr (0.45% NS if corrected Na⁺ is high)
- **Add 5% dextrose** when glucose reaches **200 mg/dL** (prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin)

#### 2. Insulin
- **Regular insulin IV infusion**: 0.1 U/kg bolus → 0.1 U/kg/hr
- OR 0.14 U/kg/hr (no bolus)
- Goal: ↓ glucose by **50-70 mg/dL/hr**
- Continue insulin until AG normalizes and pH >7.3
- **Do NOT start insulin if K⁺ <3.3** — correct potassium first

#### 3. Potassium Replacement
| Serum K⁺ | Action |
|---|---|
| **<3.3 mEq/L** | Hold insulin, replace K⁺ aggressively (40 mEq/hr) |
| **3.3-5.3 mEq/L** | Add 20-40 mEq/L to each liter of IV fluid |
| **>5.3 mEq/L** | Do not supplement; recheck in 2 hours |

#### 4. Bicarbonate
- Only if **pH <6.9** (controversial)
- 100 mEq NaHCO₃ in 400 mL water over 2 hours

#### 5. Monitoring
- Hourly glucose, q2-4h electrolytes, ABG
- Continuous cardiac monitoring

### Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin
- When patient eating, AG closed, pH >7.3, HCO₃⁻ >18
- Overlap SC insulin and IV insulin by **1-2 hours** before stopping drip

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of DKA

| Complication | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Cerebral edema** | Most feared in children; rapid fluid/glucose correction; presents with headache, confusion → herniation |
| **Hypokalemia** | Insulin drives K⁺ into cells; if not replaced → arrhythmia |
| **Hypoglycemia** | Excess insulin without dextrose |
| **ARDS** | Fluid overload |
| **Thromboembolism** | Dehydration, hypercoagulability |
| **Mucormycosis** | Rhinocerebral; acidotic, hyperglycemic environment promotes growth |

### Prognosis
- Mortality: 2-5% with treatment (higher in elderly, severe)
- Recurrence common if underlying cause not addressed
- Education and insulin compliance prevent recurrence
