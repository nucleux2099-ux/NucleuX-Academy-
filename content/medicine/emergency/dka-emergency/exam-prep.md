# DKA & Hypoglycemia — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **DKA triad:** Hyperglycemia + anion gap metabolic acidosis + ketonemia
- ★ **First step in DKA management:** IV normal saline (fluids before insulin!)
- ★ **K+ <3.3 → hold insulin** until K+ repleted (prevent fatal hypokalemia)
- ★ **Continue insulin until AG normalizes** (not just glucose normalization)
- ★ **Add dextrose when glucose <200** — to prevent hypoglycemia while clearing ketones
- ★ **Cerebral edema** = most feared complication of DKA treatment in children
- ★ **Euglycemic DKA:** Normal glucose + acidosis + ketones — seen with SGLT2 inhibitors
- ★ **HHS vs DKA:** HHS = higher glucose (>600), higher osmolality (>320), no significant ketosis, higher mortality
- ★ **Insulinoma:** ↑ insulin + ↑ C-peptide + low glucose → 72-hour supervised fast diagnostic
- ★ **Sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycemia** = prolonged; treat with dextrose + octreotide

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A Type 1 diabetic presents with glucose 450, pH 7.1, HCO3 8, serum K+ 3.0. The FIRST step in management is:

a) IV regular insulin bolus
b) IV normal saline + potassium replacement
c) IV sodium bicarbonate
d) Subcutaneous insulin

**Answer: b) IV normal saline + potassium replacement**

*K+ <3.3 = DO NOT give insulin (will shift K+ further into cells → cardiac arrest). Replace K+ first (20-40 mEq/hr) until K+ >3.3, then start insulin. Fluids are always the first step in DKA anyway. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 405*

### MCQ 2
In DKA management, when should insulin infusion be stopped?

a) When blood glucose reaches <200 mg/dL
b) When anion gap normalizes and patient can eat
c) When ketones are no longer detected in urine
d) After 24 hours of treatment

**Answer: b) When anion gap normalizes and patient can eat**

*Glucose falls before ketoacidosis resolves. Continue IV insulin (with dextrose) until AG normalizes, HCO3 >15, pH >7.3, and patient can eat. Then switch to SC insulin with 1-2h overlap. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 405*

### MCQ 3
A 50-year-old obese diabetic presents with glucose 1100 mg/dL, serum osmolality 340 mOsm/kg, pH 7.35, no ketonuria, and altered sensorium. The diagnosis is:

a) DKA
b) Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State
c) Lactic acidosis
d) Uremic encephalopathy

**Answer: b) Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State**

*HHS: glucose >600, osmolality >320, pH >7.3, no significant ketosis. More common in Type 2 elderly. Enough insulin to prevent ketosis but not enough to prevent hyperglycemia. Mortality 10-20% (higher than DKA). Treatment: aggressive fluid resuscitation. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 405*

### MCQ 4
A patient is found unconscious with glucose 30 mg/dL. He has high serum insulin and high C-peptide levels. The most likely diagnosis is:

a) Exogenous insulin injection
b) Insulinoma
c) Adrenal insufficiency
d) Liver failure

**Answer: b) Insulinoma**

*Insulinoma: endogenous hyperinsulinism → both insulin AND C-peptide are high (they're co-secreted). Exogenous insulin: high insulin but LOW C-peptide (exogenous insulin doesn't contain C-peptide). Diagnose with 72-hour supervised fast. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 113*

### MCQ 5
A diabetic on SGLT2 inhibitor presents with nausea, vomiting, and Kussmaul breathing. Glucose is 180 mg/dL. ABG shows pH 7.18, HCO3 10. The diagnosis is:

a) DKA
b) HHS
c) Euglycemic DKA
d) Lactic acidosis

**Answer: c) Euglycemic DKA**

*SGLT2 inhibitors cause glycosuria → lower glucose → BUT also promote ketogenesis. Result: ketoacidosis with near-normal glucose = euglycemic DKA. Often missed because glucose isn't very high. High index of suspicion needed. Check ketones! 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 404*
