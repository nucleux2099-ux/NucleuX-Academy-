# DKA & Hypoglycemia — Explorer

## Overview

**Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)** and **Hypoglycemia** are the two most important acute metabolic emergencies in diabetes. DKA is characterized by hyperglycemia, metabolic acidosis, and ketonemia due to absolute/relative insulin deficiency. Hypoglycemia is the most common acute complication of diabetes treatment.

📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 404-405

## DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

## Key Points

- **Triad:** Hyperglycemia (usually >250) + Metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3, HCO3 <18) + Ketonemia/ketonuria
- Primarily **Type 1 DM** (but can occur in Type 2 — ketosis-prone DM, SGLT2i-induced euglycemic DKA)
- **Precipitants (5 I's):** Infection (#1), Insulin omission, Infarction, Inebriation, Initial presentation
- **Anion gap metabolic acidosis** (AG = Na - Cl - HCO3; normally 10-12; elevated in DKA)
- **Total body K+ is DEPLETED** despite normal/high serum K+ (acidosis shifts K+ out of cells)
- **Treatment priorities:** Fluids → Insulin → Potassium → Bicarbonate (if pH <6.9)

## Pathophysiology

1. **Insulin deficiency** + counter-regulatory hormone excess (glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, GH)
2. → ↑ Lipolysis → ↑ Free fatty acids → hepatic β-oxidation → **ketone bodies** (acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone)
3. → ↑ Hepatic gluconeogenesis + ↓ peripheral glucose uptake → **hyperglycemia**
4. → Osmotic diuresis → **dehydration** (average 5-7L deficit)
5. → Electrolyte losses: K+, Na+, Cl-, PO4, Mg
6. → **Metabolic acidosis** (ketoacids consume HCO3)

## Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria (ADA)

| | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glucose (mg/dL) | >250 | >250 | >250 |
| pH | 7.25-7.30 | 7.00-7.24 | <7.00 |
| HCO3 (mEq/L) | 15-18 | 10-14 | <10 |
| Anion gap | >10 | >12 | >12 |
| Mental status | Alert | Alert/drowsy | Stupor/coma |
| Ketones | Positive | Positive | Positive |

### DKA vs HHS (Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State)

| Feature | DKA | HHS |
|---|---|---|
| DM type | Usually Type 1 | Type 2 |
| Glucose | >250 (usually 300-800) | >600 (often >1000) |
| pH | <7.3 | >7.3 |
| Ketones | Strongly positive | Trace/negative |
| Osmolality | Variable (usually <320) | >320 |
| Mortality | 1-5% | 10-20% |

## Management of DKA

### Step 1: Fluids (FIRST priority)
- **NS 0.9%** 1-1.5L in first hour → then 250-500 mL/hr
- Switch to **0.45% NS** when corrected Na normalizes
- Add **D5 0.45% NS** when glucose <200 (prevent hypoglycemia; continue insulin to clear ketones)

### Step 2: Insulin
- **Regular insulin bolus** 0.1 U/kg IV → infusion 0.1 U/kg/hr
- Target glucose fall: **50-70 mg/dL per hour**
- Continue insulin until **AG normalizes** and patient can eat (NOT just glucose normalization)
- Switch to SC insulin with 1-2 hour overlap before stopping IV

### Step 3: Potassium (CRITICAL)
| Serum K+ | Action |
|---|---|
| <3.3 | **Hold insulin**, give K+ 20-40 mEq/hr until >3.3 |
| 3.3-5.3 | Give K+ 20-30 mEq/L in each liter of fluid |
| >5.3 | Hold K+, recheck in 2 hours |

> **Exam Pearl:** NEVER give insulin if K+ <3.3 — insulin shifts K+ intracellularly → fatal hypokalemia → cardiac arrest.

### Step 4: Bicarbonate
- Only if **pH <6.9**: NaHCO3 100 mEq in 400mL water + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours
- Not recommended if pH >7.0 (may worsen intracellular acidosis, hypokalemia, cerebral edema)

### Monitoring
- BG hourly; electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, pH every 2-4 hours
- Calculate **corrected sodium** = measured Na + 1.6 × [(glucose - 100)/100]
- **Cerebral edema** — dreaded complication in children; presents with headache, altered consciousness; treat with mannitol

## HYPOGLYCEMIA

## Key Points

- **Whipple's triad:** Symptoms of hypoglycemia + low plasma glucose (<70 mg/dL) + symptom resolution with glucose correction
- **Most common acute complication** of insulin/sulfonylurea therapy
- **Autonomic symptoms** (glucose <70): Tremor, sweating, palpitations, hunger, anxiety
- **Neuroglycopenic symptoms** (glucose <50): Confusion, seizures, focal deficits, coma
- **Hypoglycemia unawareness:** Loss of autonomic warning signs (recurrent hypoglycemia → reset glucose thresholds)

## Causes

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Drug-induced** (#1) | Insulin, sulfonylureas (glibenclamide > glimepiride) |
| **Insulinoma** | Whipple's triad + ↑ insulin + ↑ C-peptide + ↓ glucose; 72-hour fast diagnostic |
| **Exogenous insulin** | ↑ insulin + ↓ C-peptide (vs insulinoma where both elevated) |
| **Adrenal insufficiency** | ↓ cortisol → ↓ gluconeogenesis |
| **Liver failure** | ↓ glycogen stores + ↓ gluconeogenesis |
| **Alcohol** | Blocks gluconeogenesis (NAD+ depletion from alcohol metabolism) |

## Management of Hypoglycemia

| Severity | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Mild (conscious)** | 15-20g fast-acting glucose (juice, glucose tablets); recheck in 15 min |
| **Severe (unconscious)** | **IV D25/D50** (25-50 mL of 50% dextrose) OR **IM glucagon** 1mg (if no IV access) |
| **Sulfonylurea-induced** | Prolonged; may need continuous D10 drip + octreotide (inhibits insulin release) |

> **Exam Pearl:** **Insulinoma** = high insulin + high C-peptide + low glucose. **Exogenous insulin** = high insulin + LOW C-peptide. **Sulfonylurea abuse** = high insulin + high C-peptide (mimics insulinoma) — differentiate with sulfonylurea drug screen.
