# Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

> 📚 **Sources:** Harrison's 22nd Ed Ch. 321, Sleisenger & Fordtran's GI and Liver Disease
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

GERD is a condition where retrograde flow of gastric contents into the esophagus causes troublesome symptoms and/or complications. The pathophysiology involves failure of the anti-reflux barrier (lower esophageal sphincter dysfunction, hiatal hernia) combined with impaired esophageal clearance and gastric factors. It exists as a spectrum from non-erosive reflux disease (NERD) to erosive esophagitis to Barrett's esophagus.

## Key Points

- **Most common GI diagnosis** in outpatient settings
- **LES dysfunction:** Transient relaxations (most common mechanism)
- **Hiatal hernia:** Major anatomical contributor
- **Non-erosive reflux disease (NERD):** 50-70% of GERD patients
- **Barrett's esophagus:** Premalignant, requires surveillance

## Clinical Features

### Typical Symptoms
- **Heartburn:** Retrosternal burning, worse after meals, lying down
- **Regurgitation:** Effortless return of gastric contents to mouth
- **Water brash:** Hypersalivation in response to reflux

### Atypical/Extraesophageal Symptoms
- **Pulmonary:** Chronic cough, asthma exacerbation, aspiration pneumonia
- **ENT:** Laryngitis, hoarseness, globus sensation, sore throat
- **Dental:** Enamel erosion
- **Cardiac:** Non-cardiac chest pain (must exclude cardiac first)

### Alarm Features (Warrant Urgent Endoscopy)
- Dysphagia or odynophagia
- Unintentional weight loss
- GI bleeding (hematemesis, melena)
- Anemia
- Persistent vomiting
- Age >55-60 with new symptoms

### Risk Factors
- Obesity
- Hiatal hernia
- Pregnancy
- Smoking
- Certain foods (coffee, chocolate, alcohol, fatty foods, citrus)
- Medications (CCBs, nitrates, anticholinergics, bisphosphonates)
- Scleroderma

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
- Typical symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation) + response to PPI trial
- No testing needed in young patients without alarm features

### Investigations

**1. Upper GI Endoscopy (EGD)**
- Indicated for: Alarm features, refractory symptoms, screening for Barrett's
- Grades erosive esophagitis (LA Classification):
  - A: Mucosal breaks <5mm
  - B: Mucosal breaks >5mm, non-circumferential
  - C: Circumferential breaks <75% of circumference
  - D: Circumferential breaks >75%

**2. Ambulatory pH Monitoring (24-48 hour)**
- Gold standard for quantifying acid exposure
- DeMeester score >14.7 = pathological reflux
- Indicated when: Atypical symptoms, refractory GERD, pre-surgical evaluation
- Wireless (Bravo) capsule vs catheter-based

**3. Esophageal Impedance-pH Monitoring**
- Detects both acid and non-acid reflux
- Useful in patients on PPI therapy

**4. Esophageal Manometry**
- Evaluates LES pressure and esophageal motility
- Required before anti-reflux surgery
- Rules out achalasia (mimics GERD)

**5. Barium Swallow**
- Detects hiatal hernia, strictures
- Less commonly used now

## Management

### Lifestyle Modifications (All Patients)
- Weight loss (if overweight)
- Elevate head of bed 6-8 inches
- Avoid lying down within 3 hours of meals
- Avoid trigger foods (patient-specific)
- Smoking cessation
- Avoid tight-fitting clothes

### Pharmacotherapy

**Step-Up Approach (Mild symptoms):**
1. Antacids/Alginates → H2RA → PPI

**Step-Down Approach (Moderate-severe, preferred):**
1. PPI for 8 weeks → H2RA/low-dose PPI → On-demand

**Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) - First Line:**
| Drug | Standard Dose |
|------|---------------|
| Omeprazole | 20mg daily |
| Esomeprazole | 40mg daily |
| Pantoprazole | 40mg daily |
| Rabeprazole | 20mg daily |
| Lansoprazole | 30mg daily |

- Take 30-60 minutes before meals (maximum efficacy)
- Once daily for maintenance
- Twice daily for erosive esophagitis or refractory symptoms

**H2 Receptor Antagonists:**
- Famotidine 20mg BD
- Less effective than PPIs
- Useful for nocturnal symptoms (add to PPI)
- Tachyphylaxis develops

**Other Agents:**
- Alginates (Gaviscon): Raft-forming, add-on therapy
- Prokinetics (Metoclopramide): Limited role, side effects
- Baclofen: Reduces transient LES relaxations (refractory GERD)

### Refractory GERD (No response to 8 weeks of BID PPI)
1. Confirm compliance and proper timing
2. Consider ambulatory pH testing (on or off PPI)
3. Evaluate for functional heartburn, eosinophilic esophagitis
4. Consider surgical consultation

### Surgical Management

**Indications:**
- Refractory symptoms despite optimal medical therapy
- Patient preference (young, don't want lifelong PPI)
- Large hiatal hernia
- Complications (stricture, Barrett's with dysplasia)

**Procedures:**
- **Nissen fundoplication:** 360° wrap (laparoscopic preferred)
- **Toupet (partial):** 270° wrap (for weak peristalsis)
- **LINX device:** Magnetic sphincter augmentation

**Outcomes:**
- 85-90% initial success
- Some need PPI long-term despite surgery
- Side effects: Dysphagia, bloating, inability to belch/vomit

### Endoscopic Therapies
- Stretta (radiofrequency)
- TIF (transoral incisionless fundoplication)
- Limited long-term data

## Barrett's Esophagus

### Definition
Replacement of normal squamous epithelium with intestinal metaplasia (goblet cells) in distal esophagus

### Risk Factors
- Chronic GERD (>5 years)
- Male sex (8:1)
- Age >50
- Caucasian
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Family history of Barrett's/esophageal adenocarcinoma

### Surveillance Protocol
| Finding | Surveillance Interval |
|---------|----------------------|
| No dysplasia | EGD every 3-5 years |
| Low-grade dysplasia | EGD every 6-12 months OR endoscopic eradication |
| High-grade dysplasia | Endoscopic eradication therapy |

### Treatment
- **PPI therapy:** All patients (may slow progression)
- **Endoscopic eradication:** For dysplasia
  - Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)
  - Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)
- **Esophagectomy:** For refractory HGD or carcinoma

## PPI Considerations

### Long-term Side Effects (Associations, causation debated)
- C. difficile infection
- Community-acquired pneumonia
- Hypomagnesemia
- B12 deficiency
- Osteoporosis/fractures
- Chronic kidney disease
- Dementia (weak association)

### Recommendations
- Use lowest effective dose
- Periodic reassessment of need
- Not to be withheld if clearly indicated
- Monitor magnesium if on diuretics

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Heartburn + regurgitation + response to PPI = GERD** (clinical diagnosis)
2. **Alarm features = Urgent EGD** (dysphagia, weight loss, bleeding)
3. **24-hour pH monitoring:** Gold standard for diagnosis
4. **DeMeester score >14.7:** Pathological reflux
5. **Manometry before fundoplication:** Rule out achalasia, assess motility
6. **LA Classification:** A, B, C, D (severity of erosive esophagitis)
7. **Barrett's = Intestinal metaplasia** (goblet cells on biopsy)
8. **Barrett's surveillance:** Every 3-5 years if no dysplasia
9. **HGD in Barrett's → Endoscopic eradication** (RFA/EMR)
10. **PPI timing:** 30-60 minutes before meals for best efficacy
11. **Nissen fundoplication:** 360° wrap, most common anti-reflux surgery
12. **GERD mimics:** Achalasia, eosinophilic esophagitis, functional heartburn
13. **Obesity is the strongest modifiable risk factor**
