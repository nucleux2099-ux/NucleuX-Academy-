# Acute Pancreatitis

> 📚 **Sources:** Harrison's 22nd Ed Ch. 346, Atlanta Classification 2012
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Acute pancreatitis is an inflammatory condition of the pancreas characterized by acute onset of epigastric pain with elevated pancreatic enzymes and/or characteristic imaging findings. The pathophysiology involves premature activation of trypsinogen to trypsin within acinar cells, triggering autodigestion and inflammatory cascade. Gallstones and alcohol account for ~80% of cases. Severity ranges from mild interstitial edema to severe necrotizing pancreatitis with multi-organ failure.

## Key Points

- **GET SMASHED:** Mnemonic for causes
- **Gallstones + Alcohol = 80%** of cases
- **Atlanta Classification:** Mild, Moderately Severe, Severe
- **Early aggressive fluid resuscitation:** Cornerstone of treatment
- **Necrotizing pancreatitis:** Higher mortality, may need intervention

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Epigastric pain** (95%)
  - Sudden onset, severe, constant
  - Radiates to back (50%)
  - Worse after eating, lying flat
  - Relieved by sitting forward
- Nausea and vomiting (80%)
- Anorexia
- Abdominal distension

### Signs

**General:**
- Tachycardia, hypotension (severe cases)
- Fever
- Jaundice (if biliary cause)
- Respiratory distress (ARDS)

**Abdominal:**
- Epigastric tenderness, guarding
- Decreased bowel sounds (ileus)
- Abdominal distension

**Specific Signs (Severe/Hemorrhagic Pancreatitis):**
| Sign | Description | Indicates |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Cullen's sign** | Periumbilical ecchymosis | Retroperitoneal hemorrhage |
| **Grey Turner's sign** | Flank ecchymosis | Retroperitoneal hemorrhage |
| **Fox's sign** | Inguinal ecchymosis | Rare, severe hemorrhage |

## Etiology

### GET SMASHED Mnemonic
- **G**allstones (40%)
- **E**thanol/Alcohol (30%)
- **T**rauma
- **S**teroids
- **M**umps (and other viruses)
- **A**utoimmune
- **S**corpion sting, Spider bite
- **H**yperlipidemia, Hypercalcemia, Hypothermia
- **E**RCP
- **D**rugs (Azathioprine, Valproate, Didanosine, Thiazides, Sulfonamides)

### Other Causes
- Pancreas divisum
- Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction
- Pancreatic tumors
- Genetic (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR mutations)
- Idiopathic (15-20%)

## Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria (2 of 3 Required)
1. **Abdominal pain** characteristic of pancreatitis
2. **Serum amylase or lipase ≥3x upper limit of normal**
3. **Characteristic imaging findings** on CT/MRI/US

### Laboratory

**Pancreatic Enzymes:**
| Enzyme | Rise | Peak | Duration | Notes |
|--------|------|------|----------|-------|
| Amylase | 2-12 hrs | 24 hrs | 3-5 days | Non-specific |
| Lipase | 4-8 hrs | 24 hrs | 8-14 days | More specific, preferred |

- Degree of elevation does NOT correlate with severity
- Lipase preferred (more specific, stays elevated longer)

**Other Labs:**
- CBC: Leukocytosis, hemoconcentration (Hct >44% = severe)
- BMP: Elevated BUN, creatinine (hypovolemia)
- LFTs: Elevated if biliary; ALT >150 U/L suggests gallstone pancreatitis
- Calcium: Hypocalcemia (saponification)
- Triglycerides: If >1000 mg/dL, likely cause
- ABG: Hypoxemia, metabolic acidosis
- CRP: >150 mg/L at 48 hrs predicts severe disease

### Imaging

**Ultrasound (Initial):**
- Evaluate for gallstones (cause)
- May show pancreatic edema
- Limited by bowel gas

**CT with IV Contrast (Gold standard for complications):**
- NOT needed for diagnosis if clinical criteria met
- Indicated for: Uncertain diagnosis, severe disease, clinical deterioration
- Best done 72-96 hours after onset (necrosis demarcation)
- CT Severity Index (Balthazar score)

**MRCP:**
- Evaluate biliary/pancreatic duct
- Detect choledocholithiasis
- Alternative to ERCP for diagnosis

**EUS:**
- Detect microlithiasis
- Guide intervention

### Severity Assessment

**Atlanta Classification 2012:**
| Severity | Definition |
|----------|------------|
| **Mild** | No organ failure, no local complications |
| **Moderately Severe** | Transient organ failure (<48 hrs) OR local complications |
| **Severe** | Persistent organ failure (>48 hrs) |

**Predictive Scores:**
- **APACHE II:** Complex, best validated
- **Ranson's criteria:** At admission and 48 hours
- **BISAP:** Simpler, validated (BUN, Impaired mental status, SIRS, Age >60, Pleural effusion)
- **Harmless Acute Pancreatitis Score (HAPS):** Rules out severe disease

**BISAP Score (1 point each, ≥3 = severe):**
- **B**UN >25 mg/dL
- **I**mpaired mental status (GCS <15)
- **S**IRS (≥2 criteria)
- **A**ge >60 years
- **P**leural effusion

## Management

### Initial Management (All Patients)

**1. Fluid Resuscitation (Most Important!)**
- Lactated Ringer's preferred
- Goal: 250-500 mL/hour (first 12-24 hours)
- Monitor: Urine output (>0.5 mL/kg/hr), BUN, Hct
- Caution in cardiac/renal disease

**2. Pain Control**
- IV opioids (Hydromorphone, Fentanyl)
- Avoid morphine (theoretical sphincter of Oddi spasm, not proven)

**3. Nutritional Support**
- **Mild:** Early oral feeding (within 24-48 hrs if tolerating)
- **Severe:** Enteral nutrition via NG/NJ tube (preferred over TPN)
- Parenteral only if enteral not feasible

**4. Monitoring**
- Vitals, urine output
- Serial labs (CBC, BMP, calcium)
- ICU if organ failure

### Specific Management

**Gallstone Pancreatitis:**
- **Cholecystectomy** during same admission (mild) or after recovery (severe)
- **ERCP within 24-72 hours** if:
  - Concomitant cholangitis
  - Persistent biliary obstruction
  - NOT routine (no benefit if stones have passed)

**Antibiotics:**
- NOT indicated for prophylaxis (even in necrosis)
- Indicated only for: Infected necrosis, cholangitis, extrapancreatic infection

**Triglyceride-induced Pancreatitis:**
- Insulin infusion (activates lipoprotein lipase)
- Consider plasmapheresis if TG >1000 mg/dL with severe disease
- Fibrates for maintenance

### Local Complications

| Complication | Timing | Features | Management |
|--------------|--------|----------|------------|
| **Acute peripancreatic fluid collection** | <4 weeks | No defined wall | Usually resolves |
| **Pancreatic pseudocyst** | >4 weeks | Encapsulated by fibrous wall | Drain if symptomatic |
| **Acute necrotic collection** | <4 weeks | Contains necrotic tissue | Monitor |
| **Walled-off necrosis** | >4 weeks | Encapsulated necrosis | Intervention if infected/symptomatic |

**Infected Necrosis:**
- Suspect if fever, rising WBC, clinical deterioration after day 7-10
- Confirm: CT-guided FNA or gas bubbles on CT
- Treatment:
  - Antibiotics (Carbapenems penetrate necrosis)
  - Delayed intervention (>4 weeks if possible)
  - Step-up approach: Percutaneous drainage → Endoscopic necrosectomy → Surgical necrosectomy

### Systemic Complications
- Acute kidney injury
- ARDS
- DIC
- Multiorgan dysfunction syndrome
- Hyperglycemia

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Lipase >3x ULN + abdominal pain = Pancreatitis** (no imaging needed)
2. **Lipase more specific than amylase** for pancreatitis
3. **ALT >150 U/L → Think gallstones** as cause
4. **Aggressive early fluid resuscitation** = Most important treatment
5. **CT at 72-96 hours** best for assessing necrosis
6. **Antibiotics NOT prophylactic** - only for infected necrosis
7. **Enteral nutrition preferred over TPN** (maintains gut barrier)
8. **ERCP only if cholangitis or persistent obstruction**
9. **Cullen's and Grey Turner's signs:** Hemorrhagic pancreatitis
10. **Hypocalcemia mechanism:** Saponification (fatty acids bind calcium)
11. **Ranson's >3 or APACHE II >8:** Predicts severe disease
12. **Pseudocyst:** Develops after 4 weeks, has fibrous wall
13. **Step-up approach for infected necrosis:** Drain first, necrosectomy last
14. **Cholecystectomy during same admission** for mild gallstone pancreatitis
