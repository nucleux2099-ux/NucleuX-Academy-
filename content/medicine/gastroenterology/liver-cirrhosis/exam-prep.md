# Liver Cirrhosis & Portal Hypertension — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **HVPG >12 mmHg** = risk of variceal bleeding
- ★ **SAAG ≥1.1** = portal hypertension (transudative); <1.1 = non-portal (exudative)
- ★ **SBP:** PMN ≥250 in ascitic fluid; monomicrobial; treat with ceftriaxone + albumin
- ★ **Lactulose + Rifaximin** = treatment of hepatic encephalopathy
- ★ **Child-Pugh C** = decompensated cirrhosis; 1-yr survival ~45%
- ★ **Spider angiomata** in SVC territory = estrogen excess; palmar erythema = same mechanism
- ★ **Asterixis** (flapping tremor) = hallmark of hepatic encephalopathy (also uremia, CO2 narcosis)
- ★ **Terlipressin + albumin** = treatment of HRS; bridge to transplant
- ★ **Propranolol** (NSBB) = primary and secondary prophylaxis of variceal bleeding
- ★ **TIPS** = for refractory variceal bleeding or refractory ascites; contraindicated in encephalopathy

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A cirrhotic patient with tense ascites undergoes paracentesis. Ascitic fluid shows PMN 500/μL, protein 1.0 g/dL, and SAAG 1.4. The most likely diagnosis and treatment?

a) Secondary peritonitis; surgical exploration
b) SBP; IV ceftriaxone + albumin
c) Tuberculous peritonitis; ATT
d) Malignant ascites; chemotherapy

**Answer: b) SBP; IV ceftriaxone + albumin**

*PMN ≥250 + SAAG ≥1.1 (portal HTN) = SBP until proven otherwise. Low protein in ascites is a risk factor. Treatment: ceftriaxone × 5 days + IV albumin (1.5g/kg day 1, 1g/kg day 3). Repeat paracentesis at 48h to confirm ↓ PMN. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 338*

### MCQ 2
The most common cause of death in a patient with acute variceal bleeding is:

a) Exsanguination
b) Hepatic encephalopathy
c) Rebleeding within 6 weeks
d) Hepatorenal syndrome

**Answer: c) Rebleeding within 6 weeks**

*6-week mortality after first variceal bleed is ~20%. Rebleeding is the most common cause of death. This is why secondary prophylaxis (EVL + NSBB) is essential. Without prophylaxis, rebleed rate is 60% at 1 year. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 338*

### MCQ 3
A cirrhotic patient presents with confusion and asterixis. Serum ammonia is elevated. Which is the most common precipitant?

a) Excess dietary protein
b) GI bleeding
c) Hepatocellular carcinoma
d) Portal vein thrombosis

**Answer: b) GI bleeding**

*GI bleed → protein load in gut → ↑ ammonia production by gut bacteria → hepatic encephalopathy. Other precipitants: infection (SBP), constipation, hypokalemia, diuretics, sedatives. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 338*

### MCQ 4
Which scoring system is used for liver transplant prioritization?

a) Child-Pugh score
b) MELD score
c) Glasgow-Blatchford score
d) Ranson's criteria

**Answer: b) MELD score**

*MELD (Model for End-stage Liver Disease) uses bilirubin, INR, and creatinine. Higher MELD = sicker patient = higher transplant priority. Child-Pugh is used for prognosis and surgical risk but NOT for transplant allocation. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 337*

### MCQ 5
Cruveilhier-Baumgarten syndrome is characterized by:

a) Rectal varices with bleeding
b) Recanalized umbilical vein with venous hum at umbilicus
c) Esophageal varices with splenomegaly
d) Retroperitoneal varices

**Answer: b) Recanalized umbilical vein with venous hum at umbilicus**

*Patent paraumbilical vein → caput medusae + continuous venous hum (thrill) auscultated at the umbilicus = Cruveilhier-Baumgarten sign/syndrome. It indicates portal hypertension with portosystemic shunting through the falciform ligament. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 338*
