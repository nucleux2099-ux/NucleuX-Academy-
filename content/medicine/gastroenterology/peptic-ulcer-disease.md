# Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

> 📚 **Sources:** Harrison's 22nd Ed Ch. 323, Sleisenger & Fordtran's GI and Liver Disease
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Peptic ulcer disease is characterized by mucosal defects extending through the muscularis mucosa in the stomach or duodenum. It results from an imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and protective factors (mucus, bicarbonate, prostaglandins, mucosal blood flow). H. pylori infection and NSAID use account for >90% of cases, making this a largely preventable and treatable condition.

## Key Points

- **H. pylori:** Causes 70-90% of duodenal ulcers, 30-60% of gastric ulcers
- **NSAIDs:** Second major cause, inhibit prostaglandin synthesis
- **Duodenal ulcers:** More common, pain relieved by food
- **Gastric ulcers:** Pain worsened by food, must exclude malignancy
- **Complications:** Bleeding (most common), perforation, obstruction, penetration

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

**Classic Dyspeptic Pain:**
- Epigastric pain, burning/gnawing quality
- Periodicity (weeks-months of symptoms, then remission)
- Nocturnal pain (acid secretion peaks at night)

| Feature | Duodenal Ulcer | Gastric Ulcer |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Relation to food | Relieved by food | Worsened by food |
| Timing | 2-5 hours after meals | During or shortly after meals |
| Night pain | Common | Less common |
| Weight | Normal or increased | Weight loss (fear of eating) |
| Malignancy risk | Very rare | Must be excluded |
| Location | First part of duodenum (anterior wall most common) | Lesser curvature |

**Other symptoms:**
- Nausea, bloating
- Early satiety
- Waterbrash (hypersalivation)
- Heartburn

### Complications

**1. Hemorrhage (Most Common - 15-20%)**
- Hematemesis, melena
- Coffee-ground vomitus
- Signs of hypovolemia
- Most common cause: Posterior DU eroding into gastroduodenal artery

**2. Perforation (5-10%)**
- Sudden, severe epigastric pain ("like a knife")
- Board-like rigidity
- Peritonitis
- Free air under diaphragm on erect CXR
- Most common: Anterior DU

**3. Gastric Outlet Obstruction (2-5%)**
- Early satiety, nausea, vomiting (non-bilious)
- Weight loss
- Succession splash
- Hypochloremic hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis

**4. Penetration**
- Ulcer penetrates into adjacent organ (pancreas most common)
- Back pain radiating to back
- Elevated amylase/lipase

## Diagnosis

### H. pylori Testing

**Non-invasive Tests:**
| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Urea breath test (UBT) | 95-100% | 95-100% | Best non-invasive, active infection |
| Stool antigen test | 90-95% | 90-95% | Good alternative to UBT |
| Serology (IgG) | 85% | 80% | Can't distinguish active from past |

**Invasive Tests (endoscopy required):**
- Rapid urease test (CLO test) - Most commonly used
- Histology - Gold standard
- Culture - For resistance testing

**Important:** Stop PPI 2 weeks, antibiotics 4 weeks before testing (false negatives)

### Endoscopy (EGD)

**Indications:**
- Age >55 with new dyspepsia
- Alarm features (weight loss, dysphagia, bleeding, anemia, persistent vomiting)
- Failure of empiric therapy
- All gastric ulcers (need biopsy to exclude malignancy)

**Findings:**
- Clean-based ulcer: Low rebleed risk
- Adherent clot, visible vessel, active bleeding: High rebleed risk

**Forrest Classification (bleeding ulcers):**
| Class | Description | Rebleed Risk |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Ia | Spurting hemorrhage | 90% |
| Ib | Oozing hemorrhage | 50% |
| IIa | Visible vessel | 40% |
| IIb | Adherent clot | 20% |
| IIc | Flat pigmented spot | 10% |
| III | Clean base | 5% |

### Other Investigations
- CBC, LFTs, renal function
- Serum gastrin (if Zollinger-Ellison suspected)
- CT abdomen if perforation suspected

## Management

### General Measures
- Stop NSAIDs
- Stop smoking
- Avoid alcohol
- Dietary modifications (avoid spicy food if symptomatic)

### Acid Suppression

**Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) - First Line:**
- Omeprazole 20mg BD
- Pantoprazole 40mg daily
- Esomeprazole 40mg daily

**Duration:**
- Duodenal ulcer: 4-8 weeks
- Gastric ulcer: 8-12 weeks
- Complicated ulcer: Higher dose, longer duration

**H2 Receptor Antagonists:**
- Ranitidine 150mg BD
- Famotidine 20mg BD
- Less effective than PPIs

### H. pylori Eradication

**First-Line Triple Therapy (14 days):**
- PPI (standard dose BD) +
- Clarithromycin 500mg BD +
- Amoxicillin 1g BD (or Metronidazole 500mg BD if penicillin allergy)

**Bismuth Quadruple Therapy (14 days):**
- PPI BD +
- Bismuth subsalicylate QID +
- Metronidazole 500mg TID +
- Tetracycline 500mg QID

**Concomitant Therapy (14 days):**
- PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin + Metronidazole (all BD)

**Confirm eradication:** UBT or stool antigen 4 weeks after completing therapy

### NSAID Ulcer Management
- Stop NSAID if possible
- PPI therapy
- If NSAID necessary:
  - Use lowest effective dose
  - Co-prescribe PPI
  - Consider COX-2 selective inhibitor (lower GI risk but CV concerns)
  - Misoprostol (prostaglandin analogue)

### Bleeding Ulcer Management

**Resuscitation:**
- IV access, fluid resuscitation
- Blood transfusion if Hb <7 g/dL (or <8-9 in CAD)
- Correct coagulopathy

**Pre-endoscopy:**
- IV PPI bolus (Pantoprazole 80mg) + infusion (8mg/hr)
- Consider prokinetic (Erythromycin 250mg IV)

**Endoscopic therapy (Forrest Ia-IIb):**
- Injection (epinephrine)
- Thermal (bipolar, heater probe)
- Mechanical (clips)
- Combination therapy superior

**Post-endoscopy:**
- High-dose IV PPI for 72 hours, then oral
- Clear liquids in 24 hours, advance diet
- Test and treat H. pylori

**Rebleeding/Failure:**
- Repeat endoscopy
- Angiographic embolization
- Surgery (last resort)

### Perforation
- Surgical emergency
- NPO, NG tube, IV fluids, antibiotics
- Omental patch repair (Graham patch)
- Consider definitive ulcer surgery if stable

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **H. pylori causes 70-90% of DU, 30-60% of GU**
2. **Duodenal ulcer:** Pain relieved by food, anterior wall perforates, posterior bleeds
3. **Gastric ulcer:** Always biopsy to rule out malignancy
4. **Erect CXR:** Free air under diaphragm = perforation
5. **Gastroduodenal artery:** Most common source of massive GI bleed (posterior DU)
6. **PPI before H. pylori testing = False negative** (stop 2 weeks prior)
7. **Confirm eradication:** UBT 4 weeks after treatment completion
8. **Zollinger-Ellison:** Multiple/recurrent ulcers, unusual locations, high gastrin
9. **NSAID ulcers:** Often painless, present with bleeding
10. **Triple therapy:** PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin x 14 days
11. **Succession splash:** Gastric outlet obstruction (retained gastric contents)
12. **Forrest Ia/Ib:** Active bleeding, needs endoscopic intervention
13. **Misoprostol contraindicated in pregnancy** (abortifacient)
