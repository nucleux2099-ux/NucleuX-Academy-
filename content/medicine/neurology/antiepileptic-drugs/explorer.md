# Antiepileptic Drugs — Explorer

## Overview

**Antiepileptic drugs (AEDs)** are the mainstay of epilepsy management. Drug selection depends on **seizure type, patient demographics, side effect profile, and drug interactions**. The goal is **seizure freedom with minimal side effects** using monotherapy when possible.

## Key Points

- Drug choice depends on seizure type (focal vs generalized)
- **Sodium valproate** = broadest spectrum AED
- **Carbamazepine** and **Lamotrigine** = first-line for focal seizures
- **Valproate** = first-line for generalized seizures (not in women of childbearing age)
- **Levetiracetam** = increasingly used first-line due to fewer interactions
- Many AEDs are **teratogenic** — valproate worst (neural tube defects)

## Classification of AEDs

### By Mechanism

| Mechanism | Drugs |
|---|---|
| **Na⁺ channel blockers** | Carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine, oxcarbazepine, lacosamide |
| **Ca²⁺ channel (T-type) blockers** | Ethosuximide |
| **GABA enhancers** | Benzodiazepines, phenobarbital, vigabatrin, tiagabine |
| **SV2A binding** | Levetiracetam, brivaracetam |
| **Multiple mechanisms** | Valproate, topiramate, zonisamide |
| **Glutamate antagonists** | Perampanel (AMPA), felbamate |

### By Seizure Type — Drug Selection

| Seizure Type | First-Line | Alternatives | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focal (partial)** | Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine, Levetiracetam | Oxcarbazepine, topiramate, lacosamide | — |
| **Generalized tonic-clonic** | Valproate, Lamotrigine, Levetiracetam | Topiramate, carbamazepine | — |
| **Absence** | Ethosuximide, Valproate | Lamotrigine | Carbamazepine, phenytoin (worsen) |
| **Myoclonic** | Valproate, Levetiracetam | Clonazepam, topiramate | Carbamazepine, phenytoin (worsen) |
| **Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy** | Valproate (M), Levetiracetam (F) | Lamotrigine | Carbamazepine |

## Individual Drug Profiles

### Phenytoin

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Na⁺ channel blocker |
| Indications | Focal seizures, GTCS, status epilepticus (IV fosphenytoin) |
| Kinetics | **Zero-order** (saturable metabolism) — small dose changes → large level changes |
| Side effects | Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, coarsening of facial features, cerebellar atrophy, osteomalacia |
| Interactions | CYP450 inducer; many drug interactions |
| Special | **Purple glove syndrome** (IV extravasation); teratogenic (fetal hydantoin syndrome) |

### Carbamazepine

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Na⁺ channel blocker |
| Indications | Focal seizures (first-line), trigeminal neuralgia, bipolar disorder |
| Side effects | Hyponatremia (SIADH), aplastic anemia, SJS/TEN (HLA-B*1502 — East Asian), diplopia, ataxia |
| Interactions | CYP3A4 inducer; **autoinduction** (induces own metabolism) |
| ⚠️ | **Worsens absence and myoclonic seizures** |

### Sodium Valproate

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Multiple: Na⁺ channel, T-type Ca²⁺, ↑GABA, HDAC inhibitor |
| Indications | **Broadest spectrum**: All seizure types, bipolar, migraine prophylaxis |
| Side effects | Weight gain, hair loss (curly regrowth), tremor, hepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, thrombocytopenia |
| ⚠️ Teratogenicity | **Most teratogenic AED** — neural tube defects (spina bifida), autism; **AVOID in women of childbearing age** |
| Special | Hyperammonemia (even without liver failure) |

### Lamotrigine

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Na⁺ channel blocker, glutamate release inhibitor |
| Indications | Focal and generalized seizures; bipolar disorder |
| Side effects | **SJS/TEN** (especially rapid titration or with valproate); must titrate slowly |
| Interactions | Valproate doubles lamotrigine levels (inhibits glucuronidation) |
| Advantage | Safe in pregnancy (relatively); no weight gain; no cognitive effects |

### Levetiracetam

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | SV2A protein binding |
| Indications | Focal and generalized seizures |
| Side effects | **Psychiatric**: irritability, aggression, behavioral changes, depression |
| Advantage | No hepatic metabolism, minimal drug interactions, IV form available |
| Special | Increasingly used first-line due to safety and interaction profile |

### Ethosuximide

| Property | Details |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | T-type Ca²⁺ channel blocker |
| Indications | **Absence seizures only** (first-line for pure absence) |
| Side effects | GI upset, headache, rarely blood dyscrasias |
| Special | Does NOT work for any other seizure type |

### Other AEDs

| Drug | Key Points |
|---|---|
| **Topiramate** | Na⁺ channel + GABA + carbonic anhydrase inhibitor; weight loss, kidney stones, word-finding difficulty, glaucoma |
| **Oxcarbazepine** | Keto-analog of CBZ; fewer interactions, still causes hyponatremia |
| **Pregabalin/Gabapentin** | Ca²⁺ channel α2δ ligand; adjunctive for focal; also neuropathic pain |
| **Vigabatrin** | Irreversible GABA-transaminase inhibitor; visual field defects; drug of choice for infantile spasms (West syndrome) |
| **Phenobarbital** | GABA-A enhancer; oldest AED; sedation, cognitive impairment |
| **Clobazam** | Benzodiazepine; adjunctive; less sedating than clonazepam |

## Special Situations

### Status Epilepticus Protocol

1. **0-5 min**: ABCs, glucose, IV access
2. **5-20 min**: IV lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg) or IM midazolam
3. **20-40 min**: IV phenytoin/fosphenytoin (20 mg/kg) or valproate or levetiracetam
4. **>40 min (refractory)**: ICU — propofol/midazolam/thiopental infusion

### Women & Epilepsy
- Avoid valproate in women of childbearing age
- Lamotrigine or levetiracetam preferred in pregnancy
- **Folate supplementation** (5 mg/day) before conception
- Enzyme-inducing AEDs reduce OCP efficacy (CBZ, phenytoin, phenobarbital)
- Breastfeeding generally safe with most AEDs

### Drug Withdrawal
- Consider after **2 years seizure-free**
- Taper over ≥2-3 months
- Higher relapse risk: JME (lifelong treatment usually needed), structural lesion, abnormal EEG

## Complications & Prognosis

### AED Side Effects Summary

| Side Effect | Drugs |
|---|---|
| **Teratogenicity** | Valproate > phenytoin > carbamazepine > phenobarbital |
| **SJS/TEN** | Lamotrigine, carbamazepine, phenytoin |
| **Hepatotoxicity** | Valproate (especially <2 years) |
| **Hyponatremia** | Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine |
| **Weight gain** | Valproate, pregabalin, gabapentin |
| **Weight loss** | Topiramate, zonisamide |
| **Osteoporosis** | Enzyme inducers (phenytoin, CBZ, phenobarbital) |
| **Gingival hyperplasia** | Phenytoin |
