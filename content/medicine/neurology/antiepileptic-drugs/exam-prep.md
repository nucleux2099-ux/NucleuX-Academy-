# Antiepileptic Drugs — Exam Prep

## Quick Summary

AED choice depends on seizure type. Valproate = broadest spectrum but most teratogenic. CBZ/lamotrigine = focal seizures first-line. Ethosuximide = absence seizures only. CBZ worsens absence/myoclonic. Levetiracetam increasingly preferred (few interactions).

## High Yield Points ★

- ★ **Valproate** = broadest spectrum AED; **AVOID in women of childbearing age** (NTDs)
- ★ **Ethosuximide** = T-type Ca²⁺ channel blocker; ONLY for absence seizures
- ★ **CBZ worsens** absence and myoclonic seizures
- ★ **Phenytoin** = zero-order kinetics; gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism
- ★ **Lamotrigine** = SJS risk (titrate slowly); valproate doubles its level
- ★ **Levetiracetam** = SV2A binding; psychiatric side effects (irritability)
- ★ **Status epilepticus**: Lorazepam → Phenytoin/Valproate → Propofol/Midazolam infusion
- ★ **Vigabatrin** = drug of choice for infantile spasms; causes visual field defects
- ★ **Topiramate** = causes weight loss, kidney stones, word-finding difficulty
- ★ **HLA-B*1502** screening before CBZ in East Asians (SJS risk)
- ★ **Enzyme inducers** (CBZ, phenytoin, phenobarbital) reduce OCP efficacy

## Mnemonics

- **Enzyme inducers: "PC BRAS"** — Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Barbiturates, Rifampicin, Alcohol (chronic), St John's wort
- **Phenytoin side effects: "PHEN"** — P-450 inducer, Hirsutism, Enlarged gums, Nystagmus
- **Drugs worsening absence**: "Can't Play" — Carbamazepine, Phenytoin
