# Motor Neuron Disease — Explorer

## Overview

**Motor neuron disease (MND)** is a group of progressive neurodegenerative disorders affecting **upper motor neurons (UMN)** and/or **lower motor neurons (LMN)**. **Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)** is the most common form. MND characteristically spares **sensory neurons, eye movements, and sphincters** ("3 S's spared: Sensory, Sphincter, Saccades").

## Key Points

- **ALS** = combined UMN + LMN degeneration (most common MND)
- Progressive, fatal — median survival **3-5 years**
- **No sensory involvement** — purely motor
- Eye movements and sphincters typically spared
- Diagnosis: clinical + EMG (El Escorial criteria)
- Only drug shown to improve survival: **Riluzole** (~3 months)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | UMN | LMN | Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ALS (classic)** | ✓ | ✓ | Combined UMN+LMN, most common form |
| **Progressive muscular atrophy (PMA)** | ✗ | ✓ | Pure LMN; better prognosis |
| **Primary lateral sclerosis (PLS)** | ✓ | ✗ | Pure UMN; slowest progression |
| **Progressive bulbar palsy (PBP)** | ± | ✓ | Bulbar muscles; dysarthria, dysphagia; worst prognosis |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- Age 50-70 (peak onset)
- Male > female (1.5:1)
- **Familial** in 5-10% — SOD1 (most common), C9orf72 (most common overall genetic cause), FUS, TDP-43
- Sporadic in 90%
- Possible associations: smoking, military service, heavy metals

### Pathophysiology
1. Degeneration of motor neurons in **motor cortex, brainstem, and spinal cord**
2. **Glutamate excitotoxicity** — basis for riluzole (glutamate antagonist)
3. **TDP-43 protein** aggregation — hallmark pathology in most sporadic ALS
4. SOD1 mutations → oxidative stress, protein misfolding
5. **C9orf72** hexanucleotide repeat expansion — links ALS with frontotemporal dementia (FTD)

## Clinical Features

### UMN Signs
- Spasticity, hypertonia
- Hyperreflexia
- Extensor plantar response (Babinski+)
- Pseudobulbar palsy (spastic tongue, brisk jaw jerk, emotional lability)

### LMN Signs
- **Wasting**, fasciculations
- Weakness, hypotonia
- Hyporeflexia/areflexia
- Bulbar palsy (flaccid tongue with fasciculations, nasal speech)

### Clinical Pattern

| Region | UMN Features | LMN Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Limb** | Spasticity, hyperreflexia | Wasting, fasciculations (hand small muscles early) |
| **Bulbar** | Spastic dysarthria, brisk jaw jerk | Tongue wasting + fasciculations, nasal speech |
| **Respiratory** | — | Diaphragm weakness → type 2 respiratory failure |

### What is SPARED in MND
- **Sensory system** — no numbness/tingling
- **Eye movements** — extraocular muscles spared
- **Sphincter function** — no incontinence (Onuf's nucleus spared)
- **Cerebellar function**

### ALS-FTD Overlap
- 15% of ALS patients develop **frontotemporal dementia**
- Especially with **C9orf72** mutations
- Behavioral changes, personality change, executive dysfunction

## Diagnosis

### El Escorial Criteria (Revised)
- **Definite ALS**: UMN + LMN signs in 3 regions
- **Probable ALS**: UMN + LMN signs in 2 regions (UMN above LMN)
- **Possible ALS**: UMN + LMN in 1 region, or UMN in ≥2 regions
- Regions: bulbar, cervical, thoracic, lumbosacral

### Investigations

| Test | Findings |
|---|---|
| **EMG/NCS** | Active denervation (fibrillation potentials, fasciculations) + chronic reinnervation in multiple regions; normal sensory NCS |
| **MRI brain/spine** | Exclude structural causes (cervical myelopathy, tumors) |
| **Genetic testing** | If familial — SOD1, C9orf72 |
| **Pulmonary function** | FVC — monitor respiratory decline |
| **Blood tests** | CK mildly elevated; rule out mimics (anti-GM1, thyroid) |

## Management

### Disease-Modifying

| Drug | Mechanism | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| **Riluzole** | Glutamate antagonist | Extends survival by ~3 months |
| **Edaravone** | Free radical scavenger | Slows functional decline in some patients |

### Symptomatic Management

| Symptom | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Spasticity** | Baclofen, tizanidine |
| **Sialorrhea** | Amitriptyline, glycopyrrolate, botulinum toxin |
| **Pseudobulbar affect** | Dextromethorphan-quinidine |
| **Cramps** | Quinine (limited evidence) |
| **Pain** | NSAIDs, gabapentin |
| **Dysphagia** | PEG tube when swallowing unsafe |
| **Respiratory failure** | **Non-invasive ventilation (NIV)** — improves survival and quality of life |
| **Communication** | Augmentative devices (eye-tracking) |

### Multidisciplinary Care
- Neurology, pulmonology, speech therapy, physiotherapy, dietetics, palliative care
- **Advance directives** — early discussion

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- **Respiratory failure** — most common cause of death
- Aspiration pneumonia
- Malnutrition (dysphagia)
- Pressure sores
- Depression
- Complete paralysis (locked-in-like state)

### Prognosis
- Median survival: **3-5 years** from symptom onset (ALS)
- **Bulbar onset**: worse prognosis (~2 years)
- **PLS**: slowest, >10 years
- **PMA**: intermediate, 5-10 years
- 10% survive >10 years (Stephen Hawking — extreme outlier)
