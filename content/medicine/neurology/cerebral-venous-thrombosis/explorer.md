# Cerebral Venous Thrombosis — Explorer

## Overview

**Cerebral venous thrombosis (CVT)** is thrombosis of the dural venous sinuses and/or cerebral veins. It accounts for **<1% of all strokes** but is an important cause of **stroke in the young**, especially in women (OCP, pregnancy, puerperium). It can cause venous infarction which is often hemorrhagic.

## Key Points

- Important cause of **stroke in young women**
- **Superior sagittal sinus** = most commonly affected
- Risk factors: OCP, pregnancy/puerperium, hypercoagulable states, infections
- Presents with headache (most common), seizures, focal deficits, raised ICP
- **MRI + MR venography** = investigation of choice
- CT: "Empty delta sign" (filling defect in sagittal sinus)
- Treatment: **Anticoagulation with heparin** (even if hemorrhagic infarction present)

## Definition & Classification

### By Sinus Involved

| Sinus | Frequency | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Superior sagittal sinus** | 60-70% | Raised ICP, bilateral/parasagittal infarcts, seizures |
| **Transverse/lateral sinus** | 40-50% | Headache, papilledema, may mimic idiopathic intracranial hypertension |
| **Sigmoid sinus** | Often with transverse | Associated with otitis media/mastoiditis |
| **Cavernous sinus** | 5% | Proptosis, chemosis, ophthalmoplegia, facial pain; often infectious |
| **Deep veins (internal cerebral, vein of Galen)** | Rare | Bilateral thalamic lesions, coma, worst prognosis |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Prothrombotic states** | OCP (most common acquired cause), pregnancy/puerperium, Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin III deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome |
| **Infections** | Otitis media, mastoiditis, sinusitis, meningitis, orbital cellulitis |
| **Inflammatory** | SLE, Behçet disease, IBD, sarcoidosis |
| **Hematologic** | Polycythemia, PNH, sickle cell, thrombocytosis |
| **Malignancy** | Hypercoagulability, dural invasion |
| **Others** | Dehydration, head injury, neurosurgery, lumbar puncture |

### Pathophysiology
1. Venous thrombosis → impaired venous drainage
2. Increased venous pressure → decreased CSF absorption → **raised ICP**
3. Venous congestion → vasogenic edema
4. Venous infarction (non-arterial territory) — often **hemorrhagic**
5. Unlike arterial stroke, infarcts do NOT follow arterial territories

## Clinical Features

| Feature | Frequency |
|---|---|
| **Headache** | 90% (most common; progressive, may be thunderclap) |
| **Papilledema** | 50% |
| **Seizures** | 40% (more common than in arterial stroke) |
| **Focal deficits** | 40-60% (hemiparesis, aphasia) |
| **Altered consciousness** | 20-30% |
| **Cranial nerve palsies** | CN VI (raised ICP), cavernous sinus syndrome |

### Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis
- Painful ophthalmoplegia (CN III, IV, VI)
- Proptosis, chemosis
- Facial sensory loss (CN V1, V2)
- Often secondary to facial/orbital/sinus infection
- Bilateral involvement common

### Special Presentations
- May mimic **idiopathic intracranial hypertension** (headache + papilledema + CN VI palsy)
- May present as **encephalopathy** (deep vein thrombosis)
- **Postpartum** headache + seizures → consider CVT

## Diagnosis

| Investigation | Finding |
|---|---|
| **MRI + MR venography** | Gold standard; shows thrombus (absent flow) and parenchymal changes |
| **CT venography** | Alternative; "empty delta sign" in posterior sagittal sinus |
| **NCCT** | Cord sign (hyperdense vein), hemorrhagic infarct in non-arterial territory |
| **CT with contrast** | Empty delta sign (filling defect in sinus) |
| **D-dimer** | Elevated (sensitivity ~97%); useful to rule out |
| **Thrombophilia workup** | Factor V Leiden, protein C/S, antithrombin III, APLA |
| **LP** | Raised opening pressure; may show RBCs; only if no SOL |

### Imaging Clues to CVT (vs Arterial Stroke)
- Infarct NOT in arterial territory
- **Hemorrhagic infarction** in young patient
- **Bilateral** parasagittal lesions (SSS thrombosis)
- **Bilateral thalamic** lesions (deep vein thrombosis)

## Management

### Anticoagulation (Mainstay)
- **LMWH** or **IV unfractionated heparin** → transition to **oral warfarin** (INR 2-3) for 3-12 months
- **Anticoagulate even with hemorrhagic infarction** — hemorrhage is result of venous congestion, not indication to withhold
- Duration: 3-6 months (provoked), 6-12 months (unprovoked/thrombophilia), lifelong (recurrent/severe thrombophilia)

### Additional Management
- **Seizure treatment** — levetiracetam or valproate (avoid phenytoin — enzyme inducer with warfarin)
- **Raised ICP management** — acetazolamide, LP (therapeutic), shunting if refractory
- **Treat underlying cause** — stop OCP, treat infection, manage thrombophilia
- **Decompressive surgery** — for large hemorrhagic infarcts with herniation risk
- **Endovascular thrombectomy** — for deteriorating patients despite anticoagulation

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- Hemorrhagic infarction
- Status epilepticus
- Raised ICP → visual loss
- Hydrocephalus
- Recurrent thrombosis
- Death (mortality 5-15%)

### Prognosis
- Better than arterial stroke — **80% good recovery**
- Mortality: 5-15%
- Recurrence: 2-5% per year
- Poor prognostic factors: Deep vein thrombosis, coma, hemorrhage, malignancy, male sex, older age

---

*"Young woman with headache, seizures, and hemorrhagic infarct in non-arterial territory = CVT until proven otherwise."*
