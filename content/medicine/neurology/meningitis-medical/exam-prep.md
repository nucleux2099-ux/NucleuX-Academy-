# Meningitis & Encephalitis — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **S. pneumoniae** = #1 cause of bacterial meningitis in adults; **N. meningitidis** = #1 in adolescents
- ★ **CSF: turbid, ↑ neutrophils, ↓ glucose, ↑ protein** = bacterial meningitis
- ★ **CSF: clear, lymphocytes, normal glucose, mild ↑ protein** = viral meningitis
- ★ **Dexamethasone** before/with first antibiotic dose — reduces mortality in pneumococcal meningitis
- ★ **Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome:** Meningococcal sepsis → bilateral adrenal hemorrhage → DIC → shock
- ★ **HSV encephalitis:** Temporal lobe; MRI shows T2 hyperintensity; PCR diagnostic; IV acyclovir
- ★ **Cryptococcal meningitis:** HIV with CD4 <100; India ink, CSF CrAg; treat with amphotericin B + flucytosine
- ★ **TB meningitis:** Basal meningitis; CN palsies (VI most common); CSF ADA >10; treat 12 months + steroids
- ★ **Hearing loss** = most common permanent sequela of bacterial meningitis
- ★ **Do NOT delay antibiotics** for CT/LP — if CT needed, give antibiotics FIRST, then CT, then LP

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 20-year-old college student presents with high fever, severe headache, and petechial rash. CSF shows turbid fluid with neutrophilic pleocytosis, low glucose. Gram stain shows gram-negative diplococci. The most likely organism is:

a) Streptococcus pneumoniae
b) Neisseria meningitidis
c) Haemophilus influenzae
d) Listeria monocytogenes

**Answer: b) Neisseria meningitidis**

*Young adult + petechial rash + gram-negative diplococci = N. meningitidis (meningococcus). Can cause fulminant septicemia with DIC (Waterhouse-Friderichsen). Close contacts need chemoprophylaxis (rifampicin/ciprofloxacin/ceftriaxone). 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 436*

### MCQ 2
In a patient with suspected bacterial meningitis and papilledema, the FIRST step is:

a) Lumbar puncture
b) CT head → then LP
c) Empiric IV antibiotics → CT head → then LP
d) MRI brain

**Answer: c) Empiric IV antibiotics → CT head → then LP**

*Signs of raised ICP (papilledema, focal deficits, altered sensorium) require CT before LP (risk of herniation). But NEVER delay antibiotics — start empiric ceftriaxone + vancomycin immediately, then CT, then LP when safe. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 436*

### MCQ 3
A patient with HIV (CD4 count 50) presents with chronic headache and fever. CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis with low glucose. India ink stain is positive. Treatment of choice is:

a) IV Acyclovir
b) Amphotericin B + Flucytosine
c) IV Ceftriaxone
d) ATT

**Answer: b) Amphotericin B + Flucytosine**

*Cryptococcal meningitis: HIV with CD4 <100; subacute course; India ink shows encapsulated yeast (only 50% sensitive); CSF CrAg is best test (>95% sensitive). Induction: Amphotericin B + Flucytosine × 2 weeks → Fluconazole consolidation. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 213*

### MCQ 4
HSV encephalitis most commonly affects which brain region?

a) Frontal lobe
b) Temporal lobe
c) Parietal lobe
d) Cerebellum

**Answer: b) Temporal lobe**

*HSV-1 has tropism for temporal lobe (enters via olfactory/trigeminal nerves). MRI: T2/FLAIR hyperintensity in temporal lobes (often asymmetric). CSF PCR for HSV is diagnostic. Treat empirically with IV acyclovir — don't wait for confirmation. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 437*

### MCQ 5
The most common long-term sequela of bacterial meningitis is:

a) Epilepsy
b) Sensorineural hearing loss
c) Hydrocephalus
d) Cognitive impairment

**Answer: b) Sensorineural hearing loss**

*SNHL is the most common permanent sequela (especially after pneumococcal meningitis). Occurs due to cochlear inflammation. Dexamethasone (given early) reduces this risk. Audiometry should be performed after recovery. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 436*
