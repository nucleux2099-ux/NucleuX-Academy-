# Huntington Disease — Explorer

## Overview

**Huntington disease (HD)** is an **autosomal dominant** neurodegenerative disorder caused by **CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion** in the **HTT gene** (chromosome 4). It is characterized by the triad of **chorea, psychiatric disturbances, and dementia**. It is progressive and fatal, with no cure.

## Key Points

- **Autosomal dominant** with complete penetrance (≥40 repeats)
- Gene: HTT on chromosome 4p16.3
- **CAG repeat expansion** → polyglutamine tract in huntingtin protein
- **Anticipation** — earlier onset in successive generations (especially paternal transmission)
- Onset: typically **30-50 years**
- Pathology: **caudate nucleus atrophy** → "box-car" ventricles on imaging

## Definition & Classification

| Classification | CAG Repeats | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | <27 | No disease |
| Intermediate | 27-35 | No disease, but may expand in offspring |
| Reduced penetrance | 36-39 | May or may not develop disease |
| **Full penetrance** | **≥40** | Will develop disease |
| Juvenile HD | Usually >60 repeats | Onset <20 years; rigid (Westphal variant) |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Genetics
- **Autosomal dominant** — 50% chance of inheritance
- **Anticipation** — repeat length increases across generations, especially through **paternal** transmission
- Longer repeats → earlier onset
- **De novo** mutations rare (intermediate alleles may expand)

### Pathophysiology
1. Mutant huntingtin protein → misfolding and aggregation
2. **Toxic gain of function** — intranuclear inclusions
3. Preferential loss of **medium spiny neurons** (GABAergic) in **caudate and putamen**
4. Loss of GABA and ACh in striatum
5. Progressive cortical atrophy (later stages)
6. Mitochondrial dysfunction, excitotoxicity, oxidative stress

## Clinical Features

### Triad
1. **Chorea** — involuntary, irregular, unpredictable dance-like movements
2. **Psychiatric disturbances** — often earliest features
3. **Cognitive decline** → dementia (subcortical pattern)

### Motor Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Chorea** | Early: fidgetiness → late: generalized involuntary movements |
| **Dystonia** | Sustained abnormal postures (more in juvenile form) |
| **Rigidity** | Westphal variant (juvenile) — akinetic-rigid, NOT choreic |
| **Eye movements** | Impaired saccades (difficulty initiating voluntary saccades) |
| **Gait** | Unsteady, dance-like, wide-based |
| **Dysarthria, dysphagia** | Progressive |
| **Motor impersistence** | Cannot maintain sustained tongue protrusion ("jack-in-the-box" tongue) |

### Psychiatric Features (Often precede motor by years)
- **Depression** (most common psychiatric feature)
- Irritability, aggression
- Apathy
- Obsessive-compulsive behaviors
- Psychosis (less common)
- **Suicide risk**: 4x general population

### Cognitive Features
- **Subcortical dementia**: Executive dysfunction, slowed processing, poor concentration
- Memory retrieval impaired (recognition relatively preserved)
- Progressive to global dementia

### Juvenile Huntington (Westphal Variant)
- Onset <20 years, usually paternal transmission
- **Rigid-akinetic** (not choreic)
- **Seizures** common
- Rapid progression
- Usually >60 CAG repeats

## Diagnosis

| Test | Findings |
|---|---|
| **Genetic testing** | Definitive — CAG repeat count ≥40 on HTT gene |
| **MRI brain** | Caudate atrophy → "box-car" ventricles (dilated frontal horns) |
| **CT brain** | Loss of caudate head convexity |
| **PET** | Reduced glucose metabolism in caudate |

### Predictive (Presymptomatic) Testing
- Available for at-risk individuals (parent with HD)
- Requires **genetic counseling** before and after
- Ethical considerations — autonomy, right not to know
- Testing of minors generally discouraged

## Management

### No Disease-Modifying Treatment Available

### Symptomatic Treatment

| Symptom | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Chorea** | **Tetrabenazine** (VMAT2 inhibitor — depletes dopamine); deutetrabenazine |
| **Chorea (alternative)** | Haloperidol, olanzapine, risperidone |
| **Depression** | SSRIs (citalopram, sertraline) |
| **Psychosis** | Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, quetiapine) |
| **Rigidity** | Levodopa (Westphal variant) |
| **Irritability/aggression** | SSRIs, mood stabilizers |

### Supportive Care
- Multidisciplinary team (neurology, psychiatry, physiotherapy, speech therapy, genetics)
- Genetic counseling for family
- Nutritional support (increased caloric needs due to chorea)
- Speech and swallowing therapy
- Advance care planning

### Emerging Therapies
- **Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs)** — targeting huntingtin mRNA (trials ongoing)
- Gene silencing strategies
- **Tominersen** — intrathecal ASO (mixed trial results)

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- **Aspiration pneumonia** (most common cause of death)
- Severe weight loss
- Falls and injuries
- Suicide
- Institutionalization

### Prognosis
- Progressive and fatal
- Mean duration from onset to death: **15-20 years**
- Juvenile form: more rapid, ~8-10 years
- Cause of death: pneumonia, cardiovascular disease, suicide
