# Tension Headache — Explorer

## Overview

**Tension-type headache (TTH)** is the **most common primary headache**, affecting up to 80% of the population. It presents as a bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild-to-moderate headache without the typical features of migraine (no nausea, no aura). Often described as a "band-like" sensation around the head.

## Key Points

- **Most common** headache worldwide
- Bilateral, non-pulsating, mild-moderate
- No nausea/vomiting (mild nausea allowed in chronic TTH)
- No aura, no autonomic features
- Duration: **30 min to 7 days**
- Often stress-related; responds to simple analgesics

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Infrequent episodic** | <1 day/month (<12 days/year) |
| **Frequent episodic** | 1-14 days/month for >3 months (12-180 days/year) |
| **Chronic TTH** | ≥15 days/month for >3 months (>180 days/year) |

### ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria

At least **10 episodes** with:
- Duration **30 min to 7 days**
- ≥2 of: Bilateral, Pressing/tightening (non-pulsating), Mild-moderate, Not aggravated by activity
- Both: No nausea/vomiting AND no more than one of photophobia/phonophobia

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors / Triggers
- **Stress** (most common trigger)
- Poor posture, prolonged screen time
- Sleep deprivation or excess sleep
- Anxiety, depression
- Jaw clenching (TMJ dysfunction)
- Caffeine withdrawal

### Pathophysiology
- **Peripheral mechanism** (episodic): Myofascial tenderness, increased pericranial muscle tension
- **Central mechanism** (chronic): Central sensitization, impaired supraspinal pain modulation
- Reduced pain thresholds in pericranial muscles
- Nitric oxide may play a role

## Clinical Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Quality** | Pressing, tightening, "band-like" |
| **Location** | Bilateral (frontal, temporal, occipital) |
| **Intensity** | Mild to moderate (does NOT prohibit activities) |
| **Duration** | 30 min to 7 days |
| **Aggravation** | Not worsened by routine physical activity |
| **Associated** | No nausea/vomiting; may have mild photo OR phonophobia (not both) |
| **Pericranial tenderness** | Common on palpation |

### What TTH is NOT
- Not unilateral (usually)
- Not pulsating
- Not severe
- Not associated with nausea/vomiting
- Not associated with aura
- Not worsened by physical activity

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical diagnosis** — no investigations needed for typical TTH
- Must exclude secondary causes if red flags present
- **Red flags**: Sudden onset, progressive, systemic symptoms, neurological signs, papilledema, age >50 new onset
- **Headache diary** useful for classification

## Management

### Acute Treatment

| Agent | Notes |
|---|---|
| **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen 400 mg, naproxen 500 mg) | First-line |
| **Paracetamol** (1 g) | Alternative |
| **Aspirin** (1 g) | Effective |
| **Combination analgesics** | Paracetamol + caffeine |
| ⚠️ Avoid opioids, barbiturates | Risk of medication overuse headache |

- **Limit analgesic use** to <15 days/month (simple) or <10 days/month (combination)

### Prophylaxis (Chronic TTH)

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Amitriptyline** | 10-75 mg at night | First-line prophylaxis; NNT ~3 |
| **Nortriptyline** | 25-75 mg | Less sedating alternative |
| **Mirtazapine** | 15-30 mg | If TCA not tolerated |
| **Venlafaxine** | 150 mg | SNRI option |

### Non-Pharmacological

- **Stress management**, relaxation techniques
- Regular exercise, adequate sleep
- Physiotherapy, posture correction
- Cognitive behavioral therapy
- Biofeedback, acupuncture
- Limit screen time, ergonomic workspace

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- **Medication overuse headache** — most important complication
- Chronic TTH → significant disability and work loss
- Associated depression and anxiety

### Prognosis
- Episodic TTH has excellent prognosis
- Chronic TTH more difficult to treat
- ~15% of episodic TTH evolves to chronic
- Proper lifestyle modification improves outcomes significantly
