# Stroke (Ischemic & Hemorrhagic) — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **CT head (non-contrast)** = FIRST investigation in acute stroke (to rule out hemorrhage before thrombolysis)
- ★ **IV alteplase (tPA)** within **4.5 hours** of symptom onset for ischemic stroke (0.9 mg/kg, max 90mg)
- ★ **Mechanical thrombectomy** up to **24 hours** for large vessel occlusion (DAWN, DEFUSE-3 trials)
- ★ **MCA stroke:** Contralateral hemiparesis (face + arm > leg) + hemisensory loss + aphasia (if dominant) or neglect (if non-dominant)
- ★ **ACA stroke:** Contralateral leg > arm weakness + personality changes + urinary incontinence
- ★ **PCA stroke:** Contralateral homonymous hemianopia + visual agnosia
- ★ **Lacunar infarcts:** Pure motor (posterior limb internal capsule), pure sensory (thalamus), ataxic hemiparesis, dysarthria-clumsy hand
- ★ **Hemorrhagic stroke:** Hypertension = #1 cause; basal ganglia (putamen) = most common site
- ★ **Subarachnoid hemorrhage:** "Worst headache of life"; berry aneurysm; CT head → LP (xanthochromia) if CT negative
- ★ **Secondary prevention:** Aspirin + statin + BP control + treat AF (anticoagulation if cardioembolic)

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 65-year-old hypertensive presents with sudden left-sided hemiparesis and speech difficulty 2 hours ago. CT head shows no hemorrhage. The next best step is:

a) MRI brain
b) IV alteplase (tPA)
c) Aspirin 325mg
d) Carotid Doppler

**Answer: b) IV alteplase (tPA)**

*Ischemic stroke within 4.5h window + no contraindications → IV tPA (0.9 mg/kg, 10% bolus, rest over 60 min). CT was done to rule out hemorrhage (tPA contraindicated in hemorrhagic stroke). Do NOT give aspirin within 24h of tPA. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 425*

### MCQ 2
A patient presents with right homonymous hemianopia and difficulty reading (alexia without agraphia). The most likely vascular territory is:

a) MCA
b) ACA
c) PCA (left)
d) Basilar artery

**Answer: c) PCA (left)**

*Left PCA stroke → right homonymous hemianopia + visual agnosia/alexia without agraphia (disconnection of visual cortex from angular gyrus). PCA supplies occipital lobe, medial temporal lobe, thalamus. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 425*

---

### MCQ 3
The most common site of hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage is:

a) Cerebellum
b) Pons
c) Putamen (basal ganglia)
d) Thalamus

**Answer: c) Putamen (basal ganglia)**

*Putamen (~35-50%) > thalamus > pons > cerebellum > lobar. Hypertensive hemorrhage occurs from rupture of Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms in small perforating arteries (lenticulostriate branches of MCA for putamen). 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 425*

### MCQ 4
A patient presents with "worst headache of life," neck stiffness, and photophobia. CT head is normal. The next best step is:

a) MRI brain
b) Lumbar puncture
c) CT angiography
d) Repeat CT in 24 hours

**Answer: b) Lumbar puncture**

*CT sensitivity for SAH is 95% in first 12h but decreases over time. If clinical suspicion is high and CT is negative → LP showing xanthochromia (yellow supernatant from bilirubin) or RBCs with non-clearing in successive tubes. If positive → CT angiography to find aneurysm. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 425*

### MCQ 5
A 70-year-old with AF develops sudden onset right hemiparesis with aphasia. The most likely mechanism of stroke is:

a) Atherothrombotic
b) Cardioembolic
c) Lacunar
d) Watershed

**Answer: b) Cardioembolic**

*AF → left atrial thrombus → cerebral embolism (typically MCA territory). Cardioembolic strokes are usually sudden, maximal at onset. Secondary prevention: oral anticoagulation (DOACs preferred over warfarin — NOAC: dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban). CHA₂DS₂-VASc score guides decision. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 425*
