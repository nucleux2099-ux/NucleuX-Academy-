# Febrile Seizures — Explorer

## Overview

**Febrile seizures** are seizures occurring in children aged **6 months to 5 years**, associated with fever (≥38°C/100.4°F), without evidence of CNS infection or other defined cause. They are the **most common seizure type in childhood**, affecting 2-5% of children. Most are benign and self-limiting.

## Key Points

- Age: **6 months to 5 years**
- Most common seizure type in childhood
- **Simple febrile seizures** = benign, excellent prognosis
- **No routine investigations** needed for simple febrile seizures in well-appearing child ≥12 months
- **LP** indicated if meningitis suspected (especially <12 months)
- Recurrence risk: ~30% after first episode
- Risk of epilepsy: only slightly increased (2-4% vs 1% general population)

## Definition & Classification

| Feature | Simple Febrile Seizure | Complex Febrile Seizure |
|---|---|---|
| **Duration** | <15 minutes | ≥15 minutes |
| **Type** | Generalized (tonic-clonic) | Focal features |
| **Recurrence in 24 hrs** | No | Yes (≥1 more in same illness) |
| **Post-ictal** | Brief | May be prolonged; Todd's paralysis |
| **Frequency** | 80% of febrile seizures | 20% |
| **Prognosis** | Excellent | Slightly higher epilepsy risk |

### Febrile Status Epilepticus
- Febrile seizure lasting >30 minutes
- Requires emergency treatment
- ~5% of febrile seizures

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors for First Febrile Seizure
- **Family history** of febrile seizures (strong genetic component)
- Peak age: **12-18 months**
- High fever, rapid rise in temperature
- Viral infections (most common trigger): HHV-6 (roseola), influenza, adenovirus
- Post-vaccination (DPT, MMR — risk very small)

### Risk Factors for Recurrence (~30%)
- Young age at first seizure (<18 months)
- Family history of febrile seizures
- Lower temperature at seizure onset
- Brief duration of fever before seizure
- Frequent febrile illnesses

### Pathophysiology
- Immature thermoregulatory mechanisms in developing brain
- Genetic susceptibility (polygenic)
- Rate of temperature rise more important than peak temperature
- Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β) lower seizure threshold

## Clinical Features

- **Seizure with fever** in appropriate age group
- Most are generalized tonic-clonic
- Typically occur on **day 1 of fever** (often first sign of illness)
- Child appears well between episodes (simple FS)
- **No meningeal signs** (if present → investigate for meningitis)
- **Normal neurological exam** post-ictally (after brief recovery period)

### When to Worry (Red Flags)
- Age <6 months or >5 years
- Focal seizure or focal neurological deficit post-ictally
- Prolonged seizure (>15 min)
- Multiple seizures within 24 hours
- Bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, petechial rash
- Pre-existing neurological abnormality
- Incomplete immunization

## Diagnosis

### Simple Febrile Seizure — Minimal Workup

| Age | Investigations |
|---|---|
| **≥12 months, well-appearing** | No routine investigations needed; treat fever, observe |
| **6-12 months** | Consider LP (especially if unimmunized for Hib/pneumococcal) |
| **<6 months** | LP recommended (high risk of meningitis) |

### When to Investigate Further
- Complex febrile seizure → consider EEG, MRI
- Signs of meningitis → **LP mandatory**
- Prolonged post-ictal state → imaging
- Developmental delay → further workup

### Investigations (When Indicated)

| Test | Indication |
|---|---|
| **LP/CSF** | Suspected meningitis |
| **Blood glucose** | Prolonged seizure, poor recovery |
| **Electrolytes** | Dehydration, vomiting, diarrhea |
| **EEG** | Complex febrile seizure; NOT for simple FS (often abnormally interpreted) |
| **MRI brain** | Complex/focal features, developmental concerns |

## Management

### Acute Management (During Seizure)
1. **Position safely**, protect airway
2. **Do NOT put anything in mouth**
3. If seizure >5 minutes: **Rectal diazepam** (0.5 mg/kg) or **buccal/nasal midazolam** (0.2 mg/kg)
4. If >10-15 min: IV lorazepam, follow status epilepticus protocol
5. **Identify and treat fever source** (antipyretics for comfort, NOT to prevent recurrence)

### Post-Seizure
- **Reassurance** to parents — this is the most important intervention
- Explain benign nature and excellent prognosis
- Fever management for comfort (paracetamol/ibuprofen)
- **No daily AEDs needed** for simple febrile seizures
- **No evidence** that antipyretics prevent recurrence

### Indications for Prophylaxis (Controversial/Rare)

| Type | Agent |
|---|---|
| **Intermittent** | Rectal/oral diazepam at onset of fever (if very frequent/prolonged FS) |
| **Continuous** (rarely indicated) | Valproate, phenobarbital (significant side effects > benefit) |

### What NOT to Do
- ❌ Do NOT start daily AEDs for simple febrile seizures
- ❌ Do NOT order routine EEG for simple febrile seizure
- ❌ Do NOT order routine neuroimaging for simple febrile seizure
- ❌ Do NOT promise that antipyretics will prevent recurrence

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- Recurrence: ~30% after first, ~50% after second
- **Febrile status epilepticus** — 5%
- Epilepsy risk: 2-4% (slightly above general population 1%)
- Complex febrile seizures have slightly higher epilepsy risk (~6-8%)
- **No evidence** of cognitive or developmental harm from simple FS

### Risk Factors for Epilepsy After Febrile Seizures
- Complex febrile seizures
- Family history of epilepsy (not just FS)
- Pre-existing neurodevelopmental abnormality
- Recurrent febrile seizures

### Prognosis
- **Excellent** for simple febrile seizures
- No intellectual impairment
- No increased mortality
- Most children outgrow by age 5-6
