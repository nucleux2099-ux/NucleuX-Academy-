# Stroke Rehabilitation — Explorer

## Overview

**Stroke rehabilitation** is a multidisciplinary process aimed at maximizing functional recovery and quality of life after stroke. **Neuroplasticity** — the brain's ability to reorganize and form new neural connections — is the biological basis of recovery. Early and intensive rehabilitation improves outcomes.

## Key Points

- Begin rehabilitation **within 24-48 hours** of stroke (if medically stable)
- **Multidisciplinary team** approach is essential
- Maximum recovery occurs in **first 3-6 months** (but continues for years)
- Goals: maximize independence, prevent complications, improve quality of life
- Modified Rankin Scale (mRS) = key outcome measure

## Definition & Classification

### Phases of Rehabilitation

| Phase | Timing | Setting | Goals |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute** | Days 1-7 | Stroke unit | Medical stability, prevent complications, early mobilization |
| **Subacute** | Weeks 1-12 | Inpatient rehabilitation | Intensive therapy, functional recovery |
| **Chronic** | >3 months | Outpatient/community | Maintenance, adaptation, community reintegration |

### Stroke Unit Benefit
- Stroke unit care reduces mortality, dependency, and institutionalization
- **NNT = 18** to prevent one death or dependency
- Elements: dedicated ward, MDT, protocols, early mobilization

## Components of Rehabilitation

### Multidisciplinary Team

| Member | Role |
|---|---|
| **Neurologist/Physician** | Medical management, secondary prevention |
| **Physiotherapist** | Motor recovery, gait training, balance, spasticity |
| **Occupational therapist** | ADL training, upper limb, assistive devices, home modification |
| **Speech-language pathologist** | Aphasia therapy, swallowing assessment (dysphagia), cognitive-communication |
| **Neuropsychologist** | Cognitive rehabilitation, mood assessment |
| **Social worker** | Discharge planning, community resources |
| **Dietitian** | Nutritional optimization, dysphagia diet |
| **Nurse** | Continence, skin care, medication, patient education |

### Motor Rehabilitation

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Task-specific training** | Practicing functional tasks (gait, reaching, grasping) |
| **Constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT)** | Restraining unaffected limb to force use of affected limb |
| **Robot-assisted therapy** | Repetitive task training with robotic devices |
| **Mirror therapy** | Uses mirror to create visual feedback of affected limb moving |
| **Electrical stimulation (FES)** | Neuromuscular stimulation for muscle activation |
| **Treadmill training** | Body-weight supported treadmill for gait retraining |

### Communication Rehabilitation
- **Broca's aphasia** (non-fluent): Speech production exercises, melodic intonation therapy
- **Wernicke's aphasia** (fluent): Comprehension strategies, compensatory techniques
- **Dysarthria**: Articulation exercises, voice amplification

### Swallowing Rehabilitation
- Bedside swallow screen before oral intake
- Videofluoroscopy / FEES for formal assessment
- Texture-modified diet, postural techniques, swallow exercises
- PEG tube if prolonged unsafe swallowing

## Common Post-Stroke Complications

| Complication | Frequency | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Spasticity** | 25-40% | Physiotherapy, baclofen, botulinum toxin, splinting |
| **Post-stroke depression** | 30-50% | SSRIs (first-line), CBT |
| **Shoulder pain** | 20-70% | Positioning, sling, suprascapular nerve block |
| **Falls** | 25-50% | Balance training, environmental modification |
| **DVT/PE** | 10-50% | Early mobilization, compression stockings, anticoagulation |
| **Pressure sores** | Variable | Repositioning, pressure-relieving devices |
| **Urinary incontinence** | 40-60% | Bladder training, containment |
| **Post-stroke seizures** | 5-10% | AEDs if seizures occur |
| **Central post-stroke pain** | 5-10% | Amitriptyline, pregabalin |
| **Post-stroke fatigue** | 40-70% | Pacing, graded exercise |

## Outcome Measures

| Scale | Measures |
|---|---|
| **Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)** | Global disability (0-6); most used in trials |
| **Barthel Index** | ADL independence (0-100) |
| **NIHSS** | Stroke severity (0-42) |
| **FIM (Functional Independence Measure)** | Motor and cognitive function |

## Prognosis

### Predictors of Recovery

| Good Prognostic Factors | Poor Prognostic Factors |
|---|---|
| Young age | Large infarct volume |
| Mild stroke (low NIHSS) | Hemorrhagic transformation |
| Early rehabilitation | Severe neglect or anosognosia |
| Social support | Global aphasia |
| Motivated patient | Pre-existing dementia |
| Intact cognition | Medical comorbidities |

### Timeline
- **Fastest recovery**: First 3 months
- **Continued improvement**: Up to 1-2 years
- **Plateau**: Often by 6-12 months, but late gains possible
- Upper limb recovery is slower than lower limb/gait
