# Brain Tumors (Medical) — Explorer

## Overview

**Brain tumors** can be primary (arising from CNS tissue) or secondary (metastatic). In adults, **metastases** are the most common intracranial tumors overall, while **glioblastoma (GBM)** is the most common primary malignant brain tumor and **meningioma** is the most common primary benign tumor. Clinical features depend on location and rate of growth.

## Key Points

- **Metastases** = most common overall intracranial tumors in adults
- **GBM** = most common primary malignant brain tumor
- **Meningioma** = most common primary benign brain tumor
- In children: posterior fossa tumors predominate (medulloblastoma, pilocytic astrocytoma)
- Presentation: headache, seizures, focal deficits, raised ICP signs
- **MRI with gadolinium** = imaging of choice

## Definition & Classification

### WHO Classification (Simplified — Common Tumors)

| Tumor | Grade | Origin | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pilocytic astrocytoma** | I | Astrocytes | Children, cerebellum, cystic with mural nodule, excellent prognosis |
| **Diffuse astrocytoma** | II | Astrocytes | Young adults, IDH-mutant, may progress |
| **Anaplastic astrocytoma** | III | Astrocytes | Intermediate grade |
| **Glioblastoma (GBM)** | IV | Astrocytes | Most common malignant primary; "butterfly" lesion, necrosis, poor prognosis |
| **Oligodendroglioma** | II-III | Oligodendrocytes | "Fried egg" cells, calcification, 1p/19q co-deletion, better prognosis |
| **Meningioma** | I-III | Meningothelial cells | Extra-axial, dural-based, "tail sign," mostly benign |
| **Schwannoma (vestibular)** | I | Schwann cells | CP angle, bilateral = NF2, hearing loss |
| **Medulloblastoma** | IV | Embryonal | Children, posterior fossa, drop metastasis via CSF |
| **Ependymoma** | II-III | Ependymal cells | 4th ventricle, children, may cause hydrocephalus |
| **Craniopharyngioma** | I | Rathke's pouch | Suprasellar, calcified, visual field defects, bitemporal hemianopia |
| **Pituitary adenoma** | I | Pituitary cells | Sellar, hormonal effects + bitemporal hemianopia |

### Metastatic Brain Tumors

| Primary | Frequency |
|---|---|
| **Lung** | Most common source (~50%) |
| **Breast** | Second most common |
| **Melanoma** | High propensity for brain metastasis; hemorrhagic |
| **Renal cell carcinoma** | Hemorrhagic metastasis |
| **Colorectal** | Less common |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Ionizing radiation** — most established risk factor
- Genetic syndromes: NF1 (optic glioma), NF2 (bilateral schwannoma, meningioma), VHL (hemangioblastoma), Li-Fraumeni (GBM), Turcot (medulloblastoma/GBM)
- Immunosuppression → primary CNS lymphoma
- Age: GBM peaks 65-75 years

### Genetic Syndromes & Associated Tumors

| Syndrome | Gene | Tumor |
|---|---|---|
| **NF1** | NF1 (chr 17) | Optic pathway glioma, neurofibroma |
| **NF2** | NF2 (chr 22) | Bilateral vestibular schwannoma, meningioma |
| **VHL** | VHL (chr 3) | Hemangioblastoma, RCC |
| **Li-Fraumeni** | TP53 | GBM, various cancers |
| **Turcot** | APC/MMR | Medulloblastoma or GBM with colon polyps |
| **Tuberous sclerosis** | TSC1/TSC2 | Subependymal giant cell astrocytoma (SEGA) |

## Clinical Features

### General Presentations

| Feature | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Headache** | Raised ICP; worse in morning, coughing, bending |
| **Seizures** | Cortical irritation; may be first presentation |
| **Focal neurological deficit** | Depends on location |
| **Personality/cognitive changes** | Frontal lobe tumors |
| **Papilledema** | Raised ICP |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Raised ICP (projectile, worse in morning) |

### By Location

| Location | Features |
|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Personality change, anosmia (olfactory groove meningioma), motor deficits |
| **Temporal** | Seizures (most epileptogenic), aphasia (dominant), memory |
| **Parietal** | Sensory loss, spatial neglect, agraphia |
| **Occipital** | Visual field defects |
| **Cerebellopontine angle** | Hearing loss, facial weakness, cerebellar signs (schwannoma) |
| **Posterior fossa (children)** | Ataxia, hydrocephalus, vomiting |
| **Sellar/suprasellar** | Bitemporal hemianopia, hormonal dysfunction |

## Diagnosis

| Investigation | Details |
|---|---|
| **MRI brain with gadolinium** | Gold standard; characterizes tumor, edema, enhancement |
| **CT brain** | Faster; shows calcification (oligodendroglioma, craniopharyngioma), hemorrhage |
| **MR spectroscopy** | Choline ↑ (tumor), NAA ↓; lactate (high-grade) |
| **PET scan** | Metabolic activity, differentiates recurrence from radiation necrosis |
| **Stereotactic biopsy** | Definitive histological diagnosis |
| **Metastatic workup** | CT chest/abdomen/pelvis for metastatic disease |

## Management

### Principles
- **Surgery**: Maximum safe resection when possible
- **Radiation**: Adjuvant for high-grade tumors, stereotactic radiosurgery for metastases
- **Chemotherapy**: Temozolomide for GBM (with MGMT promoter methylation = better response)
- **Steroids**: Dexamethasone for peritumoral edema (reduces vasogenic edema)
- **Anticonvulsants**: Only if seizures occur (not prophylactic for all tumors)

### Specific Tumors

| Tumor | Key Management |
|---|---|
| **GBM** | Surgery + RT + Temozolomide (Stupp protocol); median survival 15 months |
| **Meningioma** | Surgical excision; observation if small/asymptomatic |
| **Metastases** | Stereotactic radiosurgery ± WBRT; surgery for solitary large metastasis |
| **Medulloblastoma** | Surgery + craniospinal RT + chemotherapy |
| **Pituitary adenoma** | Transsphenoidal surgery; bromocriptine/cabergoline for prolactinoma |
| **Primary CNS lymphoma** | High-dose methotrexate (NOT surgery — diffuse tumor) |

## Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
- Raised ICP and herniation
- Seizures (30-50%)
- Cognitive decline
- Radiation necrosis
- Treatment side effects (chemo, RT)

### Prognosis by Tumor

| Tumor | Median Survival |
|---|---|
| **GBM** | 15 months (with treatment) |
| **Anaplastic astrocytoma** | 3-5 years |
| **Oligodendroglioma** | 10-15 years |
| **Meningioma** | Excellent if completely excised |
| **Pilocytic astrocytoma** | >90% cure |
| **Brain metastases** | 6-12 months |
