# Acute Coronary Syndrome — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **Troponin** is the gold standard biomarker; elevated in STEMI & NSTEMI, normal in UA
- ★ **Door-to-balloon <90 min** (PCI) or **door-to-needle <30 min** (thrombolysis)
- ★ Most MIs occur at sites of **<50% stenosis** (plaque vulnerability ≠ degree of stenosis)
- ★ **Anterior MI** (LAD) → worst prognosis; **Inferior MI** (RCA) → may involve RV
- ★ **RV infarction:** Hypotension + clear lungs + JVP ↑ → fluids (NOT nitrates/diuretics!)
- ★ **Free wall rupture** (day 3-5) → tamponade → death; **Papillary muscle rupture** (day 5-7) → acute severe MR
- ★ **Dressler syndrome:** Autoimmune pericarditis 2-10 weeks post-MI → treat with aspirin/NSAIDs
- ★ **VF** = #1 cause of death in first hour; **Pump failure** = #1 after hospitalization
- ★ **New LBBB** in clinical context = STEMI equivalent → treat as STEMI
- ★ **DAPT** (aspirin + ticagrelor/clopidogrel) for 12 months post-ACS

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 55-year-old male with acute chest pain has ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF. He is hypotensive (BP 80/60) with JVP elevation and clear lung fields. The most likely diagnosis is:

a) Anterior wall STEMI with cardiogenic shock
b) Inferior wall STEMI with right ventricular infarction
c) Cardiac tamponade
d) Massive pulmonary embolism

**Answer: b) Inferior wall STEMI with right ventricular infarction**

*Inferior MI (leads II, III, aVF) + hypotension + elevated JVP + clear lungs = classic RV infarction triad. Confirm with V4R (ST elevation). Management: IV fluids, avoid nitrates and diuretics. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 269*

### MCQ 2
A patient develops a new pansystolic murmur with pulmonary edema 5 days after anterior STEMI. The most likely complication is:

a) Ventricular septal rupture
b) Papillary muscle rupture
c) Free wall rupture
d) Dressler syndrome

**Answer: b) Papillary muscle rupture**

*Day 5-7 post-MI: papillary muscle rupture → acute severe MR → pansystolic murmur + pulmonary edema. VSR also causes pansystolic murmur but with step-up in O2 saturation in RV. Both need urgent surgical repair. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 269*

### MCQ 3
Which of the following is an ABSOLUTE contraindication to thrombolysis in STEMI?

a) Menstruation
b) Prior ischemic stroke 6 months ago
c) Active peptic ulcer bleeding
d) Uncontrolled hypertension (>180/110)

**Answer: c) Active peptic ulcer bleeding**

*Absolute contraindications: active bleeding, hemorrhagic stroke (ever), ischemic stroke <3 months, aortic dissection, intracranial neoplasm, active internal bleeding. Prior ischemic stroke >3 months and uncontrolled HTN are relative contraindications. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 269*

### MCQ 4
The investigation of choice for detecting reinfarction within 2 weeks of an acute MI is:

a) Troponin I
b) CK-MB
c) LDH
d) Myoglobin

**Answer: b) CK-MB**

*CK-MB falls to normal by 48-72 hours, so a re-rise indicates reinfarction. Troponin remains elevated for 10-14 days, making it unreliable for detecting reinfarction in this window. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 269*

### MCQ 5
A patient with STEMI receives thrombolysis. Which is the BEST indicator of successful reperfusion?

a) Resolution of chest pain
b) >50% resolution of ST elevation within 60-90 minutes
c) Appearance of Q waves
d) Normalization of troponin

**Answer: b) >50% resolution of ST elevation within 60-90 minutes**

*Criteria for successful thrombolysis: ≥50% ST resolution in 60-90 min, relief of chest pain, reperfusion arrhythmias (AIVR — accelerated idioventricular rhythm), early peak of CK-MB. ST resolution is the most objective and reliable. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 269*
