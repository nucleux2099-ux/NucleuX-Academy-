## Overview

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) encompasses a spectrum of clinical presentations resulting from acute myocardial ischemia, including **unstable angina (UA)**, **non-ST elevation MI (NSTEMI)**, and **ST elevation MI (STEMI)**. It is caused by disruption of an atherosclerotic plaque with subsequent thrombus formation in a coronary artery.

## Key Points

- **Plaque rupture/erosion** with superimposed thrombus is the central pathology
- Classified into STEMI, NSTEMI, and unstable angina based on **ECG and biomarkers**
- Troponin is the **gold standard biomarker** — elevated in STEMI and NSTEMI, normal in UA
- STEMI requires **emergent reperfusion** (PCI or thrombolysis)
- **Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT)** is cornerstone of management
- Risk stratification using **TIMI** or **GRACE** score
- **Door-to-balloon time** < 90 min for primary PCI; **door-to-needle** < 30 min for thrombolysis
- Killip classification for hemodynamic assessment

## Definition & Classification

| Type | ECG | Troponin | Thrombus |
|------|-----|----------|----------|
| **STEMI** | ST elevation ≥ 2 contiguous leads | Elevated | Complete occlusion (red thrombus) |
| **NSTEMI** | ST depression / T wave inversion | Elevated | Partial occlusion (white thrombus) |
| **Unstable Angina** | ST depression / T inversion / Normal | Normal | Partial occlusion |

### Universal Classification of MI
| Type | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| **Type 1** | Spontaneous — plaque rupture/erosion |
| **Type 2** | Supply-demand mismatch (anemia, tachycardia, hypotension) |
| **Type 3** | Sudden cardiac death with ischemic symptoms |
| **Type 4a** | Related to PCI |
| **Type 4b** | Stent thrombosis |
| **Type 5** | Related to CABG |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Risk Factors
- **Non-modifiable:** Age (M >45, F >55), male sex, family history of premature CAD
- **Modifiable:** Smoking, hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, obesity, sedentary lifestyle

### Pathogenesis
1. **Atherosclerotic plaque** develops over years (lipid-rich core, thin fibrous cap = vulnerable plaque)
2. **Plaque rupture/erosion** exposes subendothelial collagen
3. **Platelet adhesion and aggregation** → platelet plug
4. **Coagulation cascade activation** → fibrin-rich thrombus
5. **Coronary occlusion** → myocardial ischemia → necrosis (if sustained)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Plaque vulnerability depends on lipid core size and fibrous cap thickness, NOT degree of stenosis. Most MIs occur at sites of <50% stenosis.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Chest pain:** Retrosternal, crushing/squeezing, radiating to left arm/jaw/back
- Duration >20 minutes (unlike stable angina)
- Associated with **diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea**
- **Atypical presentations:** Epigastric pain, isolated dyspnea, syncope — common in elderly, diabetics, women

### Signs
- Anxiety, diaphoresis, pallor
- **S4 gallop** (reduced ventricular compliance)
- **S3 gallop** (if LV dysfunction)
- New **mitral regurgitation murmur** (papillary muscle dysfunction)
- **Killip Class** assessment:

| Class | Features | Mortality |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| I | No heart failure | 6% |
| II | Crackles, S3, JVP ↑ | 17% |
| III | Pulmonary edema | 38% |
| IV | Cardiogenic shock | 81% |

## Diagnosis

### ECG (obtain within 10 minutes)
- **STEMI:** ST elevation ≥ 1mm in limb leads, ≥ 2mm in chest leads, in ≥ 2 contiguous leads
- **NSTEMI/UA:** ST depression ≥ 0.5mm, T wave inversion, or normal ECG
- **New LBBB** in appropriate clinical context = STEMI equivalent

### Localization by ECG

| Leads | Territory | Artery |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| V1-V4 | Anterior | LAD |
| V5-V6, I, aVL | Lateral | LCx |
| II, III, aVF | Inferior | RCA |
| V1-V2 (reciprocal) | Posterior | RCA/LCx |
| V3R, V4R | Right ventricle | RCA |

### Biomarkers
- **Troponin I/T:** Most sensitive and specific; rises in 3-6 hrs, peaks 12-24 hrs
- **High-sensitivity troponin:** Detectable within 1-3 hours
- **CK-MB:** Useful for detecting reinfarction (falls faster than troponin)
- **Myoglobin:** Earliest to rise but non-specific (not recommended)

### Other Investigations
- Chest X-ray (pulmonary edema, cardiomegaly)
- Echocardiography (RWMA, EF, complications)
- Coronary angiography (definitive)

## Management

### Immediate (MONA-B)
- **M**orphine (if pain persists despite nitrates)
- **O**xygen (only if SpO2 <94%)
- **N**itrates (sublingual → IV if ongoing pain; **contraindicated in RV infarction**)
- **A**spirin 325mg loading dose (chew)
- **B**eta-blocker (oral; avoid in cardiogenic shock, severe bradycardia)

### Antiplatelet Therapy
- **Aspirin** 325mg load → 75-81mg daily indefinitely
- **P2Y12 inhibitor:** Ticagrelor (preferred) or Clopidogrel → DAPT for 12 months

### Anticoagulation
- **Heparin** (UFH or enoxaparin) during acute phase
- **Fondaparinux** in NSTEMI (if conservative strategy)

### Reperfusion (STEMI)
- **Primary PCI** (preferred) — door-to-balloon < 90 min
- **Thrombolysis** — if PCI not available within 120 min; door-to-needle < 30 min
  - Agents: Tenecteplase, Alteplase, Streptokinase
  - **Contraindications:** Active bleeding, recent surgery, aortic dissection, prior hemorrhagic stroke

### NSTEMI/UA Management
- Early invasive strategy (angiography within 24-72 hrs) for high-risk patients
- Conservative strategy with stress testing for low-risk patients

### Long-term (Secondary Prevention)
- **DAPT** (Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) for 12 months
- **Statin** (high-intensity: Atorvastatin 80mg / Rosuvastatin 40mg)
- **ACE inhibitor/ARB** (especially if EF <40%)
- **Beta-blocker** (especially if EF <40%)
- **Lifestyle:** Smoking cessation, cardiac rehabilitation, dietary changes

## Complications & Prognosis

### Early Complications (within 1 week)
| Timing | Complication |
|--------|-------------|
| Hours | Arrhythmias (VT/VF), cardiogenic shock |
| 1-3 days | Pericarditis (early), RV infarction |
| 3-5 days | Free wall rupture → tamponade |
| 5-7 days | Papillary muscle rupture → acute MR |
| 5-7 days | Ventricular septal rupture |

### Late Complications
- **Dressler syndrome** (autoimmune pericarditis, 2-10 weeks post-MI)
- **LV aneurysm** (persistent ST elevation on ECG)
- **LV thrombus** (especially anterior MI) → systemic embolization
- **Heart failure** (chronic LV remodeling)

### Prognosis
- In-hospital mortality: STEMI ~7%, NSTEMI ~5%
- 1-year mortality: ~10-15% overall
- **Key prognostic factors:** Age, Killip class, EF, number of vessels involved

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most common cause of death in the first hour of MI is **ventricular fibrillation**. The most common cause of death after hospitalization is **pump failure (cardiogenic shock)**.
