## Overview

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, characterized by **disorganized atrial electrical activity** leading to irregular ventricular response. It significantly increases the risk of **stroke** (5-fold) and can lead to heart failure due to loss of atrial kick and tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy.

## Key Points

- **Irregularly irregular** pulse is the clinical hallmark
- ECG shows **absent P waves** with irregular R-R intervals and fibrillatory baseline
- Most common cause: **Hypertension**; in developing countries: **Rheumatic mitral stenosis**
- **CHA₂DS₂-VASc score** guides anticoagulation for stroke prevention
- **Rate control** (target HR <110 bpm) is preferred in most patients
- **Rhythm control** for symptomatic patients or recent-onset AF
- Anticoagulation is needed if CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥ 2 (males) or ≥ 3 (females)
- **DOACs** are preferred over warfarin (except in mechanical valves/moderate-severe MS)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Duration | Characteristics |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| **Paroxysmal** | <7 days | Self-terminating, usually <48 hrs |
| **Persistent** | >7 days | Requires cardioversion to terminate |
| **Long-standing persistent** | >12 months | Continuous, rhythm control attempted |
| **Permanent** | Accepted | Rate control strategy adopted |
| **Valvular AF** | Any | Associated with moderate-severe mitral stenosis or mechanical valve |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes (PIRATES mnemonic)
- **P**ulmonary: PE, COPD, pneumonia
- **I**schemia / Infarction
- **R**heumatic heart disease (mitral stenosis)
- **A**lcohol ("holiday heart"), Anemia
- **T**hyrotoxicosis, Theophylline
- **E**lectricity (cardioversion), Electrolytes
- **S**epsis, Surgery (post-cardiac)

### Pathophysiology
1. **Trigger:** Ectopic foci (usually from **pulmonary veins**)
2. **Substrate:** Atrial fibrosis, dilatation, electrical remodeling
3. **Perpetuation:** Multiple re-entrant wavelets maintain AF
4. Loss of **atrial contraction** → reduced cardiac output by 15-25%
5. **Stasis** in left atrial appendage → thrombus → stroke

> **Clinical Pearl:** "AF begets AF" — the longer AF persists, the harder it is to convert to sinus rhythm due to electrical and structural remodeling.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Palpitations** (most common)
- Dyspnea, fatigue, exercise intolerance
- Dizziness, presyncope
- May be **asymptomatic** (detected incidentally)

### Signs
- **Irregularly irregular pulse** — hallmark finding
- Pulse deficit (apical rate > radial rate)
- Variable intensity of S1
- Absent 'a' wave in JVP
- Signs of underlying cause (thyroid enlargement, mitral stenosis murmur)
- Signs of complications (stroke, heart failure)

### Complications
- **Stroke / systemic embolism** (most feared)
- Heart failure (tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy)
- Hemodynamic instability (especially with rapid ventricular rate)

## Diagnosis

### ECG
- **Absent P waves** — replaced by chaotic fibrillatory waves (f waves)
- **Irregularly irregular R-R intervals**
- Narrow QRS (unless aberrant conduction or pre-existing BBB)
- Ventricular rate typically 100-180 bpm (if untreated)

### Additional Investigations
- **TFTs** (rule out hyperthyroidism in all new AF)
- Echocardiography (LV function, valvular disease, LA size)
- **TEE** (transesophageal echo) — to detect LA appendage thrombus before cardioversion
- Coagulation profile, renal/liver function

### Stroke Risk Assessment — CHA₂DS₂-VASc

| Factor | Points |
|--------|--------|
| **C**ongestive heart failure | 1 |
| **H**ypertension | 1 |
| **A**ge ≥ 75 | 2 |
| **D**iabetes | 1 |
| **S**troke/TIA/thromboembolism | 2 |
| **V**ascular disease (MI, PAD, aortic plaque) | 1 |
| **A**ge 65-74 | 1 |
| **S**ex category (female) | 1 |

### Bleeding Risk — HAS-BLED Score
Hypertension, Abnormal renal/liver function, Stroke, Bleeding, Labile INR, Elderly, Drugs/alcohol

## Management

### Acute Management
1. **Hemodynamically unstable** → **DC cardioversion** (synchronized)
2. **Rate control** (stable patients):
   - **Beta-blockers** (metoprolol, atenolol) — first line
   - **Calcium channel blockers** (diltiazem, verapamil) — if beta-blockers contraindicated
   - **Digoxin** — adjunct in heart failure
   - **Amiodarone** — if refractory
3. **Rhythm control** (if < 48 hrs or adequately anticoagulated):
   - Pharmacological: Amiodarone, Flecainide (pill-in-pocket)
   - Electrical: DC cardioversion

### Anticoagulation
- **CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥ 2 (males) / ≥ 3 (females):** Anticoagulate
- **Score 1 (males) / 2 (females):** Consider anticoagulation
- **Score 0 (males) / 1 (females):** No anticoagulation needed
- **DOACs** (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban) — preferred
- **Warfarin** (target INR 2-3) — for valvular AF (moderate-severe MS, mechanical valves)

### Long-term Rate vs Rhythm Control

| Feature | Rate Control | Rhythm Control |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Goal | HR < 110 bpm | Maintain sinus rhythm |
| Drugs | BB, CCB, Digoxin | Amiodarone, Flecainide, Sotalol |
| Procedures | AV node ablation + pacemaker | Catheter ablation (PV isolation) |
| Preferred when | Elderly, asymptomatic | Young, symptomatic, HFrEF |

### Catheter Ablation
- **Pulmonary vein isolation** — curative in many patients
- Indicated for symptomatic AF refractory to medical therapy
- Better outcomes in paroxysmal AF

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Stroke risk:** 5% per year without anticoagulation (reduced to ~1% with DOACs)
- **Heart failure:** 20-30% of AF patients develop HF
- **Mortality:** 1.5-2x increased compared to matched controls
- Recurrence after cardioversion: ~50% at 1 year without antiarrhythmics
- **AF ablation** success rate: 70-80% at 1 year for paroxysmal AF

> **Clinical Pearl:** Always check TFTs in new-onset AF. Treating hyperthyroidism may restore sinus rhythm without needing antiarrhythmics.
