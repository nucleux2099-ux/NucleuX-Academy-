## Overview

Acute pericarditis is **inflammation of the pericardium** presenting with chest pain, pericardial friction rub, and characteristic ECG changes. It is the most common pericardial disease encountered in clinical practice. **Viral/idiopathic** causes account for 80-90% of cases in developed countries; **tuberculosis** is common in India.

## Key Points

- **Triad:** Chest pain (pleuritic, relieved by sitting forward) + friction rub + ECG changes
- ECG: **Diffuse concave ST elevation** + **PR depression** (Stage 1)
- **Pericardial friction rub** is pathognomonic (best heard with diaphragm, leaning forward)
- Most cases are **idiopathic/viral** — self-limiting
- Treatment: **NSAIDs + Colchicine** (reduces recurrence by 50%)
- **Avoid corticosteroids** as first-line (increased recurrence)
- Must differentiate from **STEMI** (concave vs convex ST elevation, diffuse vs territorial)
- **Colchicine** for 3 months — cornerstone of therapy (COPE and CORE trials)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Duration |
|------|----------|
| **Acute** | < 4-6 weeks |
| **Incessant** | >4-6 weeks but <3 months |
| **Recurrent** | Recurrence after symptom-free interval of ≥4-6 weeks |
| **Chronic** | >3 months |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Idiopathic** (80-90%) — assumed viral
- **Viral:** Coxsackie B, echovirus, adenovirus, EBV, HIV
- **Bacterial:** TB (most common in India), purulent (S. aureus, Strep)
- **Post-MI:** Early (within 24-72 hrs — reactive); Late = **Dressler syndrome** (2-10 weeks, autoimmune)
- **Autoimmune:** SLE, RA, scleroderma, sarcoidosis
- **Uremia** (CKD/dialysis patients)
- **Malignancy:** Lung, breast, lymphoma
- **Drugs:** Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid
- **Post-cardiac surgery:** Postpericardiotomy syndrome
- **Radiation**

### Pathophysiology
- Inflammation of pericardial layers → fibrin deposition → friction rub
- Exudation of fluid → pericardial effusion
- Inflammatory mediators → chest pain
- If chronic → fibrosis → constrictive pericarditis

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Chest pain:** Sharp, pleuritic, retrosternal
  - **Worse:** Lying supine, deep inspiration, coughing
  - **Better:** Sitting up and leaning forward
- Low-grade fever, malaise
- Preceded by viral prodrome (1-2 weeks)

### Signs
- **Pericardial friction rub** — scratchy, superficial, 3-component (best heard with diaphragm, patient leaning forward in end-expiration)
  - 3 components: Atrial systole, ventricular systole, ventricular diastole
  - May be evanescent (comes and goes)
- Tachycardia
- Signs of effusion if present

## Diagnosis

### ECG (4 Stages)

| Stage | Timing | Changes |
|-------|--------|---------|
| **Stage 1** | Days 1-7 | **Diffuse concave ST elevation** + **PR depression** (except aVR — PR elevation) |
| **Stage 2** | Week 1-2 | ST normalizes, T waves flatten |
| **Stage 3** | Week 2-3 | Diffuse T wave inversions |
| **Stage 4** | Weeks-months | ECG normalizes |

### Differentiating Pericarditis from STEMI

| Feature | Pericarditis | STEMI |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| ST elevation | Diffuse, concave ("saddle-shaped") | Territorial, convex |
| Reciprocal changes | Absent (except aVR) | Present |
| PR segment | Depressed | Normal |
| Q waves | Absent | Present (evolving) |
| Troponin | Normal or mildly elevated | Significantly elevated |

### Blood Tests
- **CRP/ESR:** Elevated (useful for monitoring)
- **Troponin:** Mildly elevated in myopericarditis
- CBC, renal function, TFTs, ANA, RF (based on clinical suspicion)
- **TB workup** in endemic areas (ADA, PCR)

### Echocardiography
- May be **normal** in isolated pericarditis
- Pericardial effusion (if present)
- Rule out tamponade
- Assess ventricular function

### Chest X-ray
- Usually normal in uncomplicated pericarditis
- Enlarged silhouette if significant effusion

## Management

### First-Line: NSAIDs + Colchicine
- **Aspirin** 750-1000mg TDS × 1-2 weeks then taper over 3-4 weeks
  OR **Ibuprofen** 600mg TDS × 1-2 weeks then taper
- **Colchicine** 0.5mg BD × 3 months (0.5mg OD if <70kg)
  - Reduces recurrence by ~50% (COPE, CORE trials)
  - Monitor CBC (rare bone marrow suppression)

### Second-Line: Corticosteroids
- Only if **contraindication** to NSAIDs/colchicine or **autoimmune/uremic** cause
- Low-dose prednisolone (0.25-0.5 mg/kg/day) with slow taper
- **Avoid as first-line** — increases recurrence rate

### Specific Causes
- **TB pericarditis:** ATT + corticosteroids (reduces mortality and constrictive pericarditis)
- **Uremic:** Intensify dialysis; NSAIDs less effective
- **Purulent:** IV antibiotics + surgical drainage
- **Post-MI/Dressler:** Aspirin preferred (NSAIDs may impair healing)

### Activity Restriction
- Avoid strenuous activity until symptoms resolve and CRP normalizes
- Athletes: Return to sport only after complete resolution (minimum 3 months)

### Recurrent Pericarditis
- Colchicine long-term (6-12 months)
- Corticosteroids (if refractory)
- **Anakinra** (IL-1 receptor antagonist) — for colchicine-resistant, corticosteroid-dependent recurrence
- Pericardiectomy (last resort)

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Recurrence:** 15-30% (reduced to ~15% with colchicine)
- **Pericardial effusion** → tamponade (rare in viral)
- **Constrictive pericarditis** (<1% in idiopathic; 20-30% in TB/purulent)
- **Myopericarditis** — troponin elevation, wall motion abnormalities

### Prognosis
- **Idiopathic/viral:** Excellent; most resolve in 1-2 weeks
- **TB:** Good if treated early; risk of constriction
- **Purulent:** High mortality without drainage
- **Malignant:** Poor (reflects underlying disease)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The combination of diffuse ST elevation + PR depression on ECG is virtually diagnostic of acute pericarditis. Always compare with prior ECG and look for the absence of reciprocal changes to differentiate from STEMI.
