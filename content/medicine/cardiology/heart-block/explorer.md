## Overview

Heart block refers to a delay or interruption in the conduction of electrical impulses from the atria to the ventricles through the **AV node and His-Purkinje system**. It ranges from benign first-degree block to life-threatening complete (third-degree) heart block requiring pacemaker implantation.

## Key Points

- **First-degree:** Prolonged PR interval (>200ms) — benign, no treatment needed
- **Second-degree Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach):** Progressive PR prolongation → dropped beat — usually benign
- **Second-degree Mobitz Type II:** Constant PR with sudden dropped beats — risk of progression to complete block
- **Third-degree (Complete):** No AV conduction — atria and ventricles beat independently
- **Inferior MI** → AV nodal block (usually transient, responsive to atropine)
- **Anterior MI** → infranodal block (poor prognosis, needs pacing)
- **Atropine** is first-line for symptomatic bradycardia; **pacing** for complete/Mobitz II block
- ECG is the primary diagnostic tool

## Definition & Classification

| Type | ECG Features | Block Level | Prognosis |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **First-degree** | PR > 200ms, all P waves conducted | AV node | Benign |
| **Mobitz Type I** | Progressive PR prolongation → dropped QRS | AV node | Usually benign |
| **Mobitz Type II** | Constant PR, sudden non-conducted P | Below AV node (His/Purkinje) | Risk of complete block |
| **2:1 Block** | Every other P conducted | Could be either | Assess with context |
| **High-grade** | ≥2 consecutive non-conducted P waves | Infranodal | Needs pacing |
| **Third-degree** | Complete AV dissociation | AV node or below | Needs pacing |

### Escape Rhythms in Complete Block

| Level | Rate | QRS |
|-------|------|-----|
| **Junctional** | 40-60 bpm | Narrow |
| **Ventricular** | 20-40 bpm | Wide |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Ischemic:** Inferior MI (AV nodal), Anterior MI (infranodal)
- **Degenerative:** Lev disease (fibrosis of conduction system), Lenègre disease
- **Drugs:** Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, amiodarone
- **Infectious:** Myocarditis, Lyme disease (reversible), Chagas disease
- **Infiltrative:** Sarcoidosis, amyloidosis
- **Post-surgical:** Valve surgery, septal myectomy
- **Congenital:** Anti-Ro/La antibodies in neonatal lupus (SLE mother)
- **Metabolic:** Hyperkalemia

### Pathophysiology
- AV node receives blood from **RCA** (85%) — vulnerable in inferior MI
- **His bundle** has dual blood supply — more resistant
- Bundle branches supplied by **LAD** — vulnerable in anterior MI
- Block at AV node → junctional escape (more reliable, narrow QRS)
- Block below AV node → ventricular escape (unreliable, wide QRS, risk of asystole)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms (depend on degree and ventricular rate)
- **First-degree/Mobitz I:** Usually asymptomatic
- **Mobitz II/Third-degree:**
  - Dizziness, presyncope, **syncope (Stokes-Adams attacks)**
  - Fatigue, exercise intolerance
  - Dyspnea
  - **Cannon 'a' waves** in JVP (atria contracting against closed TV)

### Signs
- **Bradycardia** (regular in complete block)
- **Variable S1 intensity** (changing PR relationship in complete block)
- **Cannon 'a' waves** in JVP
- Signs of low cardiac output
- Signs of underlying cause (MI, drug toxicity)

## Diagnosis

### ECG (Primary Diagnostic Tool)

**First-degree:**
- PR interval > 200ms, all P waves followed by QRS

**Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach):**
- Progressive PR prolongation → eventually P not conducted → cycle repeats
- RR interval shortens before dropped beat
- Grouped beating pattern

**Mobitz Type II:**
- Constant PR interval → sudden non-conducted P wave
- QRS often wide (infranodal block)
- May show 2:1, 3:1 ratios

**Third-degree (Complete):**
- **Complete AV dissociation** — P waves march through at one rate, QRS at another
- No relationship between P waves and QRS complexes
- Escape rate: Junctional (40-60, narrow) or Ventricular (20-40, wide)

### Additional Investigations
- Blood tests: Electrolytes (K+), digoxin level, TFTs
- Echocardiography: Structural heart disease
- Coronary angiography: If ischemia suspected
- Lyme serology: In endemic areas
- Cardiac MRI: Sarcoidosis, myocarditis

## Management

### Acute Management
1. **Assess hemodynamic stability**
2. **Atropine 0.5mg IV** (up to 3mg) — effective for AV nodal block; NOT for infranodal
3. **Isoprenaline infusion** — if atropine fails
4. **Temporary pacing** (transcutaneous → transvenous) — for symptomatic Mobitz II or complete block

### Permanent Pacemaker Indications
- **Third-degree heart block** (symptomatic or asymptomatic with wide QRS)
- **Mobitz Type II** (even if asymptomatic — risk of sudden complete block)
- **Symptomatic Mobitz Type I** (rare indication)
- **Post-MI complete block** that doesn't resolve
- **Alternating bundle branch block**

### Drug-Induced Block
- **Stop the offending drug**
- Digoxin toxicity: Digibind (digoxin-specific antibodies)
- Beta-blocker/CCB overdose: Glucagon, calcium, high-dose insulin

### No Treatment Needed
- Asymptomatic first-degree block
- Asymptomatic Mobitz Type I (especially in athletes — vagal tone)

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Stokes-Adams attacks** (syncope from transient asystole)
- **Sudden cardiac death** (especially Mobitz II progressing to complete block)
- **Heart failure** (chronic bradycardia)
- **Pacemaker complications:** Infection, lead displacement, pacemaker syndrome

### Prognosis
- First-degree: Excellent
- Mobitz I: Excellent (rarely progresses)
- Mobitz II: Poor without pacing (high risk of complete block)
- Complete block: Depends on level and underlying cause; good with pacing

> **Clinical Pearl:** In complete heart block, look at the QRS width to determine the escape level. Narrow QRS = junctional escape (more stable). Wide QRS = ventricular escape (unstable, needs urgent pacing).
