## Overview

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is a **genetic cardiac disease** characterized by **asymmetric left ventricular hypertrophy** (typically involving the interventricular septum) in the absence of a hemodynamic cause. It is the **most common cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes** and has autosomal dominant inheritance.

## Key Points

- **Autosomal dominant** — mutations in sarcomeric proteins (β-myosin heavy chain, myosin-binding protein C)
- **Asymmetric septal hypertrophy (ASH)** with normal or reduced LV cavity size
- **LVOT obstruction** in ~70% (HOCM) — worsened by decreased preload/afterload or increased contractility
- **Systolic anterior motion (SAM)** of the mitral valve is characteristic
- **Sudden cardiac death** in young athletes — most feared complication
- **Diastolic dysfunction** is the primary hemodynamic problem
- **Avoid:** Digoxin, vasodilators, strenuous exercise, dehydration
- Treatment: Beta-blockers, verapamil; ICD for SCD risk; Mavacamten (new)

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Obstruction | Features |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Obstructive (HOCM)** | LVOT gradient ≥ 30 mmHg | ~70%, classic presentation |
| **Non-obstructive** | LVOT gradient < 30 mmHg | ~30% |
| **Apical HCM** | None | "Ace of spades" on ventriculography, common in Japanese |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Genetics
- **Autosomal dominant** with variable penetrance
- Most common mutations:
  - **β-myosin heavy chain (MYH7)** — 40%
  - **Myosin-binding protein C (MYBPC3)** — 40%
  - Troponin T, Troponin I, α-tropomyosin

### Pathophysiology
1. **Sarcomere dysfunction** → abnormal force generation
2. **Myocyte hypertrophy + disarray** → asymmetric septal thickening
3. **Diastolic dysfunction** → impaired relaxation → elevated filling pressures
4. **LVOT obstruction** (in HOCM):
   - Thickened septum narrows outflow tract
   - **SAM of mitral valve** → leaflet pulled into outflow by Venturi effect
   - SAM also causes **mitral regurgitation**
5. **Myocardial ischemia** (increased O₂ demand, compressed intramural arteries)
6. **Arrhythmias** (disorganized myocyte architecture)

### Factors Affecting LVOT Gradient

| Increases Gradient (↑ Obstruction) | Decreases Gradient (↓ Obstruction) |
|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| ↓ Preload (dehydration, Valsalva) | ↑ Preload (squatting, leg elevation) |
| ↓ Afterload (vasodilators) | ↑ Afterload (phenylephrine, squatting) |
| ↑ Contractility (digoxin, exercise) | ↓ Contractility (beta-blockers) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** HCM murmur INCREASES with Valsalva and standing (reduced preload). It DECREASES with squatting and leg elevation. This is opposite to most other murmurs.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnea on exertion** (most common — diastolic dysfunction)
- **Syncope/presyncope** (LVOT obstruction or arrhythmia)
- **Chest pain** (angina — demand ischemia)
- **Palpitations** (AF, VT)
- May be **asymptomatic** — diagnosed on screening or post-SCD

### Signs
- **Ejection systolic murmur** at left lower sternal border
  - Increases with Valsalva/standing
  - Decreases with squatting
- **Bifid (bisferiens) pulse** — in HOCM
- **Prominent 'a' wave** in JVP (reduced RV compliance)
- **S4 gallop** (stiff ventricle)
- **Double apical impulse**
- MR murmur (from SAM)

## Diagnosis

### ECG
- **LVH** by voltage criteria
- Deep **Q waves** (septal depolarization) — especially lateral leads
- T wave inversions (especially in apical HCM — giant T inversions)
- May show WPW pattern, AF

### Echocardiography (Key Investigation)
- **Asymmetric septal hypertrophy** (septum:posterior wall ratio > 1.3)
- Wall thickness ≥ 15mm (in the absence of another cause)
- **SAM of anterior mitral leaflet**
- **LVOT gradient** (measured with continuous-wave Doppler)
- Small LV cavity, hyperdynamic systolic function
- Diastolic dysfunction
- Mitral regurgitation

### Cardiac MRI
- Gold standard for morphology
- Late gadolinium enhancement (fibrosis) — correlates with SCD risk

### Genetic Testing
- Confirmatory; useful for family screening

## Management

### Lifestyle
- **Avoid competitive sports and strenuous exercise**
- Avoid dehydration
- Screen first-degree relatives (echo + genetic testing)

### Medical
- **Beta-blockers** (first-line) — reduce HR, improve diastolic filling, reduce LVOT gradient
- **Verapamil** — if beta-blockers not tolerated
- **Disopyramide** — negative inotrope, reduces gradient
- **Mavacamten** — cardiac myosin inhibitor (new, specifically for obstructive HCM)
- **Avoid:** Digoxin, vasodilators, diuretics (reduce preload), ACEi/ARB (reduce afterload)

### Interventional (for refractory HOCM)
- **Septal myectomy** (Morrow procedure) — surgical gold standard
- **Alcohol septal ablation** — percutaneous alternative (inject alcohol into septal perforator)

### SCD Prevention — Risk Stratification
- **ICD implantation** for high-risk patients

**ESC SCD Risk Factors:**
- Family history of SCD
- Unexplained syncope
- Massive LVH (≥ 30mm)
- NSVT on Holter
- Abnormal BP response to exercise
- LGE on MRI

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Sudden cardiac death** (annual risk 0.5-1% in adults, higher in young)
- Atrial fibrillation (20-25%) → stroke risk
- Heart failure (progressive diastolic → eventual systolic)
- Infective endocarditis (on mitral valve)
- "Burnt-out" HCM → systolic dysfunction → resembles DCM

### Prognosis
- Annual mortality ~1% (much lower than historically thought)
- Most patients have near-normal life expectancy with proper management
- SCD risk highest in adolescents and young adults

> **Clinical Pearl:** HCM is the most common cause of sudden cardiac death in athletes <35 years. Pre-participation screening with ECG and echocardiography can be life-saving.
