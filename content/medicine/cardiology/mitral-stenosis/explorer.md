## Overview

Mitral stenosis (MS) is the narrowing of the mitral valve orifice that obstructs blood flow from the left atrium to the left ventricle during diastole. **Rheumatic heart disease** is the most common cause worldwide, especially in developing countries. MS leads to left atrial dilatation, pulmonary hypertension, and right heart failure.

## Key Points

- **Rheumatic heart disease** is the cause in >95% of cases in India
- Normal mitral valve area: **4-6 cm²**; severe MS: **<1 cm²**
- Classic auscultatory finding: **Opening snap + low-pitched mid-diastolic rumble** at apex
- **Atrial fibrillation** is the most common complication
- **Hemoptysis** may occur (bronchial vein rupture from pulmonary HTN)
- Medical: Rate control, anticoagulation (if AF), diuretics
- Intervention: **PTMC (percutaneous transvenous mitral commissurotomy)** for suitable valves
- Pregnancy worsens MS (increased blood volume and heart rate)

## Definition & Classification

### Severity Grading

| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|-----------|------|----------|--------|
| **Valve area (cm²)** | >1.5 | 1.0-1.5 | <1.0 |
| **Mean gradient (mmHg)** | <5 | 5-10 | >10 |
| **PA systolic pressure** | <30 | 30-50 | >50 |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Rheumatic heart disease** (>95%) — post-streptococcal autoimmune
- Calcific (degenerative) — elderly
- Congenital (parachute mitral valve, cor triatriatum)
- SLE (Libman-Sacks endocarditis)
- Carcinoid
- Mucopolysaccharidoses

### Pathological Changes in Rheumatic MS
- **Commissural fusion** (most important)
- Leaflet thickening and fibrosis
- Chordal shortening and fusion
- Calcification (in advanced disease)
- "Fish-mouth" or "buttonhole" orifice

### Pathophysiology
1. Mitral valve obstruction → **LA pressure rises**
2. LA dilatation → **atrial fibrillation**
3. Elevated LA pressure transmitted to pulmonary veins → **pulmonary congestion**
4. Reactive pulmonary arteriolar vasoconstriction → **pulmonary arterial hypertension**
5. Chronic PH → **RV hypertrophy → RV failure**
6. **Cardiac output decreases** (fixed obstruction limits LV filling)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In MS, the left ventricle is typically normal or small (underfilled). The pathology is "behind" the valve — in the LA and pulmonary vasculature.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnea on exertion** (earliest and most common)
- Orthopnea, PND
- **Hemoptysis** (rupture of bronchial veins)
- Palpitations (AF)
- Fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance
- **Precipitants of symptoms:** Pregnancy, AF, exercise, anemia, fever, thyrotoxicosis

### Signs

**Face:** **Mitral facies** (malar flush — bilateral cyanotic flush on cheeks due to low CO and PH)

**Pulse:** Low volume; irregularly irregular if AF

**JVP:** Elevated if RV failure; prominent 'a' wave (if in sinus rhythm with PH)

**Palpation:**
- Tapping apex beat (palpable S1)
- Left parasternal heave (RV hypertrophy)
- Palpable P2

**Auscultation (Classic Findings):**
- **Loud S1** (forceful closure of pliable valve leaflets; decreases with calcification)
- **Opening snap (OS)** — high-pitched, after S2 (closer to S2 = more severe)
- **Low-pitched mid-diastolic rumble** — best heard at apex, left lateral position, with bell
- **Pre-systolic accentuation** — atrial contraction in sinus rhythm (lost in AF)
- **Loud P2** — if pulmonary hypertension
- **Graham Steell murmur** — early diastolic murmur of pulmonary regurgitation (severe PH)

### Severity Clues on Auscultation
- **Shorter S2-OS interval** = more severe (higher LA pressure opens valve earlier)
- **Longer duration of diastolic murmur** = more severe

## Diagnosis

### ECG
- **P mitrale** (bifid P wave in lead II) — LA enlargement (if sinus rhythm)
- Atrial fibrillation
- RVH pattern (right axis deviation, tall R in V1) — if PH present

### Chest X-ray
- **Straightening of left heart border** (LA appendage enlargement)
- **Double density sign** (enlarged LA seen through heart shadow)
- **Splaying of carina** (LA lifts left main bronchus)
- **Upper lobe pulmonary venous diversion** (cephalization)
- Kerley B lines (septal edema)
- Enlarged pulmonary arteries (if PH)

### Echocardiography (Investigation of Choice)
- **Valve area** by planimetry and pressure half-time
- **Mean transmitral gradient**
- **Wilkins score** (for PTMC suitability): Leaflet mobility, thickening, calcification, subvalvular involvement (each 0-4; total ≤8 favorable for PTMC)
- LA size, PA pressure, RV function
- Rule out LA thrombus (TEE before PTMC)

### Cardiac Catheterization
- Transmitral gradient
- PA pressures
- Gorlin formula for valve area (rarely used now)

## Management

### Medical
- **Diuretics** — for pulmonary congestion
- **Rate control in AF** — Beta-blockers or digoxin (control ventricular rate)
- **Anticoagulation** — Warfarin (target INR 2-3) for AF, prior embolism, or LA thrombus
  - DOACs NOT recommended in valvular AF (mitral stenosis)
- **Penicillin prophylaxis** — secondary prevention of rheumatic fever
- **IE prophylaxis** — for dental procedures (if prosthetic valve)

### Interventional

**PTMC (Balloon Mitral Valvotomy):**
- **Preferred intervention** for suitable valves
- Indications: Symptomatic moderate-severe MS with favorable valve morphology
- **Wilkins score ≤ 8**, no significant MR, no LA thrombus
- Success rate >90%, low complication rate

**Surgical:**
- **Open mitral commissurotomy** — if PTMC not suitable
- **Mitral valve replacement (MVR)** — calcified, distorted valve
  - Mechanical valve: Lifelong warfarin
  - Bioprosthetic: Limited durability (10-15 years)

### MS in Pregnancy
- High-risk (especially if valve area <1.5 cm²)
- Rate control with beta-blockers
- Diuretics if needed
- PTMC can be performed in second trimester if medically refractory
- Avoid warfarin in first trimester (teratogenic)

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Atrial fibrillation** (most common complication)
- **Systemic thromboembolism** — stroke (especially with AF + LA thrombus)
- **Pulmonary hypertension** → RV failure
- **Hemoptysis** (can be massive)
- **Infective endocarditis** (lower risk than regurgitant lesions)
- **Pulmonary edema** (precipitated by AF, pregnancy, exercise)

### Prognosis
- Without intervention: 10-year survival ~50-60% once symptoms develop
- After PTMC: Excellent for 10-15 years if good valve morphology
- After MVR: 10-year survival ~70%

> **Clinical Pearl:** In India, always suspect rheumatic MS in a young woman presenting with AF, hemoptysis, or dyspnea — especially with mitral facies and an apical diastolic rumble.
