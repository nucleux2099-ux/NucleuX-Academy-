## Overview

Myocarditis is **inflammation of the myocardium** characterized by myocyte necrosis and inflammatory infiltrate, most commonly caused by **viral infections**. It can mimic acute MI and is an important cause of **acute heart failure and sudden cardiac death** in young adults.

## Key Points

- **Viral infection** is the most common cause (Coxsackie B, Parvovirus B19, adenovirus)
- Presentation mimics **acute MI** (chest pain, ST changes, troponin elevation)
- **Cardiac MRI** is the best non-invasive diagnostic tool (Lake Louise criteria)
- **Endomyocardial biopsy** is the gold standard but rarely done
- Most cases are **self-limiting** with supportive care
- Can progress to **dilated cardiomyopathy** in 10-20%
- **Avoid NSAIDs** in acute phase (increase myocardial damage in animal models)
- Giant cell myocarditis and eosinophilic myocarditis have worse prognosis

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Features | Cause |
|------|----------|-------|
| **Lymphocytic** | Most common, self-limiting | Viral |
| **Giant cell** | Aggressive, high mortality | Autoimmune |
| **Eosinophilic** | Eosinophilic infiltrate | Hypersensitivity, parasites |
| **Granulomatous** | Granulomas in myocardium | Sarcoidosis |

### Clinical Classification
- **Fulminant:** Acute severe, distinct onset, hemodynamic compromise
- **Acute:** Insidious onset, dilated ventricle, may progress to DCM
- **Chronic active:** Ongoing inflammation with progressive dysfunction
- **Chronic persistent:** Persistent infiltrate but preserved function

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Viral** (most common): Coxsackie B (classic), Parvovirus B19, Adenovirus, EBV, HIV, Influenza, SARS-CoV-2
- **Bacterial:** Diphtheria (toxin-mediated), Lyme disease, Mycoplasma
- **Parasitic:** Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi — Latin America)
- **Autoimmune:** SLE, sarcoidosis, giant cell myocarditis
- **Drug/toxin:** Doxorubicin, checkpoint inhibitors, cocaine
- **Hypersensitivity:** Sulfonamides, penicillin, methyldopa

### Pathophysiology
1. **Direct viral cytotoxicity** → myocyte damage
2. **Immune response** → T-cell and antibody-mediated injury
3. Inflammatory infiltrate + myocyte necrosis
4. Cytokine release → further damage
5. **Molecular mimicry** → autoimmune perpetuation
6. Fibrosis and remodeling → DCM (in chronic cases)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Often preceded by **viral prodrome** (1-2 weeks) — fever, myalgia, pharyngitis, GI symptoms
- **Chest pain** (pericardial involvement — myopericarditis)
- Dyspnea, fatigue
- Palpitations
- Syncope or sudden death (arrhythmia)

### Signs
- Tachycardia (out of proportion to fever)
- **S3 gallop** (ventricular dysfunction)
- Signs of heart failure (elevated JVP, pulmonary crackles, edema)
- Pericardial friction rub (if myopericarditis)
- Arrhythmias

## Diagnosis

### Blood Tests
- **Troponin** elevated (confirms myocyte necrosis)
- **CRP/ESR** elevated
- **BNP/NT-proBNP** elevated (if heart failure)
- Viral serology (limited utility)
- CBC (leukocytosis, eosinophilia if eosinophilic)

### ECG
- Diffuse ST elevation (pericarditis pattern) or regional ST changes
- T wave inversions
- Sinus tachycardia
- Arrhythmias (VT, AV block)
- May mimic STEMI (perform angiography to exclude MI)

### Echocardiography
- Global or regional wall motion abnormalities
- Reduced EF (may be normal in mild cases)
- Pericardial effusion
- LV dilatation (in severe cases)

### Cardiac MRI (Best Non-Invasive Test)
**Lake Louise Criteria** (≥ 2 of 3):
1. **T2-weighted:** Myocardial edema (high signal)
2. **T1-weighted/EGE:** Hyperemia, capillary leak
3. **Late gadolinium enhancement:** Myocardial necrosis/fibrosis (epicardial/midwall pattern — NOT subendocardial as in MI)

### Endomyocardial Biopsy (Gold Standard)
- **Dallas criteria:** Inflammatory infiltrate + myocyte necrosis
- Indicated in: Fulminant myocarditis, suspected giant cell myocarditis, new-onset HF unresponsive to therapy
- Limited by sampling error

## Management

### Supportive (Mainstay)
- **Bed rest** and activity restriction (avoid exercise for 3-6 months)
- Standard **heart failure therapy** if EF reduced (ACEi, BB, diuretics)
- **Avoid NSAIDs** (increased inflammation in animal studies)
- Anticoagulation if severe LV dysfunction (thrombus risk)

### Arrhythmia Management
- Temporary pacing for complete heart block
- Antiarrhythmics for VT
- ICD: Avoid permanent ICD in acute phase (may recover); reassess at 3-6 months

### Specific Therapy
- **Giant cell myocarditis:** Immunosuppression (cyclosporine + corticosteroids)
- **Eosinophilic:** Corticosteroids
- **Sarcoid:** Corticosteroids
- **Chagas:** Benznidazole (acute phase)
- **Fulminant:** MCS (mechanical circulatory support) — ECMO, Impella as bridge

### Avoid
- NSAIDs (acute phase)
- Exercise (minimum 3-6 months)
- Premature ICD implantation

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Full recovery** in majority (50-70%) — especially lymphocytic
- **Dilated cardiomyopathy** in 10-20%
- **Sudden cardiac death** (VT/VF)
- **Chronic heart failure**
- **Giant cell myocarditis:** 5-year transplant-free survival ~10% without immunosuppression

### Prognosis
- Fulminant myocarditis paradoxically has better long-term prognosis (if survived acutely) — complete recovery in ~90%
- Acute myocarditis: ~70% recover, ~20% develop DCM
- Giant cell: Poor without immunosuppression or transplant

> **Clinical Pearl:** Young patient with chest pain + troponin elevation + normal coronaries on angiography = think myocarditis. Cardiac MRI will confirm.
