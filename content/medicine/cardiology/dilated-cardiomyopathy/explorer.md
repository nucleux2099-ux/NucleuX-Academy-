## Overview

Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) is characterized by **dilatation and impaired systolic function** of the left ventricle (or both ventricles) in the absence of abnormal loading conditions (valvular disease, hypertension) or coronary artery disease sufficient to explain the dysfunction. It is the **most common cardiomyopathy** and the leading indication for cardiac transplantation.

## Key Points

- **Four-chamber dilatation** with reduced ejection fraction is the hallmark
- Most common cause of **systolic heart failure** in young adults
- **Idiopathic** (50%), familial/genetic (30-50%), viral myocarditis, alcohol, peripartum
- Presents with symptoms of **biventricular failure**
- Echocardiography shows **dilated LV with globally reduced contractility**
- Treatment follows standard **heart failure guidelines** (ACEi/ARB, BB, MRA, SGLT2i)
- **ICD** for primary prevention if EF ≤ 35% despite 3 months of GDMT
- Cardiac transplant for refractory cases

## Definition & Classification

| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Idiopathic DCM** | No identifiable cause |
| **Familial DCM** | Autosomal dominant (TTN mutations most common) |
| **Alcoholic cardiomyopathy** | >80g/day for >5 years; reversible if alcohol stopped |
| **Peripartum cardiomyopathy** | Last month of pregnancy to 5 months postpartum |
| **Tachycardia-mediated** | Chronic tachycardia (AF, SVT); reversible |
| **Chemotherapy-induced** | Doxorubicin, Trastuzumab |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Idiopathic** (most common — ~50%)
- **Genetic:** Titin (TTN), Lamin A/C, Desmoplakin mutations — autosomal dominant
- **Post-viral myocarditis:** Coxsackie B, Parvovirus B19, HIV
- **Toxic:** Alcohol, cocaine, doxorubicin, cobalt
- **Metabolic:** Thyroid disease, thiamine deficiency (wet beriberi), selenium deficiency (Keshan disease)
- **Peripartum:** Immune/hormonal mechanisms
- **Autoimmune:** SLE, sarcoidosis

### Pathophysiology
1. Myocyte injury → progressive loss of functioning myocardium
2. **Ventricular dilatation** (Frank-Starling mechanism initially compensates)
3. Increased wall stress → neurohormonal activation (RAAS, SNS)
4. Further remodeling → reduced ejection fraction
5. Mitral annular dilatation → **functional mitral regurgitation**
6. Stasis → **intracardiac thrombus** risk

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnea** (exertional → orthopnea → PND)
- Fatigue, exercise intolerance
- Palpitations (arrhythmias)
- Abdominal distension, ankle swelling (right heart failure)
- Syncope (arrhythmias or low output)

### Signs
- **Displaced apex** beat (dilated LV) — laterally and inferiorly
- **S3 gallop** (volume overload)
- Functional **MR murmur** (pansystolic at apex)
- Elevated JVP
- Bilateral basal crepitations
- Hepatomegaly, pedal edema, ascites
- Proportional pulse pressure (low pulse pressure)

## Diagnosis

### Echocardiography (Key Investigation)
- **Dilated LV** (LVEDD >55mm)
- **Reduced EF** (<40%)
- Global hypokinesis (diffuse, unlike regional wall motion abnormality in IHD)
- Functional MR
- LA and RV dilatation in advanced cases
- May see LV thrombus

### Other Investigations
- **ECG:** Sinus tachycardia, poor R wave progression, LBBB, atrial fibrillation
- **Chest X-ray:** Cardiomegaly, pulmonary congestion, pleural effusion
- **BNP/NT-proBNP:** Elevated
- **Cardiac MRI:** Midwall fibrosis pattern (not subendocardial as in IHD)
- **Coronary angiography:** To exclude ischemic etiology
- **Genetic testing:** If familial DCM suspected
- **Endomyocardial biopsy:** Rarely needed; for specific diagnoses (sarcoidosis, giant cell myocarditis)

## Management

### Medical (Standard HF Therapy)
- **ACE inhibitor/ARB** (or ARNI — Sacubitril/Valsartan)
- **Beta-blocker** (Carvedilol, Bisoprolol, Metoprolol succinate)
- **Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist** (Spironolactone/Eplerenone)
- **SGLT2 inhibitor** (Dapagliflozin/Empagliflozin)
- **Diuretics** for congestion (not mortality benefit)
- **Digoxin** — symptom relief in AF
- **Anticoagulation** — if AF, LV thrombus, or very low EF

### Device Therapy
- **ICD:** Primary prevention if EF ≤ 35% after 3 months of GDMT
- **CRT (Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy):** If EF ≤ 35% + LBBB + QRS ≥ 150ms
- **LVAD (Left Ventricular Assist Device):** Bridge to transplant or destination therapy

### Specific Causes
- **Alcohol:** Abstinence → may recover fully
- **Peripartum:** Bromocriptine may help; may recover in 50%
- **Tachycardia-mediated:** Rate/rhythm control → full recovery

### Cardiac Transplant
- For patients with refractory symptoms despite maximal therapy

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Arrhythmias:** AF, VT, VF — sudden cardiac death
- **Thromboembolism:** LV thrombus → stroke
- **Progressive heart failure**
- **Functional MR** worsening
- 5-year survival: ~50% without transplant (improving with modern therapy)
- Better prognosis: Younger age, shorter duration, absence of LBBB, reversible cause

> **Clinical Pearl:** In a young patient with new-onset heart failure, always check for reversible causes — thyroid function, alcohol history, viral serology, iron studies, and drug history (chemotherapy).
