## Overview

Myocardial infarction (MI) is **irreversible myocardial necrosis** resulting from prolonged ischemia, most commonly due to acute coronary artery occlusion by thrombus over a ruptured atherosclerotic plaque. STEMI represents complete coronary occlusion requiring **emergent reperfusion therapy**.

## Key Points

- **STEMI** = complete occlusion → transmural infarction; **NSTEMI** = partial occlusion → subendocardial
- Diagnosis: **Chest pain + ECG changes + troponin elevation**
- **ST elevation** ≥ 1mm in ≥ 2 contiguous leads (or ≥ 2mm in V1-V3)
- **Primary PCI** preferred (door-to-balloon <90 min); thrombolysis if PCI unavailable
- **Troponin** is the gold standard biomarker; rises in 3-6 hours
- **Anterior MI** (LAD) → worst prognosis; **inferior MI** (RCA) → may cause heart block
- Complications: Arrhythmias, cardiogenic shock, mechanical (free wall rupture, VSD, papillary muscle rupture)
- Secondary prevention: DAPT, statin, ACEi, beta-blocker, cardiac rehab

## Definition & Classification

### By ECG Pattern
| Type | ECG | Pathology |
|------|-----|-----------|
| **STEMI** | ST elevation + Q waves (later) | Transmural necrosis |
| **NSTEMI** | ST depression / T inversion | Subendocardial necrosis |

### By Territory

| Territory | Leads | Artery | Complications |
|-----------|-------|--------|---------------|
| **Anterior** | V1-V4 | LAD | LV failure, VT/VF, apical aneurysm |
| **Lateral** | V5-V6, I, aVL | LCx | LV failure |
| **Inferior** | II, III, aVF | RCA (85%) | Heart block, RV infarct |
| **Posterior** | Reciprocal V1-V2 | RCA/LCx | Bradycardia |
| **RV infarct** | V3R, V4R | RCA | Hypotension, clear lungs, elevated JVP |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Sequence of Events
1. **Atherosclerotic plaque rupture/erosion**
2. Platelet adhesion → aggregation → **white thrombus**
3. Coagulation cascade → **red thrombus** (fibrin-rich)
4. Complete coronary occlusion
5. Myocardial ischemia → **wavefront of necrosis** (subendocardium → transmural)

### Timeline of Necrosis
| Time | Pathological Change |
|------|-------------------|
| **0-30 min** | Reversible injury (ATP depletion) |
| **30-60 min** | Irreversible injury begins (subendocardial) |
| **3-6 hours** | Coagulative necrosis, edema |
| **1-3 days** | Neutrophil infiltration, softening |
| **3-7 days** | Macrophage infiltration, granulation tissue (WEAKEST point — risk of rupture) |
| **2-8 weeks** | Fibrosis, scar formation |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Days 3-7 post-MI is the most dangerous period for mechanical complications (free wall rupture, VSD, papillary muscle rupture) because the necrotic tissue is softest before scar formation.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Severe crushing chest pain** — retrosternal, >20 min, not relieved by rest/GTN
- Radiation to left arm, jaw, back, epigastrium
- Diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting
- Dyspnea, anxiety
- **Atypical:** Silent MI (diabetics, elderly), epigastric pain, syncope

### Signs
- Diaphoresis, pallor, anxiety
- S4 (always), S3 (if LV dysfunction)
- New murmur (MR — papillary dysfunction; VSD — harsh pansystolic)
- Hypotension (cardiogenic shock or RV infarct)
- Elevated JVP (RV infarct or heart failure)
- Killip classification for hemodynamic assessment

## Diagnosis

### ECG Evolution in STEMI
| Stage | ECG Changes |
|-------|------------|
| **Hyperacute** (minutes) | Tall, peaked T waves |
| **Acute** (hours) | ST elevation, reciprocal ST depression |
| **Evolving** (hours-days) | Q waves develop, ST elevation persists |
| **Resolved** (weeks) | T wave inversion, ST normalizes |
| **Chronic** (months) | Pathological Q waves persist |

### Biomarkers
| Marker | Rise | Peak | Duration |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| **Troponin I/T** | 3-6 hrs | 12-24 hrs | 7-14 days |
| **hs-Troponin** | 1-3 hrs | 12 hrs | 7-14 days |
| **CK-MB** | 4-6 hrs | 24 hrs | 2-3 days |

### Other Investigations
- Chest X-ray: Pulmonary edema
- Echocardiography: Regional wall motion abnormalities, EF, complications
- Coronary angiography: Definitive (before PCI)

## Management

### STEMI — Reperfusion Therapy

**Primary PCI (Preferred):**
- Door-to-balloon time < 90 minutes
- Stent placement (DES preferred)
- Pre-treatment: DAPT + heparin

**Thrombolysis (if PCI unavailable within 120 min):**
- Door-to-needle time < 30 minutes
- **Tenecteplase** (weight-based, single bolus — preferred)
- **Streptokinase** (cheaper, used in India — cannot re-use after 5 days)
- Success criteria: >50% ST resolution at 60-90 min, reperfusion arrhythmias

### Adjunctive Therapy
- **Aspirin** 325mg chew + **Ticagrelor/Clopidogrel** loading
- **Heparin** (UFH with PCI; Enoxaparin with thrombolysis)
- **Beta-blocker** (oral, within 24 hrs if stable)
- **ACEi** (within 24 hrs, especially if anterior MI or EF <40%)
- **Statin** (high-intensity)
- **Nitrates** (for ongoing pain; avoid in RV infarct)
- **Morphine** (if severe pain)
- **Oxygen** only if SpO2 <94%

### RV Infarction (Special Considerations)
- Suspect: Inferior MI + hypotension + clear lungs + elevated JVP
- **IV fluids** (preload dependent)
- **Avoid** nitrates, diuretics, morphine (reduce preload)
- Early reperfusion

### Secondary Prevention
- DAPT × 12 months
- High-dose statin lifelong
- ACEi/ARB (especially EF <40%)
- Beta-blocker
- Cardiac rehabilitation
- Lifestyle: Smoking cessation, exercise, diet

## Complications & Prognosis

### By Timing

| Timing | Complication |
|--------|-------------|
| **Immediate** | VF (most common cause of pre-hospital death) |
| **24-48 hrs** | Atrial/ventricular arrhythmias, acute MR, cardiogenic shock |
| **Day 3-5** | Free wall rupture → tamponade |
| **Day 5-7** | VSD, papillary muscle rupture |
| **2-10 weeks** | Dressler syndrome (autoimmune pericarditis) |
| **Months** | LV aneurysm, LV thrombus, HF |

### Mechanical Complications

| Complication | Presentation | Timing |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Free wall rupture** | Sudden PEA arrest, tamponade | Day 3-5 |
| **VSD** | New harsh pansystolic murmur + step-up in O₂ saturation | Day 3-5 |
| **Papillary muscle rupture** | Acute severe MR, flash pulmonary edema | Day 5-7 |

### Prognosis
- In-hospital mortality STEMI: 5-7% (with primary PCI)
- 1-year mortality: ~10%
- Key prognostic factors: EF, age, Killip class, number of vessels, time to reperfusion

> **Clinical Pearl:** VF is the most common cause of death in the first hour of MI. This is why defibrillators and bystander CPR are the most important factors in pre-hospital survival.
