## Overview

Cardiac tamponade is a life-threatening condition caused by **accumulation of fluid in the pericardial space** compressing the heart, leading to impaired diastolic filling and reduced cardiac output. It is a **medical emergency** requiring urgent pericardiocentesis.

## Key Points

- **Beck's triad:** Hypotension, muffled heart sounds, distended neck veins (JVP ↑)
- **Pulsus paradoxus** (>10 mmHg drop in SBP during inspiration) is characteristic
- **Rate of accumulation** matters more than volume — rapid 200ml can cause tamponade
- ECG shows **low voltage QRS** and **electrical alternans**
- Echocardiography is the **diagnostic investigation of choice**
- **Pericardiocentesis** is the definitive emergency treatment
- Do NOT give diuretics or vasodilators — they reduce preload and worsen tamponade
- IV fluids to maintain preload is a temporizing measure

## Definition & Classification

Cardiac tamponade occurs when pericardial fluid accumulates under pressure sufficient to impair cardiac filling. The pericardium normally contains 15-50 ml of fluid.

| Type | Onset | Common Causes |
|------|-------|---------------|
| **Acute** | Minutes-hours | Trauma, aortic dissection, post-PCI, free wall rupture |
| **Subacute** | Days-weeks | Malignancy, uremia, infection |
| **Low-pressure** | Variable | Hypovolemic states |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Causes
- **Malignancy** (most common cause of large effusions) — lung, breast, lymphoma
- **Infections:** Tuberculosis (common in India), viral, bacterial
- **Uremia** (in CKD/ESKD patients)
- **Post-MI:** Free wall rupture, Dressler syndrome
- **Trauma:** Penetrating chest injury, post-cardiac surgery
- **Aortic dissection** (Type A extending into pericardium)
- **Iatrogenic:** Post-PCI, pacemaker insertion, central line placement

### Pathophysiology
1. Pericardial fluid accumulates → intrapericardial pressure rises
2. When intrapericardial pressure exceeds right atrial/ventricular diastolic pressure → **chambers compress**
3. Right-sided chambers compress first (thinner walls)
4. Impaired diastolic filling → reduced stroke volume → reduced cardiac output
5. Compensatory tachycardia and vasoconstriction (initially)
6. **Equalization of diastolic pressures** across all chambers
7. Obstructive shock → PEA arrest if untreated

> **Clinical Pearl:** A slow effusion (e.g., malignant) allows pericardial stretch and may accumulate >1L before tamponade. A rapid effusion (e.g., trauma) may cause tamponade with only 150-200 ml.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Dyspnea (most common), orthopnea
- Chest fullness/pressure
- Lightheadedness, syncope
- Anxiety, restlessness

### Signs — Beck's Triad
1. **Hypotension** (low cardiac output)
2. **Elevated JVP** (impaired venous return)
3. **Muffled/distant heart sounds**

### Other Signs
- **Pulsus paradoxus:** >10 mmHg fall in SBP during inspiration
- Tachycardia
- Hepatomegaly
- **Absent Y descent** in JVP (impaired ventricular filling)
- Kussmaul sign may be present

### Pulsus Paradoxus — Mechanism
During inspiration → increased venous return to RV → RV distends → interventricular septum shifts left → LV filling reduced → decreased SBP

## Diagnosis

### ECG
- **Low voltage QRS** (< 5mm in limb leads)
- **Electrical alternans** (alternating QRS amplitude — pathognomonic but rare)
- Sinus tachycardia
- May show diffuse ST elevation if underlying pericarditis

### Echocardiography (Investigation of Choice)
- **RA collapse** in late diastole (earliest sign, most sensitive)
- **RV collapse** in early diastole (most specific)
- **Swinging heart** within large effusion
- **IVC plethora** (dilated, no respiratory variation)
- **Exaggerated respiratory variation** in mitral/tricuspid inflow

### Chest X-ray
- **"Water-bottle" shaped** enlarged cardiac silhouette (if large effusion)
- Clear lung fields (unlike heart failure)

### Cardiac Catheterization
- **Equalization of diastolic pressures** (RA ≈ RV diastolic ≈ PA diastolic ≈ PCWP)
- Elevated RA pressure
- Reduced cardiac output

## Management

### Emergency
1. **IV fluids** (bolus) — to increase preload (temporizing)
2. **Avoid** diuretics, vasodilators, beta-blockers
3. **Pericardiocentesis** — definitive treatment
   - Ultrasound-guided, subxiphoid approach
   - Send fluid for: cytology, culture, ADA (TB), protein, glucose, cell count

### Definitive Treatment
- **Pericardial window** — for recurrent effusions (malignant)
- **Pericardiectomy** — for constrictive disease
- **Treat underlying cause** — chemotherapy for malignant, anti-TB therapy, dialysis for uremia

### Post-procedure
- Monitor for re-accumulation
- Pericardial drain may be left in situ

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Untreated tamponade is fatal** — PEA arrest
- Prognosis depends on underlying cause
- Malignant tamponade: median survival 3-5 months
- Idiopathic/viral: Excellent prognosis after drainage
- **Recurrence:** Common with malignant and TB effusions

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key difference between tamponade and constrictive pericarditis — tamponade has **absent Y descent** (impaired filling throughout diastole), while constrictive pericarditis has a **prominent Y descent** (rapid early filling, then restriction).
