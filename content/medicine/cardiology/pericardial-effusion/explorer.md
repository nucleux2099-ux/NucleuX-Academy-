## Overview

Pericardial effusion is an **abnormal accumulation of fluid** in the pericardial space between the visceral and parietal pericardium. It may be incidental and asymptomatic or life-threatening when it causes **cardiac tamponade**. The rate of fluid accumulation, rather than the absolute volume, determines hemodynamic significance.

## Key Points

- Normal pericardial fluid: **15-50 ml**
- **Rate of accumulation** matters more than volume for hemodynamic compromise
- Most common cause globally: **Idiopathic/viral**; in India: **Tuberculosis**
- **Echocardiography** is the investigation of choice
- **Malignancy** is the most common cause of large effusions
- Tamponade physiology: RA diastolic collapse (sensitive), RV diastolic collapse (specific)
- **Pericardiocentesis** for tamponade or diagnostic uncertainty
- Pericardial fluid analysis: Cell count, protein, glucose, ADA, cytology, culture

## Definition & Classification

### By Size (on Echocardiography)

| Size | Echo-free Space | Volume |
|------|----------------|--------|
| **Small** | <10mm (posterior only) | <200 ml |
| **Moderate** | 10-20mm (circumferential) | 200-500 ml |
| **Large** | >20mm (circumferential) | >500 ml |

### By Composition
- **Transudative:** Heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism
- **Exudative:** Infection, malignancy, autoimmune
- **Hemorrhagic:** Trauma, malignancy, aortic dissection, post-MI rupture

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Common Causes

| Category | Causes |
|----------|--------|
| **Infectious** | TB (India), viral (Coxsackie, EBV, HIV), bacterial, fungal |
| **Malignant** | Lung, breast, lymphoma, mesothelioma |
| **Autoimmune** | SLE, RA, scleroderma |
| **Metabolic** | Uremia (CKD), hypothyroidism (myxedema) |
| **Cardiac** | Post-MI (Dressler), post-cardiac surgery, aortic dissection |
| **Idiopathic** | ~50% remain undiagnosed |
| **Drugs** | Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid |
| **Trauma** | Penetrating/blunt chest injury, post-procedure |

### Pathophysiology
- Pericardium is a relatively inelastic sac
- **Slow accumulation** → pericardium stretches → accommodates >1L without hemodynamic compromise
- **Rapid accumulation** → even 150-200 ml can cause tamponade
- When intrapericardial pressure exceeds chamber diastolic pressures → compression → tamponade

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Small effusion:** Often asymptomatic
- **Large effusion:** Dyspnea, chest discomfort/fullness, cough
- **Compressive symptoms:** Dysphagia (esophageal), hiccups (phrenic nerve), hoarseness (recurrent laryngeal)
- **Tamponade:** Dyspnea, lightheadedness, syncope

### Signs
- **Muffled heart sounds** (fluid insulates)
- **Ewart sign:** Dullness and bronchial breathing at left base (compressed lung)
- If tamponade: Beck's triad, pulsus paradoxus, tachycardia

## Diagnosis

### Echocardiography (Investigation of Choice)
- Echo-free space between pericardial layers
- Quantification of size
- **Tamponade signs:** RA/RV diastolic collapse, IVC plethora, respiratory variation in mitral/tricuspid inflow
- Guides pericardiocentesis

### Chest X-ray
- **"Water-bottle" heart** — globular cardiac silhouette (if >250 ml)
- Clear lung fields (differentiates from heart failure)

### ECG
- **Low voltage QRS** (<5mm in limb leads)
- **Electrical alternans** (beat-to-beat variation in QRS amplitude — swinging heart)
- Diffuse ST elevation (if underlying pericarditis)

### CT/MRI
- Better characterization of effusion (loculated, hemorrhagic)
- Pericardial thickening
- Identification of underlying cause (malignancy)

### Pericardial Fluid Analysis
| Test | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| **Cell count** | Neutrophils (bacterial), lymphocytes (TB, viral) |
| **Protein** | >3g/dL = exudate |
| **Glucose** | Low in TB, bacterial, RA |
| **ADA** | >40 U/L suggestive of TB |
| **Cytology** | Malignant cells |
| **Culture/AFB** | Infectious cause |
| **PCR** | TB, viral |

## Management

### Small Asymptomatic Effusion
- Observation + serial echocardiography
- Treat underlying cause

### Moderate-Large Effusion Without Tamponade
- **NSAIDs** (if inflammatory — pericarditis-associated)
- **Colchicine** (reduces recurrence)
- **Corticosteroids** (autoimmune causes; avoid in TB)
- **Treat underlying cause** (anti-TB, chemotherapy, dialysis)

### Tamponade — Emergency
- **Pericardiocentesis** (echo-guided, subxiphoid approach)
- IV fluids to maintain preload
- Avoid diuretics and vasodilators

### Recurrent Effusion
- **Pericardial window** (surgical or balloon)
- **Pericardiectomy** (refractory cases)
- **Intrapericardial therapy** (sclerosing agents for malignant effusion)

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Cardiac tamponade** — most dangerous complication
- **Constrictive pericarditis** — chronic inflammation → fibrosis/calcification
- **Recurrence** — especially idiopathic and TB
- Prognosis depends on underlying cause:
  - Idiopathic/viral: Excellent
  - TB: Good with appropriate treatment
  - Malignant: Poor (median survival 3-6 months)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In India, always send pericardial fluid for ADA and AFB culture/PCR. TB pericarditis is common and treatable but can be fatal if missed.
