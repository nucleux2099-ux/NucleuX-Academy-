## Overview

Valvular heart disease (VHD) encompasses disorders of any of the four cardiac valves — mitral, aortic, tricuspid, and pulmonary — causing **stenosis** (impaired opening) or **regurgitation** (impaired closure). **Rheumatic heart disease** remains the leading cause in developing countries, while **degenerative/calcific** disease predominates in developed nations.

## Key Points

- **Rheumatic heart disease** is the most common cause in India (mitral valve most affected)
- **Degenerative calcific** disease is the most common cause in developed countries (aortic valve)
- Auscultation: **Timing, location, radiation, response to maneuvers** identify the lesion
- Echocardiography is the **gold standard** for diagnosis and severity assessment
- **Aortic stenosis:** Ejection systolic murmur at right upper sternal border; syncope, angina, HF (SAD)
- **Mitral regurgitation:** Pansystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla
- Surgery/intervention when **symptomatic** or when ventricular function declines

## Definition & Classification

### Summary of Valvular Lesions

| Valve | Stenosis | Regurgitation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| **Aortic** | Ejection systolic murmur (RUSB → carotids) | Early diastolic murmur (LUSB) |
| **Mitral** | Mid-diastolic rumble (apex) | Pansystolic murmur (apex → axilla) |
| **Tricuspid** | Mid-diastolic rumble (LLSB) | Pansystolic murmur (LLSB, ↑ inspiration) |
| **Pulmonary** | Ejection systolic murmur (LUSB) | Early diastolic murmur (Graham Steell) |

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Aortic Stenosis (AS)
- **Causes:** Degenerative calcification (elderly), bicuspid aortic valve (young), rheumatic
- **Pathophysiology:** LV pressure overload → concentric LVH → diastolic dysfunction → eventually systolic failure
- **Triad of symptoms (SAD):** Syncope, Angina, Dyspnea
- **Critical AS:** Valve area <1.0 cm² (normal 3-4 cm²)

### Aortic Regurgitation (AR)
- **Causes:** Bicuspid valve, rheumatic, endocarditis, aortic root disease (Marfan, aortic dissection, syphilitic aortitis)
- **Pathophysiology:** Volume overload → eccentric LV dilatation → eventually systolic failure
- **Wide pulse pressure** (bounding pulses — Corrigan, water-hammer, de Musset, Quincke)

### Mitral Stenosis (MS)
- See dedicated topic: [Mitral Stenosis](../mitral-stenosis/)

### Mitral Regurgitation (MR)
- **Causes:** MVP (developed countries), rheumatic, endocarditis, papillary muscle dysfunction (post-MI), DCM (functional)
- **Pathophysiology:** Volume overload → LA dilatation → AF; LV volume overload → eccentric dilatation

### Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)
- **Most common:** Functional (secondary to RV dilatation from PH)
- Primary: Endocarditis (IVDU), carcinoid, Ebstein anomaly, rheumatic

## Clinical Features

### Aortic Stenosis
- **Symptoms:** Angina, syncope (exertional), dyspnea (HF) — symptoms appear late
- **Signs:**
  - Ejection systolic murmur at RUSB → carotids (harsh, crescendo-decrescendo)
  - Slow-rising pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus)
  - Narrow pulse pressure
  - S4 (stiff LV), absent A2 (calcified valve)
  - Severity: Later peaking murmur = more severe

### Aortic Regurgitation
- **Symptoms:** Dyspnea, palpitations (hyperdynamic), chest pain
- **Signs:**
  - Early diastolic decrescendo murmur at LUSB (best in sitting, leaning forward, end-expiration)
  - **Wide pulse pressure** (systolic ↑, diastolic ↓)
  - Collapsing (water-hammer) pulse
  - **Eponymous signs:** Corrigan (carotid pulsation), de Musset (head bobbing), Quincke (nail bed pulsation), Traube (pistol shot femorals), Duroziez (femoral bruit)
  - Austin Flint murmur (functional mitral diastolic murmur)
  - Displaced hyperdynamic apex

### Mitral Regurgitation
- **Symptoms:** Dyspnea, fatigue, palpitations (AF)
- **Signs:**
  - Pansystolic murmur at apex → axilla
  - Soft S1 (incomplete leaflet closure)
  - S3 (volume overload)
  - Displaced apex (LV dilatation)

## Diagnosis

### Echocardiography (Investigation of Choice)
- **Valve morphology** (thickening, calcification, vegetation)
- **Severity grading:** Valve area, gradient, regurgitant volume/fraction
- **Ventricular function:** EF, dimensions
- **Chamber sizes:** LA, LV, RV, RA
- **Pulmonary pressures**
- **Doppler:** Flow velocity, pressure gradients

### Severity Criteria — Aortic Stenosis

| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|-----------|------|----------|--------|
| Valve area (cm²) | >1.5 | 1.0-1.5 | <1.0 |
| Mean gradient (mmHg) | <20 | 20-40 | >40 |
| Peak velocity (m/s) | <3.0 | 3.0-4.0 | >4.0 |

### Additional Investigations
- ECG: LVH (AS, AR, MR), RVH (MS, TR), AF
- Chest X-ray: Chamber enlargement, pulmonary congestion, valve calcification
- Cardiac catheterization: Coronary angiography pre-surgery
- Cardiac MRI: Quantify regurgitation, assess fibrosis

## Management

### General Principles
- **Medical:** Manage symptoms, AF, HF, anticoagulation
- **Intervention:** When symptomatic or when ventricular function deteriorates

### Aortic Stenosis
- **Medical:** Limited role; avoid vasodilators
- **Surgical AVR:** Symptomatic severe AS; asymptomatic with EF <50%
- **TAVI (Transcatheter Aortic Valve Implantation):** Elderly, high surgical risk

### Aortic Regurgitation
- **Medical:** ACEi/ARB, nifedipine (afterload reduction) — chronic AR
- **Surgery:** Symptomatic severe AR; asymptomatic with EF ≤50% or LVESD >50mm

### Mitral Regurgitation
- **Primary (organic):** Surgery (repair preferred over replacement)
- **Secondary (functional):** Optimize HF therapy; MitraClip for selected patients
- Indications: Symptomatic severe MR; asymptomatic with EF ≤60% or LVESD ≥40mm

### Prosthetic Valves

| Type | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Mechanical** | Durable (lifelong) | Lifelong anticoagulation (warfarin, INR 2.5-3.5) |
| **Bioprosthetic** | No long-term anticoagulation | Limited durability (10-20 years) |

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Heart failure** (most common cause of death in VHD)
- **Atrial fibrillation** → thromboembolism
- **Infective endocarditis**
- **Sudden cardiac death** (severe AS)
- **Pulmonary hypertension** (MS, MR)

### Natural History Without Intervention
- **Severe AS with symptoms:** 50% mortality at 2 years (once symptoms appear)
- **Severe MR:** Progressive LV dilatation and failure
- **Severe AR:** Compensated for years; once symptomatic, rapid decline

> **Clinical Pearl:** In aortic stenosis, the onset of symptoms (syncope, angina, dyspnea) marks a critical turning point — average survival drops to 2-5 years without valve replacement.
