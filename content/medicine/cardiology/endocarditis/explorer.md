## Overview

Infective endocarditis (IE) is an infection of the endocardial surface of the heart, most commonly involving the **heart valves**. It is caused by bacteria (or rarely fungi) colonizing damaged or prosthetic valves, forming **vegetations**. Without treatment, IE is uniformly fatal.

## Key Points

- **Streptococcus viridans** — most common cause in native valves (subacute)
- **Staphylococcus aureus** — most common overall and in IVDU/prosthetic valves (acute)
- **Duke criteria** are used for diagnosis (2 major, or 1 major + 3 minor, or 5 minor)
- **Blood cultures** (3 sets from different sites) are the most important investigation
- **Echocardiography** — TTE first; **TEE** if TTE negative but high suspicion
- IVDU → **tricuspid valve** involvement (right-sided IE)
- Treatment: **Prolonged IV antibiotics** (4-6 weeks)
- Surgery needed in ~50% cases (valve destruction, heart failure, uncontrolled infection)

## Definition & Classification

| Feature | Acute IE | Subacute IE |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Onset** | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |
| **Organism** | S. aureus, S. pneumoniae | S. viridans, Enterococcus |
| **Valve** | Often normal valve | Pre-existing valve disease |
| **Course** | Rapidly destructive | Indolent |
| **Vegetations** | Large, friable | Small, firmly attached |

### Special Types
- **Prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE):** Early (<60 days) — S. aureus, CoNS; Late — similar to native
- **IVDU-related IE:** Tricuspid valve, S. aureus
- **Culture-negative IE:** HACEK organisms, Brucella, Coxiella, Bartonella, Tropheryma whipplei

## Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Organisms by Setting

| Setting | Common Organisms |
|---------|-----------------|
| Native valve (subacute) | S. viridans, Enterococcus |
| Native valve (acute) | S. aureus |
| IVDU | S. aureus (tricuspid) |
| Prosthetic (early) | S. aureus, CoNS, gram-negatives |
| Prosthetic (late) | S. viridans, Enterococcus |
| Culture-negative | HACEK, Coxiella, Bartonella |

### Pathogenesis
1. Endothelial damage → deposition of platelets and fibrin (**NBTE** — non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis)
2. Bacteremia → organisms colonize NBTE
3. **Vegetation** forms (platelets + fibrin + bacteria + inflammatory cells)
4. Local: Valve destruction, abscess, fistula
5. Systemic: Embolic phenomena, immune complex deposition

### Predisposing Conditions
- **Rheumatic heart disease** (most common in India)
- Bicuspid aortic valve, mitral valve prolapse
- Prosthetic valves
- Degenerative valve disease
- Congenital heart disease
- IVDU, poor dental hygiene

## Clinical Features

### Classic Triad: Fever + Murmur + Embolic Phenomena

### Symptoms
- **Fever** (most common, >90%) with chills/rigors
- Malaise, weight loss, night sweats, arthralgia
- Dyspnea (valve destruction → heart failure)

### Signs
- **New or changing murmur** (85%)
- **Splenomegaly** (30%)
- Embolic phenomena:

| Finding | Cause |
|---------|-------|
| **Janeway lesions** | Non-tender erythematous lesions on palms/soles (septic emboli) |
| **Osler nodes** | Tender nodules on finger/toe pads (immune complex) |
| **Splinter hemorrhages** | Linear in nail beds |
| **Roth spots** | Retinal hemorrhages with pale center |
| **Petechiae** | Conjunctival, oral mucosa |

- **Right-sided IE:** Septic pulmonary emboli → cough, pleuritic chest pain, cavitating lung lesions

## Diagnosis

### Modified Duke Criteria

**Major Criteria:**
1. **Positive blood cultures:** Typical organism from 2 separate cultures; or persistently positive cultures
2. **Endocardial involvement:** Vegetation/abscess on echo; new valvular regurgitation

**Minor Criteria:**
- Predisposing condition or IVDU
- Fever ≥ 38°C
- Vascular phenomena (Janeway, emboli, mycotic aneurysm, ICH)
- Immunologic phenomena (Osler, Roth, GN, +ve RF)
- Positive blood cultures not meeting major criteria

**Definite IE:** 2 major; or 1 major + 3 minor; or 5 minor

### Investigations
- **Blood cultures** (3 sets, 30 min apart, before antibiotics) — positive in 90%
- **TTE** (sensitivity 60-70%) → **TEE** if negative (sensitivity >90%)
- CBC: Anemia (normocytic), leukocytosis
- ESR/CRP: Elevated
- Urinalysis: Microscopic hematuria (immune complex GN)
- Rheumatoid factor: Positive in subacute IE

## Management

### Empirical Antibiotic Therapy
| Setting | Regimen |
|---------|---------|
| Native valve (acute) | Flucloxacillin + Gentamicin |
| Native valve (subacute) | Ampicillin + Gentamicin |
| Prosthetic valve | Vancomycin + Gentamicin + Rifampicin |
| MRSA suspected | Vancomycin or Daptomycin |

### Specific Therapy (after culture results)
- **S. viridans (penicillin-sensitive):** Penicillin G × 4 weeks (or + Gentamicin × 2 weeks)
- **Enterococcus:** Ampicillin + Gentamicin × 4-6 weeks
- **S. aureus (native):** Flucloxacillin × 4-6 weeks
- **S. aureus (prosthetic):** Flucloxacillin + Rifampicin + Gentamicin

### Surgical Indications
- **Heart failure** due to valve dysfunction
- **Uncontrolled infection** (persistent fever >7 days, abscess, fistula)
- **Prevention of embolism** (large vegetations >10mm, recurrent emboli)
- Prosthetic valve IE with S. aureus
- Fungal endocarditis

### IE Prophylaxis
- Only for **high-risk patients** (prosthetic valves, previous IE, congenital heart disease)
- Before **dental procedures** involving gingival manipulation
- **Amoxicillin 2g** orally 30-60 min before procedure

## Complications & Prognosis

- **Heart failure** (most common cause of death)
- Embolic stroke (20-40%)
- Mycotic aneurysm (cerebral, visceral)
- Renal failure (immune complex GN, emboli)
- Splenic abscess/infarction
- Perivalvular abscess (especially aortic valve)

### Mortality
- Native valve IE: 15-25%
- Prosthetic valve IE: 20-40%
- S. aureus IE: 25-40%
- Right-sided IE (IVDU): <10% (better prognosis)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In a patient with fever of unknown origin + new murmur, always think of IE. Three blood cultures before starting antibiotics can be the difference between identifying the organism and culture-negative IE.
