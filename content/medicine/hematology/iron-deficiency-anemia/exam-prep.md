# Anemia — Exam Prep

## High Yield Points

- ★ **IDA:** ↓ ferritin (best initial test), ↑ TIBC, ↓ transferrin saturation; pencil cells on smear
- ★ **Anemia of chronic disease vs IDA:** Both have ↓ iron, but ACD has ↓ TIBC and ↑ ferritin (hepcidin traps iron)
- ★ **Thalassemia trait vs IDA:** Both microcytic, but thalassemia has NORMAL iron studies + ↑ HbA2 (β-thal) + normal/↓ RDW
- ★ **Hypersegmented neutrophils** (≥5 lobes) = megaloblastic anemia (B12 or folate deficiency)
- ★ **Methylmalonic acid** elevated ONLY in B12 deficiency (not folate) — most specific differentiator
- ★ **Subacute combined degeneration** = B12 deficiency; dorsal columns + lateral corticospinal tracts
- ★ **G6PD deficiency:** Heinz bodies + bite cells; X-linked; avoid oxidant drugs (primaquine, sulfa, dapsone)
- ★ **Hereditary spherocytosis:** Osmotic fragility ↑; EMA binding test; splenectomy curative
- ★ **Direct Coombs test** positive = AIHA; warm (IgG) vs cold (IgM)
- ★ **PNH:** Ham's test (historical); flow cytometry for CD55/CD59; triad = hemolytic anemia + pancytopenia + thrombosis

## MCQs

### MCQ 1
A 28-year-old vegetarian woman has Hb 7, MCV 68, ferritin 8, TIBC 500, transferrin saturation 8%. The diagnosis and treatment?

a) Thalassemia; blood transfusion
b) Iron deficiency anemia; oral iron supplements
c) Sideroblastic anemia; pyridoxine
d) Anemia of chronic disease; treat underlying cause

**Answer: b) Iron deficiency anemia; oral iron supplements**

*Low ferritin (<30) is the most specific test for IDA. Low MCV + low ferritin + high TIBC + low transferrin sat = classic IDA. Treat with oral ferrous sulfate 325mg TID × 3-6 months. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 98*

### MCQ 2
A patient with macrocytic anemia has hypersegmented neutrophils on smear. Serum B12 is low, methylmalonic acid is elevated, homocysteine is elevated. He also has loss of vibration sense in lower limbs. The neurological manifestation is:

a) Peripheral neuropathy from folate deficiency
b) Subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord
c) Cerebellar ataxia
d) Myopathy

**Answer: b) Subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord**

*B12 deficiency → demyelination of dorsal columns (vibration, proprioception loss) + lateral corticospinal tracts (UMN signs). This does NOT occur in folate deficiency. Methylmalonic acid is elevated only in B12 deficiency. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 100*

### MCQ 3
A patient develops acute hemolysis after eating fava beans. Peripheral smear shows bite cells and Heinz bodies. The most likely diagnosis is:

a) Hereditary spherocytosis
b) G6PD deficiency
c) Sickle cell disease
d) PNH

**Answer: b) G6PD deficiency**

*G6PD deficiency (X-linked): oxidant stress (fava beans, primaquine, sulfa drugs, infections) → denatured Hb → Heinz bodies (supravital stain) → splenic pitting → bite cells. Diagnose with G6PD enzyme assay (NOT during acute episode — false normal from reticulocytosis). 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 99*

### MCQ 4
A patient with anemia has low iron, low TIBC, and elevated ferritin. This pattern is most consistent with:

a) Iron deficiency anemia
b) Anemia of chronic disease
c) Thalassemia trait
d) Sideroblastic anemia

**Answer: b) Anemia of chronic disease**

*ACD: hepcidin (acute phase reactant) → blocks ferroportin → iron trapped in macrophages. Result: ↓ serum iron (unavailable), ↓ TIBC (liver reduces transferrin), ↑ ferritin (iron stores full). Treat the underlying cause; EPO if needed. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 98*

### MCQ 5
The most specific test to differentiate B12 deficiency from folate deficiency is:

a) Serum homocysteine
b) Serum methylmalonic acid
c) Reticulocyte count
d) Bone marrow biopsy

**Answer: b) Serum methylmalonic acid**

*Both B12 and folate deficiency → ↑ homocysteine. But methylmalonic acid is elevated ONLY in B12 deficiency (B12 is a cofactor for methylmalonyl-CoA mutase). This is the most specific differentiator. 📚 Harrison's, 22nd Ed, Ch. 100*
