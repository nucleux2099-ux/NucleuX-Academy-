# Glomerulonephritis

> 📚 **Sources:** Harrison's 22nd Ed Ch. 313, Brenner & Rector's The Kidney
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Glomerulonephritis (GN) refers to inflammatory diseases affecting the glomerulus, presenting with varying degrees of hematuria, proteinuria, hypertension, and renal dysfunction. GN can be primary (kidney-limited) or secondary (systemic disease). The clinical presentation ranges from asymptomatic urinary abnormalities to rapidly progressive renal failure. Diagnosis requires integration of clinical features, serology, and often renal biopsy. Treatment depends on the underlying etiology and severity.

## Key Points

- **Nephritic syndrome:** Hematuria (RBC casts), mild proteinuria, HTN, edema, ↓GFR
- **Nephrotic syndrome:** Heavy proteinuria (>3.5g/day), hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia
- **RPGN:** Rapid decline (days-weeks), crescents on biopsy, urgent treatment needed
- **RBC casts** = pathognomonic for glomerular hematuria
- **Low C3** suggests post-infectious GN, MPGN, lupus, or cryoglobulinemia

## Classification

### Clinical Syndromes

| Syndrome | Features |
|----------|----------|
| **Nephritic** | Hematuria (RBC casts), mild proteinuria (<3g), HTN, oliguria, edema |
| **Nephrotic** | Heavy proteinuria (>3.5g), hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia |
| **RPGN** | Rapid GFR decline over days-weeks, crescents on biopsy |
| **Asymptomatic** | Microscopic hematuria ± mild proteinuria |

### By Etiology

| Type | Low Complement | Normal Complement |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Primary GN** | MPGN Type I | IgA nephropathy, FSGS, Minimal change |
| **Secondary GN** | Lupus, Post-infectious, Cryoglobulinemia | ANCA vasculitis, Anti-GBM |

## Major Types of GN

### IgA Nephropathy (Berger's Disease)
- **Most common GN worldwide**
- Presents: Episodic gross hematuria coinciding with URI ("synpharyngitic")
- Mesangial IgA deposits on immunofluorescence
- Variable prognosis: 20-40% progress to ESKD over 20 years
- Treatment: ACEi/ARBs, steroids if progressive

### Post-Infectious GN (PIGN)
- Typically post-streptococcal (2-4 weeks after pharyngitis, 3-6 weeks after skin)
- "Lumpy-bumpy" subepithelial immune deposits
- Low C3, elevated ASO titer
- Self-limited in children, worse prognosis in adults
- Treatment: Supportive

### Membranous Nephropathy
- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in adults
- Primary (anti-PLA2R antibodies) vs Secondary (malignancy, lupus, infections)
- "Spike and dome" appearance, subepithelial deposits
- Rule out malignancy in older patients
- Treatment: ACEi/ARBs, consider rituximab or cyclophosphamide

### Minimal Change Disease
- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children
- Effacement of podocyte foot processes on EM
- Normal light microscopy, negative IF
- Excellent response to steroids
- Can be secondary to NSAIDs, lymphoma

### FSGS (Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis)
- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in African Americans
- Primary vs Secondary (obesity, HIV, heroin, reflux nephropathy)
- Poor prognosis, often progresses to ESKD
- Treatment: ACEi/ARBs, steroids, consider calcineurin inhibitors

### MPGN (Membranoproliferative GN)
- "Tram-track" appearance (double contours)
- Low C3
- Types: Immune complex-mediated vs Complement-mediated (C3GN)
- Often secondary (HCV, cryoglobulinemia, monoclonal gammopathy)
- Treatment: Treat underlying cause

## RPGN Classification

### Type I: Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture)
- Linear IgG along GBM
- Pulmonary hemorrhage + GN = Goodpasture syndrome
- Treatment: Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + steroids

### Type II: Immune Complex
- Lupus, IgA, Post-infectious, MPGN
- Granular "lumpy-bumpy" deposits
- Treatment: Depends on underlying cause

### Type III: Pauci-immune (ANCA+)
- GPA, MPA, EGPA
- Minimal immune deposits on IF
- ANCA positive (c-ANCA/PR3 or p-ANCA/MPO)
- Treatment: Cyclophosphamide/rituximab + steroids

## Diagnosis

### Workup
- **Urinalysis:** Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts
- **24h urine or UPCR:** Quantify proteinuria
- **Serology:** C3/C4, ANA, ANCA, Anti-GBM, Anti-PLA2R, ASO, Hepatitis B/C
- **Renal biopsy:** Often required for definitive diagnosis

### Serologic Clues

| Finding | Suggests |
|---------|----------|
| **Low C3, Normal C4** | Post-infectious GN, MPGN, C3GN |
| **Low C3 and C4** | Lupus, Cryoglobulinemia |
| **c-ANCA (PR3)** | GPA (Wegener's) |
| **p-ANCA (MPO)** | MPA, EGPA, Drug-induced |
| **Anti-GBM** | Goodpasture syndrome |
| **Anti-PLA2R** | Primary membranous nephropathy |
| **Elevated ASO** | Post-streptococcal GN |

## Treatment Principles

1. **ACEi/ARBs:** All proteinuric patients (reduce intraglomerular pressure)
2. **BP control:** Target <130/80
3. **Treat underlying cause:** Infection, malignancy, drugs
4. **Immunosuppression:** If immune-mediated GN (steroids, cyclophosphamide, rituximab)
5. **Plasmapheresis:** Anti-GBM disease, severe ANCA vasculitis

## High-Yield Points

1. **RBC casts** = glomerular origin (pathognomonic for GN)
2. **IgA nephropathy:** Synpharyngitic hematuria (coincides with URI, unlike PIGN which is latent)
3. **Post-streptococcal GN:** Low C3, normal C4, elevated ASO
4. **Anti-PLA2R** = primary membranous (rule out cancer if negative)
5. **RPGN** = crescents on biopsy = urgent treatment needed
6. **Goodpasture:** Linear IgG on IF + pulmonary hemorrhage
