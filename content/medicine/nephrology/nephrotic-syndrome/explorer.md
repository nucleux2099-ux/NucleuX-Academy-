# Nephrotic Syndrome

> 📚 **Sources:** Harrison's 22nd Ed Ch. 313, Brenner & Rector's The Kidney
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by the classic tetrad: heavy proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia (<3 g/dL), peripheral edema, and hyperlipidemia. It results from increased glomerular permeability to proteins. Unlike nephritic syndrome, hematuria is minimal and GFR may be preserved initially. The syndrome predisposes to thromboembolism, infection, and acute kidney injury. Management targets the underlying glomerular disease and prevents complications.

## Key Points

- **Tetrad:** Proteinuria >3.5g/day + Hypoalbuminemia + Edema + Hyperlipidemia
- **Most common cause in children:** Minimal change disease (80%)
- **Most common cause in adults:** Diabetic nephropathy, Membranous nephropathy
- **Complications:** Thromboembolism (renal vein thrombosis), infection, AKI
- **Lipiduria:** Maltese crosses (cholesterol) and oval fat bodies

## Causes by Age

### Children
| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| **Minimal change disease** | 80% |
| **FSGS** | 10% |
| **Membranous** | 5% |
| **MPGN** | 5% |

### Adults
| Cause | Characteristics |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Diabetic nephropathy** | Most common overall, history of DM |
| **Membranous** | Most common primary cause; rule out malignancy |
| **FSGS** | Common in African Americans |
| **Minimal change** | Associated with NSAIDs, lymphoma |
| **Amyloidosis** | Systemic features, Congo red staining |
| **Lupus** | Young females, multisystem involvement |

## Pathophysiology

### Proteinuria
- Loss of charge barrier (podocyte foot process effacement)
- Loss of size barrier (GBM damage)
- Results in albuminuria and loss of other proteins

### Edema Formation
1. Hypoalbuminemia → ↓Oncotic pressure → Fluid extravasation
2. Intravascular volume depletion → RAAS activation → Na+ retention
3. "Underfill" theory explains some, but "overfill" also contributes

### Hyperlipidemia
- ↑Hepatic lipoprotein synthesis (compensatory to hypoalbuminemia)
- ↓Lipoprotein lipase activity
- ↑LDL, VLDL, triglycerides; ↓HDL

### Thromboembolism
- Loss of antithrombin III in urine
- Increased hepatic synthesis of clotting factors
- Platelet hyperaggregability
- **Renal vein thrombosis** especially in membranous nephropathy

## Clinical Features

### Classic Presentation
- **Edema:** Periorbital (morning), pedal (evening), anasarca
- **Weight gain:** Rapid, due to fluid retention
- **Frothy urine:** High protein content
- **Fatigue, malaise**

### Complications

| Complication | Mechanism |
|--------------|-----------|
| **Thromboembolism** | Loss of AT-III, ↑clotting factors; DVT, PE, renal vein thrombosis |
| **Infection** | Loss of immunoglobulins; SBP (S. pneumoniae) in children |
| **AKI** | Intravascular depletion, contrast nephropathy, renal vein thrombosis |
| **Hypocalcemia** | Loss of vitamin D binding protein |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Loss of thyroid binding globulin |

### Renal Vein Thrombosis
- Higher risk in membranous nephropathy
- Acute: Flank pain, hematuria, ↓GFR
- Chronic: May be asymptomatic
- Diagnosis: Doppler ultrasound, CT venography
- Treatment: Anticoagulation

## Diagnosis

### Workup
- **Urinalysis:** Proteinuria, lipiduria (oval fat bodies, Maltese crosses)
- **24h urine protein or UPCR:** >3.5 g/day confirms nephrotic range
- **Serum albumin:** <3 g/dL
- **Lipid panel:** ↑Total cholesterol, ↑LDL, ↑TG
- **Renal function:** Creatinine, GFR

### Serologic Testing
- **ANA:** Lupus
- **HBV/HCV:** Secondary membranous, MPGN
- **Anti-PLA2R:** Primary membranous
- **SPEP/UPEP:** Amyloidosis, myeloma
- **Glucose/HbA1c:** Diabetic nephropathy

### Renal Biopsy
- Indicated in adults for most cases
- Children: Trial of steroids first (presumed MCD); biopsy if steroid-resistant
- Essential for diagnosis and guiding treatment

## Treatment

### General Measures
| Intervention | Details |
|--------------|---------|
| **ACEi/ARBs** | Reduce proteinuria, slow progression |
| **Sodium restriction** | <2g/day to reduce edema |
| **Diuretics** | Loop diuretics ± thiazides for edema |
| **Statin therapy** | For hyperlipidemia, CV risk reduction |
| **Anticoagulation** | Consider if albumin <2.0 or high thrombotic risk |
| **Pneumococcal vaccine** | Prevent SBP, especially in children |

### Specific Treatment by Cause

| Cause | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Minimal change** | Steroids (excellent response) |
| **FSGS** | Steroids, consider calcineurin inhibitors if resistant |
| **Membranous** | ACEi/ARBs first; if progressive: rituximab or cyclophosphamide |
| **Diabetic** | Glycemic control, ACEi/ARBs, SGLT2i |
| **Lupus** | Immunosuppression per class |

### When to Anticoagulate
- Albumin <2.0-2.5 g/dL
- Membranous nephropathy (highest risk)
- Documented thrombosis
- Immobility, prior VTE

## High-Yield Points

1. **Minimal change** = most common in children; excellent steroid response
2. **Membranous** = most common primary cause in adults; rule out malignancy
3. **Anti-PLA2R** positive = primary membranous (70-80%)
4. **Renal vein thrombosis** = especially membranous; consider if sudden ↑proteinuria or ↓GFR
5. **Maltese crosses** under polarized light = lipiduria (cholesterol)
6. **SBP risk** = loss of immunoglobulins; vaccinate against pneumococcus
