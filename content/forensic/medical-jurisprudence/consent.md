# Consent in Medical Practice

> 📚 **Sources:** Reddy's Forensic Medicine (Ch. 2), Parikh's Textbook, Medical Council of India Guidelines
> **Level:** UG

## Core Concept

**Consent** is the voluntary agreement by a person in the possession of sound mind to permit an act to be performed on their body. 

In medical practice, treatment without valid consent constitutes **assault** (civil wrong) or **battery** (criminal offense). The principle of **autonomy** — patient's right to decide — is fundamental to medical ethics.

## Key Points

### Types of Consent

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Implied** | Understood from conduct | Extending arm for BP check |
| **Express** | Verbal or written statement | Signing surgical consent form |
| **Informed** | After full disclosure | Explaining surgery risks/benefits |
| **Proxy** | Given by authorized person | Parent for child |
| **Blanket** | General consent for all treatment | Admission form (NOT sufficient for surgery) |

### Elements of Valid Consent
1. **Voluntary** — Free from coercion, undue influence, fraud
2. **Informed** — After disclosure of relevant information
3. **Given by competent person** — Sound mind, legal age
4. **Specific** — For the particular procedure
5. **Revocable** — Can be withdrawn at any time

### Who Can Give Consent

| Patient Category | Who Gives Consent |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Adult (≥18 years) of sound mind | Self |
| Minor (<18 years) | Parent or legal guardian |
| Unconscious/mentally incapacitated | Next of kin or guardian |
| Emergency (no one available) | Treating doctor (implied consent) |
| Married minor | Spouse cannot consent; need guardian |

### Informed Consent Components
Patient must be told:
1. **Diagnosis** — Nature of illness
2. **Proposed treatment** — What will be done
3. **Risks and benefits** — Material risks
4. **Alternatives** — Other treatment options
5. **Consequences of refusing** — What happens without treatment
6. **Prognosis** — Expected outcome

### Special Situations

**Emergency without consent:**
- Section 92 IPC provides protection
- Treatment to prevent imminent harm
- Document why consent couldn't be obtained

**Therapeutic privilege:**
- Withholding information that may harm patient
- Very limited application
- Must be used judiciously

**Research consent:**
- More detailed than therapeutic consent
- Right to withdraw at any time
- Institutional Ethics Committee approval

## Medico-Legal Aspects

### Legal Framework
- **Indian Contract Act, 1872** — Consent must be free
- **Section 87 IPC** — Consent to act not intended to cause harm
- **Section 88 IPC** — Consent to act done in good faith for benefit
- **Section 89 IPC** — Consent by guardian for minor's benefit
- **Section 90 IPC** — Consent given under fear/misconception is invalid
- **Section 92 IPC** — Emergency treatment without consent

### What Makes Consent Invalid
- Given under **fear of injury**
- Given under **misconception of fact**
- Given by person of **unsound mind**
- Given by person **under intoxication**
- Given by person **under 12 years** (for IPC purposes)
- **Fraud** in obtaining consent

### Documentation Requirements
- **Written consent preferred** (not always mandatory but evidence)
- Must include:
  - Patient identification
  - Procedure name
  - Risks explained
  - Patient's signature or thumb impression
  - Witness signature
  - Date and time
  - Doctor's name and signature

### Consent in Specific Procedures

| Procedure | Special Requirements |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **HIV testing** | Pre- and post-test counseling, confidentiality |
| **Organ transplant** | THOA guidelines, committee approval |
| **Sterilization** | Written consent, cooling period |
| **Abortion** | Woman's consent (not husband's), MTP Act |
| **Mental illness** | MHCA 2017 provisions |

## Examination Findings

### Evaluating Capacity to Consent
Patient must demonstrate ability to:
1. **Understand** the information given
2. **Appreciate** how it applies to their situation
3. **Reason** about options
4. **Express** a choice

### Red Flags for Invalid Consent
- Patient appears intoxicated
- Signs of coercion (family pressure)
- Language barrier not addressed
- Patient not asking any questions
- Rushing through consent process

## High-Yield for Exams

### Must-Know MCQ Points
1. **Age of consent (general):** 18 years
2. **Consent of guardian valid for IPC:** Child under 12 years (Section 89)
3. **Emergency treatment without consent:** Section 92 IPC
4. **MTP (abortion) requires consent of:** Pregnant woman only (not husband)
5. **Blanket consent is:** Not valid for specific surgical procedures
6. **Implied consent example:** Patient extending arm for injection
7. **Therapeutic privilege:** Doctor may withhold info that may harm patient

### Landmark Cases
- **Samira Kohli vs Dr. Prabha Manchanda (2008):** Informed consent mandatory; removal of reproductive organs without specific consent = assault
- **Spring Meadows Hospital vs Harjol Ahluwalia (1998):** Consent for operation doesn't include consent for experimental procedures

### Common Viva Questions
- Q: Can a 16-year-old give consent for surgery?
  - A: No, need parent/guardian consent (exception: married minor, certain MTP Act provisions)
  
- Q: Is verbal consent valid?
  - A: Yes, legally valid, but written consent is evidence of consent

- Q: Can consent be withdrawn during procedure?
  - A: Yes, patient has right to withdraw; doctor must stop if safely possible
