# Medical Ethics

> 📚 **Sources:** Reddy's Forensic Medicine (Ch. 2), MCI Code of Ethics 2002, Parikh's Textbook
> **Level:** UG

## Core Concept

**Medical Ethics** refers to the moral principles and values that guide medical professionals in their practice. It provides a framework for decision-making in complex situations where science alone cannot provide answers.

The study of medical ethics is called **Deontology** (Greek: *deontos* = duty).

## Key Points

### Four Pillars of Medical Ethics (Beauchamp & Childress)

| Principle | Meaning | Application |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Autonomy** | Respect patient's right to decide | Informed consent, confidentiality |
| **Beneficence** | Do good for the patient | Treatment aimed at patient's benefit |
| **Non-maleficence** | Do no harm (Primum non nocere) | Avoid unnecessary risk, prevent harm |
| **Justice** | Fair distribution of resources | Equal treatment, no discrimination |

### MCI Code of Ethics 2002 (Key Points)

**Duties to Patients:**
- Maintain professional secrecy
- Obtain informed consent
- Not discriminate based on caste, religion, nationality
- Prescribe generic drugs when possible
- Not refuse emergency treatment

**Duties to Profession:**
- Maintain honor and dignity
- Not advertise or solicit patients
- Not associate with unqualified persons
- Report unethical conduct by colleagues
- No fee-splitting or kickbacks

**Duties to Self:**
- Maintain professional competence (CME)
- Not practice under influence of alcohol/drugs
- Dress professionally
- Display registration clearly

### Important Ethical Concepts

**Confidentiality:**
- Duty to keep patient information secret
- Exceptions:
  - Court order
  - Notifiable diseases
  - To prevent serious harm to others
  - With patient's consent

**Privileged Communication:**
- Legal protection for confidential doctor-patient communication
- **India:** No absolute privilege (unlike lawyer-client)
- Court can compel testimony

**Medical Paternalism:**
- Doctor deciding "for patient's benefit" without consent
- **Outdated concept** — autonomy now prioritized
- Limited role in therapeutic privilege

**Euthanasia:**
- **Active:** Administering lethal substance — ILLEGAL in India
- **Passive:** Withdrawing life support — Permitted with safeguards
- **Aruna Shanbaug case (2011):** Supreme Court allowed passive euthanasia
- **Common Cause case (2018):** Living will (Advance Directive) recognized

### Ethical Dilemmas

| Situation | Ethical Conflict |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Jehovah's Witness refusing blood transfusion | Autonomy vs Beneficence |
| Terminally ill requesting assisted suicide | Autonomy vs Non-maleficence |
| Resource allocation in pandemic | Justice vs Individual benefit |
| Disclosing HIV status to spouse | Confidentiality vs Preventing harm |
| Treating violent patient | Non-maleficence vs Duty to treat |

## Medico-Legal Aspects

### Professional Misconduct (MCI)
Actions that can lead to disciplinary action:

**Serious:**
- Conviction by court of law
- Adultery with patient
- Substance abuse
- Issuing false certificates
- Sex determination (PCPNDT Act)

**Unethical:**
- Advertising
- Secret commission/kickbacks
- Fee-splitting
- Signing prescription without examination
- Prescribing for commission

**Punishment:**
- Warning
- Removal from register (temporary/permanent)

### State Medical Councils
- Maintain register of practitioners
- Handle complaints of misconduct
- Can remove/suspend registration
- Appeal to Medical Council of India

### Important Laws

| Law | Relevance |

| **PCPNDT Act 1994** | Sex determination prohibited |
| **MTP Act 1971** | Conditions for legal abortion |
| **THOA 1994** | Organ transplant ethics |

## Examination Findings

### Ethical Analysis Framework
When facing ethical dilemma:
1. **Identify the conflict** — Which principles clash?
2. **Gather facts** — Clinical, legal, social context
3. **Stakeholders** — Who is affected?
4. **Options** — What choices exist?
5. **Evaluate** — Apply ethical principles
6. **Decide and justify** — Document reasoning

### Ethics Committee Referral
Consider referral when:
- Complex end-of-life decisions
- Conflict between patient/family wishes
- Experimental treatment decisions
- Resource allocation dilemmas

### Common Viva Questions
- Q: What is therapeutic privilege?
  - A: Doctor's right to withhold info that may harm patient — limited use
  
- Q: Is euthanasia legal in India?
  - A: Passive euthanasia legal with safeguards; active euthanasia illegal

- Q: What is a Living Will?
  - A: Advance directive refusing life-support in terminal illness

### IPC Sections on Professional Ethics
- **Section 269:** Negligent act spreading infection
- **Section 270:** Malignant act spreading infection
- **Section 52:** "Good faith" — with due care and attention
