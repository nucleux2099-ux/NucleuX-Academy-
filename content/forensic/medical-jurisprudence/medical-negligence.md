# Medical Negligence

> 📚 **Sources:** Reddy's Forensic Medicine (Ch. 2), Parikh's Textbook, Supreme Court Judgments
> **Level:** UG

## Core Concept

**Medical Negligence** is the failure of a medical professional to exercise the degree of care and skill expected of a reasonably competent practitioner in the same field, resulting in harm to the patient.

It is NOT every unsuccessful treatment — medicine involves inherent uncertainty. The standard is **reasonable care**, not perfection.

## Key Points

### Essential Elements (4 Ds)
1. **Duty** — Doctor-patient relationship existed
2. **Dereliction** — Breach of that duty (substandard care)
3. **Direct causation** — Breach caused the harm
4. **Damage** — Actual harm/injury occurred

ALL four must be present for negligence claim.

### Bolam Test (Standard of Care)
From **Bolam v. Friern Hospital (1957):**

> "A doctor is not negligent if he acts in accordance with a practice accepted as proper by a responsible body of medical opinion, even if other doctors would have acted differently."

Modified by **Bolitho test (1998):**
- The practice must also be **logically defensible**
- Court can reject illogical expert opinion

### Types of Negligence

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Commission** | Wrong treatment given |
| **Omission** | Required treatment not given |
| **Criminal** | Rash/negligent act causing death |
| **Civil** | Tort leading to compensation |

### Acts Constituting Negligence
- Wrong diagnosis without proper examination
- Operating on wrong patient/site/side
- Foreign body left in surgical site
- Wrong drug/dose administered
- Delayed/denied treatment
- Failure to obtain informed consent
- Failure to refer to specialist when indicated
- Breach of confidentiality
- Inadequate postoperative care

### What is NOT Negligence
- **Error of judgment** — Reasonable choice that proved wrong
- **Therapeutic misadventure** — Known complication despite proper care
- **System failure** — Not individual doctor's fault
- **Different but accepted treatment** — Variation in practice

## Medico-Legal Aspects

### Legal Framework

**Civil Liability:**
- **Consumer Protection Act (CPA) 2019** — Most common route
- **Tort law** — Compensation for damage

**Criminal Liability:**
- **Section 304A IPC** — Death by negligence (2 years + fine)
- **Section 337 IPC** — Hurt by negligent act (6 months)
- **Section 338 IPC** — Grievous hurt by negligence (2 years)

### Recent Amendment — BNS 2023
- **Section 106(1) BNS:** Death by negligence — 5 years
- **Section 106(2) BNS:** Death by rash driving — 7 years
- **Medical professionals exemption:** Section 106(1) with "rashness" not applicable if acting in good faith

### Consumer Protection Act 2019
- Medical services = "service" under CPA
- Compensation from Consumer Forum
- **District Commission:** Up to ₹1 crore
- **State Commission:** ₹1 crore to ₹10 crore
- **National Commission:** Above ₹10 crore
- Limitation period: 2 years from cause of action

### Vicarious Liability
- **Hospital/employer liable** for employee's negligence
- Private hospital liable for consultant's negligence if:
  - Using hospital facilities
  - Hospital holds out as employer
- Government hospital: Claim against State

## Examination Findings

### Documentation for Defense
- Complete medical records
- Informed consent form
- Proper investigation reports
- Treatment protocol followed
- Referral notes (if applicable)
- Communication records

### Expert Opinion Requirements
In medical negligence cases:
- Expert witness (usually from same specialty)
- Testimony on standard of care
- Whether deviation occurred
- Whether deviation caused harm

## High-Yield for Exams

### Must-Know MCQ Points
1. **Bolam test:** Practice accepted by responsible body of medical opinion
2. **Res ipsa loquitur:** "The thing speaks for itself" — negligence obvious (wrong site surgery)
3. **4 Ds of negligence:** Duty, Dereliction, Direct causation, Damage
4. **Section 304A IPC:** Death by negligence (2 years)
5. **CPA limitation period:** 2 years
6. **Contributory negligence:** Patient's fault reduces compensation
7. **Vicarious liability:** Employer liable for employee's negligence

### Landmark Cases

| Case | Principle |
|------|-----------|
| **Bolam v. Friern Hospital (1957)** | Standard of care by peer practice |
| **Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005)** | Criminal prosecution only for gross negligence |
| **Indian Medical Association v. VP Shantha (1995)** | Medical profession under CPA |
| **Malay Kumar Ganguly v. Sukumar (2009)** | Res ipsa loquitur application |

### Jacob Mathew Guidelines (Supreme Court)
For criminal prosecution of doctors:
1. Private complaint: Refer to competent doctor/committee first
2. No arrest unless absolutely necessary
3. Simple summons in first instance
4. Negligence must be **gross** — not merely absence of ordinary care

### Common Viva Questions
- Q: Difference between error of judgment and negligence?
  - A: Error of judgment = reasonable choice that proved wrong; Negligence = substandard care below expected level

- Q: Can a doctor be prosecuted criminally for negligence?
  - A: Yes, under Section 304A IPC, but only for gross negligence (Jacob Mathew case)

### Defense Against Negligence Claims
1. **No doctor-patient relationship**
2. **Standard of care met** (Bolam defense)
3. **No causation** — Harm not due to breach
4. **Contributory negligence** — Patient's fault
5. **Limitation** — Claim too late
6. **Informed consent** — Known risk accepted
7. **Emergency doctrine** — Circumstances forced deviation
