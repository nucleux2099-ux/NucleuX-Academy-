# Courts & Medical Evidence

> 📚 **Sources:** Reddy's Forensic Medicine (Ch. 1-2), Parikh's Textbook, Indian Evidence Act 1872
> **Level:** UG

## Core Concept

Medical professionals frequently interact with the legal system as **witnesses**. Understanding court structure, types of evidence, and how to give testimony is essential for effective medico-legal practice.

**Expert witness** testimony helps the court understand medical facts that are beyond common knowledge.

## Key Points

### Indian Court Hierarchy

| Level | Court | Jurisdiction |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Supreme Court | Highest appellate court, constitutional matters |
| 2 | High Court | Appellate, original in serious crimes |
| 3 | Sessions Court | Serious crimes (homicide, rape) |
| 4 | Magistrate Court | Less serious offenses |

**Civil Courts:** District Court → High Court → Supreme Court

### Types of Witnesses

| Type | Role | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| **Fact witness** | Testifies about observed facts | Treating doctor about treatment given |
| **Expert witness** | Gives opinion based on expertise | Forensic expert on cause of death |
| **Hostile witness** | Turns against the party who called them | Witness changing statement |

### Medical Professional as Witness
- **Summons** — Must attend court when summoned
- **Oath** — Testimony under oath
- **Examination-in-chief** — By party who called
- **Cross-examination** — By opposing party
- **Re-examination** — By original party

**Conduct in Court:**
- Dress formally
- Speak clearly and confidently
- Answer only what is asked
- Say "I don't know" if uncertain
- Don't volunteer information
- Bring all relevant records
- Stick to facts, avoid speculation

### Types of Evidence

**Classification:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Direct** | Evidence of the fact itself (eyewitness) |
| **Circumstantial** | Inference drawn from facts |
| **Documentary** | Written records, photographs |
| **Physical** | Objects (weapon, clothing) |
| **Expert** | Opinion by qualified expert |
| **Hearsay** | What someone else said (usually inadmissible) |

**Medical Evidence:**
- Autopsy reports
- Clinical records
- Injury certificates
- FSL reports
- Expert testimony
- Photographs/diagrams

### Expert Witness Testimony

**Requirements:**
- Qualified by training, education, experience
- Gives opinion within area of expertise
- Opinion based on facts of the case
- Must explain reasoning

**Can Be Asked:**
- Opinion on cause of death
- Whether injuries self-inflicted
- Time since death
- Nature of weapon
- Compatibility with alleged events

**Cannot Be Asked:**
- Guilt or innocence (court's decision)
- Questions outside expertise
- Speculative answers

## Medico-Legal Aspects

### Relevant IPC/CrPC Sections

| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Section 45 IEA** | Expert opinion admissible |
| **Section 293 CrPC** | Reports by government scientific experts as evidence |
| **Section 174 CrPC** | Police investigation of unnatural death |
| **Section 176 CrPC** | Magistrate's inquest |
| **Section 53 CrPC** | Medical examination of accused |
| **Section 53A CrPC** | Examination of accused in rape cases |
| **Section 164A CrPC** | Medical examination of rape victim |

### Summons and Conduct Orders
- **Summons** — Notice to appear in court
- **Conduct Order** — Order to produce document/record
- Non-compliance = contempt of court (fine/imprisonment)

### Professional Immunity
- **No absolute immunity** for doctors in testimony
- Must answer all questions (unlike lawyer-client privilege)
- Patient's consent not required for court-ordered testimony
- Protected when giving honest opinion in good faith

### Hostile Cross-Examination Tactics

**Common strategies by lawyers:**
- Attacking qualifications
- Highlighting contradictions
- Asking leading questions
- Creating doubt about certainty
- Using time pressure

**How to handle:**
- Stay calm and composed
- Take time to think before answering
- Ask for clarification if needed
- Don't argue with lawyer
- Defer to records if memory unclear

## Examination Findings

### Preparing for Court
1. **Review all records** thoroughly
2. **Refresh memory** about the case
3. **Know your qualifications** (will be asked)
4. **Prepare notes** (can refer with court permission)
5. **Discuss with prosecutor** (understand what's expected)
6. **Dress formally**
7. **Arrive early**

### Giving Testimony — Best Practices
- Face the judge/jury when answering
- Speak clearly and at moderate pace
- Use simple language (avoid jargon)
- Define medical terms if used
- Use diagrams to explain if helpful
- Acknowledge limitations honestly
- Don't memorize — understand the case

## High-Yield for Exams

### Must-Know MCQ Points
1. **Expert opinion admissible under:** Section 45 IEA
2. **Scientific expert reports admissible under:** Section 293 CrPC
3. **Expert can give:** Opinion within expertise
4. **Expert cannot determine:** Guilt or innocence
5. **Hostile witness:** Changes statement to favor opposite party
6. **Hearsay evidence:** Generally inadmissible
7. **Leading questions:** Not allowed in examination-in-chief
8. **Dying declaration:** Exception to hearsay (Section 32 IEA)

### Inquest Types

| Type | Authority | Purpose |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Police inquest** (Sec 174 CrPC) | Police officer | Preliminary investigation |
| **Magistrate's inquest** (Sec 176 CrPC) | Executive Magistrate | Deaths in custody, suspicious deaths |
| **Coroner's inquest** | Coroner | Not in India (UK system) |

### Common Viva Questions
- Q: Difference between fact witness and expert witness?
  - A: Fact witness reports observations; expert witness gives opinions based on expertise
  
- Q: Can doctor refuse to attend court?
  - A: No, summons is legally binding; non-compliance is contempt

- Q: What is leading question?
  - A: Question suggesting the answer; not allowed in examination-in-chief
