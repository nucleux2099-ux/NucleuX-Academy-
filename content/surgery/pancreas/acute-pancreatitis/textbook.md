## 1. History — From "Saponification of the Omentum" to Step-Up Necrosectomy

Acute pancreatitis was a death sentence before the 20th century — a disease recognized only at autopsy. Reginald Fitz (the same Fitz who named appendicitis) published the first comprehensive clinical-pathological description in 1889, distinguishing hemorrhagic, suppurative, and gangrenous forms. In 1901, Eugene Opie at Johns Hopkins proposed the **common channel theory** — a gallstone impacted at the ampulla of Vater permits bile reflux into the pancreatic duct, triggering pancreatitis. While oversimplified, this hypothesis drove decades of surgical thinking.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1652 | **Tulp** — First probable clinical description of acute pancreatitis |
| 1882 | **Balser** — Described "fat necrosis" of the omentum in pancreatitis |
| 1889 | **Reginald Fitz** — First systematic clinical-pathological classification |
| 1901 | **Opie** — Common channel hypothesis (gallstone at ampulla) |
| 1925 | **Lord Moynihan** — Called pancreatitis "the most terrible of all calamities that occur in connection with the abdominal viscera" |
| 1974 | **Ranson** — First prognostic scoring system |
| 1992 | **Atlanta Classification** — Standardized definitions and severity grading |
| 2010 | **PANTER trial** — Step-up approach superior to open necrosectomy |
| 2012 | **Revised Atlanta Classification** — Redefined local complications and severity |

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56*

Lord Moynihan's dramatic statement remains apt — severe acute pancreatitis carries mortality of 15–30% even with modern intensive care, making it one of the few abdominal catastrophes where the surgeon's instinct to operate must often be restrained.

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

🟢 **Embryology:** The pancreas develops from **two separate buds** of the foregut endoderm during the **5th week** of gestation. The **dorsal bud** (from the dorsal mesentery) gives rise to the body, tail, and part of the head. The **ventral bud** (from the hepatic diverticulum, closely associated with the bile duct) forms the uncinate process and the remaining head. During the **7th week**, the ventral bud rotates clockwise (180°) behind the duodenum to fuse with the dorsal bud. The ventral duct fuses with the distal dorsal duct to form the **main pancreatic duct (of Wirsung)**, while the proximal dorsal duct persists as the **accessory duct (of Santorini)**.

🔵 **Pancreas divisum** — failure of ductal fusion — occurs in **5–10%** of the population and is the most common congenital pancreatic anomaly. It may predispose to recurrent acute pancreatitis by forcing the majority of exocrine secretion through the smaller minor papilla.

🟢 **Surgical anatomy relevant to pancreatitis:**

- **Retroperitoneal location** — the body and tail lie in the anterior pararenal space; inflammation tracks along fascial planes (explaining Grey Turner and Cullen signs)
- **Intimate relationship with splenic vein** — runs along the posterior surface of the body and tail; pancreatic inflammation → **splanchnic vein thrombosis** (splenic vein, portal vein, SMV)
- **Blood supply:** Head — superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries (GDA and SMA branches); Body/tail — branches of splenic artery (dorsal pancreatic, great pancreatic, caudal pancreatic arteries)
- **Ductal anatomy:** Main duct joins the common bile duct at the **ampulla of Vater** in 60–70%; separate openings in 30–40%

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The pancreas lacks a true capsule — it is invested only by a thin layer of connective tissue. This anatomical fact explains why pancreatic inflammation spreads so readily into peripancreatic tissues, retroperitoneum, and transverse mesocolon. It also explains why necrosis collections are often poorly defined early in the disease course.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.50A–B; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.80*

## 3. Pathophysiology — Autodigestion and the SIRS Cascade

### The Central Event: Premature Trypsinogen Activation

🟢 Under normal conditions, pancreatic acinar cells synthesize digestive enzymes as inactive **zymogens** (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, prophospholipase A2). Trypsinogen is activated to trypsin only in the duodenum by enterokinase. Multiple safeguards prevent premature activation: compartmentalization in zymogen granules, pancreatic secretory trypsin inhibitor (PSTI/SPINK1), autolysis of prematurely activated trypsin, and ductal flushing.

🟢 In acute pancreatitis, these safeguards fail:

```
Inciting event (gallstone, alcohol, etc.)
    → Premature intracellular trypsinogen → trypsin conversion
    → Trypsin activates other zymogens (cascade amplification)
    → Autodigestion of pancreatic parenchyma
    → Release of DAMPs + cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, PAF)
    → Local inflammation → SIRS → organ failure
```

🔵 **Mechanisms by etiology:**
- **Gallstones:** Transient ampullary obstruction → ductal hypertension → acinar cell injury. The common channel theory (bile reflux) is now considered less important than the duct hypertension mechanism.
- **Alcohol:** Multiple mechanisms — direct toxic metabolite injury (fatty acid ethyl esters), increased ductal permeability, sphincter of Oddi spasm, oxidative stress. Requires years of heavy use (>4–5 drinks/day for >5 years), yet only 5% of heavy drinkers develop pancreatitis — implying cofactors (genetic, dietary, smoking).
- **Hypertriglyceridemia:** Free fatty acids generated by pancreatic lipase are directly toxic to acinar cells. Typically occurs at triglycerides **>1000 mg/dL**.

### The Two-Phase Model

🟢 **This is fundamental to understanding management decisions:**

| Phase | Timing | Pathology | Cause of Death |
|-------|--------|-----------|----------------|
| **Early** | Week 1–2 | SIRS → organ failure (pulmonary, renal, cardiovascular) | Organ failure (MOF) |
| **Late** | After week 2 | Local complications — necrosis, infection, pseudocyst, WON | Infected necrosis + sepsis |

🔵 The early phase mortality is driven entirely by **organ failure** — not by local complications. This is why early surgery for necrosis is harmful (adding surgical insult to an already maximal inflammatory state) and why ICU-level supportive care is the cornerstone of early management.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The concept of "determinant-based classification" (Dellinger et al., 2012) proposes that severity is determined by the interaction of two independent determinants: **(peri)pancreatic necrosis** (local) and **organ failure** (systemic). The worst outcomes occur when both are present — infected necrosis PLUS persistent organ failure carries mortality of **30–50%**.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.82*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

🟢 **The classic triad:**
1. **Severe epigastric pain** — acute onset, boring, radiating to the back ("belt-like"), worse supine, improved by leaning forward (the "pancreatic position")
2. **Nausea and vomiting** — persistent, bilious, does not relieve the pain
3. **Abdominal tenderness** — epigastric, with guarding; surprisingly, rigidity is often absent early ("soft board" — less peritoneal irritation than expected given the severity of pain)

🔵 **Pain characteristics by etiology:**
- Gallstone pancreatitis: acute, sudden onset (after a fatty meal), may have prior biliary colic
- Alcoholic pancreatitis: typically after a binge, onset over hours, often with prior episodes

### Physical Signs

🟢 **Mild disease:** Epigastric tenderness, mild tachycardia, low-grade fever.

🟢 **Severe disease:**
- Hemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension)
- Respiratory distress (pleural effusions, ARDS)
- Oliguria (renal failure)
- Altered mental status

🔵 **Ecchymosis signs** (appear at 48–72 hours; indicate retroperitoneal hemorrhage from necrotizing pancreatitis):
- **Cullen sign** — Periumbilical ecchymosis
- **Grey Turner sign** — Flank ecchymosis
- **Fox sign** — Inguinal ligament ecchymosis (less commonly cited)
- Present in <3% of cases; when present, mortality exceeds 37%

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS) occurs in 10–15% of severe acute pancreatitis. Massive retroperitoneal edema, visceral edema from aggressive fluid resuscitation, and ascites raise intra-abdominal pressure. Diagnosis: intravesical pressure >20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction. Management: percutaneous drainage of ascites; surgical decompression (midline laparostomy) as last resort. ACS independently doubles mortality in severe pancreatitis.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.38*

## 5. Diagnosis

### The 2-of-3 Rule

🟢 **Diagnosis requires at least 2 of 3 criteria:**

1. **Characteristic abdominal pain** — acute onset, severe, epigastric, radiating to back
2. **Serum lipase or amylase ≥3× upper limit of normal**
3. **Characteristic findings on imaging** (CT, MRI, or ultrasound)

🟢 **Enzyme pearls:**
- **Lipase** is preferred over amylase — more sensitive (94% vs 83%), more specific, stays elevated longer (8–14 days vs 3–5 days)
- Amylase can be normal in alcoholic pancreatitis (burned-out gland) and hypertriglyceridemic pancreatitis (assay interference)
- Degree of enzyme elevation does **NOT** correlate with severity
- Other causes of elevated amylase: salivary gland disease, macroamylasemia, renal failure, bowel obstruction, perforated ulcer

🔵 **Additional labs to obtain:**
- CBC, BMP, LFTs, calcium, triglycerides, lactate
- **ALT >150 U/L** has a **positive predictive value of 95%** for gallstone etiology (Tenner criteria)
- CRP >150 mg/L at 48 hours predicts severe disease

### Imaging

🟢 **Transabdominal ultrasound** — First imaging study in ALL patients. Purpose: identify gallstones/CBD dilatation (etiology), NOT to diagnose pancreatitis (bowel gas limits pancreatic visualization in 25–50%).

🟢 **CT with IV contrast (CECT):**
- **NOT indicated in the first 72 hours** unless diagnostic uncertainty or clinical deterioration
- Necrosis is best delineated after 72–96 hours (earlier imaging underestimates extent)
- Non-enhancing pancreatic parenchyma = necrosis
- Indications for CT: diagnostic doubt, failure to improve at 48–72 hours, clinical deterioration, suspected complications

🔵 **MRCP:**
- Superior to CT for detecting choledocholithiasis (sensitivity 95–100%)
- Indicated when gallstone etiology suspected but US is non-diagnostic
- Better characterization of ductal anatomy and fluid collections

🔵 **EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound):**
- Gold standard for detecting microlithiasis and small CBD stones
- Sensitivity >95% for choledocholithiasis
- Can be combined with therapeutic ERCP in the same session

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Early CT (within 24–48 hours) should be reserved for cases where the diagnosis is in doubt (other causes of acute abdomen must be excluded). Performing CT purely for severity assessment in the first 72 hours adds radiation without changing management — the clinical trajectory (organ failure, SIRS) is a better early predictor of severity than CT findings.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.82*

## 6. Classification

### Etiology — GET SMASHED

🟢 **The classic mnemonic:**

| Letter | Cause | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **G** | Gallstones | #1 cause (40–50%); F>M |
| **E** | Ethanol | #2 cause (25–35%); M>F; requires >4–5 drinks/day × years |
| **T** | Trauma | Blunt abdominal, post-ERCP (most common iatrogenic cause) |
| **S** | Steroids | Controversial; may be coincidental with underlying disease |
| **M** | Mumps / Infections | Also Coxsackie B, CMV, HIV, Mycoplasma |
| **A** | Autoimmune | Type 1 (IgG4-related) and Type 2; respond to steroids |
| **S** | Scorpion sting | *Tityus trinitatis*; also spider bites |
| **H** | Hyperlipidemia, Hypercalcemia, Hypothermia | Triglycerides >1000; hyperparathyroidism |
| **E** | ERCP | 3–5% post-ERCP pancreatitis; rectal indomethacin reduces risk |
| **D** | Drugs | Azathioprine, valproate, didanosine, GLP-1 agonists |

🟢 **Gallstones + alcohol = 80% of all cases.** In 15–25% no cause is found ("idiopathic") — many of these are occult microlithiasis or biliary sludge, detectable by EUS.

### Revised Atlanta Classification (2012)

🟢 **Severity grading — three tiers:**

| Severity | Definition | Mortality |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Mild** | No organ failure, no local/systemic complications | <1% |
| **Moderately severe** | Transient organ failure (<48h) OR local/systemic complications WITHOUT persistent organ failure | <5% |
| **Severe** | Persistent organ failure (>48h) — single or multiorgan | 15–30% |

🟢 **Organ failure** is assessed by the **Modified Marshall Score** (respiratory, renal, cardiovascular) — score ≥2 in any system = organ failure.

### Local Complications — The Atlanta Terminology

🔵 **This classification depends on TWO variables: presence of necrosis and timing (<4 weeks vs >4 weeks):**

| | < 4 weeks | > 4 weeks |
|---|-----------|-----------|
| **Interstitial (no necrosis)** | Acute peripancreatic fluid collection (APFC) | Pseudocyst |
| **Necrotizing** | Acute necrotic collection (ANC) | Walled-off necrosis (WON) |

- **APFC** — Homogeneous fluid, no wall, adjacent to pancreas. Most resolve spontaneously.
- **Pseudocyst** — Encapsulated, well-defined wall, NO solid debris, homogeneous fluid. Requires intervention only if symptomatic.
- **ANC** — Heterogeneous (fluid + solid necrotic debris), no definable wall.
- **WON** — Encapsulated, mature wall, contains solid necrotic debris. The target of necrosectomy when infected.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The critical distinction is between pseudocyst and WON — they look similar on ultrasound but have fundamentally different contents (fluid vs. solid debris) and require different interventions. MRI or CT easily differentiates them. Draining a WON as if it were a pseudocyst (simple catheter drainage) will fail because of solid debris that cannot be evacuated through a drain.

### Prognostic Scoring Systems

🔵 **Ranson's Criteria** (1974) — The original; 11 parameters (5 at admission, 6 at 48 hours):

*At admission:* Age >55, WBC >16,000, Glucose >200, LDH >350, AST >250
*At 48 hours:* Hct drop >10%, BUN rise >5, Ca²⁺ <8, PaO₂ <60, Base deficit >4, Fluid sequestration >6L

Score ≥3 = severe disease. Limitation: requires 48 hours for complete assessment; cannot be recalculated.

🔵 **APACHE II** — Most validated ICU scoring system. Score ≥8 at admission predicts severe disease. Advantage: can be recalculated daily. Disadvantage: complex (14 variables).

🔵 **BISAP** (Bedside Index for Severity in Acute Pancreatitis) — Simple, practical:
- **B**UN >25 mg/dL
- **I**mpaired mental status (GCS <15)
- **S**IRS (≥2 criteria)
- **A**ge >60
- **P**leural effusion

Score ≥3: mortality 5–22%. Performs comparably to APACHE II with far fewer variables.

🔵 **CTSI (CT Severity Index) / Balthazar Score:**

| Grade | CT Findings | Points |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| A | Normal pancreas | 0 |
| B | Focal/diffuse enlargement | 1 |
| C | Peripancreatic inflammation | 2 |
| D | Single fluid collection | 3 |
| E | ≥2 collections or gas | 4 |

Add necrosis score: None (0), <30% (2), 30–50% (4), >50% (6). Maximum CTSI = 10.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.82*

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management — The First 72 Hours

🟢 **The cornerstone is aggressive supportive care, NOT surgery.**

**A. Fluid Resuscitation:**
- **Ringer's lactate** preferred over normal saline (less hyperchloremic acidosis, possibly anti-inflammatory via lactate)
- **Goal-directed** resuscitation: 5–10 mL/kg/hr initially, titrated to clinical targets (UOP >0.5 mL/kg/hr, HR <120, MAP >65)
- 🔵 Recent evidence (WATERFALL trial, 2022) suggests **moderate** rather than aggressive resuscitation may be safer — aggressive fluids (20 mL/kg bolus + 3 mL/kg/hr) increased fluid overload without improving outcomes
- Monitor for fluid overload (pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome)

**B. Pain Management:**
- 🟢 Multimodal analgesia; **opioids are acceptable** — the old prohibition against morphine (theorized to cause sphincter of Oddi spasm) is NOT supported by evidence
- 🔵 Epidural analgesia for severe pain — improves splanchnic perfusion (theoretical benefit)
- NSAIDs as adjuncts when not contraindicated

**C. Nutrition:**
- 🟢 **Early oral feeding** (within 24 hours) if tolerated — low-fat solid diet; no need for bowel rest
- 🔵 If oral intake not tolerated: **enteral nutrition via nasojejunal tube** — superior to TPN (fewer infectious complications, lower cost, maintains gut barrier)
- 🟢 **TPN** is a last resort — only if enteral route completely fails
- 🟢 The concept of "pancreatic rest" (NPO) is **outdated** — early enteral feeding does not worsen pancreatitis

**D. Antibiotics:**
- 🟢 **NO prophylactic antibiotics** — multiple RCTs and meta-analyses show no benefit in preventing infected necrosis
- Antibiotics ONLY for documented infection: infected necrosis (gas on CT, positive FNA culture), cholangitis, other specific infections

### Specific Management Issues

#### ERCP in Gallstone Pancreatitis

🟢 **ERCP is indicated ONLY with concurrent cholangitis** — within 24 hours.
- 🔵 ERCP is NOT routine for "predicted severe" gallstone pancreatitis without cholangitis. The APEC trial (2009) showed no benefit of early ERCP in severe gallstone pancreatitis without bile duct obstruction.
- 🟢 If CBD stone suspected without cholangitis → MRCP or EUS first; ERCP only if stone confirmed.
- 🔵 Post-ERCP pancreatitis prophylaxis: **rectal indomethacin 100 mg** (reduces risk by ~50%) + aggressive pancreatic duct stenting in high-risk patients.

#### Cholecystectomy Timing

🟢 **Mild gallstone pancreatitis:** Cholecystectomy during the **same admission** — delays increase recurrence risk (up to 18% recurrence within 6 weeks of discharge).
- 🔵 The PONCHO trial (2015) confirmed same-admission cholecystectomy reduced gallstone-related complications from 17% to 5%.

🔵 **Severe gallstone pancreatitis:** Cholecystectomy deferred until inflammation resolves (typically 6–8 weeks). Earlier surgery in a hostile abdomen with peripancreatic collections risks major morbidity.

#### The Step-Up Approach to Infected Necrosis

🔵 **The paradigm shift in managing infected pancreatic necrosis:**

The traditional approach was open necrosectomy — an operation with 25–40% mortality and devastating morbidity. Three landmark trials changed practice:

| Trial | Year | Key Finding |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **PANTER** | 2010 | Step-up approach (percutaneous drain → VARD) vs. open necrosectomy: step-up reduced MOF + death (12% vs 40%) |
| **TENSION** | 2018 | Endoscopic transgastric approach vs. surgical step-up: similar outcomes, but endoscopic had fewer fistulae and shorter LOS |
| **POINTER** | 2021 | Immediate catheter drainage vs. postponed drainage with antibiotics: no difference — supports antibiotics-first with drainage on demand |

🔵 **Current recommended algorithm:**
1. **Antibiotics** (carbapenems or fluoroquinolone + metronidazole) — can delay intervention by weeks
2. **Percutaneous catheter drainage** (retroperitoneal approach preferred) or **endoscopic transgastric drainage** — resolves infection in ~35% without further intervention
3. **Minimally invasive necrosectomy** — VARD (video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement) or endoscopic necrosectomy — if drainage insufficient
4. **Open necrosectomy** — absolute last resort

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Timing matters enormously. Intervention before **4 weeks** (before WON matures) is associated with significantly higher morbidity. The goal is to delay intervention as long as possible — ideally >4 weeks from onset — to allow the necrotic collection to become walled-off, making it safer to drain and debride. The only exception is deteriorating sepsis unresponsive to antibiotics.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.55–56; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.83*

## 8. Complications

### Local Complications

🟢 **Pseudocyst:**
- Encapsulated collection of amylase-rich fluid WITHOUT solid debris
- Arises from ductal disruption in interstitial (edematous) pancreatitis
- Most resolve spontaneously; intervene if symptomatic (pain, gastric outlet obstruction, infection) or >6 cm persisting >6 weeks
- Options: endoscopic cystogastrostomy (preferred), percutaneous drainage, surgical cystogastrostomy/cystojejunostomy

🔵 **Walled-Off Necrosis (WON):**
- Encapsulated collection containing solid necrotic debris
- The mature form of an acute necrotic collection (>4 weeks)
- When sterile: conservative management
- When infected: step-up approach (see Treatment)

🔵 **Pancreatic Duct Disruption:**
- Partial disruption → peripancreatic collection
- Complete disruption ("disconnected duct syndrome") → the tail segment is isolated; persistent external fistula or recurrent collections
- Management: endoscopic transpapillary stenting (partial); distal pancreatectomy or endoscopic transmural drainage (complete/disconnected)

### Vascular Complications

🔵 **Pseudoaneurysm:**
- Splenic artery (most common), GDA, pancreaticoduodenal arteries
- Results from enzymatic erosion of arterial wall
- Sentinel bleed → catastrophic hemorrhage
- Diagnosis: CT angiography
- Treatment: **angioembolization** (first-line); surgery if embolization fails

🔴 **Splanchnic Vein Thrombosis (SVT):**
- Occurs in **5–20%** of acute pancreatitis; higher in necrotizing disease
- Involves splenic vein (most common), portal vein, and/or SMV
- Splenic vein thrombosis → **left-sided (sinistral) portal hypertension** → isolated gastric varices (short gastric and gastroepiploic collaterals)
- Most cases are clinically silent and resolve with pancreatitis resolution
- Anticoagulation: recommended for portal/SMV thrombosis with >50% luminal occlusion or symptomatic; controversial for isolated splenic vein thrombosis
- Splenectomy is reserved for recurrent variceal bleeding from left-sided portal hypertension

🔴 **SS Pearl (Sarath's thesis topic!):** SVT in acute pancreatitis is underdiagnosed. Routine contrast-enhanced CT in the portal venous phase detects SVT in up to 20% of patients with necrotizing pancreatitis. Early detection matters because portal/SMV thrombosis can progress to mesenteric ischemia and cavernous transformation. Current evidence favors **early anticoagulation** when thrombus extends to the portal vein or SMV, even in the presence of peripancreatic inflammation.

### Systemic Complications

🟢 **Organ failure** (Revised Atlanta — Modified Marshall Score):
- **Respiratory:** PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300 (ALI/ARDS) — most common organ failure
- **Renal:** Creatinine ≥1.9 mg/dL
- **Cardiovascular:** Systolic BP <90 despite fluids

🔵 **Other systemic complications:** DIC, hyperglycemia/diabetic ketoacidosis (endocrine destruction), abdominal compartment syndrome (see Section 4), pleural effusions (left > right), pericardial effusion.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54–56; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.56*

## 9. Special Topics

### Post-ERCP Pancreatitis

🔵 The most common complication of ERCP (3–5% overall; up to 15–30% in high-risk patients). Risk factors: female sex, suspected sphincter of Oddi dysfunction, difficult cannulation, pancreatic duct injection, prior post-ERCP pancreatitis.

🔵 **Prevention:**
- **Rectal indomethacin** (100 mg PR) — proven in multiple RCTs to reduce risk by 50%
- Prophylactic pancreatic duct stenting (5 Fr, with spontaneous migration design)
- Aggressive post-procedure hydration (Ringer's lactate)
- Wire-guided cannulation reduces risk vs. contrast injection

### Autoimmune Pancreatitis (AIP)

🔴 **Two types:**
- **Type 1:** IgG4-related disease; affects older males; elevated serum IgG4; may mimic pancreatic cancer (focal mass, "sausage-shaped" pancreas with "halo" sign); responds dramatically to steroids
- **Type 2:** Not IgG4-related; associated with IBD (UC); granulocytic epithelial lesion (GEL) on biopsy

🔴 HISORt criteria (Histology, Imaging, Serology, Other organ involvement, Response to steroids) are used for diagnosis. The key clinical challenge is distinguishing AIP from pancreatic adenocarcinoma — EUS-FNA with core biopsy is essential.

### Acute Pancreatitis in Pregnancy

🔵 Most commonly due to gallstones (>70%). Incidence: 1 in 1,000–10,000 pregnancies. Lipase preferred for diagnosis (amylase may be physiologically elevated). US is first-line imaging; MRCP for bile duct evaluation. Cholecystectomy is safest in the **second trimester**. Conservative management for mild disease; ERCP with radiation shielding if cholangitis develops.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.54, 56A; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.82–83*

## 10. Other Important Topics

### Recurrent Acute Pancreatitis
🔵 Defined as ≥2 episodes with complete resolution between episodes. Occurs in ~20% of patients. Workup should include EUS (microlithiasis, pancreas divisum, small tumors), genetic testing (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR mutations) in young patients, and consideration of autoimmune pancreatitis. Cholecystectomy prevents recurrence in gallstone pancreatitis; cessation of alcohol and smoking is essential.

### Chronic Pancreatitis — Brief Cross-Reference
🔵 Recurrent acute pancreatitis may progress to chronic pancreatitis (the "necrosis-fibrosis" sequence). Key features: chronic pain, exocrine insufficiency (steatorrhea), endocrine insufficiency (diabetes — "type 3c"), pancreatic calcifications, ductal irregularities. See Chapter [future cross-reference] for detailed discussion.

### Nutrition in Severe Pancreatitis
🔵 **Nasogastric vs. nasojejunal feeding:** Recent evidence suggests nasogastric feeding is non-inferior to nasojejunal feeding and is technically simpler to establish. The critical principle is early enteral nutrition regardless of route — reduces infectious complications, maintains gut barrier, and decreases mortality compared to TPN.

🔵 A useful framework:

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Gallstones and alcohol account for 80%** of acute pancreatitis. Use GET SMASHED for the full etiological list.
2. **Diagnosis requires 2 of 3:** characteristic pain, lipase/amylase ≥3× ULN, imaging findings. **Lipase is preferred** over amylase.
3. **Severity grading (Revised Atlanta):** Mild (no organ failure), Moderately severe (transient OF <48h), Severe (persistent OF >48h).
4. **CT should NOT be done in the first 72 hours** unless diagnostic doubt — necrosis is underestimated and management doesn't change.
5. **Treatment is supportive:** Ringer's lactate resuscitation, pain management (opioids are fine), early oral feeding, NO prophylactic antibiotics.
6. **Cholecystectomy during same admission** for mild gallstone pancreatitis — delays increase recurrence to 18%.
7. **ERCP only if cholangitis** — NOT routine for predicted severe gallstone pancreatitis.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **Two-phase model:** Early deaths (week 1–2) from organ failure/SIRS; late deaths (after week 2) from infected necrosis and sepsis.
9. **Atlanta local complications:** APFC → pseudocyst (no necrosis); ANC → WON (necrosis). Timing cutoff: 4 weeks.
10. **Step-up approach** for infected necrosis: drainage → minimally invasive necrosectomy → open necrosectomy. PANTER trial showed step-up reduced MOF + death from 40% to 12%.
11. **BISAP score** is the simplest validated bedside predictor; APACHE II the most validated overall. Ranson's cannot be recalculated.
12. **ALT >150 U/L** has 95% PPV for gallstone etiology. CRP >150 at 48h predicts severe disease.
13. **Delay necrosectomy to >4 weeks** whenever possible — allows WON maturation and reduces surgical morbidity dramatically.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **Splanchnic vein thrombosis** occurs in up to 20% of necrotizing pancreatitis — splenic vein thrombosis causes left-sided portal hypertension with isolated gastric varices; portal/SMV thrombosis warrants anticoagulation.
15. **Abdominal compartment syndrome** (intravesical pressure >20 mmHg + organ dysfunction) complicates 10–15% of severe disease and doubles mortality; manage with percutaneous ascites drainage before surgical decompression.
16. **WATERFALL trial (2022):** Aggressive fluid resuscitation may cause harm — moderate, goal-directed resuscitation preferred.
17. **POINTER trial (2021):** Immediate catheter drainage is not superior to antibiotics alone for infected necrosis — supports a "drainage on demand" strategy.
18. **Disconnected duct syndrome** after necrotizing pancreatitis isolates the distal pancreas, causing persistent fistulae or recurrent collections — requires transmural drainage or distal pancreatectomy.
