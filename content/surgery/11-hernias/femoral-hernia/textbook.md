## 1. History — The Hernia That Hides in Plain Sight

Femoral hernia has a long and humbling surgical history — a hernia frequently misdiagnosed, often incarcerated, and historically lethal. For centuries it was conflated with inguinal hernia until anatomists began to distinguish the two.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1561 | **Ambroise Paré** — Among the first to describe herniae in the groin region, though without distinguishing femoral from inguinal |
| 1724 | **Jean-Louis Petit** — First clear anatomical description of femoral hernia as a distinct entity |
| 1804 | **Antonio de Gimbernat** — Described the lacunar ligament and proposed incising it to release strangulated femoral hernias |
| 1876 | **Thomas Annandale** — Described the femoral approach for repair |
| 1898 | **Georg Lotheissen** — Described the preperitoneal approach, later refined by McEvedy |
| 1936 | **Henry** — Popularized the anterior (inguinal canal) approach |
| 1942 | **McEvedy** — Modified Lotheissen's high approach using a vertical incision over the medial inguinal canal |

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.12; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44*

Gimbernat's insight — that the rigid lacunar ligament is the agent of strangulation — remains the single most important anatomical concept in understanding why femoral hernias behave so dangerously.

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

### The Femoral Canal

🟢 The femoral canal is the medial compartment of the **femoral sheath**, a funnel-shaped fascial sleeve that surrounds the femoral vessels as they pass deep to the inguinal ligament.

🟢 **Boundaries of the femoral canal:**

| Boundary | Structure |
|----------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | Inguinal ligament (Poupart's ligament) |
| **Posterior** | Pectineal ligament (Cooper's ligament) and pectineus muscle |
| **Lateral** | Femoral vein |
| **Medial** | Lacunar ligament (Gimbernat's ligament) — the sharp, crescent-shaped edge |

🟢 **The femoral ring** is the abdominal opening of the femoral canal — the site through which a femoral hernia protrudes. It is approximately **1.25 cm** in diameter, normally filled with fat and a lymph node (**Cloquet's node** / node of Rosenmüller).

🟢 **Why femoral hernias strangulate so often:** The femoral ring is bounded by unyielding structures on three sides — the inguinal ligament anteriorly, Cooper's ligament posteriorly, and the lacunar ligament medially. Only the femoral vein laterally offers any "give." Once bowel enters this rigid ring, it cannot expand, and vascular compromise occurs rapidly.

🔵 **The femoral sheath** is formed by transversalis fascia (anterior) and iliac fascia (posterior). It contains three compartments:
- **Lateral:** Femoral artery
- **Intermediate:** Femoral vein
- **Medial:** Femoral canal (containing lymphatics and fat)

The femoral nerve lies **outside** the femoral sheath, lateral to the artery, separated by the iliac fascia.

🔵 **Coverings of a femoral hernia** (from deep to superficial):
1. Peritoneum
2. Femoral septum (extraperitoneal fat and connective tissue closing the femoral ring)
3. Femoral sheath (transversalis fascia continuation)
4. Cribriform fascia (covering the saphenous opening)
5. Subcutaneous fat and skin

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Aberrant Obturator Artery:** In approximately 25–30% of individuals, the obturator artery arises from the inferior epigastric artery (or external iliac) rather than the internal iliac. When it does, it frequently courses along the medial border of the femoral ring, draped over the lacunar ligament — creating a "corona mortis" (crown of death). Blind division of the lacunar ligament during emergency femoral hernia repair can result in catastrophic hemorrhage. **Always palpate and inspect the lacunar ligament before dividing it.**

🔵 **Why femoral hernias are more common in women:**
- The female pelvis is wider, resulting in a wider femoral canal
- Cooper's ligament is more horizontal in women
- Pregnancy stretches the transversalis fascia and widens the femoral ring
- Despite this, the overall incidence is low — inguinal hernias remain the most common groin hernia in both sexes

## 3. Epidemiology & Risk Factors

🟢 **Key epidemiological facts:**

- Femoral hernias account for **2–4% of all groin hernias** (some series report up to 6%)
- **Female:male ratio** ≈ 4:1 (but inguinal hernia is still commoner in women overall)
- **Peak incidence:** 40–70 years
- **Right side** predominates (~60%) — possibly because the sigmoid colon shields the left femoral ring
- **Bilateral** in 10–15% of cases

🟢 **Risk factors:**
- Female sex
- Increasing age
- Multiparity (pregnancy-related weakening of pelvic floor)
- Raised intra-abdominal pressure (chronic cough, constipation, heavy lifting)
- Previous inguinal hernia repair (especially tissue repairs — Shouldice, Bassini)
- Low BMI / thin body habitus (less preperitoneal fat to plug the femoral ring)

🔵 **The post-herniorrhaphy femoral hernia:** This is an important entity. After anterior inguinal hernia repair (particularly tissue-based repairs that narrow the deep ring or tighten the posterior wall), the transversalis fascia is effectively strengthened superiorly but may redirect intra-abdominal forces inferiorly through the femoral ring. Reported in 1–4% of open inguinal repairs. Mesh repairs that cover the myopectineal orifice (preperitoneal approaches, laparoscopic TEP/TAPP) virtually eliminate this risk.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Femoral hernias constitute a disproportionate share of **emergency hernia surgery**. While they represent only 2–4% of groin hernias overall, they account for **20–40% of strangulated groin hernias**. The strangulation rate for femoral hernias is approximately **22–45%** — dramatically higher than the 1–3% for inguinal hernias.

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### The Typical Presentation

🟢 **The classical patient:** A thin, elderly woman presenting with a small, tense, irreducible lump **below and lateral to the pubic tubercle** (distinguishing it from an inguinal hernia which appears above and medial to the pubic tubercle).

🟢 **Key clinical features:**
- Often **small** — may be easily overlooked, especially in obese patients
- Frequently **irreducible** at first presentation (up to 40%)
- Located in the **upper medial thigh**, in the region of the femoral triangle
- Does not have a cough impulse if incarcerated (the rigid ring prevents expansion)
- The mass typically appears **lateral to the pubic tubercle** — this is the single most important clinical landmark

🟢 **Relationship to the pubic tubercle — the key differentiator:**

| Hernia Type | Relation to Pubic Tubercle |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Inguinal** | Above and medial |
| **Femoral** | Below and lateral |
| **Saphena varix** | Below and lateral (but has a cough impulse, compressible, bluish tinge) |

🔵 **Why femoral hernias are so often missed:**
1. Small size — may be obscured by subcutaneous fat
2. Often painless until complication occurs
3. Frequently misdiagnosed as inguinal hernia, lymph node, lipoma, or saphena varix
4. In **Richter's hernia** (partial circumference of bowel wall incarcerated), there may be no clinical features of obstruction — only ischemia and strangulation of the anti-mesenteric border

### Presentation with Complications

🟢 **Strangulation** — the most feared complication:
- Sudden onset of severe groin pain
- Tender, irreducible lump with overlying erythema
- Features of small bowel obstruction: colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, absolute constipation
- Systemic signs: tachycardia, fever, leukocytosis

🔵 **Richter's hernia** deserves special emphasis with femoral hernias:
- Only a portion (usually the anti-mesenteric wall) of the bowel circumference is trapped
- **No obstruction** — stool and gas continue to pass
- The patient may present with **strangulation without obstruction** — a deceptive combination
- Particularly common in femoral hernias because the femoral ring is small enough to trap only part of the bowel wall
- Can progress to perforation and fecal fistula if missed

🔵 **Maydl's hernia:** A "W-loop" hernia where two adjacent loops of bowel enter the sac, with the intervening segment (within the abdomen) becoming ischemic. Rare but catastrophic — the strangulated segment is retrograde and not within the hernia sac, so it may be missed at operation.

🟢 **De Garengeot's hernia:** The appendix within a femoral hernia sac. Rare (<1% of femoral hernias) but analogous to Amyand's hernia (appendix in inguinal hernia). Presents as a tender groin lump, sometimes mimicking strangulated femoral hernia. Treatment: appendectomy + hernia repair.

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.12*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

🟢 **Femoral hernia is primarily a clinical diagnosis.** The key examination findings are:
- Lump below the inguinal ligament, lateral and inferior to the pubic tubercle
- The lump does NOT reduce with pressure applied superiorly (unlike inguinal hernias)
- Absence of a cough impulse (if incarcerated)

🟢 **Differential diagnosis of a groin lump:**

| Diagnosis | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Inguinal hernia** | Above and medial to pubic tubercle; cough impulse |
| **Femoral hernia** | Below and lateral to pubic tubercle; often irreducible |
| **Saphena varix** | Compressible; bluish tinge; disappears on lying down; positive Cruveilhier sign (cough impulse with thrill) |
| **Lymph node** | Firm, mobile; look for distal infection/malignancy |
| **Lipoma** | Soft, lobulated, non-tender |
| **Femoral artery aneurysm** | Expansile, pulsatile |
| **Psoas abscess** | Fluctuant, cross-fluctuation to iliac fossa; signs of TB/spinal disease |
| **Ectopic testis** | Undescended/maldescended testis; confirm ipsilateral empty scrotum |

### Imaging

🔵 **Ultrasound:** First-line imaging when clinical doubt exists:
- Sensitivity 90%, specificity 82% for femoral hernias
- Can distinguish from vascular structures (Doppler), lymph nodes, and lipomas
- Demonstrates the fascial defect relative to the femoral vessels
- Dynamic scanning during Valsalva may reveal reducible contents

🔵 **CT scan:** The most accurate imaging modality:
- Sensitivity and specificity both >95%
- Precisely identifies the hernia sac, contents (omentum vs. bowel), and relationship to femoral vessels
- Essential in emergency settings to assess bowel viability, perforation, or obstruction
- Distinguishes femoral from inguinal hernia definitively (hernia neck below vs. above the inguinal ligament)

🔵 **MRI:** Excellent soft-tissue resolution but rarely necessary. Reserved for equivocal cases or occult groin hernias.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Preoperative imaging (CT or ultrasound) is invaluable in the elective setting for distinguishing femoral from inguinal hernias, as clinical misdiagnosis occurs in **up to 40% of cases** — particularly in obese patients or when an inguinal hernia and femoral hernia coexist.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.133*

## 6. Classification

🟢 **By reducibility:**

| Type | Definition | Urgency |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Reducible** | Contents return to abdomen spontaneously or with gentle pressure | Elective repair |
| **Irreducible (incarcerated)** | Contents cannot be reduced but are not ischemic | Urgent repair |
| **Strangulated** | Vascular compromise of contents | Emergency repair |

🔵 **By content (named hernias):**

| Type | Contents | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Richter's** | Partial circumference of bowel wall | Strangulation without obstruction |
| **Maydl's (W-hernia)** | Two loops with retrograde strangulation | Ischemic segment is intra-abdominal |
| **De Garengeot's** | Appendix | Mimics strangulation; needs appendectomy |
| **Littre's** | Meckel's diverticulum | Rare; may present as Richter's-type |

🔵 **By anatomical classification:**
- **Prevascular** — In front of the femoral vessels (extremely rare)
- **Retrovascular** — Behind the femoral vessels (rare; Narath's hernia)
- **Typical (medial)** — Through the femoral canal (vast majority)
- **Lacunar (Laugier's)** — Through a defect in the lacunar ligament

🔴 **EHS (European Hernia Society) Classification:** Femoral hernias are classified as "F" in the EHS groin hernia classification system (L = lateral inguinal, M = medial inguinal, F = femoral), with size grading (1 = ≤1 finger, 2 = 1–2 fingers, 3 = >2 fingers). This standardized nomenclature allows comparison across studies and audits.

## 7. Treatment

### Fundamental Principle

🟢 **All femoral hernias should be repaired when diagnosed.** This is one of the few absolute indications for elective hernia repair — the risk of strangulation is too high (22–45%) to justify watchful waiting.

🟢 **Contrast with inguinal hernia:** Watchful waiting is an accepted strategy for minimally symptomatic inguinal hernias (per the Fitzgibbons trial). This does NOT apply to femoral hernias.

### A. Open Surgical Approaches

🟢 **Three classical approaches:**

| Approach | Route | Eponym | Best For |
|----------|-------|--------|----------|
| **Low (crural)** | Below inguinal ligament, directly over femoral canal | Lockwood | Reducible, small hernias |
| **High (inguinal)** | Through inguinal canal, approaching from above | Lotheissen-McEvedy | Emergency; need to assess bowel |
| **Preperitoneal** | Through lower abdominal incision, preperitoneal plane | McEvedy (vertical) / Henry (transverse) | Emergency with suspected strangulation |

#### The Low (Lockwood) Approach

🟢 **Technique:**
1. Transverse incision over the femoral canal, below the inguinal ligament
2. Incise cribriform fascia to expose the hernial sac
3. Open the sac, inspect contents, reduce viable contents
4. Transfixe and ligate the sac at the neck
5. Repair: suture the inguinal ligament to the pectineal (Cooper's) ligament, narrowing the femoral ring

**Limitations:**
- Cannot assess bowel viability if strangulated (no access to peritoneal cavity)
- Cannot perform bowel resection
- Limited space for mesh placement
- Therefore: **not suitable for emergency/strangulated cases**

#### The High (Lotheissen-McEvedy) Approach

🔵 **Technique:**
1. Vertical or oblique incision over the medial inguinal canal extending above the inguinal ligament
2. Open the external oblique aponeurosis → retract cord/round ligament
3. Incise the transversalis fascia to enter the preperitoneal space
4. Identify the femoral hernia sac from above
5. Reduce contents — inspect bowel viability under direct vision
6. If bowel is gangrenous → extend incision, perform resection and anastomosis
7. Repair the femoral ring with mesh or sutures

🔵 **Advantages:**
- Access to peritoneal cavity — can assess bowel and perform resection
- Better exposure of the femoral ring from above
- **Preferred approach for emergency femoral hernia repair**

#### The Inguinal (Henry) Approach

🔵 Transverse suprainguinal incision, entering the preperitoneal space. Similar access as McEvedy but with a transverse (cosmetically superior) incision. Less commonly taught but functionally equivalent.

### B. Tissue Repairs

🟢 **Principles of all tissue repairs:** Close the femoral ring by approximating the inguinal ligament to Cooper's ligament (pectineal ligament) using interrupted non-absorbable sutures.

🔵 **Named tissue repairs:**

| Repair | Technique |
|--------|-----------|
| **McVay (Cooper's ligament repair)** | Inguinal ligament sutured to Cooper's ligament from pubic tubercle to femoral vein; requires a relaxing incision in the anterior rectus sheath |
| **Bassini modification** | Inguinal ligament to Cooper's ligament without relaxing incision |
| **Plug repair** | Cone of polypropylene mesh placed in the femoral ring and sutured in place |

🟢 **Critical caution:** When suturing, **never place a suture lateral to the femoral vein** — this risks narrowing or occluding the vein, causing DVT or lower limb edema.

### C. Mesh Repairs

🔵 **Mesh is the standard of care for elective femoral hernia repair** — recurrence rates are significantly lower with mesh (1–2%) compared to tissue repair (5–15%).

**Options:**
- **Mesh plug:** Cone or umbrella-shaped plug placed in the femoral canal from below (Lockwood approach). Simple but associated with mesh migration and chronic pain.
- **Preperitoneal flat mesh:** Placed in the preperitoneal space via McEvedy or laparoscopic approach. Covers the entire myopectineal orifice. **Preferred.**

🔵 **In contaminated fields (bowel resection):**
- Avoid synthetic mesh (risk of infection and mesh removal)
- Options: primary tissue repair, or biosynthetic mesh (e.g., GORE BIO-A, Phasix)
- 🔴 Recent evidence suggests that biosynthetic mesh in clean-contaminated fields has acceptable infection rates (5–8%) with lower recurrence than tissue repair alone

### D. Laparoscopic Repair

🔵 **TEP (Totally Extraperitoneal) and TAPP (Transabdominal Preperitoneal)** are the preferred approaches for elective femoral hernia repair in centers with expertise.

**Advantages:**
- Cover the entire myopectineal orifice (inguinal + femoral + obturator) with a single mesh
- Simultaneously identify and repair occult contralateral hernias (found in 10–25%)
- Reduced chronic pain compared to open repair
- Faster recovery, less wound morbidity

🟢 **TAPP technique (simplified):**
1. Pneumoperitoneum; three ports (umbilical camera, two working ports)
2. Incise peritoneum above the hernia defect
3. Reduce the hernia sac (may need gentle traction and counter-traction)
4. Identify the femoral ring, Cooper's ligament, inferior epigastric vessels
5. Place a large mesh (≥15 × 10 cm) covering the myopectineal orifice
6. Fix mesh to Cooper's ligament (tacks, glue, or self-gripping mesh)
7. Close the peritoneal flap

🟢 **TEP technique:** Similar mesh placement but entirely in the preperitoneal space without entering the peritoneal cavity. Avoids peritoneal incision and closure. Steeper learning curve.

🔴 **SS Pearl — The "Triangle of Doom" and "Triangle of Pain":**

| Danger Zone | Boundaries | Contents at Risk |
|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| **Triangle of Doom** | Vas deferens (medially) and gonadal vessels (laterally) | External iliac vessels |
| **Triangle of Pain** | Iliopubic tract (superiorly) and gonadal vessels (medially) | Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, femoral branch of genitofemoral nerve |
| **Corona Mortis** | Along the superior pubic ramus | Aberrant obturator vessels (25–30% incidence) |

**Rule:** No tacks or staples below the iliopubic tract lateral to the deep inguinal ring. Fix mesh only to Cooper's ligament and the transversus abdominis aponeurosis superiorly.

🔵 **Robotic repair (rTAPP):** Increasingly performed with articulating instruments that may facilitate dissection in a narrow pelvis. No proven superiority over standard laparoscopy for femoral hernias; higher cost.

## 8. Emergency Femoral Hernia — Special Considerations

🟢 **Emergency femoral hernia repair carries significantly higher morbidity and mortality than elective repair:**

| Parameter | Elective | Emergency |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Mortality** | <0.5% | 5–13% |
| **Bowel resection rate** | Negligible | 15–40% |
| **Wound infection** | 2% | 10–15% |
| **Recurrence** | 1–5% | 5–15% |
| **Length of stay** | 1 day (or day-case) | 5–10 days |

🟢 **Operative priorities in emergency:**
1. **Approach:** McEvedy (high) — allows bowel assessment and resection
2. **Release the constriction:** Carefully divide the lacunar ligament medially (after checking for corona mortis)
3. **Assess bowel viability:** Warm saline packs, wait 5–10 minutes; look for color, peristalsis, mesenteric pulsation
4. **Resect if non-viable:** Primary anastomosis if possible; stoma if grossly contaminated
5. **Repair the defect:** Tissue repair if contaminated; mesh if clean

🔵 **Indicators of bowel viability:**

| Viable | Non-viable |
|--------|-----------|
| Pink color returns | Persistent black/grey/green |
| Peristalsis present | No peristalsis |
| Mesenteric pulsation | No pulsation |
| Serosal sheen | Dull, lusterless serosa |
| Bleeding on incision | No bleeding |

🔵 **If viability is uncertain:** Options include:
- Second-look laparotomy at 24–48 hours
- IV fluorescein + Wood's lamp (fluorescence = viable)
- ICG (indocyanine green) fluorescence angiography (🔴 most accurate; increasingly available)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The Danish Hernia Database showed that emergency femoral hernia repair had a **7-fold higher mortality** compared to elective repair, and that **delay from symptom onset to surgery >24 hours** doubled the bowel resection rate. This underpins the recommendation for **mandatory elective repair of all diagnosed femoral hernias** — waiting is not an option.

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44*

## 9. Outcomes & Complications

🟢 **Recurrence rates:**

| Approach | Recurrence |
|----------|-----------|
| Open tissue repair | 5–15% |
| Open mesh (plug or preperitoneal) | 1–4% |
| Laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP) | 0.5–2% |

🔵 **Complications of femoral hernia repair:**

| Complication | Notes |
|-------------|-------|
| **Wound infection** | Higher in emergency; lower with laparoscopic |
| **Seroma/hematoma** | Common with preperitoneal dissection |
| **Chronic groin pain** | Less common than after inguinal hernia repair |
| **Femoral vein compression** | From mesh or sutures placed too laterally → leg edema, DVT |
| **Recurrence** | Higher with tissue repair; lowest with laparoscopic mesh |
| **Mesh infection** | Rare in elective; risk increases with bowel resection |
| **Corona mortis bleeding** | Can be torrential; direct pressure + suture ligation |

🔵 **Long-term outcomes (laparoscopic vs. open):** The Swedish Hernia Register data shows that laparoscopic repair has the lowest recurrence rate (0.7% at 5 years) for femoral hernias, with significantly lower rates of reoperation for chronic pain compared to open techniques.

## 10. Special Situations

### Femoral Hernia Found During Inguinal Hernia Repair

🔵 This is surprisingly common — occult femoral hernias are found in 5–10% of patients undergoing inguinal hernia repair, particularly with laparoscopic approaches.

- **Open anterior approach:** If discovered, can convert to McVay repair (Cooper's ligament repair) or place a plug in the femoral canal
- **Laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP):** The preperitoneal mesh already covers the femoral ring — no additional procedure needed. This is a **major advantage** of laparoscopic groin hernia repair

🟢 **Key teaching point:** Failure to identify a coexisting femoral hernia during inguinal hernia repair is a common cause of "recurrent" inguinal hernia — the patient actually has a **missed femoral hernia**, not a true recurrence.

### Femoral Hernia in Men

🔵 Though less common, femoral hernias in men carry the **same strangulation risk** and should be repaired urgently. Clinical misdiagnosis as inguinal hernia is even more common in men (because femoral hernia is not expected). Imaging is particularly valuable in this population.

### Femoral Hernia in Pregnancy

🔵 Rare. Management follows general principles for surgery in pregnancy:
- Elective repair postponed to second trimester or postpartum
- Emergency surgery at any gestational age if strangulated
- Open approach preferred in emergency; laparoscopic feasible in experienced hands

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Femoral hernias account for 2–4% of groin hernias** but 20–40% of strangulated groin hernias. The strangulation risk (22–45%) mandates repair at diagnosis.
2. **Anatomy:** The femoral canal is bounded by the inguinal ligament (anterior), Cooper's ligament (posterior), lacunar ligament (medial), and femoral vein (lateral). The rigid, unyielding ring causes strangulation.
3. **Clinical landmark:** Femoral hernia is **below and lateral to the pubic tubercle**; inguinal hernia is above and medial.
4. **F > M** (~4:1), but inguinal hernia is still commoner in women overall. Peak age: 40–70 years.
5. **All femoral hernias must be repaired** — watchful waiting is NOT acceptable (unlike asymptomatic inguinal hernias).
6. **Richter's hernia** (partial circumference of bowel wall) is particularly common with femoral hernias — causes strangulation without obstruction.
7. **Emergency repair mortality (5–13%)** is vastly higher than elective (<0.5%). Early diagnosis and repair saves lives.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **Laparoscopic repair (TEP/TAPP)** is the preferred elective approach — covers the entire myopectineal orifice, lowest recurrence (0.5–2%), identifies occult contralateral hernias.
9. **McEvedy (high) approach** is preferred for emergency repair — allows bowel assessment and resection.
10. **Corona mortis** (aberrant obturator artery over lacunar ligament) is present in 25–30%. Check before dividing the lacunar ligament.
11. **Post-herniorrhaphy femoral hernia** occurs in 1–4% after open inguinal tissue repairs. Preperitoneal mesh repairs prevent this.
12. **Missed femoral hernia** is a common cause of "recurrent" inguinal hernia — always examine the femoral ring during groin hernia surgery.
13. **Mesh is standard** for elective repair; tissue repair or biosynthetic mesh if contaminated field.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **Two-disease model in femoral hernias:** Richter's type and complete bowel loop incarceration have fundamentally different presentations and outcomes — Richter's presents late with peritonitis, not obstruction.
15. **Triangle of Doom, Triangle of Pain, and Corona Mortis** are the three danger zones in laparoscopic groin hernia surgery — know the boundaries.
16. **ICG fluorescence angiography** is the most accurate method for assessing bowel viability at emergency surgery.
17. **Danish Hernia Database:** >24-hour delay to emergency surgery doubles bowel resection rate; emergency mortality is 7× elective.
18. **EHS classification (L/M/F)** standardizes reporting — all surgeons should use it for audit and comparison.
