## Definition

An inguinal hernia is a protrusion of abdominal contents through a defect in the inguinal canal. It is the most common type of hernia, accounting for approximately 75% of all abdominal wall hernias. The lifetime risk is 27% for men and 3% for women.

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 60, p.1023*

---

## Surgical Anatomy

### The Inguinal Canal

The inguinal canal is an oblique passage approximately 4 cm long, extending from the deep (internal) inguinal ring to the superficial (external) inguinal ring. It runs parallel to and immediately above the medial half of the inguinal ligament.

#### Boundaries

| Boundary | Structure |
|----------|-----------|
| **Anterior wall** | External oblique aponeurosis (entire length); internal oblique (lateral 1/3) |
| **Posterior wall (floor)** | Transversalis fascia (entire length); conjoint tendon (medial 1/3) |
| **Roof** | Arching fibers of internal oblique and transversus abdominis |
| **Floor** | Inguinal ligament; lacunar ligament medially |

#### Deep (Internal) Inguinal Ring
- Located at the midpoint of the inguinal ligament
- Midway between ASIS and pubic symphysis
- An opening in the transversalis fascia
- Lies lateral to the inferior epigastric vessels

#### Superficial (External) Inguinal Ring
- Triangular opening in the external oblique aponeurosis
- Located above and medial to the pubic tubercle
- Bounded by medial and lateral crura

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 44, p.1093*

### Contents of the Inguinal Canal

**In males:** Spermatic cord containing:
- Vas deferens
- Testicular artery
- Cremasteric artery
- Artery to vas deferens
- Pampiniform plexus of veins
- Genital branch of genitofemoral nerve
- Sympathetic fibers
- Lymphatics
- Remnant of processus vaginalis

**In females:** Round ligament of uterus

**Both sexes:** Ilioinguinal nerve (runs outside the cord, within the canal)

### Hesselbach's Triangle

A critical anatomical landmark for understanding direct hernias:

| Border | Structure |
|--------|-----------|
| **Lateral** | Inferior epigastric vessels |
| **Medial** | Lateral border of rectus abdominis muscle |
| **Inferior** | Inguinal ligament (Poupart's ligament) |

Direct inguinal hernias protrude through this triangle, medial to the inferior epigastric vessels.

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 37, p.1498*

### Myopectineal Orifice of Fruchaud

Henri Fruchaud described a unified concept: all groin hernias (inguinal and femoral) occur through a single weak area—the myopectineal orifice. This is bounded by:
- Superiorly: Internal oblique and transversus abdominis
- Inferiorly: Pectineal line of pubis
- Medially: Rectus abdominis
- Laterally: Iliopsoas

This concept revolutionized laparoscopic hernia repair, as a single mesh can cover all potential hernia sites.

## Classification

### By Anatomy (Nyhus Classification)

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **I** | Indirect; normal deep ring |
| **II** | Indirect; dilated deep ring; posterior wall intact |
| **IIIa** | Direct hernia |
| **IIIb** | Indirect hernia with weak posterior wall; pantaloon hernia |
| **IIIc** | Femoral hernia |
| **IV** | Recurrent hernia (IVa=direct, IVb=indirect, IVc=femoral, IVd=combined) |

### By Relation to Inferior Epigastric Vessels

| Type | Position | Origin |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Indirect** | Lateral to vessels | Through deep ring; congenital |
| **Direct** | Medial to vessels | Through Hesselbach's triangle; acquired |

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 60, p.1028*

## Pathophysiology

### Indirect Inguinal Hernia

The processus vaginalis is an outpouching of peritoneum that precedes testicular descent through the inguinal canal during fetal development. Normally, it obliterates after birth. A patent processus vaginalis provides a preformed sac for indirect inguinal hernia.

**Predisposing factors:**
- Patent processus vaginalis (congenital)
- Increased intra-abdominal pressure (chronic cough, constipation, prostatism)
- Collagen disorders (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan)
- Family history

### Direct Inguinal Hernia

Results from acquired weakness in the transversalis fascia within Hesselbach's triangle. The posterior wall of the inguinal canal bulges forward.

**Predisposing factors:**
- Age-related collagen weakness
- Chronic raised intra-abdominal pressure
- Smoking (impairs collagen synthesis)
- Previous lower abdominal surgery

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 44, p.1096*

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Swelling in the groin, may extend to scrotum
- Aching discomfort, especially with prolonged standing or exertion
- Sensation of dragging or heaviness
- May be asymptomatic (incidental finding)

### Signs

#### Inspection
- Visible swelling in groin ± scrotum
- Cough impulse visible
- Position: Above and medial to pubic tubercle

#### Palpation
- Palpable cough impulse
- Reducible (usually) with gurgling sensation
- Cannot get above it (originates above inguinal ligament)
- Position of deep ring: Midpoint of inguinal ligament

#### Special Tests

**Deep Ring Occlusion Test:**
1. Reduce the hernia completely
2. Place finger firmly over the deep ring (midpoint of inguinal ligament)
3. Ask patient to cough or strain
4. If hernia is controlled → Indirect
5. If hernia still appears → Direct

**Invagination Test (Zieman's Technique):**
Invaginate scrotal skin with finger into superficial ring. Direct hernia hits fingertip; indirect hernia hits finger pulp.

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 60, p.1031*

## Complications

### Irreducibility
Hernia contents cannot be returned to the abdomen. Causes include:
- Adhesions between sac contents and wall
- Large size with small neck
- Sliding hernia

### Obstruction
When bowel within the hernia becomes obstructed. Features:
- Colicky abdominal pain
- Vomiting
- Abdominal distension
- Absolute constipation

### Strangulation
Blood supply to herniated contents is compromised. A surgical emergency.

**Features:**
- Severe pain (out of proportion)
- Tender, tense, irreducible swelling
- No cough impulse
- Signs of intestinal obstruction
- Eventually: Sepsis, gangrene, perforation

### Special Types

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Richter's hernia** | Only anti-mesenteric border of bowel in sac; can strangulate without obstruction |
| **Littre's hernia** | Meckel's diverticulum in sac |
| **Amyand's hernia** | Appendix in sac |
| **Sliding hernia** | Cecum or sigmoid forms part of sac wall |
| **Pantaloon hernia** | Direct + indirect components straddling epigastric vessels |
| **Maydl's hernia** | W-loop of bowel; middle segment strangulates in abdomen |

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 37, p.1510*

## Management

### Indications for Surgery
- All symptomatic inguinal hernias
- Asymptomatic: Controversial; "watchful waiting" acceptable for minimally symptomatic patients

### Contraindications (Relative)
- Severe cardiopulmonary disease
- Uncontrolled ascites
- Very large scrotal hernia (may need staged approach)

### Surgical Options

#### Open Repairs

**1. Lichtenstein Tension-Free Repair (Gold Standard)**
- Mesh placed over posterior wall
- Recurrence rate: <1%
- Can be done under local anesthesia
- Tension-free principle

**2. Shouldice Repair**
- Four-layer reconstruction of posterior wall
- No mesh
- Recurrence: 1-2% (expert hands)
- Requires expertise

**3. Bassini Repair**
- Historical importance
- Sutures conjoint tendon to inguinal ligament
- Higher recurrence (5-10%)

#### Laparoscopic Repairs

**1. TEP (Totally Extraperitoneal)**
- Preperitoneal space approached without entering peritoneal cavity
- Mesh placed in preperitoneal space
- Preferred for bilateral and recurrent hernias
- Learning curve is steep

**2. TAPP (Transabdominal Preperitoneal)**
- Peritoneal cavity entered first
- Peritoneum incised, mesh placed, peritoneum closed
- Better visualization
- Risk of visceral injury

#### Comparison: Open vs Laparoscopic

| Parameter | Open (Lichtenstein) | Laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP) |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Anesthesia | Local/Regional/GA | General required |
| Chronic pain | Higher (5-10%) | Lower (2-5%) |
| Recovery | 2-4 weeks | 1-2 weeks |
| Bilateral repair | Two incisions | Single setup |
| Recurrent hernia | More difficult | Better visualization |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Learning curve | Short | Long |

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 44, p.1110-1118*

## Postoperative Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Notes |
|--------------|-----------|-------|
| **Chronic inguinodynia** | 5-10% | Most common; nerve injury |
| **Seroma** | 5-8% | Usually resolves; aspirate if large |
| **Hematoma** | 2-5% | Meticulous hemostasis important |
| **Wound infection** | 1-2% | Higher in emergency surgery |
| **Recurrence** | <1-2% | Technique-dependent |
| **Testicular atrophy** | <0.5% | Vascular injury |
| **Mesh infection** | <1% | May require mesh removal |
| **Vas deferens injury** | <1% | Infertility risk (bilateral) |

## Key Points Summary

1. Most common hernia; 27% lifetime risk in males
2. Indirect = lateral to epigastrics, through deep ring, congenital
3. Direct = medial to epigastrics, through Hesselbach's, acquired
4. Deep ring = midpoint of inguinal ligament
5. Lichtenstein mesh repair = gold standard open repair
6. TEP/TAPP = preferred for bilateral and recurrent
7. Strangulation = surgical emergency
8. Chronic pain = most common complication of repair
