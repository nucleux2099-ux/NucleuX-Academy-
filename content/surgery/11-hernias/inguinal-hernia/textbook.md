## 1. History — From Trusses to Tension-Free

Inguinal hernia repair is among the oldest operations in surgery. Egyptian papyri from 1500 BCE describe groin swellings reduced by manual pressure. Celsus (25 BCE–50 CE) provided the first written account of hernia surgery — dividing the sac and excising the testicle with it. For nearly two millennia thereafter, the treatment remained crude: cautery, castration, and trusses.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1884 | **Edoardo Bassini** — Revolutionary anatomic repair: opened the inguinal canal, performed high ligation, reconstructed the posterior wall. Reduced mortality from ~7% to <1% |
| 1889 | **Halsted** — Modified Bassini; placed the cord subcutaneously |
| 1945 | **McVay (Cooper ligament repair)** — Sutured conjoint tendon to Cooper's ligament; also repairs femoral hernias |
| 1953 | **Shouldice** — Four-layer continuous suture repair of the posterior wall; best tissue repair ever devised (recurrence <1% at Shouldice Hospital) |
| 1989 | **Lichtenstein** — Tension-free mesh repair; transformed hernia surgery forever. Coined the concept that tension causes recurrence |
| 1990 | **Ger** — First laparoscopic hernia repair |
| 1992–95 | **TEP and TAPP** — Standardization of laparoscopic approaches |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.72; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.6*

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Bassini's operation was so transformative that it marks the true beginning of modern hernia surgery. Every subsequent repair — Shouldice, McVay, Lichtenstein, laparoscopic — is fundamentally a modification of Bassini's principle: reconstruct the posterior inguinal wall.

## 2. Anatomy — The Foundation of Hernia Surgery

**Anatomy is king in inguinal hernia surgery.** No other operation demands such precise understanding of layered anatomy. Surgeons who understand the inguinal region three-dimensionally operate with confidence; those who don't invite complications.

### The Inguinal Canal

🟢 The inguinal canal is an oblique passage approximately **4 cm long**, running superomedially from the deep (internal) ring to the superficial (external) ring. It transmits the spermatic cord in males and the round ligament in females.

🟢 **Walls of the inguinal canal:**

| Wall | Structure |
|------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | External oblique aponeurosis (entire length); internal oblique contributes laterally |
| **Posterior (floor)** | Transversalis fascia (entire length); conjoint tendon (falx inguinalis) reinforces medially |
| **Roof** | Arching fibers of internal oblique and transversus abdominis |
| **Inferior** | Inguinal (Poupart's) ligament — the rolled-under edge of the external oblique aponeurosis |

🟢 **The two rings:**
- **Deep (internal) ring:** An opening in the transversalis fascia, located at the midpoint of the inguinal ligament, **lateral** to the inferior epigastric vessels. This is where indirect hernias originate.
- **Superficial (external) ring:** A triangular opening in the external oblique aponeurosis, superomedial to the pubic tubercle.

### Hesselbach's Triangle

🟢 **This is the site of direct inguinal hernias.** Bounded by:
- **Medially:** Lateral border of the rectus abdominis
- **Laterally:** Inferior epigastric vessels
- **Inferiorly:** Inguinal ligament

The floor of Hesselbach's triangle is the transversalis fascia — the weakest layer of the abdominal wall. Direct hernias push through this fascial floor, medial to the inferior epigastric vessels.

### The Myopectineal Orifice of Fruchaud

🔵 **This concept unifies all groin hernias.** Henri Fruchaud (1956) described a single opening in the musculoaponeurotic layer of the abdominal wall through which ALL groin hernias — direct, indirect, and femoral — must pass.

**Boundaries:**
- **Superiorly:** Internal oblique and transversus abdominis arch
- **Inferiorly:** Cooper's ligament (pectineal ligament) and the superior pubic ramus
- **Medially:** Rectus abdominis and its sheath
- **Laterally:** Iliopsoas muscle

The inferior epigastric vessels divide this orifice into a **supravascular** compartment (inguinal hernias) and an **infravascular** compartment (femoral hernias). This concept is the anatomical basis for laparoscopic repair — a single mesh covering the entire myopectineal orifice addresses all potential hernia sites simultaneously.

### Spermatic Cord Contents

🟢 **The mnemonic "3-3-3":**
- **3 Fascial layers:** External spermatic fascia (from EO), cremasteric fascia and muscle (from IO), internal spermatic fascia (from transversalis fascia)
- **3 Arteries:** Testicular artery (aorta), artery to vas (inferior vesical), cremasteric artery (inferior epigastric)
- **3 Other structures:** Vas deferens, pampiniform plexus of veins, genital branch of genitofemoral nerve

🔵 **The ilioinguinal nerve** lies ON the cord (not within it), between the external and internal oblique layers. The iliohypogastric nerve runs parallel but more cephalad. Both are at risk during open repair.

### Nerves at Risk

🔵 **Three nerves of surgical significance:**

| Nerve | Origin | Course | Injury Consequence |
|-------|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Ilioinguinal** | L1 | Between IO and EO, exits superficial ring | Numbness: medial thigh, scrotum/labia |
| **Iliohypogastric** | T12–L1 | Between IO and EO, more cephalad | Numbness: suprapubic area |
| **Genital branch of genitofemoral** | L1–L2 | Enters canal through deep ring, within cord | Cremaster reflex lost; chronic pain |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh (L2–L3) passes under or through the inguinal ligament near the ASIS. It is NOT a cord structure but can be injured during laparoscopic repair when tacking mesh lateral to the deep ring — causing meralgia paresthetica. The "Triangle of Pain" (bounded by the vas deferens medially and spermatic vessels laterally) contains this nerve, the femoral nerve, and the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve. **Never place tacks or staples in this triangle.**

🔴 **The "Triangle of Doom"** (same boundaries but deeper plane) contains the external iliac vessels. Dissection here risks catastrophic hemorrhage.

## 3. Pathophysiology — Why Hernias Happen

### Embryological Basis

🟢 **The processus vaginalis** is a peritoneal diverticulum that descends through the inguinal canal during testicular descent (7th–9th month of gestation). Normally, it obliterates after birth.

```
Processus vaginalis (peritoneal outpouching)
    → Testis descends through inguinal canal
    → PV normally obliterates postnatally
    → Patent PV → potential for INDIRECT hernia
    → Complete patency → inguinal hernia extending to scrotum
    → Distal patency only → communicating hydrocele
    → Isolated distal patency → encysted hydrocele of cord
```

🟢 **Indirect inguinal hernia = congenital** in origin (patent processus vaginalis). This is why indirect hernias are more common in children and young adults, and why they enter the canal **through the deep ring, lateral to the inferior epigastric vessels.**

🟢 **Direct inguinal hernia = acquired** weakness of the transversalis fascia in Hesselbach's triangle, **medial to the inferior epigastric vessels.** These are hernias of aging, associated with collagen degradation.

### Risk Factors and Connective Tissue Biology

🟢 **Risk factors for inguinal hernia:**
- Male sex (lifetime risk: 27% male vs. 3% female)
- Age
- Family history (8× increased risk with first-degree relative)
- Chronic cough (COPD)
- Constipation/straining
- Prostatism
- Ascites
- Heavy lifting (occupational)
- Previous appendectomy (RIF incision → nerve damage → muscle atrophy)
- Low BMI (less preperitoneal fat)

🔵 **Collagen metabolism:** Direct hernias show altered collagen Type I to Type III ratio — increased Type III (thinner, less cross-linked) weakens the transversalis fascia. Similar connective tissue defects are seen in patients with recurrent hernias, AAA, and joint hypermobility, suggesting a systemic collagen disorder.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Smoking impairs collagen synthesis through metalloproteinase upregulation — smokers have higher hernia rates AND higher recurrence rates. Smoking cessation 4 weeks before elective repair reduces wound complications.

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

🟢 **The typical presentation:**
- Groin swelling that appears on standing, straining, or coughing
- Reduces on lying down (reducible hernia)
- Dull ache or dragging sensation, worse at end of day
- May descend into the scrotum (inguinoscrotal hernia) — especially indirect

🟢 **The clinical spectrum — escalating emergency:**

| Type | Definition | Urgency |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Reducible** | Contents return to abdomen spontaneously or with gentle pressure | Elective repair |
| **Irreducible (incarcerated)** | Contents cannot be returned; no obstruction or ischemia | Urgent — attempt reduction, plan early surgery |
| **Obstructed** | Incarcerated + intestinal obstruction (distension, vomiting, absolute constipation) | Emergency |
| **Strangulated** | Vascular compromise of hernia contents → ischemia/gangrene | Surgical emergency — mortality 5–10% if delayed |

🟢 **Red flags for strangulation:** Severe constant pain (no longer reducible), tender irreducible lump, overlying skin erythema/edema, signs of peritonitis, tachycardia, fever, leukocytosis.

### Physical Examination

🟢 **Examine the patient standing — always.** A supine exam misses many hernias.

🟢 **Invagination technique (males):**
1. Invaginate scrotal skin with index finger into the superficial inguinal ring
2. Ask patient to cough or perform Valsalva
3. **Indirect hernia:** Impulse felt at the **tip** of the finger (coming down the canal from the deep ring)
4. **Direct hernia:** Impulse felt against the **pad/pulp** of the finger (pushing forward through Hesselbach's triangle)

🟢 **Distinguishing inguinal from femoral hernia:**

| Feature | Inguinal | Femoral |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Position relative to pubic tubercle** | Above and medial | Below and lateral |
| **Cough impulse** | Usually present | Often absent |
| **Sex predilection** | M >> F | F > M (but still more inguinal overall in females) |
| **Reducibility** | Usually reducible | Often irreducible |
| **Strangulation risk** | Lower | **Very high** (narrow femoral ring) |

🔵 **Zieman's technique:** Three fingers placed over the deep ring, Hesselbach's triangle, and the femoral ring simultaneously while the patient coughs — localizes the impulse to one site.

🔵 **Silk glove sign (pediatric):** Thickened cord/processus vaginalis felt rolling under the examining finger — suggests patent processus vaginalis even without visible hernia.

## 5. Diagnosis

🟢 **Inguinal hernia is a clinical diagnosis.** In the vast majority of cases, history and physical examination suffice. Imaging is reserved for diagnostic uncertainty.

### When to Image

🔵 **Indications for imaging:**
- Clinically equivocal groin swelling
- Recurrent groin pain without palpable hernia (occult hernia)
- Athletic pubalgia / sports hernia (diagnosis of exclusion)
- Obese patients with difficult examination
- Differentiating inguinal from femoral hernia when clinical exam is inconclusive
- Evaluating complications (obstruction, strangulation)

🔵 **Imaging modalities:**

| Modality | Sensitivity | Best For |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Ultrasound** | 86–97% | First-line when imaging needed; dynamic exam with Valsalva; operator-dependent |
| **CT** | 90–95% | Complicated hernias, obstruction, alternative diagnoses; does not require Valsalva (static) |
| **MRI** | 94–97% | Occult hernias, athletic pubalgia, posterior wall defects; gold standard for equivocal cases |
| **Herniography** | Historical | Contrast injected intraperitoneally; largely abandoned |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Dynamic MRI with Valsalva maneuver has the highest diagnostic accuracy for occult hernias and can identify posterior wall insufficiency even before a clinically apparent hernia develops. It is particularly valuable in athletes with chronic groin pain where the differential includes adductor tendinopathy, osteitis pubis, and hip pathology.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.72*

## 6. Classification

🟢 **Direct vs. Indirect vs. Femoral — The Anatomical Basis:**

| Type | Relation to Inf. Epigastric Vessels | Route | Typical Patient |
|------|-------------------------------------|-------|-----------------|
| **Indirect** | Lateral | Through deep ring, down canal, may reach scrotum | Young males, children |
| **Direct** | Medial | Through Hesselbach's triangle (posterior wall) | Older males |
| **Femoral** | Below inguinal ligament | Through femoral ring (below & lateral to pubic tubercle) | Older females |

### Nyhus Classification

🟢 **The most widely used surgical classification:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **I** | Indirect hernia; normal deep ring (pediatric) |
| **II** | Indirect hernia; dilated deep ring, intact posterior wall |
| **IIIa** | Direct hernia (posterior wall deficiency) |
| **IIIb** | Indirect hernia with posterior wall deficiency (massive, sliding, or pantaloon hernia) |
| **IIIc** | Femoral hernia |
| **IV** | Recurrent hernia (IVa = direct; IVb = indirect; IVc = femoral; IVd = combined) |

🔵 **EHS (European Hernia Society) Classification:** Describes hernia by location (L = lateral/indirect, M = medial/direct, F = femoral) and size (1 = <1.5 cm, 2 = 1.5–3 cm, 3 = >3 cm). Simpler, increasingly adopted internationally, facilitates audit and comparison across centers.

🔵 **Gilbert Classification (7 types):** Subdivides indirect and direct by deep ring integrity and posterior wall strength. Less commonly used but important historically.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Pantaloon hernia:** A combined direct + indirect hernia straddling the inferior epigastric vessels — the "legs" of the pantaloon sit on either side of the vessels. Always look for this intraoperatively; missing the direct component during indirect repair guarantees early recurrence.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.6*

### A. Watchful Waiting

🔵 **The evidence for observation in minimally symptomatic hernias:**

The landmark Fitzgibbons RCT (2006) randomized 720 men with minimally symptomatic inguinal hernias to watchful waiting vs. Lichtenstein repair. Key findings:
- WW is **safe** — risk of acute incarceration requiring emergency surgery was only **1.8 per 1000 patient-years**
- However, **crossover rate was 72% at 7.5 years** due to increasing pain
- O'Dwyer et al. confirmed: pain progression is the rule, not the exception

🟢 **Current consensus:** WW is acceptable for minimally symptomatic inguinal hernias in men. **Femoral hernias should ALWAYS be repaired** due to high strangulation risk (22–45% present as emergencies). Symptomatic hernias should be offered repair.

### B. Open Repair

#### Lichtenstein Tension-Free Mesh Repair (Gold Standard)

🟢 **Principle:** A prosthetic mesh reinforces the posterior wall without the tension created by suture-based tissue approximation. Tension was identified as the primary cause of recurrence in tissue repairs.

🟢 **Technique essentials:**
1. Inguinal incision 2 cm above and parallel to inguinal ligament
2. Open external oblique aponeurosis — identify and preserve ilioinguinal nerve
3. Mobilize spermatic cord; identify and reduce indirect sac (or transect and ligate large sacs)
4. Place polypropylene mesh over the posterior wall — overlap medially onto pubic tubercle
5. Suture mesh inferiorly to inguinal ligament, superiorly to internal oblique/conjoint tendon
6. Create a slit laterally for the cord; suture tails to create a new snug deep ring
7. Close external oblique over the cord

🟢 **Results:** Recurrence rate <1–2%. Can be done under local anesthesia as day-case surgery.

🔵 **Mesh choice:** Lightweight, large-pore polypropylene mesh is preferred over heavyweight mesh — less foreign body reaction, less chronic pain, comparable recurrence. Self-gripping meshes (e.g., ProGrip) eliminate the need for fixation sutures.

#### Shouldice Repair

🔵 **The best non-mesh tissue repair.** A four-layer continuous suture reconstruction of the posterior wall using non-absorbable suture. At the Shouldice Hospital (Toronto), recurrence is <1% — but this is not reproducible in general surgical practice (recurrence 5–15% elsewhere). Reserved for:
- Young patients who decline mesh
- Contaminated fields where mesh is contraindicated
- Surgeon expertise in the technique

#### Bassini Repair

🟢 **Historical importance** — the first true anatomical repair. Sutures the "triple layer" (internal oblique, transversus abdominis, transversalis fascia) to the inguinal ligament. Recurrence 5–10%. Largely replaced by Lichtenstein but still taught and performed in resource-limited settings.

### C. Laparoscopic / Minimally Invasive Repair

🔵 **Two approaches:**

| Feature | TEP | TAPP |
|---------|-----|------|
| **Full name** | Totally Extraperitoneal | Transabdominal Preperitoneal |
| **Peritoneal entry** | No | Yes (peritoneum incised, then closed) |
| **Space** | Preperitoneal space | Peritoneal cavity → preperitoneal |
| **Learning curve** | Steeper | Easier |
| **Adhesion risk** | Lower | Higher (peritoneal breach) |
| **Bilateral repair** | Easy (same space) | Easy |
| **Mesh coverage** | Entire myopectineal orifice | Entire myopectineal orifice |
| **Anesthesia** | General | General |

🔵 **Advantages of laparoscopic over open:**
- Less postoperative pain
- Earlier return to work and activity (by ~7 days)
- Fewer wound infections
- Superior for **bilateral** and **recurrent** hernias (different anatomical plane from prior open repair)
- Identifies and repairs concurrent contralateral occult hernias

🔵 **Disadvantages:**
- Requires general anesthesia
- Longer operative time (especially early in learning curve)
- Higher cost (equipment, mesh)
- Risk of serious vascular injury (external iliac vessels)
- Not ideal for large inguinoscrotal hernias or incarcerated hernias (relative contraindication)

🟢 **Landmark evidence — EU Hernia Trialists Collaboration (2002):** Meta-analysis of 41 RCTs (>7000 patients): Laparoscopic repair had fewer wound infections, less chronic pain, faster recovery — but longer operative time, higher serious complication rate, and a learning curve of ~30–50 cases.

🔵 **LEVEL Trial (Eker et al., 2012):** TEP vs. Lichtenstein for primary unilateral hernia — no difference in recurrence at 5 years; TEP had less chronic pain.

### D. Robotic Hernia Repair

🔴 **An emerging approach.** Robot-assisted TAPP (rTAPP) offers 3D visualization and wristed instrumentation. Early data suggest:
- Comparable outcomes to laparoscopic TEP/TAPP
- Easier learning curve than laparoscopic TEP
- Significantly higher cost (robotic platform, instruments)
- Longer operative time initially
- No proven superiority in outcomes — awaiting large RCTs

Currently adopted in high-volume centers, particularly in the United States. The role remains undefined pending cost-effectiveness data.

### E. Emergency Hernia Repair

🟢 **Strangulated hernia = surgical emergency.** Delay increases mortality.

🔵 **Operative principles:**
1. **Resuscitate** — IV fluids, nasogastric decompression if obstructed, antibiotics
2. **Do NOT attempt forceful reduction** at the external ring — risk of *reduction en masse* (entire sac with strangulated contents returned to abdomen)
3. **Open the sac** — assess bowel viability
4. **Viability criteria:** Color (pink), peristalsis, mesenteric pulsation; warm saline wraps for 10–15 minutes if borderline; Doppler/fluorescein if available
5. **Resect if gangrenous** — primary anastomosis or stoma depending on contamination/patient fitness
6. **Mesh use in emergency:**
   - Clean field (viable bowel, no perforation) → mesh repair acceptable
   - Contaminated field (bowel resection, perforation) → tissue repair preferred; biological mesh as alternative
   - 🔴 Recent evidence (BioLIFE trial) suggests biologic mesh may be used in contaminated fields with acceptable outcomes, but data are limited

## 8. Complications

### Early Complications

🟢 **Common early complications:**
- **Seroma/hematoma:** Most common; usually self-limiting; aspiration if symptomatic
- **Wound infection:** 1–2% (open); <1% (laparoscopic); increased with emergency repair
- **Urinary retention:** Especially after bilateral repair or spinal anesthesia
- **Ischemic orchitis:** From damage to testicular artery or pampiniform plexus venous congestion; presents 2–5 days post-op with painful swollen testis; may lead to testicular atrophy (0.5% open, <0.1% laparoscopic)

### Late Complications

🔵 **Recurrence:**
- Lichtenstein: <1–2%
- Laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP): 1–3%
- Shouldice (expert): <1%
- Tissue repairs (general): 5–15%
- Most recurrences present within 2 years; causes include missed hernia type, inadequate mesh overlap, technical error, infection

🔵 **Chronic post-herniorrhaphy pain (Inguinodynia):**
- **Defined as:** Pain persisting >3 months after surgery, affecting daily activities
- **Incidence:** 10–12% after open repair; 5–8% after laparoscopic; **severe** chronic pain in 1–3%
- **Causes:** Nerve entrapment (ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric, genital branch of GFN), mesh-related inflammation (meshoma), mesh fixation into periosteum
- **Risk factors:** Young age, female sex, preoperative pain, prior groin pain, open repair, heavyweight mesh

🔴 **Management of inguinodynia:**
1. **Conservative first:** NSAIDs, gabapentinoids, local nerve blocks (diagnostic AND therapeutic)
2. **Selective neurectomy:** If a specific nerve is identified — ilioinguinal neurectomy most common
3. **Triple neurectomy:** Simultaneous division of ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric, and genital branch — provides relief in 80% of refractory cases
4. **Mesh removal (meshectomy):** Considered when pain is attributed to mesh — technically demanding, risk of recurrence, performed only at specialized centers
5. **Laparoscopic retroperitoneal neurectomy** — for genital branch of GFN specifically

🔵 **Mesh complications:** Migration (rare with modern fixation), erosion into bladder/vas deferens (very rare), chronic foreign body reaction, mesh infection (late — may require explantation).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.73*

## 9. Special Types

### Femoral Hernia

🟢 **Anatomy:** The femoral ring is the most superior part of the femoral canal, bounded by:
- **Anteriorly:** Inguinal ligament
- **Posteriorly:** Cooper's ligament (pectineal ligament)
- **Medially:** Lacunar (Gimbernat's) ligament — the sharp edge that causes strangulation
- **Laterally:** Femoral vein

🟢 **Clinical significance:**
- Accounts for ~5% of groin hernias but **40% of groin hernia emergencies**
- Strangulation rate: **22–45%** (vs. 2–4% for inguinal)
- More common in females (but even in females, inguinal hernias are still more common than femoral)
- **All femoral hernias should be repaired at diagnosis** — watchful waiting is contraindicated

🔵 **Surgical approaches:**

| Approach | Route | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|----------|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **McEvedy** | High vertical/oblique, extraperitoneal | Excellent access; can assess bowel viability and resect if needed | Larger incision |
| **Lockwood (low/crural)** | Infrainguinal, below inguinal ligament | Simple, direct | Limited access; cannot resect bowel |
| **Lotheissen** | Through inguinal canal, transinguinal | Can address concurrent inguinal hernia | More complex dissection |
| **Laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP)** | Preperitoneal | Mesh covers femoral + inguinal sites | Requires GA; expertise |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** McEvedy's approach is preferred for emergency femoral hernia repair because it provides adequate access for bowel resection and anastomosis if strangulation has occurred — the crural (Lockwood) approach does NOT.

### Pediatric Inguinal Hernia

🟢 **Key differences from adult hernias:**
- **Almost exclusively indirect** (patent processus vaginalis)
- Male:female ratio = 6:1 to 10:1
- Right-sided predominance (60%) — the right processus vaginalis closes later
- **Incarceration risk highest in first year of life** (30% in infants <6 months)

🟢 **Treatment — Herniotomy (high ligation of sac):**
- No posterior wall repair or mesh needed — the posterior wall is intact
- The sac (patent PV) is dissected from cord structures at the deep ring and high-ligated
- Laparoscopic repair increasingly used — allows inspection of contralateral side

🔵 **Timing:** Repair as soon as possible after diagnosis in infants (high incarceration risk). Term infants: within 2 weeks. Premature infants: before discharge from NICU or at 50–60 weeks post-conceptional age (to reduce anesthetic risk).

🔵 **Contralateral exploration:** Historically routine; now guided by laparoscopic inspection of the contralateral internal ring (patent PV found in 20–30% of cases).

### Sports Hernia / Athletic Pubalgia

🔵 **Not a true hernia.** A syndrome of chronic exertional groin pain caused by musculotendinous injury or weakness at the pubic attachments — typically involving the rectus abdominis insertion, conjoined tendon, or external oblique aponeurosis.

- No palpable hernia; exam may show tenderness at the pubic tubercle
- Diagnosis: clinical + MRI (shows posterior wall bulging, pubic bone marrow edema)
- Treatment: physiotherapy first; surgical repair (Muschaweck technique — minimal repair of the posterior wall) if conservative measures fail

### Other Rare Types

🔴 **Amyand's hernia:** Appendix within an inguinal hernia sac. If inflamed → appendectomy + tissue repair (no mesh in infected field). If normal → reduce, repair with mesh.

🔴 **Richter's hernia:** Only the antimesenteric border of bowel wall herniates — can strangulate WITHOUT causing obstruction. Classic trap: patient has no obstructive symptoms but develops gangrenous bowel. More common with femoral hernias.

🔴 **Littre's hernia:** A Meckel's diverticulum within the hernia sac.

🔴 **Sliding hernia:** Part of the hernia sac wall is formed by a viscus (sigmoid on the left, cecum on the right). The viscus is NOT within the sac — it IS the sac. Risk of visceral injury during dissection.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.72–73; Fischer 7th Ed, Ch.29–30*

## 10. Other Important Topics

### Mesh vs. No Mesh

🔵 **The central debate of modern hernia surgery:**
- Mesh reduces recurrence by 50–75% compared to tissue repair (EU Hernia Trialists)
- However, mesh is associated with chronic pain (foreign body, nerve entrapment)
- Lightweight, large-pore meshes reduce foreign body reaction
- **Biological meshes** (porcine/bovine dermis, SIS) are reserved for contaminated fields — higher recurrence than synthetic but lower infection risk

🔵 **Danish Hernia Database (>100,000 repairs):** Confirmed that Lichtenstein is the most common repair worldwide, with recurrence <2% at 10 years. Also showed that laparoscopic repair has equivalent recurrence but lower chronic pain rates.

### The Mesh Controversy

🔴 **Medico-legal implications:** Mesh litigation has exploded globally, primarily for vaginal mesh but with spillover to hernia mesh. Despite this, **hernia mesh remains evidence-based and recommended by all major guidelines.** The key is informed consent, appropriate mesh selection, and proper surgical technique.

### Bilateral Inguinal Hernia

🔵 Laparoscopic repair (TEP or TAPP) is preferred for bilateral hernias — both sides repaired through the same ports, same anesthesia, without additional incisions. Open bilateral repair requires two separate groin incisions.

### Recurrent Hernia

🔵 **Rule of alternate planes:**
- If primary repair was open (anterior approach) → laparoscopic (posterior approach) for recurrence
- If primary repair was laparoscopic → open repair for recurrence
- This avoids operating through scar tissue from the previous repair

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.44; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.6–7*

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Inguinal hernia is the most common hernia.** Lifetime risk: 27% males, 3% females. Indirect > direct.
2. **Indirect hernias** emerge lateral to the inferior epigastric vessels through the deep ring; **direct hernias** push through Hesselbach's triangle medial to these vessels.
3. **Hesselbach's triangle:** Rectus (medial), inferior epigastric vessels (lateral), inguinal ligament (inferior).
4. **Patent processus vaginalis** is the embryological basis of indirect inguinal hernia — the most common hernia in children and young adults.
5. **Clinical diagnosis:** Standing exam, cough impulse, invagination technique. Imaging rarely needed.
6. **The clinical spectrum:** Reducible → irreducible → obstructed → strangulated. Strangulation = surgical emergency.
7. **Lichtenstein tension-free mesh repair** is the gold standard for open inguinal hernia repair. Recurrence <1–2%.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **Fruchaud's myopectineal orifice** unifies all groin hernias — one mesh covering this orifice is the basis of laparoscopic repair.
9. **TEP vs. TAPP:** Both are valid laparoscopic approaches; TEP avoids peritoneal entry; TAPP has easier learning curve. Both are superior for bilateral and recurrent hernias.
10. **Watchful waiting** is safe for minimally symptomatic inguinal hernias — but 72% cross over to surgery by 7.5 years (Fitzgibbons trial).
11. **Femoral hernias** have the highest strangulation risk (22–45%) — always repair at diagnosis. McEvedy's approach preferred for emergencies.
12. **Chronic inguinodynia** affects 10–12% after open repair; triple neurectomy provides relief in 80% of refractory cases.
13. **Pediatric hernias** are indirect (patent PV); herniotomy (high ligation) is sufficient — no mesh, no posterior wall repair needed.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **Triangle of Pain** (vas + spermatic vessels) and **Triangle of Doom** (same boundaries, deeper) — never place tacks here during laparoscopic repair; risk of nerve injury and vascular catastrophe.
15. **Richter's hernia** strangulates WITHOUT causing obstruction — only the antimesenteric bowel wall is trapped. A classic diagnostic trap, especially with femoral hernias.
16. **Sliding hernia:** The viscus forms the sac wall (sigmoid left, cecum right) — recognize it to avoid visceral injury during sac dissection.
17. **Pantaloon hernia:** Combined direct + indirect straddling the inferior epigastric vessels — missing either component guarantees recurrence.
18. **Danish Hernia Database** (>100,000 repairs): Lichtenstein recurrence <2% at 10 years; laparoscopic repair has equivalent recurrence with less chronic pain. The largest hernia outcomes registry in the world.
