## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Lower GI Bleeding:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Colonic blood supply | — | Identifying the bleeding source requires knowledge of IMA territory and hemorrhoidal arterial supply |
| Anatomy | Hemorrhoidal plexus | — | Hemorrhoids are the commonest cause of LGIB — understanding the superior/middle/inferior rectal artery network is key |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Differentiate causes of LGIB by age group (pediatric: Meckel's; adult: diverticulosis, hemorrhoids, CRC)
- Apply a systematic diagnostic algorithm — colonoscopy, CT angiography, tagged RBC scan
- Identify indications for emergency surgery in massive LGIB

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Connection |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| Radiology | CT angiography, colonoscopy, Tc-99m RBC scan | — | CT angiography detects active bleeding >0.3 mL/min; nuclear scan for intermittent bleeding |

## ➡️ What's Next

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **Hemorrhoids** — Most common cause of LGIB — essential to master management
2. **Diverticular Disease** — Second most common cause in the elderly
3. **Colorectal Cancer** — Must be excluded in any adult with new-onset rectal bleeding

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Lower GI bleeding integrates anatomy (colonic blood supply, anorectal vasculature), radiology (CT angiography, nuclear medicine), and pathology (diverticulosis, angiodysplasia, malignancy). The clinical approach is algorithmic — resuscitate first, then localize, then treat.
