# Lower GI Bleeding

> **Source**: Sleisenger Ch 20, 121, 118
> **Topic System**: [[Colon Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts

> [!summary] One-liner
> LGIB is distal to ligament of Treitz, 80-85% self-limited, most commonly from diverticulosis (30-40%); remember 10-15% of hematochezia is actually from UGIB source, so rule out upper source if unstable.

---

## Definition & Epidemiology

**Lower GI Bleeding (LGIB)** = Bleeding distal to the ligament of Treitz

- **Hospitalization rate:** ~40% of GI bleeding admissions
- **Self-limited:** 80-85%
- **Mortality:** 2-4% (lower than UGIB)
- **Recurrence:** 10-40%

---

## Causes by Age

| Age Group | Common Causes |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Infants/Children** | Meckel's diverticulum, intussusception |
| **Adults <50** | Hemorrhoids, IBD, infectious colitis |
| **Adults 50-80** | Diverticulosis, angiodysplasia, malignancy |
| **Elderly >80** | Angiodysplasia, diverticulosis, ischemic colitis |

### Cause Distribution (Adults)

| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| Diverticulosis | 30-40% |
| Hemorrhoids | 15-20% |
| Angiodysplasia | 10-15% |
| Colorectal neoplasia | 10-15% |
| Colitis (IBD, ischemic, infectious) | 10-15% |

## Clinical Presentation

| Term | Appearance | Source |
|------|------------|--------|
| **BRBPR** | Fresh red blood | Distal colon/anorectal |
| **Maroon stool** | Darker red-brown | Proximal colon, small bowel |
| **Melena** | Black, tarry | Usually UGIB, but can be right colon |

> ⚠️ **Critical:** 10-15% of severe hematochezia is from an UPPER GI source!

## Specific Causes

### Diverticular Bleeding
- **Most common cause** in adults
- Usually **RIGHT-sided** (despite left-sided diverticula)
- Painless, massive bleeding
- 80% stop spontaneously
- 25% rebleed

### Angiodysplasia
- Degenerative vascular malformation
- **Right colon** predominant
- Associated with aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome)
- Chronic, intermittent bleeding
- May need multiple treatments

### Ischemic Colitis
- **"Watershed" areas:** Splenic flexure, rectosigmoid
- Triad: Pain → Urgency → Bloody diarrhea
- CT: Thumbprinting, wall thickening
- Most self-limited; surgery if gangrenous

## Diagnostic Modalities

| Test | Indication | Details |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Colonoscopy** | First-line | Within 24h, after prep |
| **CT Angiography** | Active bleeding, failed colonoscopy | Detects ≥0.5 mL/min |
| **Tagged RBC scan** | Intermittent bleeding | Detects ≥0.1 mL/min |
| **Angiography** | Active bleeding | Therapeutic + diagnostic |

---

## Transfusion Thresholds

| Threshold | Population |
|-----------|------------|
| Hgb <7 g/dL | Most patients (restrictive) |
| Hgb <8-9 g/dL | Active ischemia, significant comorbidities |

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

> 💎 **Pearl 1:** 10-15% of hematochezia is from UGIB – always consider upper source if unstable

> 💎 **Pearl 2:** Diverticular bleeding is usually RIGHT-sided (despite left-sided diverticula)

> 💎 **Pearl 3:** Angiodysplasia + aortic stenosis = Heyde syndrome

> 💎 **Pearl 4:** Ischemic colitis: splenic flexure and rectosigmoid (watershed areas)

> 💎 **Pearl 5:** Colonoscopy within 24h is first-line for stable patients

> 💎 **Pearl 6:** Tagged RBC scan is most sensitive (0.1 mL/min) but less specific for localization
