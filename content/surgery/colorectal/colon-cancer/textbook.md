## 1. History — From Incurable Affliction to Standardized Oncological Surgery

Colon cancer has been recognized since antiquity — descriptions of rectal tumors appear in Egyptian papyri dating to 1500 BCE. However, surgical treatment was inconceivable until the antiseptic era. The first successful colectomy for cancer is attributed to **Reybard** (1833), who performed a sigmoid resection with primary anastomosis. **Billroth** followed with resections in the 1880s, but mortality rates exceeded 50%.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1833 | **Reybard** — First successful sigmoid colectomy for cancer |
| 1884 | **Billroth** — Systematic colonic resection |
| 1908 | **Miles** — Abdominoperineal resection (APR) |
| 1926 | **Lockhart-Mummery** — Proposed adenoma-carcinoma sequence |
| 1932 | **Dukes** — Staging classification for colorectal cancer |
| 1988 | **Vogelstein** — Molecular adenoma-carcinoma sequence described |
| 1991 | **Jacobs** — First laparoscopic colectomy |
| 2004 | **COST, COLOR trials** — Laparoscopic colectomy validated oncologically |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154–156*

The transformation from Dukes' staging to TNM, from open to laparoscopic and now robotic surgery, and from empiric chemotherapy to molecular-targeted therapy, represents one of the most complete evolutions in surgical oncology.

## 2. Epidemiology

🟢 **The numbers:**
- **Third most common cancer worldwide** (after lung and breast)
- **Second leading cause of cancer death** in the Western world
- Incidence: ~1.9 million new cases/year globally (GLOBOCAN 2020)
- Lifetime risk: ~4.5% (1 in 23) in developed countries
- **Peak age:** 65–75 years; rising incidence in adults <50 ("young-onset CRC")
- Male:Female ratio ~1.3:1

🟢 **Risk factors:**

| Modifiable | Non-Modifiable |
|-----------|---------------|
| Red/processed meat | Age >50 |
| Obesity (BMI >30) | Male sex |
| Smoking | Family history (first-degree relative = 2–3× risk) |
| Alcohol (>2 drinks/day) | Hereditary syndromes (FAP, Lynch) |
| Sedentary lifestyle | IBD (UC > Crohn's) |
| Low fiber diet | Prior abdominal radiation |

🔵 **Protective factors:** Aspirin/NSAIDs (COX-2 pathway), physical activity, dietary fiber, calcium, vitamin D, statins (emerging data).

🔴 **SS Pearl — Young-Onset CRC:** Incidence in adults <50 has increased ~2% per year since the mid-1990s. These tumors are more often left-sided, diagnosed at advanced stages, and have distinct molecular profiles (more microsatellite stable, more KRAS mutations). The ACS now recommends screening from age **45** (previously 50).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154*

## 3. Anatomy — Surgical Relevance

🟢 **Segmental anatomy of the colon:**

| Segment | Blood Supply | Venous Drainage | Lymphatic Pathway |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Cecum & Ascending | Ileocolic, Right colic | SMV | Along SMA |
| Hepatic flexure | Right colic, Middle colic | SMV | Along SMA |
| Transverse | Middle colic | SMV | Along SMA |
| Splenic flexure | Left branch middle colic + Left colic | IMV | Watershed zone |
| Descending | Left colic | IMV | Along IMA |
| Sigmoid | Sigmoid arteries (from IMA) | IMV → Splenic vein | Along IMA |

🟢 **Critical anatomical concepts for surgery:**
- **Marginal artery of Drummond** — Continuous arterial arcade along the mesenteric border; critical for anastomotic blood supply
- **Arc of Riolan (meandering mesenteric artery)** — Connects SMA and IMA territories; vital collateral
- **Griffiths' point** — Splenic flexure watershed zone; vulnerable to ischemia
- **Sudeck's critical point** — Junction of sigmoid and superior rectal arteries; must be preserved in low anterior resections

🔵 **Fascial planes for complete mesocolic excision (CME):**
- **Toldt's fascia** — Avascular embryological plane between mesocolon and retroperitoneum
- Medial-to-lateral dissection along this plane achieves intact mesocolic envelope
- Central vascular ligation (CVL) at the origin of feeding vessels maximizes lymph node yield

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Hohenberger's concept of **CME with CVL** (complete mesocolic excision with central vascular ligation) mirrors TME for rectal cancer. Studies from Erlangen showed 5-year survival improvement from 82% to 89% with CME. The mesocolon has been reclassified as a distinct organ (Coffey, *Lancet Gastro Hepatol*, 2016).

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52*

## 4. Pathogenesis — Molecular Pathways

### The Adenoma-Carcinoma Sequence

🟢 **Vogelstein's model (1988)** — The paradigm for most (~70%) colorectal cancers:

```
Normal epithelium
    → APC mutation (5q21) — "Gatekeeper"
    → Early adenoma (dysplastic crypts)
    → KRAS mutation (12p12)
    → Intermediate adenoma
    → SMAD4/DCC loss (18q21)
    → Late adenoma (high-grade dysplasia)
    → TP53 loss (17p13)
    → Carcinoma
    → Additional mutations → Metastasis
```

🟢 **Timeline:** Normal mucosa → cancer typically takes **10–15 years** — the basis for screening intervals.

🟢 **Adenoma features predicting malignancy:**
- Size >1 cm (10× risk vs. <1 cm)
- Villous histology (vs. tubular)
- High-grade dysplasia
- Number of polyps (≥3 = higher risk)

### Alternative Pathways

🔵 **Serrated pathway (~20–30% of CRC):**
- Sessile serrated lesions (SSLs) → CpG island methylation (CIMP-high) → BRAF V600E mutation → MLH1 methylation → MSI-high → Cancer
- Right-sided predominance, flat morphology, harder to detect endoscopically
- May explain "interval cancers" (cancers arising between screening colonoscopies)

🔵 **Lynch pathway (3–5% of CRC):**
- Germline mismatch repair (MMR) gene mutations (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2)
- Microsatellite instability (MSI-high)
- Accelerated adenoma-carcinoma progression (2–3 years vs. 10–15 years)
- Better prognosis stage-for-stage; respond to immunotherapy

🔴 **SS Pearl — Consensus Molecular Subtypes (CMS):**

| CMS | Frequency | Features | Prognosis |
|-----|-----------|----------|-----------|
| CMS1 (MSI Immune) | 14% | MSI-high, BRAF mutation, immune infiltration | Good (except relapse = poor) |
| CMS2 (Canonical) | 37% | Chromosomal instability, WNT/MYC activation | Best overall |
| CMS3 (Metabolic) | 13% | KRAS mutation, metabolic dysregulation | Intermediate |
| CMS4 (Mesenchymal) | 23% | TGF-β, stromal infiltration, EMT | Worst |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Robbins 10th Ed, Ch.17*

## 5. Hereditary Colorectal Cancer Syndromes

🟢 **The Big Two:**

### Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Gene | APC (5q21) — autosomal dominant |
| Polyps | >100 adenomatous polyps (often thousands) |
| CRC risk | ~100% by age 40 if untreated |
| Extracolonic | Desmoid tumors, duodenal adenomas, osteomas, CHRPE, thyroid cancer |
| Named variants | Gardner syndrome (polyps + osteomas + soft tissue tumors), Turcot syndrome (polyps + CNS tumors — medulloblastoma in FAP) |
| Screening | Annual flexible sigmoidoscopy from age 10–12 |
| Treatment | Prophylactic colectomy by late teens/early 20s |

🔵 **Surgical options for FAP:**
- **Total proctocolectomy + IPAA (ileal pouch-anal anastomosis)** — Gold standard
- **Total colectomy + IRA (ileorectal anastomosis)** — If few rectal polyps; requires lifelong rectal surveillance
- **Proctocolectomy + end ileostomy** — If IPAA not feasible

### Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Genes | MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM |
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant |
| CRC risk | 40–80% lifetime |
| Characteristics | Right-sided, MSI-high, mucinous/signet ring, better prognosis stage-for-stage |
| Extracolonic | Endometrial (60%), ovarian, gastric, urinary tract, small bowel, brain (Turcot — GBM) |
| Diagnosis | Amsterdam II criteria (clinical); Bethesda guidelines (pathological); IHC for MMR proteins + MSI testing → germline testing |

🟢 **Amsterdam II Criteria (3-2-1 rule):**
- ≥**3** relatives with Lynch-associated cancer
- Spanning ≥**2** generations
- ≥**1** diagnosed before age 50
- One must be a first-degree relative of the other two
- FAP excluded

🔵 **Universal testing:** Current NCCN guidelines recommend **all CRC specimens** be tested for MMR deficiency (IHC or MSI) — this is now standard of care.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Lynch patients who develop CRC should undergo **subtotal colectomy with IRA** rather than segmental resection — the risk of metachronous CRC is 16% at 10 years with segmental resection vs. <4% with subtotal colectomy.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Robbins 10th Ed, Ch.17; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154*

## 6. Clinical Presentation

🟢 **Presentation depends on tumor location:**

| Feature | Right-Sided (Cecum to Hepatic Flexure) | Left-Sided (Splenic Flexure to Sigmoid) |
|---------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Lumen | Wide lumen | Narrow lumen |
| Growth pattern | Exophytic/polypoid | Annular/constricting ("apple core") |
| Common symptoms | Occult bleeding, iron-deficiency anemia, vague RLQ mass | Change in bowel habit, hematochezia, obstructive symptoms |
| Obstruction | Uncommon (wide lumen, liquid stool) | More common |
| Classic presentation | "Unexplained iron-deficiency anemia in a male or postmenopausal female = colon cancer until proven otherwise" | Alternating constipation/diarrhea, pencil-thin stools |

🟢 **Red flag symptoms:**
- Unexplained iron-deficiency anemia (especially >50 years)
- Change in bowel habit >6 weeks
- Rectal bleeding mixed with stool
- Unexplained weight loss
- Abdominal mass
- Large bowel obstruction

🔵 **Emergency presentations (~20% of cases):**
- **Obstruction** (most common emergency presentation, especially left-sided)
- **Perforation** (localized or free; 2–10%)
- **Massive bleeding** (rare)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Right-sided tumors are more often diagnosed at advanced stages because symptoms are insidious. Molecular differences between right and left CRC have therapeutic implications — right-sided tumors respond less to anti-EGFR therapy (cetuximab/panitumumab) regardless of RAS status.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154–155*

## 7. Diagnosis & Staging

### Diagnostic Workup

🟢 **Complete colonoscopy** is the gold standard — allows biopsy and identification of synchronous lesions (3–5% have synchronous cancers).

🟢 **Standard workup:**
- Colonoscopy with biopsy
- CT chest/abdomen/pelvis (staging)
- CEA level (baseline; prognostic, not diagnostic)
- CBC, LFTs, renal function
- MMR/MSI testing on tumor specimen

🔵 **If colonoscopy incomplete** (e.g., obstructing tumor):
- CT colonography preoperatively
- Completion colonoscopy within 3–6 months postoperatively

### TNM Staging (AJCC 8th Edition)

🟢 **T Stage:**

| Stage | Depth of Invasion |
|-------|-------------------|
| Tis | Carcinoma in situ / intramucosal |
| T1 | Submucosa |
| T2 | Muscularis propria |
| T3 | Through muscularis into pericolorectal tissues |
| T4a | Penetrates visceral peritoneum |
| T4b | Directly invades other organs |

🟢 **Stage Grouping & Survival:**

| Stage | TNM | 5-Year Survival |
|-------|-----|-----------------|
| I | T1-2 N0 M0 | ~93% |
| IIA | T3 N0 M0 | ~85% |
| IIB | T4a N0 M0 | ~72% |
| IIC | T4b N0 M0 | ~60% |
| IIIA | T1-2 N1 M0 | ~83% |
| IIIB | T3-4a N1 M0 | ~64% |
| IIIC | T4b N1 or any T N2 M0 | ~44% |
| IVA | Any T, any N, M1a (one organ) | ~20% |
| IVB | Any T, any N, M1b (>1 organ) | ~10% |

🔵 **Minimum lymph node harvest:** ≥12 nodes required for adequate staging. Node yield <12 → understaging risk → consider adjuvant chemotherapy.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** **Tumor deposits** (TD) — discrete tumor foci in pericolorectal fat without identifiable lymph node tissue — are now classified as N1c in AJCC 8th edition. Their prognostic significance approaches that of lymph node metastases.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.155*

## 8. Screening

🟢 **Average-risk screening (start age 45, ACS/USPSTF):**

| Test | Interval | Sensitivity |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Colonoscopy** | Every 10 years | Gold standard |
| **FIT (fecal immunochemical test)** | Annual | 79% for CRC per round |
| **Cologuard (multi-target stool DNA)** | Every 3 years | 92% for CRC, lower specificity |
| **CT colonography** | Every 5 years | 90% for polyps >10mm |
| **Flexible sigmoidoscopy** | Every 5 years | Misses right-sided lesions |

🔵 **High-risk screening:**
- **Family history (1st degree, <60 years):** Colonoscopy at age 40 or 10 years before youngest affected relative, every 5 years
- **Lynch syndrome:** Colonoscopy every 1–2 years from age 20–25
- **FAP:** Flexible sigmoidoscopy annually from age 10–12; colectomy when polyps develop
- **IBD (≥8 years of pancolitis or ≥15 years left-sided):** Colonoscopy every 1–2 years with chromoendoscopy

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Liquid biopsy (ctDNA) screening tests like **Shield (Guardant)** are FDA-approved (2024) with 83% sensitivity for CRC but only 13% for advanced adenomas — excellent rule-in but insufficient as sole screening modality.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.154*

### A. Surgical Principles

🟢 **Oncological colectomy fundamentals:**
1. **Wide resection** of bowel with tumor (≥5 cm proximal and distal margins — though radial margin matters more)
2. **En bloc lymphadenectomy** along the feeding vessels
3. **Minimum 12 lymph nodes** harvested
4. **High ligation** of the primary feeding artery
5. **No-touch technique** (ligate vessels before mobilizing tumor)

🟢 **Operations by tumor location:**

| Tumor Site | Operation | Vessels Ligated |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Cecum / Ascending | Right hemicolectomy | Ileocolic, right colic |
| Hepatic flexure | Extended right hemicolectomy | + middle colic |
| Transverse | Extended right or transverse colectomy | Middle colic |
| Splenic flexure | Extended right or left hemicolectomy | Left branch middle colic + left colic |
| Descending | Left hemicolectomy | Left colic |
| Sigmoid | Sigmoid colectomy / High anterior resection | IMA (or sigmoid arteries) |

🔵 **Laparoscopic vs. Open:**
- COST trial (2004), COLOR trial (2005), ALCCaS (2010): Laparoscopic = equivalent oncological outcomes with faster recovery, less pain, shorter hospital stay
- Laparoscopic/robotic now standard for elective colon cancer surgery

🔵 **Complete Mesocolic Excision (CME):**
- Dissection in the embryological plane (Toldt's fascia)
- Central vascular ligation (CVL) at the origin of the vessels
- Intact mesocolic envelope (no tears or defects)
- Increases lymph node yield (average 30+ nodes)
- Erlangen data: 5-year CSS improved by 7% (Stage III)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The concept of "adequate surgery" in colon cancer is shifting. Pathological grading of mesocolic plane quality (mesocolic, intramesocolic, muscularis propria planes — analogous to Quirke's TME grading) correlates with survival. Future quality metrics will likely include mesocolic plane quality alongside node count.

### B. Special Surgical Scenarios

🟢 **Obstructing colon cancer:**
- **Right-sided:** Primary resection with ileocolic anastomosis (one-stage)
- **Left-sided options:**
  - Hartmann's procedure (resection + end colostomy) — safest in emergency/unstable patient
  - Primary resection + anastomosis ± defunctioning loop ileostomy
  - Endoscopic stenting as bridge to surgery (ESCO, CReST trials — concerns about oncological compromise; avoid in curative-intent if possible)
  - Subtotal colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis (if proximal colon dilated/ischemic)

🔵 **Perforation:** Hartmann's procedure preferred. Oncological principles should still be maintained when possible.

🔵 **Synchronous tumors (3–5%):** Subtotal colectomy if tumors in separate segments; segmental resections if technically feasible.

### C. Adjuvant Chemotherapy

🟢 **Stage I:** Surgery alone. No adjuvant chemotherapy.

🟢 **Stage III:** Adjuvant chemotherapy is **standard of care:**
- **FOLFOX** (5-FU/leucovorin/oxaliplatin) or **CAPOX** (capecitabine/oxaliplatin)
- Duration: **3 months for low-risk (T1-3 N1)** vs. 6 months for high-risk (T4 or N2) — IDEA collaboration
- Reduces recurrence by ~30%, improves OS by ~20%

🔵 **Stage II — high-risk features warranting chemotherapy:**
- T4 disease
- <12 lymph nodes examined
- Poorly differentiated histology (excluding MSI-high)
- Lymphovascular invasion (LVI)
- Perineural invasion (PNI)
- Tumor perforation or obstruction at presentation
- Positive margins

🔴 **SS Pearl — MSI-high in Stage II:** MSI-high Stage II tumors have an excellent prognosis and do **not** benefit from 5-FU monotherapy (may actually be harmed). These patients should NOT receive single-agent fluoropyrimidine. If chemo is indicated, use oxaliplatin-based regimens.

### D. Treatment of Metastatic Disease (Stage IV)

🔵 **Liver metastases (most common site):**
- **Resectable (20–30% at presentation):** Hepatic resection ± perioperative chemotherapy → 5-year survival 40–60%
- Criteria: All disease resectable with adequate margins, sufficient liver remnant (>20–30%), good functional status
- Conversion chemotherapy (FOLFOX/FOLFIRI ± targeted) can convert 15–30% of initially unresectable to resectable

🔵 **Lung metastases:** Pulmonary metastasectomy in selected patients → 5-year survival 30–50%

🔵 **Systemic therapy for unresectable Stage IV:**
- **Backbone:** FOLFOX, FOLFIRI, or FOLFOXIRI
- **Add targeted therapy based on molecular profile:**
  - RAS wild-type + left-sided → Anti-EGFR (cetuximab/panitumumab)
  - RAS mutant or right-sided → Anti-VEGF (bevacizumab)
  - MSI-high → Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab — KEYNOTE-177; now first-line)
  - BRAF V600E → Encorafenib + cetuximab (BEACON)
  - HER2-amplified → Trastuzumab + pertuzumab (MOUNTAINEER)

🔴 **SS Pearl — Immunotherapy revolution:** KEYNOTE-177 established pembrolizumab as first-line for MSI-high metastatic CRC (PFS nearly doubled). The NICHE trial showed neoadjuvant ipilimumab + nivolumab achieved pathological complete response in 67% of dMMR colon cancers — potentially practice-changing.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.155–156*

## 10. Surveillance After Curative Resection

🟢 **NCCN Guidelines:**

| Test | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| CEA | Every 3–6 months × 2 years, then every 6 months × 3 years |
| CT CAP | Annually × 5 years (Stage II high-risk and Stage III) |
| Colonoscopy | At 1 year; if normal, every 3 years; then every 5 years |

🔵 **CEA pearls:**
- Not useful for screening (low sensitivity/specificity in general population)
- Elevated preoperative CEA that normalizes postop → rising CEA = recurrence until proven otherwise
- Normal preoperative CEA does not exclude recurrence monitoring
- CEA >5 ng/mL postoperatively → investigate (CT, PET-CT)

🔴 **SS Pearl — ctDNA (circulating tumor DNA):** Post-surgical ctDNA detection (e.g., Signatera) identifies minimal residual disease with high specificity. ctDNA-positive patients have >10× recurrence risk. Trials (DYNAMIC, CIRCULATE) are evaluating ctDNA-guided adjuvant chemotherapy — may allow de-escalation in ctDNA-negative patients and intensification in ctDNA-positive patients.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.52*

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Third most common cancer worldwide.** Lifetime risk ~4.5%. Screening from age 45.
2. **Adenoma-carcinoma sequence** takes 10–15 years — the basis for screening colonoscopy.
3. **Right-sided = occult bleeding/anemia; Left-sided = obstruction/altered bowel habit.**
4. "Unexplained iron-deficiency anemia in male or postmenopausal female = colon cancer until proven otherwise."
5. **Colonoscopy** is the gold standard for diagnosis. Always look for synchronous lesions.
6. **Minimum 12 lymph nodes** must be harvested for adequate staging.
7. **Stage III = adjuvant chemotherapy** (FOLFOX or CAPOX).
8. **FAP = 100% cancer risk** → prophylactic colectomy. **Lynch = 40–80% risk** → subtotal colectomy if cancer develops.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. CME with CVL improves oncological outcomes — analogous to TME for rectal cancer.
10. IDEA collaboration: 3 months CAPOX sufficient for low-risk Stage III (T1-3 N1).
11. MSI-high Stage II tumors have excellent prognosis; do NOT give 5-FU monotherapy.
12. Universal MMR/MSI testing is now standard of care for all CRC specimens.
13. Left vs. right sidedness predicts anti-EGFR response regardless of RAS status.
14. Obstructing left-sided cancers: stenting as bridge to surgery has oncological concerns.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

15. Consensus Molecular Subtypes (CMS1–4) represent the future of CRC classification and treatment selection.
16. ctDNA (Signatera) for post-surgical MRD detection may revolutionize adjuvant therapy decisions.
17. NICHE trial: neoadjuvant immunotherapy achieves pCR in 67% of dMMR colon cancers.
18. Tumor deposits (N1c) have prognostic significance approaching lymph node metastases.
19. Mesocolic plane quality grading is an emerging surgical quality metric.
20. Young-onset CRC (<50) has distinct molecular biology and worse stage-for-stage outcomes.
