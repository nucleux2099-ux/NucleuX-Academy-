# Biliary Dyskinesia: Diagnosis & Management

> **Source**: Blumgart Ch 32, Rome IV Criteria
> **Topic System**: [[Biliary Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Biliary dyskinesia is a functional gallbladder disorder characterized by biliary-type pain in the absence of gallstones, diagnosed by a reduced gallbladder ejection fraction (<35%) on CCK-HIDA scan.

### Key Points

1. **Definition**: Biliary colic symptoms WITHOUT gallstones or sludge
2. **Pathophysiology**: Impaired gallbladder motility and/or cystic duct spasm
3. **Diagnosis**: CCK-HIDA scan with ejection fraction (EF) <35% (or <40% by some criteria)
4. **Treatment**: Cholecystectomy for EF <35% with typical symptoms
5. **Response rate**: 70-90% symptom relief after cholecystectomy (lower than for gallstones)

### Clinical Relevance

- **When to suspect**: Classic biliary colic symptoms with negative US for stones
- **Caveat**: Controversial diagnosis; ensure other causes excluded
- **Decision point**: Surgery based on low EF + reproduction of symptoms during CCK infusion

## Detailed Notes

### Rome IV Criteria

**All of the following must be present:**
1. Biliary pain (episodic, epigastric/RUQ, lasting 30+ minutes)
2. Normal liver enzymes (bilirubin, ALP, AST, ALT)
3. Absence of bile duct dilation
4. Absence of structural abnormality (stones, sludge)

**Supportive criteria:**
- Low gallbladder ejection fraction (<40%)
- Normal EGD (rule out peptic disease)

### CCK-HIDA Scan (Cholescintigraphy)

**Procedure:**
1. Inject Tc-99m HIDA (taken up by hepatocytes, excreted in bile)
2. Wait for GB visualization (usually 30-60 min)
3. Infuse CCK (cholecystokinin) over 30-60 minutes
4. Calculate ejection fraction (EF)

**Interpretation:**
| EF | Interpretation |
|-----|----------------|
| >35% | Normal |
| <35% | Abnormal (suggests dyskinesia) |
| <15% | Severely reduced |

**Critical point**: Symptom reproduction during CCK infusion is important for diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | How to Distinguish |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Gallstones** | US positive |
| **Peptic ulcer** | EGD, H. pylori testing |
| **GERD** | pH study, PPI trial |
| **Functional dyspepsia** | Rome IV criteria, no low EF |
| **Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction** | Dilated CBD, elevated LFTs |
| **IBS** | Rome IV criteria, no biliary pain pattern |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Positional, tender chest wall |

### Management

#### Surgical Management

**Indications for cholecystectomy:**
- Typical biliary symptoms
- EF <35% on CCK-HIDA
- Symptom reproduction during CCK infusion
- Other causes excluded

**Outcomes:**
| Study Type | Symptom Improvement |
|------------|-------------------|
| Overall | 70-90% |
| With symptom reproduction during HIDA | ~90% |
| Without symptom reproduction | 50-70% |

**Histopathology**: Often shows chronic cholecystitis (supports diagnosis retrospectively)

#### Non-Surgical Options (If not surgical candidate)

- PPIs (if GERD component)
- Antispasmodics
- Dietary modification (low-fat)
- Neuromodulators (for functional component)

### Controversies

1. **Diagnostic threshold**: 35% vs 40% EF cutoff varies
2. **Symptom reproduction**: Some don't require it; others consider essential
3. **Overdiagnosis concern**: Fear of unnecessary cholecystectomies
4. **Placebo effect**: Some symptom relief may be placebo

**Best practice**: Strict patient selection, realistic expectations, exclude other diagnoses thoroughly

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What is the diagnostic test for biliary dyskinesia and what's the cutoff?
   A: CCK-HIDA scan; ejection fraction <35% (or <40%) is abnormal

2. Q: What is the expected symptom improvement rate after cholecystectomy for biliary dyskinesia?
   A: 70-90%, which is lower than for gallstone disease (>90%)

3. Q: What makes a CCK-HIDA more predictive of surgical success?
   A: Reproduction of the patient's typical pain during CCK infusion
