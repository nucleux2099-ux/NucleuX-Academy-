# Cholangitis: Diagnosis & Emergency Management

> **Source**: Blumgart Ch 37-38, Tokyo Guidelines
> **Topic System**: [[Biliary Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Acute cholangitis is a life-threatening bacterial infection of the biliary system due to obstruction (usually by stones), requiring urgent biliary decompression in addition to antibiotics.

### Key Points

1. **Triad/Pentad**: Charcot's triad (pain, fever, jaundice) = 50-70%; Reynolds' pentad adds hypotension + AMS
2. **Pathophysiology**: Obstruction → stasis → bacterial overgrowth → translocation into bloodstream
3. **Urgency**: Can progress rapidly to septic shock; mortality 10-30% if untreated
4. **Treatment**: Antibiotics + urgent biliary drainage (ERCP preferred)
5. **Timing**: Mild/moderate → ERCP within 24-48h; Severe → ERCP within 12-24h

### Clinical Relevance

- **Emergency presentation**: Fever + jaundice + RUQ pain = cholangitis until proven otherwise
- **Red flags**: Hypotension, altered mental status → severe cholangitis (Reynolds' pentad)
- **Critical decision**: Resuscitation + antibiotics, then URGENT drainage (not elective)

## Detailed Notes

### Pathophysiology

```
OBSTRUCTION (stones, stricture, tumor, stent occlusion)
                    │
                    ▼
        Bile stasis + ↑ intrabiliary pressure
                    │
                    ▼
            Bacterial colonization
        (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus)
                    │
                    ▼
    Cholangio-venous/lymphatic reflux
                    │
                    ▼
              BACTEREMIA/SEPSIS
                    │
                    ▼
    Multi-organ failure if untreated
```

### Causative Organisms

| Organism | Frequency | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| **E. coli** | 25-50% | Most common |
| **Klebsiella** | 15-20% | Common |
| **Enterococcus** | 10-20% | Gram-positive |
| **Enterobacter** | 5-10% | Gram-negative |
| **Pseudomonas** | 5% | Healthcare-associated |
| **Anaerobes** | 10-20% | Bacteroides, Clostridium |

### Clinical Presentation

#### Charcot's Triad (Classic)
1. **Fever/chills** (90%)
2. **Jaundice** (60-70%)
3. **RUQ pain** (70%)

*Complete triad present in only 50-70% of cases*

#### Reynolds' Pentad (Severe/Suppurative)
- Charcot's triad PLUS:
4. **Hypotension** (shock)
5. **Altered mental status** (confusion)

*Reynolds' pentad = severe cholangitis with high mortality*

### Tokyo Guidelines (TG18) Severity

| Grade | Criteria | Drainage Timing |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **Grade I (Mild)** | No organ dysfunction, responds to antibiotics | 24-48 hours |
| **Grade II (Moderate)** | Any 2 of: WBC >12k or <4k, fever >39°C, age >75, bilirubin >5, albumin <0.7×normal | Within 24 hours |
| **Grade III (Severe)** | Organ dysfunction (CV, neuro, renal, hepatic, respiratory, coagulation) | Within 12-24 hours (as soon as possible) |

### Diagnosis

#### Diagnostic Criteria (TG18)

**Systemic inflammation:**
- Fever/chills or
- Lab evidence (WBC, CRP)

**Cholestasis:**
- Jaundice or
- Elevated LFTs

**Imaging:**
- Biliary dilation or
- Evidence of etiology (stone, stricture, stent)

#### Laboratory Findings

| Test | Typical Finding |
|------|-----------------|
| **WBC** | >12,000 or <4,000 (severe) |
| **Bilirubin** | Elevated |
| **ALP/GGT** | Elevated |
| **Blood cultures** | Positive in 30-50% |
| **Lactate** | Elevated (if severe) |

#### Imaging

- **Ultrasound**: First-line; shows dilated CBD, may see stones
- **CT**: Evaluate for complications, abscess
- **MRCP**: Define anatomy if ERCP delayed

#### Antibiotic Regimens

| Setting | Regimen |
|---------|---------|
| **Community-acquired, mild** | Piperacillin-tazobactam OR Ceftriaxone + metronidazole |
| **Community-acquired, severe** | Piperacillin-tazobactam OR Carbapenem |
| **Healthcare-associated** | Carbapenem (meropenem, imipenem) ± Vancomycin |

**Duration**: 4-7 days if source controlled; longer if persistent infection

#### Biliary Drainage Options

| Method | Success Rate | Indications |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **ERCP** | >90% | First-line for most cases |
| **PTC (Percutaneous)** | >90% | Failed ERCP, altered anatomy (Roux-en-Y), hilar obstruction |
| **Surgical** | >95% | Failed ERCP/PTC, laparotomy for other reasons |
| **EUS-guided** | Emerging | Failed ERCP, altered anatomy |

#### ERCP for Cholangitis

**Goals:**
1. Decompress biliary system
2. Remove stones if possible
3. Place stent if incomplete clearance

**Procedure:**
- Sphincterotomy
- Stone extraction (balloon, basket)
- Biliary stent if cannot clear completely
- Avoid prolonged procedure in unstable patients

### Prognosis

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Mortality** | 5-10% with treatment; 30-50% untreated |
| **Grade III** | Higher mortality (15-30%) |
| **Delayed drainage** | Worse outcomes |
| **MDR organisms** | Increased mortality |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What are Charcot's triad and Reynolds' pentad?
   A: Charcot's: Fever, jaundice, RUQ pain. Reynolds' adds hypotension and altered mental status (severe cholangitis).

2. Q: What is the drainage timing for severe (Grade III) cholangitis?
   A: Within 12-24 hours (as soon as possible after stabilization)

3. Q: When is PTC preferred over ERCP for biliary drainage?
   A: Failed ERCP, surgically altered anatomy (Roux-en-Y), or hilar/intrahepatic obstruction
