# Acute Cholecystitis: Diagnosis & Management

> **Source**: Blumgart Ch 33-34, Tokyo Guidelines
> **Topic System**: [[Biliary Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Acute cholecystitis is inflammation of the gallbladder, usually (90-95%) due to cystic duct obstruction by gallstones, presenting with RUQ pain, fever, and leukocytosis.

### Key Points

1. **Pathophysiology**: Stone impaction → GB distension → wall ischemia → inflammation ± secondary infection
2. **Diagnosis**: Clinical + Ultrasound (stones + wall thickening + pericholecystic fluid)
3. **Murphy's sign**: Inspiratory arrest during RUQ palpation (positive in ~65%)
4. **Tokyo Guidelines**: Grade severity (I-III) to guide management
5. **Treatment**: Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy (<72h preferred)

### Clinical Relevance

- **When to think about this**: RUQ pain >6 hours with fever, especially with known gallstones
- **Red flags**: Generalized peritonitis, sepsis → gangrenous or perforated cholecystitis
- **Decision point**: Early surgery vs delayed vs cholecystostomy based on severity and fitness

### Types of Cholecystitis

| Type | Cause | Features | % of Cases |
|------|-------|----------|------------|
| **Calculous** | Gallstone obstruction | Most common | 90-95% |
| **Acalculous** | Stasis, ischemia (ICU, TPN) | Critically ill, higher mortality | 5-10% |
| **Emphysematous** | Gas-forming organisms | Diabetics, men, gas in wall | Rare |
| **Gangrenous** | Wall necrosis | Severe, toxic, risk of perforation | 2-30% |

### Clinical Presentation

#### Symptoms & Signs

| Finding | Frequency | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **RUQ pain** | 95% | Constant (not colicky), >6 hours |
| **Fever** | 50-75% | Low-grade; high = complicated |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | 70% | Common |
| **Murphy's sign** | 65% | Inspiratory arrest on RUQ palpation |
| **RUQ mass** | 20-30% | Palpable GB or omentum |
| **Jaundice** | 10-20% | Suggests CBD stone or Mirizzi |

> [!tip] Clinical Pearl
> **Murphy's sign** = RUQ tenderness + inspiratory arrest during palpation
> **Sonographic Murphy's** = Maximal tenderness when probe presses over GB

### Tokyo Guidelines (TG18) Severity Grading

| Grade | Criteria | Management |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Grade I (Mild)** | No organ dysfunction, mild local inflammation | Early lap chole (<72h) |
| **Grade II (Moderate)** | WBC >18k, mass, duration >72h, marked local inflammation | Early lap chole if fit; consider delayed |
| **Grade III (Severe)** | Organ dysfunction (CV, neuro, renal, hepatic, respiratory, coagulation) | Stabilize + cholecystostomy; delayed chole |

### Diagnosis

#### Laboratory Findings

| Test | Typical Finding | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| **WBC** | Elevated (12-18k) | >18k = moderate severity |
| **CRP** | Elevated | Correlates with severity |
| **LFTs** | Mild elevation | Marked elevation → CBD stone |
| **Bilirubin** | Usually normal | Elevated → Choledocholithiasis |
| **Lipase** | Normal | Elevated → Gallstone pancreatitis |

#### Imaging

**Ultrasound (First-line)**
- Sensitivity: 95% for stones
- Findings:
  - Gallstones + sonographic Murphy's sign
  - Wall thickening >3mm
  - Pericholecystic fluid
  - GB distension >4cm diameter

**HIDA Scan (Cholescintigraphy)**
- Role: Confirms cystic duct obstruction when US equivocal
- Positive: Non-visualization of GB at 4 hours
- Sensitivity: 95% for acute cholecystitis

**CT Scan**
- Role: Evaluate complications (perforation, abscess)
- Findings: Wall thickening, pericholecystic inflammation, enhancement

#### Early vs Delayed Cholecystectomy

| Approach | Timing | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|--------|------------|---------------|
| **Early** | <72h from onset | Single admission, shorter total hospital stay, lower cost | Technically more challenging |
| **Delayed** | 6-8 weeks | Easier dissection | Recurrent symptoms (20-30%), second admission |

**Current recommendation: Early cholecystectomy (within 72h) is preferred when feasible**

#### Bailout Options in Difficult Cholecystectomy

| Scenario | Option |
|----------|--------|
| Frozen Calot's triangle | Fundus-first (dome-down) approach |
| Cannot identify CVS | Intraoperative cholangiogram |
| Severe inflammation | Subtotal cholecystectomy |
| Unsafe to proceed | Convert to open |
| Very high risk | Cholecystostomy tube, delayed chole |

### Complications

| Complication | Presentation | Management |
|--------------|--------------|------------|
| **Gangrenous cholecystitis** | Toxic, severe pain, may have relief (wall necrosis) | Urgent cholecystectomy |
| **Empyema** | High fever, sepsis, palpable tender GB | Urgent cholecystectomy |
| **Perforation** | Peritonitis (free) or abscess (contained) | Surgery + drainage |
| **Cholecystoenteric fistula** | May → gallstone ileus | Close fistula, cholecystectomy |

### Acalculous Cholecystitis

**High-risk patients:**
- ICU patients (trauma, burns, sepsis)
- TPN
- Mechanical ventilation
- Post-major surgery
- Immunosuppressed

**Pathophysiology**: Stasis + ischemia → mucosal injury → inflammation

**Key differences:**
- Higher mortality (10-50%)
- Higher perforation rate
- Diagnosis difficult (patients often sedated)
- Treatment: Percutaneous cholecystostomy if unfit for surgery

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What differentiates biliary colic from acute cholecystitis?
   A: Duration (colic <6h, cholecystitis >6h), fever, leukocytosis, Murphy's sign, US findings (wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid)

2. Q: What are the Tokyo Guidelines Grade III criteria?
   A: Presence of any organ dysfunction (cardiovascular, neurological, renal, hepatic, respiratory, coagulation)

3. Q: When is percutaneous cholecystostomy indicated?
   A: Grade III (severe) acute cholecystitis with organ dysfunction, patients unfit for surgery, or as a bridge to delayed surgery
