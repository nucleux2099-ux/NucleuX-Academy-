## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Cholelithiasis refers to the presence of gallstones in the gallbladder, formed by precipitation of cholesterol, bilirubin, or calcium salts from supersaturated bile.

### Key Points

1. **Prevalence**: 10-15% of adults; increases with age
2. **Types**: Cholesterol stones (75%), Pigment stones (25% - black or brown)
3. **"5 F's"**: Fat, Forty, Female, Fertile, Fair (traditional risk factors)
4. **Natural history**: 80% remain asymptomatic; 1-2% per year become symptomatic
5. **Treatment**: Symptomatic stones → Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (gold standard)

### The Pathogenesis Triangle

```
        SUPERSATURATION
        (excess cholesterol)
              /\
             /  \
            /    \
           /      \
          /________\
  NUCLEATION      HYPOMOTILITY
  (crystals)      (stasis)
```

### Clinical Relevance

- **When to think about this**: RUQ or epigastric pain, especially postprandial
- **Red flags**: Fever, jaundice, persistent pain >6h → complicated disease
- **Decision point**: Symptomatic = operate; Asymptomatic = usually observe

## Detailed Notes

### Types of Gallstones

| Feature | Cholesterol Stones | Black Pigment | Brown Pigment |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Composition** | >50% cholesterol | Calcium bilirubinate | Calcium bilirubinate + bacteria |
| **Prevalence** | 75% | 20% | 5% |
| **Location** | Gallbladder | Gallbladder | Bile ducts (primary) |
| **Radiopaque** | 15% | 50% | 0% |
| **Cause** | Metabolic | Hemolysis, cirrhosis | Infection, stasis |
| **Associated** | Obesity, diet | Chronic hemolysis | Biliary stricture, parasites |

### Risk Factors

**Traditional "5 F's" plus modern additions:**

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Female** | Estrogen increases cholesterol secretion |
| **Forty** | Bile salt synthesis decreases with age |
| **Fertile/Pregnant** | Progesterone decreases GB motility |
| **Fat/Obese** | Increased cholesterol synthesis |
| **Fair (Ethnicity)** | Pima Indians >70%, Northern Europeans high |
| **Family history** | Genetic polymorphisms (ABCG8, etc.) |
| **Rapid weight loss** | Bile supersaturation |
| **TPN** | Gallbladder stasis |
| **Drugs** | Fibrates, octreotide, ceftriaxone |
| **Cirrhosis** | Black pigment stones |
| **Hemolysis** | Sickle cell, spherocytosis → black pigment |

### Clinical Presentations

#### Spectrum of Disease

```
ASYMPTOMATIC → BILIARY COLIC → ACUTE CHOLECYSTITIS → COMPLICATIONS
    (80%)         (15%)            (3-5%)              (1-2%)
                                                         │
                                    ┌────────────────────┼────────────────────┐
                                    ▼                    ▼                    ▼
                              Choledocholithiasis   Gallstone         Mirizzi
                                    │               Pancreatitis      Syndrome
                                    ▼
                               Cholangitis
```

#### Biliary Colic

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Pain location** | Epigastric or RUQ (NOT actually "colic" - steady pain) |
| **Radiation** | Right scapula, back |
| **Timing** | Postprandial, especially fatty meals |
| **Duration** | 30 min to 6 hours (>6h = cholecystitis) |
| **Associated** | Nausea, vomiting |
| **Exam** | Minimal tenderness, no peritonitis |

### Diagnosis

#### Imaging Algorithm

```
    Suspected biliary colic
            │
            ▼
    ┌───────────────┐
    │  Ultrasound   │  ← First-line (sensitivity 95%)
    │  (RUQ)        │
    └───────┬───────┘
            │
    ┌───────┴───────┐
    │               │
    ▼               ▼
  Stones seen    No stones, symptoms persist
    │               │
    ▼               ▼
  Lap chole      HIDA scan (EF <35% = biliary dyskinesia)
                    or
                 EUS/MRCP (if CBD stone suspected)
```

#### Ultrasound Findings

- **Gallstones**: Hyperechoic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing
- **Mobile**: Change position with patient movement
- **Wall thickening**: >3mm suggests cholecystitis
- **Pericholecystic fluid**: Suggests inflammation

### Management

#### Asymptomatic Gallstones

**General rule: OBSERVE** (unless special circumstances)

**Indications for prophylactic cholecystectomy:**
- Porcelain gallbladder (calcified wall → cancer risk)
- Gallstones >3 cm (increased cancer risk)
- Gallbladder polyp >1 cm with stones
- Congenital hemolytic anemia (sickle cell, spherocytosis)
- Before bariatric surgery (controversial)
- Native Americans (very high cancer risk)

#### Symptomatic Gallstones

**Treatment: Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy**

```
LAPAROSCOPIC CHOLECYSTECTOMY STEPS:
1. Pneumoperitoneum (Veress or Hasson)
2. 4-port technique (umbilical, epigastric, 2 RUQ)
3. Retract fundus cephalad, infundibulum laterally
4. Dissect Calot's triangle
5. Achieve CRITICAL VIEW OF SAFETY:
   - Hepatocystic triangle cleared
   - Lower 1/3 GB separated from liver bed
   - Only 2 structures entering GB (cystic duct, cystic artery)
6. Clip and divide cystic artery
7. Clip and divide cystic duct
8. Dissect GB from liver bed
9. Extract GB in bag
10. Inspect hemostasis, irrigate
```

> [!warning] Critical View of Safety
> The CVS is MANDATORY to prevent bile duct injury.
> NEVER clip anything until CVS is achieved.
> If anatomy unclear → IOC, fundus-first, or convert.

### Complications of Gallstones

| Complication | Presentation | Management |
|--------------|--------------|------------|
| **Acute cholecystitis** | RUQ pain, fever, Murphy's sign | Cholecystectomy |
| **Choledocholithiasis** | Jaundice, elevated LFTs | ERCP → lap chole |
| **Cholangitis** | Charcot's triad (pain, fever, jaundice) | ERCP urgently |
| **Gallstone pancreatitis** | Epigastric pain, elevated lipase | ERCP if CBD stone, then chole |
| **Gallstone ileus** | SBO, pneumobilia | Enterolithotomy |
| **Mirizzi syndrome** | Jaundice, stone impacted in Hartmann's | Cholecystectomy ± CBD repair |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What are the 3 factors in the pathogenesis of cholesterol stones?
   A: Supersaturation (excess cholesterol), Nucleation (crystals form), Hypomotility (stasis)

2. Q: What percentage of gallstones remain asymptomatic?
   A: ~80% remain asymptomatic; 1-2% per year become symptomatic

3. Q: What is the Critical View of Safety and why is it important?
   A: CVS requires clearing Calot's triangle, separating lower 1/3 of GB from liver bed, and seeing only 2 structures entering GB. It prevents bile duct injury.
