## 1. History — From Ancient Wrappings to Endovenous Revolution

Varicose veins are among the oldest recognized surgical conditions. The Ebers Papyrus (~1550 BC) described serpentine leg swellings, and Hippocrates advocated puncture and compression. The condition has shaped the evolution of vascular surgery itself.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| ~400 BC | **Hippocrates** — Described varicose veins; recommended puncture, not incision |
| 1846 | **Brodie** — Recognized saphenofemoral junction (SFJ) reflux as the cause |
| 1890 | **Trendelenburg** — SFJ ligation; the Trendelenburg test named after him |
| 1907 | **Babcock** — Introduced the internal stripper for GSV removal |
| 1916 | **Homans** — Described subfascial ligation of perforators |
| 1938 | **Linton** — Subfascial perforator ligation via medial calf incision |
| 1985 | **SEPS** — Subfascial endoscopic perforator surgery introduced |
| 1999 | **Endovenous laser ablation (EVLA)** — First reported by Boné |
| 2003 | **Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)** — ClosureFAST system introduced |
| 2011 | **Cyanoacrylate closure (VenaSeal)** — Non-thermal non-tumescent technique |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.56*

The last two decades have witnessed a paradigm shift from open stripping to office-based endovenous procedures — transforming varicose vein treatment from an inpatient operation to a daycare procedure.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology

### Venous Anatomy of the Lower Limb

🟢 **Three systems of veins:**

1. **Superficial veins** — lie above the deep (muscular) fascia
   - **Great saphenous vein (GSV):** Begins anterior to the medial malleolus → ascends along the medial calf and thigh → pierces the cribriform fascia → joins the common femoral vein at the **saphenofemoral junction (SFJ)** in the groin
   - **Small saphenous vein (SSV):** Begins posterior to the lateral malleolus → ascends the posterior calf midline → joins the popliteal vein at the **saphenopopliteal junction (SPJ)**, typically at the popliteal crease (variable: 2–7 cm above crease)

2. **Deep veins** — within the muscular compartment
   - Paired veins accompany arteries: anterior tibial, posterior tibial, peroneal → popliteal → femoral → common femoral → external iliac

3. **Perforating veins** — connect superficial to deep systems, traversing the deep fascia
   - Normal flow: superficial → deep (ensured by valves)
   - Named perforators: **Cockett** (posterior tibial), **Boyd** (below knee), **Dodd/Hunterian** (thigh)

🟢 **The saphenous compartment:** The GSV and SSV travel within a fascial envelope (the "saphenous eye" on ultrasound) — formed by the superficial fascia (saphenous fascia) and deep muscular fascia. This is a key ultrasound landmark.

🟢 **Venous valves:**
- Bicuspid valves every 7–10 cm in superficial veins
- Direct blood centrally (toward the heart) and from superficial to deep
- The most constant valve is at the SFJ (terminal valve) and one just distal to it (preterminal valve)

### Physiology of Venous Return

🟢 **Mechanisms of venous return from the lower limb:**

| Mechanism | Contribution |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Calf muscle pump** (most important) | Generates pressures up to 200 mmHg during contraction; empties deep veins proximally |
| **Valvular competence** | Prevents retrograde flow during muscle relaxation |
| **Respiratory pump** | Negative intrathoracic pressure during inspiration aids venous return |
| **Vis a tergo** | Residual arterial pressure transmitted through capillaries |

🔵 **Ambulatory venous pressure (AVP):** In the upright position, standing venous pressure in the foot is ~80–90 mmHg (hydrostatic column). During calf muscle exercise, competent valves reduce this to ~20–30 mmHg. **In varicose veins with reflux, AVP remains elevated (>40 mmHg)** — this sustained venous hypertension drives all complications.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The concept of the "closed circuit" — venous reflux creates a recirculation loop: blood flows proximally through deep veins, refluxes back through incompetent SFJ/SPJ, descends in varicose tributaries, re-enters deep veins through perforators, and recirculates. This closed loop of wasted volume is what makes varicose veins hemodynamically significant, not simply the cosmetic appearance.

## 3. Pathophysiology — Why Veins Become Varicose

🟢 **Definition:** Varicose veins are **dilated, tortuous, elongated superficial veins** resulting from incompetent valves and/or weakened vein walls. They are ≥3 mm in diameter (distinguishing them from reticular veins and telangiectasias).

### Primary Varicose Veins (95%)

🟢 **Risk factors:**
- **Age** — Prevalence increases with each decade
- **Female sex** — 2–3× more common (hormonal effects on vein wall)
- **Pregnancy** — Hormonal (progesterone → smooth muscle relaxation), mechanical (uterine compression of IVC/iliac veins), increased blood volume
- **Family history** — Strongest independent risk factor; likely polygenic
- **Prolonged standing** — Occupational risk (surgeons, teachers, hairdressers)
- **Obesity** — Especially in women
- **Western lifestyle** — Low-fiber diet → constipation → straining → ↑ intra-abdominal pressure

🔵 **Pathogenesis — the vein wall hypothesis:**
The traditional "descending valve failure" model (SFJ fails first → reflux propagates distally) has been challenged. Current evidence supports a **primary vein wall abnormality:**

1. Histopathology shows disorganized smooth muscle, collagen/elastin degradation, and MMP overexpression in varicose veins — even in segments with competent valves
2. Valve failure may be *secondary* to wall dilatation (the valve cusps become too far apart to coapt)
3. "Ascending" patterns of reflux (distal incompetence without SFJ reflux) are common

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified variants in FOXC2, PIEZO1, and PPP3R1 linked to varicose veins. FOXC2 mutations cause lymphedema-distichiasis syndrome with prominent varicose veins. These findings support a fundamental structural/genetic basis rather than simple hemodynamic wear-and-tear.

### Secondary Varicose Veins (5%)

🟢 **Causes:**
- **Post-thrombotic syndrome (most common)** — Deep vein damage/obstruction → venous hypertension transmitted to superficial system
- **Pelvic tumors** compressing iliac veins
- **Arteriovenous malformations** (Klippel-Trenaunay, Parkes Weber syndromes)
- **Pregnancy** (overlap with primary)

🔵 **Critical distinction:** In secondary varicose veins from DVT, the superficial veins may be the *only remaining outflow pathway*. Stripping them would be catastrophic. **Always confirm deep venous patency before any intervention.**

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

🟢 **Common complaints:**
- Visible, bulging veins — cosmetic concern often the primary driver
- **Aching, heaviness, tiredness** in the legs — worse after prolonged standing, better with elevation
- **Throbbing and itching** along varicosities
- **Nocturnal cramps** — common but nonspecific
- Ankle swelling (mild, typically unilateral or asymmetric)

🔵 **Symptom severity does NOT correlate with the size of varicosities.** Small veins with significant reflux may cause more symptoms than large but hemodynamically insignificant varices.

### Signs

🟢 **Examination — performed with the patient standing:**

- **Visible varicosities:** Distribution indicates the underlying source
  - Medial thigh/calf → GSV territory
  - Posterior calf → SSV territory
  - Anterior thigh lateral → anterior accessory GSV
- **Ankle changes of chronic venous insufficiency (CVI):**
  - **Corona phlebectatica** — fan of intradermal veins at the ankle (earliest sign of CVI)
  - **Hemosiderin deposition** — brown discoloration (breakdown of extravasated RBCs)
  - **Lipodermatosclerosis** — fibrotic, indurated skin ("inverted champagne bottle" leg)
  - **Atrophie blanche** — ivory-white atrophic areas with surrounding hyperpigmentation
  - **Venous ulceration** — classically at the **medial gaiter area** (above medial malleolus)

### Clinical Tests (Historical/Exam Importance)

🟢 **Trendelenburg test (tourniquet test):**
1. Patient supine → elevate leg to drain veins
2. Apply tourniquet at SFJ level → patient stands
3. **Positive:** Veins stay collapsed for 30 seconds (controlled by tourniquet) then fill rapidly on release → confirms SFJ incompetence
4. If veins fill rapidly *despite* tourniquet → perforator incompetence below tourniquet level

🟢 **Modified Trendelenburg (multiple tourniquet test):** Apply tourniquets at multiple levels to localize the level of incompetent perforators.

🟢 **Tap test (Chevrier's test / Schwartz test):** Tap varicosity distally; transmitted impulse felt proximally indicates a continuous column of blood without competent valves.

🟢 **Perthes test (walking test for deep vein patency):**
1. Apply tourniquet below SFJ → patient walks/does calf raises
2. If varicosities **collapse** → deep veins patent, calf pump functional
3. If varicosities **distend and pain increases** → deep venous obstruction (do NOT strip superficial veins)

🔵 **Brodie-Trendelenburg test and Pratt's test** are additional historical maneuvers. In modern practice, these bedside tests have been largely supplanted by duplex ultrasound, but they remain important for clinical exams and resource-limited settings.

## 5. Classification

### CEAP Classification

🟢 **The international standard for chronic venous disease (updated 2020):**

**C — Clinical:**

| Class | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| C0 | No visible or palpable signs |
| C1 | Telangiectasias (<1 mm) or reticular veins (1–3 mm) |
| C2 | Varicose veins (≥3 mm) |
| C2r | Recurrent varicose veins |
| C3 | Edema |
| C4a | Pigmentation or eczema |
| C4b | Lipodermatosclerosis or atrophie blanche |
| C4c | Corona phlebectatica |
| C5 | Healed venous ulcer |
| C6 | Active venous ulcer |
| C6r | Recurrent active venous ulcer |

Each class is qualified as **s** (symptomatic) or **a** (asymptomatic).

**E — Etiology:** Ep (primary), Es (secondary — post-thrombotic), En (no cause identified), Ec (congenital)

**A — Anatomy:** As (superficial), Ad (deep), Ap (perforator), An (no location identified)

**P — Pathophysiology:** Pr (reflux), Po (obstruction), Pr,o (both), Pn (no pathophysiology identified)

🟢 **Example:** A patient with varicose veins, skin changes, and an active ulcer from primary GSV reflux = **C6s, Ep, As, Pr**

🔵 **Venous Clinical Severity Score (VCSS):** A 10-item scoring system (0–30) that tracks disease severity longitudinally and is used in clinical trials to assess treatment outcomes. Components include pain, varicose veins, edema, pigmentation, inflammation, induration, number/size/duration of ulcers, and compression use.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64*

## 6. Investigations

### Duplex Ultrasound — The Gold Standard

🟢 **Duplex ultrasound is mandatory before any intervention.** It combines B-mode imaging with Doppler flow assessment.

🟢 **What to assess:**
1. **Deep vein patency** — rule out DVT / post-thrombotic changes
2. **Source of reflux** — SFJ, SPJ, perforators
3. **Reflux duration** — pathological if >0.5 seconds in superficial veins, >1 second in deep veins (after augmentation/Valsalva)
4. **Vein diameter** — affects treatment choice (large veins may not respond to sclerotherapy)
5. **Anatomy** — GSV/SSV course, accessory veins, high/low SPJ, duplications

🟢 **Technique:** Patient standing (or reverse Trendelenburg). Reflux provoked by:
- Manual calf compression/release
- Valsalva maneuver (for SFJ)
- Pneumatic cuff deflation (standardized)

🔵 **The "saphenous eye"** — on transverse B-mode, the GSV is seen as a round structure within the hyperechoic fascial triangle (the saphenous compartment). This distinguishes the GSV from superficial tributaries — important because treating the wrong vein leads to recurrence.

🔵 **Perforator incompetence:** Outward flow (superficial-directed) >0.5 seconds duration, with perforator diameter >3.5 mm. Named perforators (Cockett I, II, III at 7, 13, 18 cm above the medial malleolus; Boyd at the tibial tuberosity; Hunterian mid-thigh) are important for surgical planning.

### Other Investigations

🔵 **CT venography / MR venography:** Reserved for suspected iliac vein obstruction (May-Thurner syndrome), pelvic congestion syndrome, or recurrent varicose veins with complex anatomy.

🔵 **Venous plethysmography (air plethysmography):** Quantifies global venous function — venous filling index, ejection fraction, residual volume fraction. Used in research settings and complex cases.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Intravascular ultrasound (IVUS):** The gold standard for diagnosing iliac vein compression (May-Thurner). CT/MR venography has only ~50% sensitivity for non-thrombotic iliac vein lesions compared to IVUS. In patients with left-sided varicosities refractory to treatment, always consider iliac outflow obstruction.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.24*

## 7. Treatment

### General Principles

🟢 **Indications for treatment:**
- Symptomatic varicose veins (C2s and above)
- Skin changes (C4–C6) — these represent chronic venous insufficiency and benefit most
- Complications: bleeding, superficial thrombophlebitis, venous ulceration
- Cosmetic concerns (after counseling about recurrence and realistic expectations)

🟢 **Conservative management — appropriate for ALL patients as baseline:**
- **Compression stockings** (class II: 23–32 mmHg) — the cornerstone
- Weight loss if obese
- Avoid prolonged standing; regular walking exercise
- Leg elevation when resting
- Skin care for eczema/dermatitis

🔵 **NICE guidelines (UK, 2013):** Recommend offering interventional treatment to all patients with symptomatic primary varicose veins. Compression alone should NOT be offered as definitive treatment if intervention is suitable — it only manages symptoms without addressing the underlying pathology.

### A. Endovenous Thermal Ablation (First-line)

🟢 **Endovenous Laser Ablation (EVLA):**
- Laser fiber inserted into GSV/SSV under ultrasound guidance
- Tumescent anesthesia infiltrated around vein (protects perivenous tissue, compresses vein onto fiber)
- Laser energy (810–1470 nm) destroys the endothelium → fibrosis → vein obliteration
- **Linear endovenous energy density (LEED):** Target 60–100 J/cm
- Performed under local anesthesia as a daycase procedure

🟢 **Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA) — ClosureFAST:**
- Segmental thermal ablation using a 7 cm heating element
- 120°C for 20 seconds per segment (2 cycles at the SFJ)
- Tumescent anesthesia required
- Possibly less postoperative pain and bruising than EVLA

🔵 **Outcomes (landmark trials):**

| Trial | Comparison | Key Finding |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **CLASS** (UK, 2019) | EVLA vs Surgery vs UGFS | EVLA and surgery equivalent at 5 years; UGFS inferior (higher recurrence) |
| **EVRA** (UK, 2018) | Early endovenous ablation vs compression for venous ulcers | Early ablation: ulcer healing 12 weeks faster; strong evidence to treat reflux in active ulcers |

🔵 **Contraindications to endothermal ablation:**
- Very tortuous veins (catheter cannot pass)
- Superficial vein course (risk of skin burns)
- Active superficial thrombophlebitis over the target segment
- Post-thrombotic deep venous disease (relative — must confirm outflow)

### B. Non-Thermal Non-Tumescent (NTNT) Techniques

🔵 **Cyanoacrylate closure (VenaSeal):**
- Medical-grade cyanoacrylate "glue" injected into the vein
- No tumescent anesthesia needed (single needle puncture)
- No risk of thermal nerve injury
- Occlusion rate ~97% at 3 years
- Main concern: foreign body reaction, phlebitis (~10–15%)

🔵 **Mechanochemical ablation (MOCA — ClariVein):**
- Rotating wire tip damages endothelium + simultaneous sclerosant injection
- No tumescent anesthesia
- Lower postoperative pain
- Slightly lower occlusion rates than thermal techniques at 5 years

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The NTNT techniques are particularly valuable for the SSV (where the sural nerve runs close to the vein — thermal injury risk 4–7%) and for veins very close to the skin. However, long-term data (>5 years) remain limited compared to EVLA/RFA.

### C. Ultrasound-Guided Foam Sclerotherapy (UGFS)

🟢 **Technique:**
- Sclerosant (sodium tetradecyl sulfate or polidocanol) mixed with air/CO₂ to create foam (Tessari method: 1:4 liquid-to-gas ratio)
- Injected into the varicose vein under ultrasound guidance
- Foam displaces blood, maximizes sclerosant–endothelium contact
- Compression applied post-procedure

🟢 **Advantages:** Cheap, office-based, no anesthesia, repeatable
**Disadvantages:** Higher recurrence (up to 30–40% at 5 years), visual disturbances (rare — foam microemboli), skin staining, thrombophlebitis

🔵 **Maximum foam volume:** Generally 10–12 mL per session to minimize neurological side effects. Patients with patent foramen ovale (PFO) — present in ~25% of population — are at theoretical risk of paradoxical embolism, though clinical events are exceedingly rare.

### D. Surgical Treatment

🟢 **Flush SFJ ligation + GSV stripping + phlebectomies:**
- The traditional gold standard, now third-line per NICE guidelines
- **Flush ligation:** SFJ divided flush with the femoral vein; all tributaries (5 named: superficial epigastric, superficial circumflex iliac, superficial external pudendal, deep external pudendal, anterolateral thigh vein) ligated
- **Stripping:** GSV stripped from groin to **knee level only** (not to the ankle — saphenous nerve runs with the GSV below knee → risk of neuralgia in 7–40%)
- **Stab avulsions/phlebectomies:** Varicose tributaries avulsed through 2–3 mm incisions

🟢 **Why groin-to-knee stripping?** The GSV below the knee may be needed as a future bypass conduit (coronary/peripheral). Below-knee stripping adds saphenous nerve injury without reducing recurrence.

🔵 **Neovascularization:** New vessel growth at the ligated SFJ is the commonest cause of recurrence after surgery (up to 50% of recurrences). This was the main impetus for developing endovenous alternatives — ablation avoids the groin wound that triggers neovascularization.

🔵 **SSV surgery:**
- SPJ ligation through a popliteal fossa incision
- SPJ level must be marked preoperatively with ultrasound (highly variable anatomy)
- SSV is NOT routinely stripped (sural nerve injury risk)
- Common peroneal nerve injury is a risk with high SPJ

### E. Treatment of Perforator Incompetence

🔵 **Indications:** Incompetent perforators contributing to recurrent varicosities or venous ulceration (C5–C6), especially in post-thrombotic limbs.

🔵 **Options:**
- **Subfascial endoscopic perforator surgery (SEPS):** Endoscopic clip/division of perforators via a proximal incision away from diseased skin. Largely replaced by percutaneous techniques.
- **Percutaneous ablation of perforators (PAPS):** Ultrasound-guided laser or RFA of incompetent perforators. Increasingly preferred.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The role of isolated perforator treatment remains controversial. The ESCHAR trial showed that superficial surgery alone (without perforator surgery) reduced ulcer recurrence from 56% to 31% at 4 years. Adding perforator surgery may benefit patients with deep venous incompetence, but evidence is limited.

## 8. Complications of Varicose Veins

🟢 **Acute complications:**
- **Hemorrhage:** Varicose veins have thin walls under high pressure. Bleeding can be torrential but is easily controlled by **elevation + direct pressure** (NOT tourniquet). Bleeding from an ankle varicosity in an elderly patient on anticoagulants can be fatal if not managed promptly.
- **Superficial thrombophlebitis:** Painful, tender, erythematous cord along a varicosity. Treat with NSAIDs, compression, and mobilization. If thrombus extends to within 3 cm of SFJ/SPJ → anticoagulation or ligation to prevent DVT extension.

🟢 **Chronic complications (CVI):**
- Edema → skin changes (C4) → ulceration (C6)
- Venous eczema (stasis dermatitis)
- Lipodermatosclerosis
- Venous ulceration — see below

### Venous Ulceration

🟢 **Key features:**
- Most common leg ulcer type (~70% of all leg ulcers)
- Location: **medial gaiter area** (above medial malleolus) — territory of incompetent Cockett perforators
- Shallow, irregular margins, with granulation tissue base
- Surrounding skin: pigmented, lipodermatosclerotic
- **Painless or mildly painful** (vs. arterial ulcers which are very painful)

🟢 **ABPI (Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index):** Must be checked before applying compression.
- ABPI >0.8: Safe to compress
- ABPI 0.5–0.8: Modified compression with caution
- ABPI <0.5: Do NOT compress — significant arterial disease

🔵 **Management of venous ulcers:**
1. **Compression** — four-layer bandaging or compression stockings (cornerstone of healing)
2. **Treat underlying reflux** — EVRA trial showed early endovenous ablation accelerated healing by 12 weeks
3. **Wound care** — dressings, debridement, infection control
4. **Pentoxifylline** — improves ulcer healing (Cochrane evidence)
5. **Skin grafting** — for large, non-healing ulcers

🔴 **SS Pearl — Marjolin's ulcer:** Any chronic non-healing venous ulcer (>10 years) should be biopsied to exclude squamous cell carcinoma arising in the ulcer bed (Marjolin's ulcer). These are aggressive, with high rates of local recurrence and metastasis.

## 9. Recurrent Varicose Veins

🔵 **Incidence:** 20–60% at 5 years (varies by treatment modality and definition).

🔵 **Causes of recurrence (mnemonic: REVAS — REcurrent Varices After Surgery/treatment):**
- **Neovascularization** at the ligated junction (most common after surgery)
- **Inadequate initial treatment** — missed tributary, incomplete stripping
- **Progression of disease** — new reflux in previously normal segments
- **Incompetent perforators** not addressed
- **Deep venous disease** — post-thrombotic reflux/obstruction

🔵 **Work-up:** Detailed duplex ultrasound mapping is essential. Consider CT/MR venography for complex cases or suspected pelvic source.

🔵 **Treatment:** UGFS is often first-line for recurrent varicose veins (avoids re-do groin surgery with its higher complication rate). Endovenous ablation of recurrent or residual truncal veins is also effective.

## 10. Special Situations

### Varicose Veins in Pregnancy

🟢 **Management:**
- Conservative: compression stockings, leg elevation, exercise
- Intervention deferred until ≥3 months postpartum (many improve spontaneously)
- Exception: acute superficial thrombophlebitis or hemorrhage requires treatment

### Pelvic Congestion Syndrome

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Pelvic venous reflux (ovarian vein or internal iliac vein incompetence) can cause varicose veins in the vulvar, perineal, and medial thigh regions — often atypical distribution not explained by SFJ/SPJ reflux. Suspect when:
- Atypical distribution (vulvar, gluteal, posterior thigh)
- Chronic pelvic pain with varicosities
- Recurrence despite adequate superficial treatment

Diagnosis: transvaginal/transabdominal duplex, CT/MR venography, selective venography. Treatment: ovarian vein embolization (coils/sclerosant).

### Klippel-Trenaunay Syndrome

🔵 **Triad:** Port-wine stain + varicose veins + limb hypertrophy (bone and soft tissue). Congenital. The lateral marginal vein (persistent embryonic vein) is pathognomonic. Deep venous abnormalities are common — detailed imaging mandatory before any superficial vein treatment.

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Varicose veins** = dilated, tortuous superficial veins ≥3 mm from valvular incompetence/wall weakness. Affect up to 30–40% of adults.
2. **The calf muscle pump** is the most important mechanism of venous return; valvular incompetence renders it inefficient.
3. **CEAP classification** is the international standard. C2 = varicose veins; C6 = active ulcer.
4. **Duplex ultrasound is mandatory** before any intervention — confirms reflux pattern, deep vein patency, and anatomy.
5. **Trendelenburg test** confirms SFJ incompetence; **Perthes test** confirms deep vein patency — both still important for exams.
6. **Endovenous thermal ablation (EVLA/RFA)** is the first-line treatment; surgery is third-line (NICE guidelines).
7. **Venous ulcers** occur at the medial gaiter area; always check ABPI before compression.
8. **GSV stripping is groin-to-knee only** — protects the saphenous nerve and preserves vein for future bypass.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. **Ambulatory venous pressure** is the key hemodynamic parameter — sustained elevation (>40 mmHg) drives CVI.
10. **CLASS trial:** EVLA and surgery equivalent at 5 years; UGFS has higher recurrence.
11. **EVRA trial:** Early endovenous ablation accelerates venous ulcer healing by 12 weeks — don't wait.
12. **Neovascularization** at the groin is the commonest cause of recurrence after surgery — endovenous techniques avoid this.
13. **Non-thermal techniques (VenaSeal, ClariVein)** spare the tumescent anesthesia step and reduce nerve injury risk; long-term data still maturing.
14. **Pelvic venous reflux** should be considered in atypical or recurrent varicose veins, especially in women.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

15. **Two-disease concept in varicose veins too:** Primary wall abnormality (genetic, structural) may precede valve failure — not the other way around.
16. **IVUS** is the gold standard for iliac vein compression (May-Thurner); CT/MR venography misses ~50% of non-thrombotic lesions.
17. **FOXC2 and PIEZO1** gene variants link varicose veins to fundamental venous wall biology.
18. **Marjolin's ulcer:** Biopsy any chronic venous ulcer >10 years to exclude SCC.
19. **ESCHAR trial:** Superficial surgery alone (without perforator treatment) reduced ulcer recurrence from 56% to 31%.
