## 1. History — From "Milk Leg" to Modern Thromboprophylaxis

The story of DVT is inseparable from the story of blood coagulation itself. For centuries, postpartum leg swelling was recognized but poorly understood — termed "milk leg" (*phlegmasia alba dolens*) under the mistaken belief that milk was diverted to the affected limb.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1644 | **Schenck** — First description of venous thrombosis of the lower extremity |
| 1846 | **Rudolf Virchow** — Described the triad of stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability; coined "thrombosis" and "embolism" |
| 1856 | Virchow demonstrated that clots from leg veins travel to the lungs — establishing the DVT–PE continuum |
| 1916 | **McLean** — Discovery of heparin (from canine liver extract) |
| 1940s | **Link** — Development of warfarin (from sweet clover disease in cattle) |
| 1960s | Introduction of venography as the gold standard for DVT diagnosis |
| 1980s | Compression ultrasonography replaces venography; LMWH developed |
| 2010s | Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) revolutionize treatment |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.24*

Virchow's triad, formulated over 170 years ago, remains the conceptual framework through which every case of venous thromboembolism is understood today — a remarkable testament to the power of pathological observation.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology of the Venous System

🟢 **The deep venous system of the lower limb** is organized into three segments — understanding this anatomy is essential for localizing thrombus and predicting clinical consequences:

### Calf (Distal) Veins
- **Paired veins** accompanying each artery: posterior tibial, anterior tibial, peroneal (fibular)
- **Soleal sinusoids** and **gastrocnemius veins** — muscular veins that are common sites of thrombus origin
- Calf muscle pump ("peripheral heart") drives venous return during ambulation

### Popliteal and Femoral Veins
- Popliteal vein: formed by confluence of tibial veins at the knee
- **Femoral vein** (formerly "superficial femoral vein" — a dangerously misleading name, as it is a deep vein)
- 🔵 The Terminologia Anatomica (2001) officially renamed it the "femoral vein" to prevent confusion — yet the old term persists in practice. A thrombus in the "superficial femoral vein" is a **proximal DVT** and carries full PE risk.

### Iliac Veins and IVC
- Common iliac veins converge to form the IVC
- Left common iliac vein crosses beneath the right common iliac artery — the anatomical basis of **May-Thurner syndrome**

🟢 **Venous valves:** Bicuspid valves direct flow centrally and prevent reflux. The valve cusps and sinuses are sites of relative stasis and are where most thrombi originate. The calf has the highest density of valves.

🟢 **The perforating veins** connect superficial to deep systems. Normally, flow is from superficial → deep. Post-thrombotic valve destruction reverses this flow, causing venous hypertension — the basis of post-thrombotic syndrome.

🔵 **Venous return physiology:**
- Calf muscle pump generates pressures of 200–300 mmHg during walking
- Valvular competence reduces ambulatory venous pressure from ~90 mmHg (standing) to ~20 mmHg
- Loss of this mechanism (valve destruction, immobility) → sustained venous hypertension → tissue damage

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.26*

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The soleal sinusoids — large, thin-walled venous reservoirs within the soleus muscle — are the most common site of initial thrombus formation. They lack valves and depend entirely on muscle contraction for emptying. This explains why immobilized patients (postoperative, paralyzed, long-haul flights) are so vulnerable — these sinusoids become stagnant blood pools.

## 3. Pathophysiology — Virchow's Triad in Detail

🟢 **Virchow's Triad** — the three pillars of thrombogenesis:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│            VIRCHOW'S TRIAD                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                             │
│  1. VENOUS STASIS                           │
│     • Immobilization, paralysis             │
│     • Long surgery, bed rest                │
│     • Venous obstruction (tumor, pregnancy) │
│     • Heart failure                         │
│                                             │
│  2. ENDOTHELIAL INJURY                      │
│     • Surgery / trauma                      │
│     • Central venous catheters              │
│     • Prior DVT                             │
│     • Vasculitis, chemotherapy              │
│                                             │
│  3. HYPERCOAGULABILITY                      │
│     • Inherited: Factor V Leiden, Protein   │
│       C/S deficiency, AT-III deficiency,    │
│       Prothrombin G20210A mutation           │
│     • Acquired: Malignancy, pregnancy, OCP, │
│       antiphospholipid syndrome, HIT,       │
│       nephrotic syndrome, myeloproliferative │
│       disorders                             │
│                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Coagulation Cascade at the Valve Cusp

🔵 **Thrombus initiation:** Stasis in valve pockets activates endothelial cells → expression of tissue factor (TF) and P-selectin → recruitment of monocytes and platelets → thrombin generation → fibrin mesh formation. The initial "red thrombus" is fibrin- and RBC-rich (distinct from the platelet-rich "white thrombus" of arterial disease).

🔵 **Thrombus propagation vs. resolution:**
- Propagation: thrombus extends proximally (toward heart); the risk of PE increases as clot reaches proximal veins
- Resolution: endogenous fibrinolysis (plasmin) + inflammatory remodeling. Complete lysis occurs in only ~50% — the remainder undergo organization, recanalization, and valve scarring → **post-thrombotic syndrome**

🔴 **SS Pearl — NETs (Neutrophil Extracellular Traps):** Recent research has identified neutrophil extracellular traps — webs of DNA, histones, and proteases expelled by activated neutrophils — as key scaffolds for venous thrombus formation. NETs bind platelets, activate factor XII, and trap RBCs. This "immunothrombosis" pathway is increasingly recognized as a therapeutic target and explains the link between infection/inflammation (including COVID-19) and VTE.

## 4. Risk Factors

🟢 **Major risk factors for DVT** — classify by Virchow's triad:

| Category | Risk Factors | Relative Risk |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Surgery** | Major orthopedic (hip/knee), abdominal, pelvic, neurosurgery | 5–50× |
| **Trauma** | Major trauma, spinal cord injury, lower limb fractures | 5–50× |
| **Immobilization** | Hospitalization, bed rest >3 days, long-haul travel (>6 hrs) | 2–10× |
| **Malignancy** | Active cancer (especially pancreatic, brain, lung, ovarian) | 4–7× |
| **Hormonal** | OCP/HRT (especially 3rd-gen progestins), pregnancy/puerperium | 3–8× |
| **Thrombophilia** | Factor V Leiden (hetero 3–8×; homo 50–80×), Prothrombin mutation, AT-III/Protein C/S deficiency | Variable |
| **Prior VTE** | Recurrence risk 25% at 5 years | 2–9× |
| **Other** | Obesity (BMI >30), age >60, CHF, nephrotic syndrome, IBD, varicose veins, central lines | 1.5–5× |

🔵 **Thrombophilia — when to test:**
- First unprovoked DVT **at age <50**
- Recurrent VTE
- VTE at unusual sites (cerebral, mesenteric, portal)
- Strong family history (≥2 first-degree relatives)
- Warfarin-induced skin necrosis (suspect Protein C deficiency)
- Neonatal purpura fulminans (homozygous Protein C/S deficiency)

🔵 **Do NOT test during acute thrombosis or while on anticoagulation** — results are unreliable (AT-III consumed in acute event; warfarin lowers Protein C/S; DOACs affect lupus anticoagulant assays). Test 2–4 weeks after stopping anticoagulation.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Trousseau's Syndrome:** Migratory superficial thrombophlebitis or recurrent VTE as the presenting manifestation of occult malignancy — most commonly pancreatic, lung, or ovarian cancer. The mechanism involves tumor-derived tissue factor–bearing microparticles and cancer procoagulant (a cysteine protease that directly activates factor X). An unprovoked DVT in a patient >40 should prompt age-appropriate cancer screening.

## 5. Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms and Signs

🟢 **Classic presentation of lower limb DVT:**
- Unilateral leg swelling (most sensitive sign)
- Pain/tenderness along the course of the deep veins
- Warmth and erythema
- Distention of superficial veins
- Low-grade fever

🟢 **Homan's sign** (calf pain on passive dorsiflexion of the foot) — historically taught but **unreliable**: sensitivity only 10–54%, poor specificity, and risk of dislodging thrombus. Not recommended as a diagnostic maneuver.

🔵 **Clinical clues by location:**

| Location | Features |
|----------|----------|
| **Calf DVT** | Posterior calf pain/tenderness; mild swelling; often asymptomatic |
| **Iliofemoral DVT** | Entire leg swollen; thigh pain; may cause significant hemodynamic compromise |
| **IVC thrombosis** | Bilateral leg swelling; flank/back pain; renal vein extension → nephrotic-range proteinuria |

### Limb-Threatening Presentations

🟢 **Phlegmasia alba dolens** ("white painful leg"):
- Massive iliofemoral DVT with arterial spasm → pale, swollen, painful leg
- Peripheral pulses may be diminished but present
- Precursor to phlegmasia cerulea dolens

🟢 **Phlegmasia cerulea dolens** ("blue painful leg"):
- Near-total venous occlusion → massive edema → venous gangrene
- Cyanotic, tensely swollen, exquisitely painful leg
- Compartment syndrome may develop; arterial inflow compromised by massive venous pressure
- Without urgent intervention: **venous gangrene → amputation** (25–50% of cases)
- 🔵 Treatment: **emergent anticoagulation + catheter-directed thrombolysis or surgical thrombectomy + fasciotomy** as needed

### Differential Diagnosis

🟢 **The differential is broad — up to 50% of clinically suspected DVTs are not confirmed on imaging:**

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Ruptured Baker's cyst** | History of knee pathology; crescent sign (ecchymosis around malleolus) |
| **Cellulitis** | Skin changes more prominent; portal of entry; systemic toxicity |
| **Muscle tear/hematoma** | Acute onset with activity; localized; ecchymosis |
| **Lymphedema** | Non-pitting; chronic; no pain |
| **Superficial thrombophlebitis** | Palpable cord along superficial vein; localized tenderness |
| **Chronic venous insufficiency** | Bilateral; skin changes (lipodermatosclerosis, stasis dermatitis); varicosities |
| **Compartment syndrome** | Pain out of proportion; tense compartment; pain on passive stretch |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.279*

## 6. Diagnosis

### Clinical Probability Assessment

🟢 **Wells Score for DVT** — the first step in every suspected DVT:

| Criterion | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| Active cancer (treatment within 6 months or palliative) | +1 |
| Paralysis/paresis or recent cast of lower extremity | +1 |
| Bedridden >3 days or major surgery within 12 weeks | +1 |
| Localized tenderness along distribution of deep veins | +1 |
| Entire leg swollen | +1 |
| Calf swelling >3 cm compared to asymptomatic side (measured 10 cm below tibial tuberosity) | +1 |
| Pitting edema (greater in symptomatic leg) | +1 |
| Collateral superficial veins (non-varicose) | +1 |
| Previously documented DVT | +1 |
| Alternative diagnosis at least as likely | −2 |

**Interpretation:**
- **≤0:** Low probability (5% prevalence)
- **1–2:** Moderate probability (17%)
- **≥3:** High probability (53%)

🔵 **Modified Wells (two-tier):** Score ≤1 = "DVT unlikely"; ≥2 = "DVT likely." The two-tier model is preferred in current guidelines (NICE, ASH).

### D-Dimer

🟢 **D-dimer** — a fibrin degradation product — is the pivotal blood test:
- **High sensitivity (~95%), low specificity (~50%)**
- Useful to **rule out** DVT in low/moderate probability patients
- A negative D-dimer + low Wells score = DVT effectively excluded (NPV >99%)
- **Not useful when positive** — elevated in pregnancy, malignancy, infection, postoperative states, elderly, trauma, DIC

🔵 **Age-adjusted D-dimer cutoff:** For patients >50 years, using **(age × 10 µg/L)** as the threshold instead of the standard 500 µg/L increases specificity by ~25% without sacrificing sensitivity. Endorsed by NICE and ASH guidelines.

### Imaging

🟢 **Compression ultrasonography (CUS)** — the diagnostic standard:
- **Proximal DVT:** Sensitivity 94–97%, Specificity 94–98%
- **Technique:** 2-point compression (common femoral vein at groin + popliteal vein at popliteal fossa). The vein should completely collapse under probe pressure. Failure to compress = thrombus.
- **Whole-leg CUS:** Extends to include calf veins. Higher sensitivity for distal DVT but lower specificity (false positives from Baker's cysts, hematomas)
- If 2-point CUS is negative but clinical suspicion remains → repeat in **5–7 days** (to catch propagating calf thrombus)

🔵 **Additional ultrasound features:**
- Intraluminal echogenicity (visible thrombus)
- Loss of normal respiratory phasicity and augmentation on Doppler
- Acute thrombus: distended vein, hypoechoic clot; chronic thrombus: shrunken vein, echogenic clot, collaterals

🔵 **Other imaging modalities:**

| Modality | Role |
|----------|------|
| **CT venography** | Useful combined with CTPA for PE workup; iliac/IVC thrombus; poor for calf veins |
| **MR venography** | Pelvic/IVC thrombi; pregnancy (no radiation); chronic DVT evaluation |
| **Ascending venography** | Historical gold standard; invasive; now reserved for interventional procedures |
| **Intravascular ultrasound (IVUS)** | 🔴 Gold standard for iliac vein assessment; essential in May-Thurner evaluation before stenting |

### Diagnostic Algorithm

🟢 **Standard approach (NICE/ASH/ACCP):**

```
Suspected DVT
    │
    ▼
Wells Score
    │
    ├─ DVT unlikely (≤1) → D-dimer
    │       │
    │       ├─ Negative → DVT excluded
    │       └─ Positive → Proximal CUS
    │               │
    │               ├─ Positive → Treat
    │               └─ Negative → Repeat CUS in 5-7 days
    │                              OR whole-leg CUS
    │
    └─ DVT likely (≥2) → Proximal CUS
            │
            ├─ Positive → Treat
            └─ Negative → D-dimer
                    │
                    ├─ Negative → DVT excluded
                    └─ Positive → Repeat CUS in 5-7 days
                                  OR whole-leg CUS / alternative imaging
```

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Anticoagulation

🟢 **Anticoagulation is the cornerstone of DVT treatment.** Begin as soon as DVT is confirmed — or even if clinical probability is high while awaiting imaging.

🟢 **Options for initial anticoagulation:**

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| **LMWH** (enoxaparin) | 1 mg/kg SC q12h or 1.5 mg/kg SC daily | Most widely used; no monitoring needed (except renal failure, pregnancy, extremes of weight) |
| **Fondaparinux** | 5–10 mg SC daily (weight-based) | Alternative to LMWH; preferred in HIT |
| **UFH** | 80 U/kg bolus → 18 U/kg/hr IV infusion (target aPTT 1.5–2.5×) | Reserved for: renal failure, high bleeding risk (short half-life), need for procedures |
| **Rivaroxaban** | 15 mg PO BID × 21 days → 20 mg daily | Single-drug approach — no need for initial parenteral |
| **Apixaban** | 10 mg PO BID × 7 days → 5 mg BID | Single-drug approach — no need for initial parenteral |

🔵 **Key principle:** Rivaroxaban and apixaban can be used as **single-drug regimens** (no initial heparin bridge needed). Edoxaban and dabigatran **require ≥5 days of initial parenteral anticoagulation** before switching.

### Long-Term Anticoagulation

🟢 **Duration of anticoagulation — the critical decision:**

| Scenario | Minimum Duration | Extended? |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Provoked DVT** (surgery, trauma, immobilization) | **3 months** | No — risk factor removed |
| **First unprovoked DVT** | **3–6 months** | Consider indefinite if low bleeding risk |
| **Recurrent unprovoked DVT** | **Indefinite** | Yes — high recurrence risk (>30% at 5 yrs) |
| **Cancer-associated DVT** | **Minimum 6 months** | Continue while cancer active; LMWH or DOACs preferred |
| **Inherited thrombophilia + unprovoked** | **Consider indefinite** | Especially homozygous FVL, AT-III deficiency, combined defects |

🔵 **DOACs vs. Warfarin — the evidence:**
- **EINSTEIN (rivaroxaban)**, **AMPLIFY (apixaban)**, **HOKUSAI (edoxaban)**, **RE-COVER (dabigatran)** — all showed noninferior efficacy with lower bleeding rates (especially apixaban)
- **ASH 2020 and ACCP 2024 guidelines:** DOACs preferred over warfarin for most patients with VTE
- **Exceptions favoring warfarin:** Antiphospholipid syndrome (DOACs inferior — TRAPS trial), severe renal impairment (CrCl <15–30 mL/min), mechanical heart valves

🔵 **Cancer-associated VTE:**
- Historically LMWH was standard (CLOT trial)
- SELECT-D and Hokusai-VTE Cancer trials: edoxaban and rivaroxaban noninferior to LMWH
- **Caution:** DOACs increase GI bleeding risk with GI/GU cancers — LMWH preferred in these tumors
- ACCP 2024: DOACs for cancer VTE EXCEPT high GI bleeding risk cancers

### Compression Therapy

🔵 **Elastic compression stockings (ECS):**
- Historically recommended for 2 years to prevent PTS
- **SOX trial (2014):** Placebo-controlled RCT showed **no benefit** of ECS in preventing PTS
- **Current recommendation:** Routine use of ECS for PTS prevention is NOT recommended (ASH 2020)
- ECS remain useful for **symptomatic relief** of acute leg swelling

### Thrombolysis and Interventional Approaches

🔵 **Catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT):**

**ATTRACT trial (2017)** — the landmark RCT:
- 692 patients with proximal DVT randomized to CDT + anticoagulation vs. anticoagulation alone
- **Primary endpoint (PTS at 24 months):** No significant difference (47% vs. 48%)
- **However:** CDT reduced moderate-to-severe PTS in the **iliofemoral subgroup** (18% vs. 28%)
- **Major bleeding:** Higher with CDT (1.7% vs. 0.3%)

🔵 **Current indications for CDT/pharmacomechanical thrombolysis:**
- **Phlegmasia cerulea dolens** (limb-threatening)
- Acute iliofemoral DVT with **severe symptoms** and **low bleeding risk**
- Symptom duration **<14 days** (for best outcomes)

🔴 **SS Pearl — May-Thurner Syndrome:** Left common iliac vein compression by the right common iliac artery accounts for 2–5% of lower extremity DVTs. Suspect in: young women, left iliofemoral DVT, recurrent left leg DVT despite anticoagulation. Diagnosis: IVUS (gold standard) or MRV/CTV. Treatment: **CDT + iliac vein stenting** — stent patency rates ~90% at 2 years. Anticoagulation alone has high recurrence due to the uncorrected anatomical compression.

### Surgical Thrombectomy

🔵 **Rarely performed in the DOAC era.** Indications:
- Phlegmasia cerulea dolens when CDT is unavailable or has failed
- Contraindication to thrombolysis with limb-threatening DVT
- **Technique:** Femoral venotomy → Fogarty balloon catheter thrombectomy → creation of temporary AV fistula (improves patency) → fasciotomy if compartment syndrome

### IVC Filters

🟢 **Indications for IVC filter:**
- **Absolute:** Proximal DVT/PE with **absolute contraindication to anticoagulation** (active hemorrhage, recent neurosurgery/hemorrhagic stroke)
- **Relative:** Recurrent PE despite adequate anticoagulation; massive PE with hemodynamic compromise and residual DVT; free-floating iliocaval thrombus

🟢 **Retrievable filters are preferred** — should be removed once anticoagulation can be safely resumed (ideally within 3–6 months)

🔵 **PREPIC-2 trial (2015):** Retrievable IVC filter + anticoagulation vs. anticoagulation alone in severe PE with proximal DVT — **no benefit** of filter addition. Filters do NOT reduce PE mortality when adequate anticoagulation is given.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Long-term IVC filter complications include IVC thrombosis (up to 30%), filter migration, perforation of IVC wall, and — paradoxically — increased risk of DVT. The FDA issued a safety advisory in 2010 recommending filter retrieval as soon as the indication resolves. Despite this, retrieval rates remain disappointingly low (~30%).

## 8. Special Situations

### DVT in Pregnancy

🟢 **Epidemiology:** VTE complicates 1 in 1,000 pregnancies. Risk is **5× higher** than nonpregnant state — highest in the **postpartum period** (first 6 weeks).

🟢 **Diagnosis:**
- D-dimer rises physiologically throughout pregnancy → **unreliable** (but a negative D-dimer in the first trimester still has value)
- **CUS is first-line.** If iliac DVT suspected (swollen entire leg, left > right) and CUS negative → **MRV** (no gadolinium)
- 🔵 Left leg DVT predominates in pregnancy (~90%) due to right iliac artery compression of the left iliac vein (exaggerated by gravid uterus)

🟢 **Treatment:**
- **LMWH is the drug of choice** (does not cross placenta)
- DOACs are **contraindicated** in pregnancy (teratogenic in animal studies; insufficient human data)
- Warfarin is **contraindicated** in the first trimester (warfarin embryopathy) and near delivery (fetal hemorrhage); may be used in 2nd trimester if necessary
- Switch to UFH infusion near delivery (shorter half-life; reversible with protamine)
- Resume anticoagulation postpartum for minimum **6 weeks** (total treatment ≥3 months)

### Upper Extremity DVT (UEDVT)

🔵 **Accounts for 4–10% of all DVTs.** Two types:
- **Primary (effort thrombosis / Paget-Schroetter syndrome):** Young, athletic patients; repetitive overhead activity compresses subclavian vein at thoracic outlet → intimal injury → thrombosis
- **Secondary:** Central venous catheters (most common cause overall), pacemaker leads, malignancy

🔵 **Treatment:**
- Anticoagulation (same principles as lower extremity)
- **Paget-Schroetter:** CDT acutely → **first rib resection** (thoracic outlet decompression) within 2–4 weeks → 3 months anticoagulation
- Catheter-related: remove catheter if possible; anticoagulate while catheter in situ if still needed

🔴 **SS Pearl:** PE risk from UEDVT is lower than lower extremity DVT (~6% vs. ~15–32%) but is not negligible. PTS of the upper extremity occurs in up to 30%.

### Isolated Distal (Calf) DVT

🔵 **The controversy:**
- Risk of propagation to proximal veins: **15–25%** (usually within 1–2 weeks)
- Risk of PE from isolated calf DVT: very low (<1% clinically significant PE)
- **Two approaches:**
  1. **Serial CUS** at 1 and 2 weeks — anticoagulate only if propagation occurs (preferred if low-risk, minimal symptoms)
  2. **Anticoagulate** for 3 months (preferred if severe symptoms, hospitalized, cancer, prior VTE, no reversible risk factor)
- ACCP 2024: suggests anticoagulation over surveillance for symptomatic distal DVT

## 9. Complications

### Pulmonary Embolism

🟢 **DVT and PE are a single disease — venous thromboembolism (VTE).**
- ~50% of patients with proximal DVT have asymptomatic PE on imaging
- ~70% of patients presenting with PE have DVT on ultrasound
- PE risk is highest with **iliofemoral DVT** and **free-floating thrombus**
- Calf DVT rarely causes clinically significant PE unless it propagates proximally

### Post-Thrombotic Syndrome (PTS)

🟢 **The most common long-term complication of DVT — affects 20–50% of patients within 2 years.**

**Pathophysiology:**
- Thrombus-mediated valve destruction → venous reflux
- Residual luminal obstruction → venous outflow impairment
- Combined reflux + obstruction → sustained ambulatory venous hypertension
- → Edema, pain, skin changes, ulceration

🟢 **Villalta Score** — the standard tool for diagnosis and severity grading:

| Component | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **5 symptoms** (scored 0–3 each): Pain, cramping, heaviness, pruritus, paresthesia | Patient-reported |
| **6 signs** (scored 0–3 each): Edema, skin induration, hyperpigmentation, venous ectasia, redness, pain on calf compression | Clinician-assessed |
| **Score ≥5** = PTS; **≥15 or venous ulcer** = Severe PTS | |

🔵 **Prevention of PTS:**
- **Adequate anticoagulation** with rapid therapeutic levels — the most important modifiable factor
- Early ambulation (bed rest does NOT reduce PE risk and worsens PTS)
- ECS: no proven benefit (SOX trial) for routine use
- CDT: may reduce severe PTS in iliofemoral DVT (ATTRACT subgroup)

🔵 **Treatment of established PTS:**
- Compression therapy (30–40 mmHg graduated stockings)
- Exercise rehabilitation programs (improve calf muscle pump function)
- Venous ulcers: compression + wound care; pentoxifylline or sulodexide as adjuncts
- 🔴 Endovascular stenting of iliac vein obstruction in severe PTS — emerging evidence with good ulcer healing rates

### Recurrent VTE

🔵 **Recurrence rates:**
- Provoked (transient risk factor): 3% per year after stopping anticoagulation
- Unprovoked: 10% per year after stopping
- Cancer-associated: 15–20% per year even on treatment
- Male sex and elevated D-dimer after stopping anticoagulation are independent risk factors for recurrence

## 10. Prophylaxis

🟢 **VTE prophylaxis is one of the most impactful interventions in hospital medicine.** Hospital-acquired VTE is the #1 preventable cause of hospital death.

### Risk Assessment

🟢 **Caprini Score** — the most widely validated surgical risk assessment tool:

| Risk Level | Caprini Score | Prophylaxis |
|------------|--------------|-------------|
| Very low | 0 | Early ambulation only |
| Low | 1–2 | Mechanical (IPC/GCS) |
| Moderate | 3–4 | Pharmacologic (LMWH/UFH) ± mechanical |
| High | ≥5 | Pharmacologic + mechanical |

🟢 **Pharmacologic options:**
- **LMWH:** Enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily (most common)
- **UFH:** 5000 U SC q8–12h
- **Fondaparinux:** 2.5 mg SC daily
- **DOACs:** Rivaroxaban 10 mg daily (approved for post-orthopedic prophylaxis)

🟢 **Mechanical prophylaxis:**
- Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) — first-line when anticoagulation is contraindicated
- Graduated compression stockings (GCS) — modest benefit; less effective than IPC
- Early ambulation — universal recommendation

🔵 **Specific surgical populations:**

| Surgery | Prophylaxis | Duration |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Major orthopedic** (THR, TKR, hip fracture) | LMWH or DOAC | **35 days** (extended prophylaxis) |
| **Major abdominal/pelvic cancer surgery** | LMWH | **28 days** (extended prophylaxis — ENOXACAN II) |
| **General surgery (moderate risk)** | LMWH or IPC | Until discharge |
| **Neurosurgery** | IPC ± LMWH (after hemostasis) | Until discharge |
| **Bariatric surgery** | LMWH (higher dose may be needed) | Until discharge + consider extension |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The PREVENT trial (2023) showed that low-dose rivaroxaban (10 mg daily) in medically ill patients for 45 days post-discharge reduced VTE by 44% compared to placebo — but increased major bleeding. This exemplifies the persistent tension between efficacy and safety in extended thromboprophylaxis for medical patients.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.64; ACCP Guidelines (Chest 2024)*

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **DVT and PE are a single disease — VTE.** Proximal DVT carries ~50% risk of concurrent (often silent) PE.
2. **Virchow's Triad:** Stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability — the universal framework for understanding VTE.
3. **Wells Score + D-dimer** is the first-line approach. A low Wells score with a negative D-dimer effectively excludes DVT (NPV >99%).
4. **Compression ultrasonography** is the imaging standard — failure of the vein to compress = DVT.
5. **Anticoagulation is the cornerstone** of treatment. DOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban) are preferred for most patients.
6. **Duration:** Provoked → 3 months; unprovoked → consider indefinite; cancer → continue while active.
7. **Phlegmasia cerulea dolens** is a surgical/interventional emergency — do not treat with anticoagulation alone.
8. **VTE prophylaxis** (Caprini score + pharmacologic/mechanical measures) is the #1 preventable cause of hospital death addressed.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. DOACs are superior to warfarin for most VTE (better safety profile, no monitoring). Exception: antiphospholipid syndrome (use warfarin).
10. Age-adjusted D-dimer (age × 10 µg/L for patients >50) increases specificity without losing sensitivity.
11. **ATTRACT trial:** CDT did not reduce overall PTS — but reduced moderate-to-severe PTS in the iliofemoral subgroup.
12. **SOX trial:** Compression stockings do NOT prevent PTS — debunking decades of practice.
13. **Cancer VTE:** DOACs noninferior to LMWH EXCEPT in GI/GU cancers (higher GI bleeding).
14. **IVC filters:** No mortality benefit when anticoagulation is possible (PREPIC-2). Use only when anticoagulation is absolutely contraindicated; retrieve promptly.
15. Extended thromboprophylaxis: 35 days for major orthopedic; 28 days for abdominal/pelvic cancer surgery.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

16. **NETs (Neutrophil Extracellular Traps)** are key scaffolds in venous thrombus formation — a paradigm shift in understanding thromboinflammation.
17. **May-Thurner syndrome** — left iliac vein compression by right iliac artery. Think of it in young women with left iliofemoral DVT. IVUS + stenting is definitive treatment.
18. **Paget-Schroetter syndrome** — effort thrombosis of the subclavian vein. CDT → first rib resection → anticoagulation.
19. **Trousseau's syndrome** — migratory thrombophlebitis as harbinger of occult malignancy (especially pancreatic).
20. **Soleal sinusoids** — valve-less venous reservoirs in the soleus muscle — are the origin of most venous thrombi during immobilization.
