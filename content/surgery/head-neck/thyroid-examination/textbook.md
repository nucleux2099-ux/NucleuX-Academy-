## Anatomical Basis of Examination

### Surface Anatomy

The thyroid gland is located in the anterior neck, at the level of C5-T1 vertebrae. It consists of two lateral lobes connected by an isthmus that crosses the 2nd-4th tracheal rings.

**Normal dimensions:**
- Each lobe: 4 × 2 × 2 cm
- Weight: 15-25 g
- The gland is larger in females and enlarges during menstruation and pregnancy

### Key Anatomical Relationships

| Structure | Relationship |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Pretracheal fascia** | Invests the gland; responsible for movement with swallowing |
| **Strap muscles** | Sternohyoid and sternothyroid lie anteriorly |
| **Carotid sheath** | Lies posterolaterally |
| **Recurrent laryngeal nerve** | Runs in tracheo-esophageal groove |
| **External laryngeal nerve** | Near superior thyroid artery |
| **Parathyroid glands** | On posterior surface |
| **Trachea** | Isthmus lies over rings 2-4 |
| **Esophagus** | Posterior to trachea |

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 50, p.805*

### Why Thyroid Moves with Swallowing

The thyroid gland is invested by the pretracheal layer of deep cervical fascia. This fascia is attached to the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage and the cricoid cartilage. During swallowing, the larynx elevates, carrying the pretracheal fascia and the attached thyroid gland upward.

This movement is **pathognomonic** for a thyroid swelling and distinguishes it from other neck masses.

## Systematic Examination Approach

### Preparation

1. **Consent:** Explain procedure to patient
2. **Position:** Patient seated comfortably, neck exposed
3. **Lighting:** Adequate illumination, preferably from the side
4. **Water:** Glass of water for swallowing test

### General Inspection

Observe from the end of the bed:

**Body habitus:**
- Thin, restless, agitated → Thyrotoxicosis
- Obese, slow, lethargic → Hypothyroidism

**Facial features:**
- Anxious, staring → Thyrotoxicosis
- Puffy, coarse, pale → Myxedema

**Obvious neck swelling:**
- Visible from a distance indicates significant enlargement

📚 *Talley & O'Connor, Clinical Examination, 8th Ed*

### Local Inspection of Neck

Examine the neck systematically:

**Location:**
- Midline (isthmus, pyramidal lobe, thyroglossal cyst)
- Lateral (lobe enlargement, solitary nodule)
- Diffuse (entire gland enlarged)

**Size classification:**
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | Not palpable |
| 1a | Palpable but not visible |
| 1b | Palpable and visible with neck extended |
| 2 | Visible with neck in normal position |
| 3 | Large goitre visible from a distance |

**Skin changes:**
- Scars (previous surgery)
- Redness (inflammation)
- Dilated veins (retrosternal extension with SVC compression)

**Movement with swallowing:**
- Provide water, ask patient to hold in mouth then swallow
- Thyroid moves UP with swallowing
- Confirms thyroid origin

**Movement with tongue protrusion:**
- Ask patient to protrude tongue
- Thyroglossal cyst moves UP (attached to foramen cecum via hyoid)
- Thyroid does NOT move with tongue protrusion

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 50, p.810*

## Palpation

### Position
The examiner should stand **behind** the seated patient. This allows bimanual palpation of both lobes and isthmus.

### Technique

**Temperature:** 
Feel with dorsum of hand. Warm thyroid suggests:
- Thyrotoxicosis (increased vascularity)
- Thyroiditis (inflammation)

**Systematic palpation:**
1. **Isthmus:** Palpate over trachea, inferior to cricoid
2. **Right lobe:** Displace trachea to right, palpate lobe
3. **Left lobe:** Displace trachea to left, palpate lobe
4. **Inferior poles:** Important to assess lower extent

**Characteristics to assess:**

| Feature | Options | Significance |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| **Size** | Small, moderate, large | Staging |
| **Shape** | Diffuse, nodular, solitary | Etiology |
| **Surface** | Smooth, nodular, irregular | Smooth=benign, irregular=?malignant |
| **Consistency** | Soft, firm, hard, variable | Hard=?malignant |
| **Tenderness** | Present/absent | Thyroiditis, hemorrhage |
| **Mobility** | Mobile, fixed | Fixed=?malignant invasion |
| **Pulsatility** | Present/absent | Toxic goitre |

**Movement with swallowing:**
While palpating, ask patient to swallow. Confirms thyroid attachment.

**Lower border:**
- Can you get below the swelling?
- If NO → Retrosternal extension

**Tracheal position:**
- Palpate in suprasternal notch
- Deviation away from swelling = space-occupying
- Deviation toward swelling = fibrosis/malignancy

📚 *Browse's Introduction to Clinical Surgery, 5th Ed*

## Special Tests

### Pemberton's Test

**Indication:** Suspected retrosternal goitre

**Technique:**
1. Patient raises both arms above head
2. Hold for 1-2 minutes

**Positive findings:**
- Facial plethora/congestion
- Cyanosis
- Distended neck veins
- Stridor

**Significance:** Retrosternal goitre causing thoracic inlet obstruction when arms raised (compresses thoracic inlet further)

### Berry's Sign

**Technique:** Palpate carotid artery lateral to thyroid mass

**Positive:** Carotid pulsation not palpable (engulfed by tumor)

**Significance:** Highly suggestive of anaplastic carcinoma or advanced thyroid malignancy

### Kocher's Test

**Technique:** Compress carotid artery against thyroid mass

**Positive:** Dilation of ipsilateral pupil

**Significance:** Suggests malignant invasion of sympathetic chain

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 50, p.812*

## Percussion

Percuss over the manubrium sterni from below upward.

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| Resonant | Normal |
| Dull | Retrosternal extension of goitre |

---

## Auscultation

Place stethoscope over each thyroid lobe. Ask patient to hold breath (eliminates breath sounds).

**Bruit (vascular murmur):**
- Indicates hypervascular gland
- Present in thyrotoxicosis (Graves' disease)
- Distinguish from transmitted carotid sounds

## Thyroid Status Assessment

Determining whether the patient is euthyroid, hyperthyroid, or hypothyroid is essential.

### Hyperthyroidism Features

**General:**
- Anxious, restless
- Heat intolerance
- Weight loss despite good appetite

**Hands:**
- Fine tremor (place paper on outstretched hands)
- Warm, sweaty palms
- Thyroid acropachy (rare, in Graves')
- Onycholysis

**Pulse:**
- Tachycardia
- Atrial fibrillation
- Bounding pulse
- Wide pulse pressure

**Eyes (especially Graves' disease):**
- Lid retraction (Dalrymple's sign) — sclera visible above iris at rest
- Lid lag (von Graefe's sign) — sclera appears on downward gaze
- Exophthalmos/Proptosis — protrusion of eyeballs
- Chemosis — conjunctival edema
- Ophthalmoplegia — limitation of eye movements
- Periorbital edema

**Reflexes:**
- Brisk

**Skin:**
- Pretibial myxedema (Graves' disease)

### Hypothyroidism Features

**General:**
- Slow, lethargic
- Cold intolerance
- Weight gain

**Face:**
- Puffy, coarse features
- Pale, dry skin
- Loss of outer third of eyebrows
- Large tongue

**Hands:**
- Cold, dry
- Carpal tunnel syndrome

**Pulse:**
- Bradycardia

**Reflexes:**
- Slow relaxing (best seen at ankle)

**Voice:**
- Hoarse (vocal cord edema)

📚 *Talley & O'Connor, Clinical Examination, 8th Ed*

## Lymph Node Examination

**Essential component** — Thyroid cancer (especially papillary) spreads early to cervical lymph nodes.

**Examination sequence:**
1. Submental
2. Submandibular
3. Pre-auricular
4. Post-auricular
5. Occipital
6. Anterior cervical (jugulodigastric, jugular chain)
7. Posterior cervical
8. Supraclavicular (Virchow's node)

**Findings suggesting malignancy:**
- Hard, fixed nodes
- Matted nodes
- Rapid growth
- Associated with hard thyroid nodule

## Differential Diagnosis of Neck Swellings

| Swelling | Moves with Swallowing | Moves with Tongue Protrusion | Other Features |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------------|----------------|
| Thyroid | Yes | No | Attached to pretracheal fascia |
| Thyroglossal cyst | Yes | Yes | Midline, attached to hyoid |
| Lymph node | No | No | Mobile, multiple possible |
| Laryngocele | No | No | Increases with Valsalva |
| Branchial cyst | No | No | Anterior to SCM, upper neck |
| Dermoid cyst | No | No | Midline, tethered to skin |

## Investigations to Request

After clinical examination, suggest appropriate investigations:

1. **Thyroid Function Tests:** TSH, Free T4, Free T3
2. **Ultrasound neck:** Size, nodules, lymph nodes, vascularity
3. **FNAC:** For suspicious nodules (TI-RADS guided)
4. **Thyroid antibodies:** If autoimmune suspected (TPO, TRAb)
5. **Thyroid scan:** If toxic nodule suspected (I-131 or Tc-99m)
6. **CT/MRI:** For retrosternal extension, malignancy staging
7. **Indirect laryngoscopy:** Vocal cord assessment pre-surgery

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 50, p.815*

## Presenting Your Findings

**Example presentation:**

"This is a 35-year-old female presenting with a swelling in the neck noticed 6 months ago.

On general inspection, the patient appears euthyroid with no obvious distress.

On local inspection, there is a diffuse swelling in the anterior neck that moves with swallowing but not with tongue protrusion, confirming thyroid origin.

On palpation from behind, the swelling involves both lobes and isthmus, is firm in consistency, non-tender, with a smooth surface. I can get below the swelling. The trachea is central.

There is no bruit on auscultation. Pemberton's test is negative.

On assessment of thyroid status, the patient appears euthyroid with normal pulse rate, no tremor, and no eye signs.

There are no palpable cervical lymph nodes.

My clinical impression is a simple multinodular goitre, and I would like to confirm with thyroid function tests and an ultrasound of the neck."

## Key Points Summary

1. Thyroid moves with swallowing (attached to pretracheal fascia)
2. Thyroglossal cyst moves with both swallowing AND tongue protrusion
3. Examine from BEHIND the patient
4. Hard, fixed nodule + lymphadenopathy = suspect malignancy
5. Berry's sign = carotid encased by tumor
6. Pemberton's test = retrosternal extension
7. Always assess thyroid STATUS and LYMPH NODES
8. Complete with investigations: TFT, USG, FNAC
