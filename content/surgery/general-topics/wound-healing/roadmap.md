## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Wound Healing:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Physiology | Homeostasis basics | — | Understanding feedback loops is essential for managing wound healing environments |
| Pathology | Tissue repair & regeneration | PA13.1 | Directly explains the phases of wound healing — hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, remodeling |
| Pathology | Acute inflammation cascade | PA6.1 | The inflammatory phase initiates wound healing; dysregulation leads to chronic wounds |
| Pathology | Chronic inflammation & granulation tissue | PA7.1 | Granulation tissue formation is the hallmark of healing by secondary intention |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Describe the 4 phases of wound healing and factors that impair each phase
- Classify wounds (clean, contaminated, infected) and choose appropriate management
- Identify complications of wound healing — keloid, hypertrophic scar, wound dehiscence

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Connection |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| Pharmacology | Topical antimicrobials for wound care | — | Silver sulfadiazine and antiseptics prevent wound infection that delays healing |
| Microbiology | Biofilm in chronic wounds | — | Biofilm formation is the primary reason chronic wounds fail to heal despite debridement |
| Pathology | Cell injury mechanisms | PA2.1 | Understanding reversible vs irreversible injury guides debridement decisions |

### Extensions

- **Pathology:** Marjolin ulcer in chronic wounds — Long-standing non-healing wounds (burn scars, chronic ulcers) can undergo malignant transformation to SCC

## ➡️ What's Next

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **Surgical Infections** — Natural progression — infected wounds are the main complication
2. **Burns** — Burns represent a major wound type requiring specialized healing knowledge

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Wound healing sits at the intersection of pathology (inflammation, repair), microbiology (infection prevention), and pharmacology (growth factors, antimicrobials). Understanding tissue repair mechanisms from pathology directly predicts surgical outcomes and guides decisions on primary closure vs healing by secondary intention.
