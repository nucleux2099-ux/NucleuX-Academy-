## Definition

Wound healing is a complex, dynamic process by which the body restores tissue integrity following injury. It involves a coordinated sequence of cellular and molecular events that results in reconstitution of tissue structure and function.

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 3, p.24*

## Phases of Wound Healing

Wound healing is classically divided into three overlapping phases:

### Phase 1: Hemostasis and Inflammation (Day 0-4)

#### Hemostasis (Immediate)
Upon tissue injury, the coagulation cascade is activated:
1. **Vascular response:** Initial vasoconstriction (5-10 minutes) followed by vasodilation
2. **Platelet activation:** Platelets adhere to exposed collagen, aggregate, and form a platelet plug
3. **Coagulation cascade:** Fibrin mesh forms, stabilizing the clot
4. **Growth factor release:** Platelets release PDGF, TGF-β, EGF initiating healing

#### Inflammation (Hours to Days)
**Neutrophil phase (24-48 hours):**
- First inflammatory cells to arrive
- Phagocytose bacteria and debris
- Release proteases and reactive oxygen species
- Die after completing task (form pus)

**Macrophage phase (48-96 hours):**
- Derived from circulating monocytes
- **Most important cell in wound healing**
- Functions:
  - Phagocytosis and debridement
  - Antigen presentation
  - Release of cytokines and growth factors
  - Recruitment of fibroblasts
  - Transition to proliferative phase

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 6, p.132-140*

### Phase 2: Proliferation (Day 4-21)

#### Angiogenesis
- Formation of new blood vessels from existing vessels
- Driven by VEGF, FGF released by macrophages
- Provides oxygen and nutrients for healing
- Creates the characteristic red, granular appearance of granulation tissue

#### Fibroplasia
- Fibroblasts migrate into the wound (chemotaxis by PDGF)
- Proliferate and synthesize extracellular matrix
- Produce collagen (initially Type III, later Type I)
- Produce proteoglycans and fibronectin

#### Epithelialization
- Keratinocytes at wound edges proliferate
- Migrate across wound bed (contact inhibition stops when edges meet)
- Requires moist environment and viable wound bed
- Occurs faster in wounds healing by primary intention

#### Wound Contraction
- Myofibroblasts (differentiated fibroblasts) contract
- Reduces wound size by pulling edges together
- More prominent in secondary intention healing
- Can cause contractures if excessive

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 9, p.248-255*

### Phase 3: Remodeling (Day 21 - 1+ years)

The longest phase, characterized by:

**Collagen Remodeling:**
- Type III collagen progressively replaced by Type I
- Collagen fibers become thicker and more organized
- Cross-linking increases tensile strength
- Ratio changes from Type III:I of 3:1 to 1:3

**Strength Gain:**
| Time | Tensile Strength |
|------|------------------|
| 1 week | 3-5% |
| 2 weeks | 10-15% |
| 6 weeks | 50% |
| 3 months | 80% |
| >1 year | 80% (maximum) |

**Scar Maturation:**
- Vascularity decreases (red → pink → pale)
- Scar becomes flatter and softer
- May take 1-2 years to mature fully

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 3, p.28*

## Types of Wound Healing

### Primary Intention (First Intention)
- Wound edges approximated (sutures, staples, adhesive)
- Minimal granulation tissue required
- Rapid healing with minimal scarring
- Example: Clean surgical incision

### Secondary Intention (Second Intention)
- Wound left open
- Heals by granulation, contraction, and epithelialization
- More scarring, longer healing time
- Example: Abscess cavity, large burns

### Tertiary Intention (Delayed Primary Closure)
- Wound initially left open (contaminated or infected)
- Closed primarily once clean (usually day 4-7)
- Combines benefits of both approaches
- Example: Contaminated traumatic wound

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 6, p.141*

## Cellular and Molecular Biology

### Key Growth Factors

| Growth Factor | Source | Function |
|---------------|--------|----------|
| **PDGF** | Platelets, macrophages | Chemotaxis, mitogen for fibroblasts |
| **TGF-β** | Platelets, macrophages | Collagen synthesis, immunomodulation |
| **EGF** | Platelets, macrophages, saliva | Epithelial proliferation |
| **VEGF** | Macrophages, keratinocytes | Angiogenesis |
| **FGF** | Macrophages, fibroblasts | Angiogenesis, fibroblast proliferation |
| **IGF-1** | Liver, local cells | Cell proliferation, protein synthesis |
| **KGF** | Fibroblasts | Keratinocyte proliferation |

### Collagen Synthesis

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the body and critical for wound strength.

**Synthesis pathway:**
1. **Transcription:** Gene activation in fibroblasts
2. **Translation:** Procollagen chains synthesized in rough ER
3. **Hydroxylation:** Proline and lysine residues hydroxylated (requires Vitamin C, Fe²⁺, O₂)
4. **Glycosylation:** Sugar residues added
5. **Triple helix formation:** Three chains form procollagen
6. **Secretion:** Procollagen secreted to extracellular space
7. **Cleavage:** Propeptides removed → tropocollagen
8. **Cross-linking:** Lysyl oxidase creates cross-links (requires copper)

**Key enzyme:** Prolyl hydroxylase
- Requires: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid), Fe²⁺, α-ketoglutarate, O₂
- Deficiency (scurvy): Unstable collagen → impaired healing

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 9, p.250*

## Factors Affecting Wound Healing

### Local Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Infection** | Most common cause of impaired healing; bacteria compete for resources, release toxins |
| **Blood supply** | Ischemia reduces oxygen and nutrient delivery |
| **Foreign bodies** | Persistent inflammatory response |
| **Radiation** | Damages fibroblasts and vasculature |
| **Wound tension** | Excessive tension disrupts healing |
| **Hematoma/Seroma** | Provides medium for infection, separates wound edges |
| **Surgical technique** | Gentle handling, appropriate sutures |
| **Wound environment** | Moist environment optimal |

### Systemic Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Diabetes mellitus** | Microangiopathy, impaired leukocyte function, neuropathy |
| **Malnutrition** | Protein deficiency, vitamin C, zinc, arginine deficiency |
| **Steroids** | Inhibit inflammatory phase, reduce collagen synthesis |
| **Smoking** | Vasoconstriction, CO binding, impaired oxidative killing |
| **Age** | Slower fibroblast proliferation, reduced collagen synthesis |
| **Immunosuppression** | Impaired inflammatory response |
| **Anemia** | Reduced oxygen delivery (though Hb >8 g/dL usually adequate) |
| **Uremia** | Impaired leukocyte function |
| **Jaundice** | Impaired fibroblast function |
| **Malignancy** | Cachexia, immunosuppression |

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 3, p.30-32*

## Abnormal Wound Healing

### Hypertrophic Scar
- Raised, red scar **within** original wound boundaries
- Develops within 1-2 months
- **Regresses spontaneously** over 12-18 months
- More common in areas of tension

### Keloid
- Raised scar **beyond** original wound boundaries
- May develop months after injury
- **Does NOT regress spontaneously**
- High recurrence after excision
- Higher incidence in dark-skinned individuals
- Common sites: Earlobes, sternum, deltoid, upper back

| Feature | Hypertrophic | Keloid |
|---------|--------------|--------|
| Boundary | Within wound | Beyond wound |
| Regression | Yes | No |
| Recurrence after excision | Lower | Higher (>50%) |
| Time of onset | 1-2 months | Months to years |
| Genetics | Less strong | Strong familial tendency |

**Treatment options:**
- Pressure therapy
- Silicone gel sheets
- Intralesional corticosteroids
- Excision + adjuvant therapy
- Radiation (for recalcitrant keloids)

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 6, p.154*

### Chronic Wounds
Wounds that fail to progress through normal healing phases within 3 months:
- **Venous ulcers:** Lower leg, gaiter area
- **Arterial ulcers:** Pressure points, painful
- **Diabetic ulcers:** Neuropathic, often painless
- **Pressure ulcers:** Over bony prominences

## Wound Classification

### By Contamination (Surgical Wounds)

| Class | Description | Infection Rate |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Clean** | No entry to GI/GU/respiratory tract; no inflammation | <2% |
| **Clean-contaminated** | Controlled entry to GI/GU/respiratory tract | 3-10% |
| **Contaminated** | Major break in sterility; fresh trauma | 10-20% |
| **Dirty** | Established infection; perforated viscus | >20% |

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 9, p.261*

### Enhancing Wound Healing
1. **Optimize nutrition:** Protein, Vitamin C, Zinc
2. **Control diabetes:** HbA1c <7%
3. **Stop smoking:** At least 4 weeks before elective surgery
4. **Maintain tissue oxygenation:** Avoid hypoxia, anemia
5. **Prevent infection:** Aseptic technique, prophylactic antibiotics
6. **Moist wound healing:** Appropriate dressings
7. **Reduce tension:** Proper closure technique

## Key Points Summary

1. Three phases: Hemostasis/Inflammation → Proliferation → Remodeling
2. Macrophage = most important cell (orchestrates healing)
3. Collagen Type III → Type I transition during remodeling
4. Maximum tensile strength = 80% (never reaches 100%)
5. Vitamin C essential for collagen hydroxylation
6. Infection = most common cause of impaired healing
7. Keloid extends beyond wound, doesn't regress
8. Hypertrophic stays within wound, regresses with time
