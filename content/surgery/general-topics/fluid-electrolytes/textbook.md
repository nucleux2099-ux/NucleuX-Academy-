## 1. Body Water — The Basics

🟢 **Total body water (TBW)** constitutes approximately **60% of body weight** in an adult male and **50%** in an adult female. The difference is due to higher adipose content in females (fat is relatively anhydrous). In neonates, TBW may be as high as 75–80%; in the elderly, it drops to 45–50%.

🟢 **Distribution of TBW:**

| Compartment | % of TBW | % of Body Weight | Volume (70 kg male) |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Intracellular (ICF)** | 2/3 (~67%) | 40% | ~28 L |
| **Extracellular (ECF)** | 1/3 (~33%) | 20% | ~14 L |
| — Interstitial | 3/4 of ECF | 15% | ~10.5 L |
| — Intravascular (plasma) | 1/4 of ECF | 5% | ~3.5 L |

🟢 **The "Rule of Thirds":** TBW is 2/3 intracellular, 1/3 extracellular. ECF is 3/4 interstitial, 1/4 intravascular. This simple ratio governs fluid distribution after IV administration.

🔵 **Third space:** A pathological, non-functional ECF compartment where fluid is sequestered (peritoneal cavity in ascites, bowel wall in obstruction, retroperitoneum in pancreatitis). This concept, introduced by Randall in the 1950s, drove aggressive perioperative fluid resuscitation for decades. Modern understanding acknowledges that "third-spacing" is partly real (edema, effusions) but was historically overestimated, leading to harmful fluid overload.

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Glycocalyx Revolution:** The endothelial glycocalyx (a 0.5–1 µm layer of proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans lining the vascular endothelium) is now recognized as the key barrier to transvascular fluid flux — not the classical Starling model of hydrostatic-oncotic pressure gradients across the capillary wall. Surgical stress, sepsis, and hypervolemia all degrade the glycocalyx, promoting interstitial edema. This explains why albumin infusion does not reliably expand plasma volume in critically ill patients — if the glycocalyx is destroyed, colloid escapes freely into the interstitium.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.3*

## 2. Composition of Body Fluid Compartments

🟢 **The key electrolyte differences:**

| Electrolyte | ECF (mEq/L) | ICF (mEq/L) |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Na⁺** | 140 | 10 |
| **K⁺** | 4 | 150 |
| **Ca²⁺** (ionized) | 2.4 | <0.001 |
| **Mg²⁺** | 2 | 40 |
| **Cl⁻** | 103 | 3 |
| **HCO₃⁻** | 24 | 10 |
| **PO₄³⁻** | 2 | 100 |
| **Protein** | 15 | 40 |

🟢 **The Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase** is the master regulator — it pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per ATP consumed, maintaining the enormous electrochemical gradients between compartments.

🟢 **Osmolality** — the concentration of osmotically active particles per kilogram of solvent:
- **Plasma osmolality ≈ 280–295 mOsm/kg**
- **Calculated:** 2(Na⁺) + Glucose/18 + BUN/2.8
- **Osmolar gap** = Measured – Calculated (normal <10; elevated in methanol, ethylene glycol, mannitol)

🔵 **Tonicity vs. Osmolality:** Osmolality counts all solutes; tonicity counts only *effective* osmoles (those that don't freely cross membranes). Urea freely crosses cell membranes, so uremia raises osmolality but NOT tonicity — it does not cause cellular dehydration. Sodium and glucose are effective osmoles.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.55*

## 3. Normal Fluid & Electrolyte Balance

### Daily Inputs and Outputs

🟢 **Daily water balance (average adult):**

| Input | Volume (mL/day) | Output | Volume (mL/day) |
|-------|-----------------|--------|-----------------|
| Oral intake | 1500–2000 | Urine | 800–1500 |
| Food water content | 500–700 | Insensible (skin + lungs) | 600–900 |
| Metabolic water | 200–300 | Stool | 100–200 |
| **Total** | **~2200–3000** | **Total** | **~1500–2600** |

🟢 **Daily electrolyte requirements (maintenance):**
- **Na⁺:** 1–2 mEq/kg/day
- **K⁺:** 0.5–1 mEq/kg/day
- **Cl⁻:** 1–2 mEq/kg/day

### IV Fluid Formulas

🟢 **Holiday-Segar formula (maintenance fluids):**
- First 10 kg: 4 mL/kg/hr
- Next 10 kg: 2 mL/kg/hr
- Each kg above 20: 1 mL/kg/hr
- *Example:* 70 kg adult = 40 + 20 + 50 = **110 mL/hr** (~2640 mL/day)

🔵 **In practice:** Most surgical patients do NOT need calculated maintenance fluids. Postoperative oral intake should be resumed as early as possible — routine IV maintenance fluids are unnecessary if oral intake is adequate. The old dictum of keeping patients NPO for days and running "1 NS with 20 KCl" is obsolete.

## 4. Composition of Common IV Fluids

🟢 **Memorize this table — it is essential for fluid prescribing:**

| Fluid | Na⁺ | K⁺ | Cl⁻ | Lactate/Acetate | Osmolality | pH |
|-------|-----|-----|-----|----------------|------------|-----|
| **0.9% NS** | 154 | 0 | 154 | 0 | 308 | 5.0 |
| **Ringer's Lactate** | 130 | 4 | 109 | Lactate 28 | 273 | 6.5 |
| **PlasmaLyte** | 140 | 5 | 98 | Acetate 27 + Gluconate 23 | 294 | 7.4 |
| **D5W** | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 252 | 4.0 |
| **D5 0.45% NS** | 77 | 0 | 77 | 0 | 406 | 4.0 |
| **3% Hypertonic saline** | 513 | 0 | 513 | 0 | 1026 | 5.0 |

🟢 **Key points:**
- **Normal saline (0.9% NS)** is NOT "normal" — it contains 154 mEq/L each of Na⁺ and Cl⁻ (vs. plasma: Na⁺ 140, Cl⁻ 103). Large volumes cause **hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis (HCMA)**.
- **Ringer's lactate** is the most physiological crystalloid for resuscitation. The lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate by the liver.
- **D5W** distributes across TBW — only 1/12 stays intravascular. It is NOT a resuscitation fluid.

🔵 **Balanced crystalloids vs. Normal saline — the modern debate:**

| Trial | N | Key Finding |
|-------|---|-------------|
| **SMART** (2018) | 15,802 | Balanced crystalloids (RL/PlasmaLyte) reduced MAKE-30 (major adverse kidney events at 30 days) vs. NS in ICU patients |
| **SALT-ED** (2018) | 13,347 | Similar findings in non-ICU ED patients |
| **PLUS** (2022) | 5,037 | No difference in 90-day mortality in ICU patients |
| **BaSICS** (2021) | 11,052 | No difference in 90-day mortality |

🔵 **Current consensus:** Balanced crystalloids (RL or PlasmaLyte) are preferred as the default resuscitation fluid. Normal saline should be reserved for specific indications: hyponatremia correction, hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis, and as a vehicle for certain drug infusions.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** RL contains 4 mEq/L K⁺ — yet it does NOT worsen hyperkalemia and is safe in renal failure patients. The SMART and BaSICS trials included renal failure patients and showed no hyperkalemia signal. In fact, NS-induced acidosis promotes potassium shift out of cells, paradoxically worsening hyperkalemia more than RL does.

## 5. Sodium Disorders

### Hyponatremia (Na⁺ < 135 mEq/L)

🟢 **The most common electrolyte disorder in hospitalized patients** (incidence ~15–30%). Classification by serum osmolality is the key first step:

```
Hyponatremia (Na⁺ < 135)
│
├── Hypertonic (Osm > 295) → Pseudohyponatremia correction needed
│   └── Hyperglycemia (Na⁺ drops ~1.6 mEq/L per 100 mg/dL glucose rise)
│       Mannitol
│
├── Isotonic (Osm 280–295) → Lab artifact
│   └── Hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia (ion-selective electrode methods
│       have largely eliminated this)
│
└── Hypotonic (Osm < 280) → TRUE hyponatremia → Assess volume status
    ├── Hypovolemic → Losses (GI, renal, third-spacing)
    │   Treatment: Volume replacement with NS
    ├── Euvolemic → SIADH, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency
    │   Treatment: Fluid restriction, salt tablets, vasopressin antagonists
    └── Hypervolemic → CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome
        Treatment: Fluid restriction, diuretics, treat underlying cause
```

🟢 **SIADH** — the most common cause of euvolemic hyponatremia in surgical patients:
- Causes: CNS disorders, pulmonary disease, drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide), pain, nausea, postoperative state
- Diagnosis: Low serum osmolality + inappropriately concentrated urine (>100 mOsm/kg) + urine Na⁺ >40 mEq/L + euvolemia + normal thyroid/adrenal function
- Treatment: Fluid restriction (first-line), hypertonic saline if severe, vasopressin receptor antagonists (tolvaptan)

🟢 **Symptoms** correlate with acuity more than absolute level:
- **Mild (125–135):** Often asymptomatic, nausea, malaise
- **Moderate (120–125):** Headache, confusion, lethargy
- **Severe (<120 or symptomatic):** Seizures, coma, respiratory arrest, cerebral edema

🟢 **Treatment principles:**
- **Acute symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures/coma):** 3% hypertonic saline — bolus 100 mL over 10 min, repeat ×2 if needed. Target: raise Na⁺ by 4–6 mEq/L in first 1–2 hours.
- **Rate of correction:** Do NOT exceed **8 mEq/L in 24 hours** (some guidelines say 10, but 8 is safer). Overcorrection → **osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS)** — devastating, irreversible pontine and extrapontine demyelination.
- **High-risk for ODS:** Chronic hyponatremia (>48 hrs), alcoholism, malnutrition, liver disease, hypokalemia.

🔵 **Desmopressin clamp:** If overcorrection occurs (Na⁺ rising too fast), administer desmopressin (DDAVP) 2 µg IV to halt free water excretion, ± D5W infusion to re-lower sodium. This rescue strategy has reduced ODS incidence dramatically.

### Hypernatremia (Na⁺ > 145 mEq/L)

🟢 **Almost always indicates a water deficit** (not sodium excess). Occurs when water loss exceeds sodium loss, or when access to water is limited (elderly, intubated patients, infants).

🟢 **Causes:**
- **Renal water loss:** Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic), osmotic diuresis
- **Extrarenal water loss:** GI (diarrhea), insensible (fever, burns)
- **Inadequate intake:** Altered mental status, no access to water

🟢 **Free water deficit calculation:**
```
Free Water Deficit (L) = TBW × [(Serum Na⁺ / 140) – 1]
Where TBW = 0.6 × body weight (kg) for males; 0.5 × for females
```

🟢 **Treatment:**
- Replace free water deficit over **48–72 hours** (not faster)
- Rate of correction: **≤10 mEq/L per 24 hours** to avoid cerebral edema
- Route: Oral/enteral water preferred; IV D5W or 0.45% NS if IV needed
- Address underlying cause (e.g., desmopressin for central DI)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Hospital-acquired hypernatremia (developing >24h after admission) carries an independent mortality of 30–50% in ICU patients. It is almost always iatrogenic — inadequate free water prescription, excessive NS use, or failure to account for insensible losses in febrile/ventilated patients. It is a marker of care quality.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.55*

## 6. Potassium Disorders

### Hypokalemia (K⁺ < 3.5 mEq/L)

🟢 **Common in surgical patients** — causes include:
- GI losses (vomiting, NG suction, diarrhea)
- Renal losses (diuretics, hyperaldosteronism, RTA)
- Intracellular shift (alkalosis, insulin, β-agonists)
- Poor intake

🟢 **Why vomiting causes hypokalemia — a commonly tested mechanism:**
Gastric fluid contains only ~10 mEq/L K⁺ — direct K⁺ loss is minimal. The real mechanism:
1. Vomiting → loss of HCl → metabolic alkalosis
2. Kidneys attempt to excrete HCO₃⁻ → but need a cation to excrete with it
3. Volume depletion activates aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption → K⁺ and H⁺ excretion
4. Result: **renal potassium wasting** is the primary driver

🟢 **Clinical features:**
- Muscle weakness, cramps, ileus (smooth muscle)
- **ECG changes (in order of severity):** Flattened T waves → ST depression → U waves → Prolonged QT → Arrhythmias (PVCs, VT, VF, torsades)
- Enhances digoxin toxicity
- Rhabdomyolysis (severe cases)

🟢 **Treatment:**
- **Mild (3.0–3.5):** Oral KCl supplementation (40–80 mEq/day)
- **Moderate (2.5–3.0):** IV KCl 10–20 mEq/hr (peripheral line max 10 mEq/hr; central line max 20 mEq/hr)
- **Severe (<2.5) or symptomatic:** IV KCl, cardiac monitoring, check and correct magnesium

🔵 **The Mg²⁺-K⁺ link:** Hypomagnesemia causes renal potassium wasting via ROMK channel activation. **Hypokalemia refractory to potassium replacement → check and replace magnesium.** This is one of the most commonly missed clinical associations.

### Hyperkalemia (K⁺ > 5.0 mEq/L)

🟢 **A surgical emergency when symptomatic.** Causes:
- Renal failure (most common)
- Tissue destruction (rhabdomyolysis, burns, tumor lysis, hemolysis)
- Drugs (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, spironolactone, succinylcholine, NSAIDs)
- Acidosis (H⁺/K⁺ exchange)
- Adrenal insufficiency

🟢 **ECG changes (progressive):**
```
Peaked T waves (earliest) → Flattened P waves → Prolonged PR 
→ Widened QRS → Sine wave → VF / Asystole
```

🟢 **Treatment — memorize this protocol:**

| Step | Agent | Mechanism | Onset | Duration |
|------|-------|-----------|-------|----------|
| 1. **Stabilize myocardium** | Calcium gluconate 10% (10 mL IV over 2–3 min) | Membrane stabilization (does NOT lower K⁺) | 1–3 min | 30–60 min |
| 2. **Shift K⁺ intracellularly** | Insulin 10 U + D50 (25 g glucose) IV | ↑ Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase activity | 15–30 min | 4–6 hr |
| | Nebulized salbutamol (10–20 mg) | β₂-mediated cellular uptake | 15–30 min | 2–4 hr |
| | NaHCO₃ (if acidotic) | H⁺/K⁺ exchange | 15–30 min | 2 hr |
| 3. **Remove K⁺ from body** | Furosemide (40–80 mg IV) | Renal excretion | 15–60 min | 6 hr |
| | Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate / Patiromer | GI binding | 1–6 hr | Until stopped |
| | **Hemodialysis** | Direct removal | Immediate | Session |

🔵 **Pseudohyperkalemia:** Hemolyzed sample, tourniquet use, fist-clenching, thrombocytosis (>500,000), leukocytosis (>100,000). Always repeat the sample before treating an asymptomatic patient with isolated K⁺ elevation.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Succinylcholine causes K⁺ release of ~0.5 mEq/L in normal patients. In patients with burns, spinal cord injury, prolonged immobilization, or upper/lower motor neuron disease, the K⁺ release can be massive (>5 mEq/L) due to upregulated extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors. Succinylcholine is contraindicated from **24 hours post-burn/injury** until full recovery (may be months to years).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.55*

## 7. Calcium Disorders

### Hypocalcemia (Ionized Ca²⁺ < 1.1 mmol/L or Total Ca²⁺ < 8.5 mg/dL)

🟢 **Surgical relevance:** Most commonly encountered after thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy (injury to or devascularization of parathyroid glands).

🟢 **Other causes:** Pancreatitis (saponification), massive transfusion (citrate chelation), hypomagnesemia, vitamin D deficiency, chronic kidney disease.

🟢 **Corrected calcium:** For every 1 g/dL decrease in albumin below 4, add 0.8 mg/dL to total calcium. *Better yet, measure ionized calcium directly.*

🟢 **Clinical features:**
- Perioral and acral paresthesias (earliest)
- Carpopedal spasm, tetany
- **Chvostek's sign:** Facial twitch on tapping facial nerve anterior to ear (60% sensitivity)
- **Trousseau's sign:** Carpal spasm with BP cuff inflated >SBP for 3 min (more specific, 94%)
- Laryngospasm (surgical emergency post-thyroidectomy)
- Prolonged QT interval → arrhythmias

🟢 **Treatment:**
- **Symptomatic/severe:** IV calcium gluconate 10% — 1–2 ampoules (10–20 mL) over 10–20 min, then continuous infusion 0.5–2 mg/kg/hr
- **Chronic/mild:** Oral calcium + vitamin D (calcitriol 0.25–0.5 µg BID)
- **Always check and correct magnesium** — hypomagnesemia causes PTH resistance

🔵 **Hungry bone syndrome:** After parathyroidectomy for hyperparathyroidism, sudden withdrawal of PTH causes avid skeletal calcium uptake. Severe, prolonged hypocalcemia requiring massive IV calcium supplementation (sometimes >10 g/day). Risk factors: large adenoma, high preoperative calcium, elevated ALP, vitamin D deficiency.

### Hypercalcemia (Total Ca²⁺ > 10.5 mg/dL)

🟢 **"Stones, bones, groans, thrones, and psychic overtones":**
- Renal calculi, nephrocalcinosis
- Bone pain, pathological fractures
- Abdominal pain, constipation, pancreatitis, PUD
- Polyuria, polydipsia
- Confusion, depression, coma

🟢 **Causes — the 90% rule:** Primary hyperparathyroidism (outpatient) and malignancy (inpatient) account for ~90% of all hypercalcemia.

🟢 **Treatment of acute hypercalcemia:**
1. **Aggressive IV NS** (200–300 mL/hr initially) — volume expansion + calciuresis
2. **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) — only AFTER adequate volume resuscitation
3. **Bisphosphonates** (zoledronic acid 4 mg IV) — inhibit osteoclast activity; onset 2–4 days
4. **Calcitonin** (4 IU/kg q12h) — rapid but transient effect (tachyphylaxis in 48 hr)
5. **Denosumab** — for bisphosphonate-refractory cases
6. **Dialysis** — for refractory/life-threatening hypercalcemia or renal failure

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Thiazide diuretics cause hypercalcemia (decreased renal calcium excretion); loop diuretics cause hypocalcemia (increased renal calcium excretion). This distinction is commonly tested and clinically relevant.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5 & Ch.38; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.409*

## 8. Magnesium Disorders

🟢 **Hypomagnesemia (Mg²⁺ < 1.7 mg/dL):** Extremely common in surgical/ICU patients (~60% of ICU patients). Often missed because magnesium is not part of standard electrolyte panels.

🟢 **Causes:** Chronic alcoholism, diuretics, GI losses (diarrhea, short gut, NG suction), PPIs (long-term use), refeeding syndrome, aminoglycosides, cisplatin.

🟢 **Clinical significance — the "forgotten electrolyte":**
- Causes refractory hypokalemia and hypocalcemia
- Potentiates digoxin toxicity
- Predisposes to cardiac arrhythmias (torsades de pointes)
- Seizures (severe)

🟢 **Treatment:** MgSO₄ 1–2 g IV over 1 hour (for symptomatic); oral magnesium oxide for chronic replacement.

🔵 **Hypermagnesemia (Mg²⁺ > 2.5 mg/dL):** Almost always iatrogenic (MgSO₄ infusion in eclampsia, antacid/laxative abuse in renal failure). Causes hyporeflexia → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest. Treatment: IV calcium gluconate + stop Mg²⁺ source.

## 9. Phosphate Disorders

🔵 **Hypophosphatemia (<2.5 mg/dL):** Critical in surgical patients, especially in:
- **Refeeding syndrome** — most dangerous context. Insulin-driven cellular phosphate uptake after re-alimentation of malnourished patients causes profound hypophosphatemia → ATP depletion → cardiac failure, respiratory failure, rhabdomyolysis, death.
- DKA treatment (insulin shifts PO₄³⁻ intracellularly)
- Chronic alcoholism
- Phosphate binders

🔵 **Treatment:** IV sodium/potassium phosphate for severe (<1 mg/dL); oral supplementation for moderate.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Refeeding syndrome prevention:** Before starting nutrition in a malnourished patient (BMI <16, >10% weight loss in 3–6 months, minimal intake for >5 days), start feeding at **10 kcal/kg/day** and advance slowly over 4–7 days. Supplement thiamine, phosphate, magnesium, and potassium prophylactically. Monitor electrolytes daily for 5 days.

## 10. Acid-Base Physiology

### The Fundamentals

🟢 **Normal arterial blood gas values:**
- pH: 7.35–7.45
- PaCO₂: 35–45 mmHg
- HCO₃⁻: 22–26 mEq/L
- Base excess: –2 to +2

🟢 **Buffer systems (in order of importance):**
1. **Bicarbonate-CO₂ system** (most important extracellular buffer)
2. Hemoglobin (intracellular)
3. Phosphate (intracellular and renal)
4. Proteins (albumin)

🟢 **The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:**
```
pH = 6.1 + log([HCO₃⁻] / 0.03 × PaCO₂)
```

🟢 **Compensation rules (memorize):**

| Disorder | Primary Change | Compensation | Expected |
|----------|---------------|--------------|----------|
| Metabolic acidosis | ↓ HCO₃⁻ | ↓ PaCO₂ | PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 (±2) — **Winter's formula** |
| Metabolic alkalosis | ↑ HCO₃⁻ | ↑ PaCO₂ | PaCO₂ ≈ 0.7 × HCO₃⁻ + 21 (±2) |
| Respiratory acidosis (acute) | ↑ PaCO₂ | ↑ HCO₃⁻ | HCO₃⁻ rises 1 per 10 mmHg PaCO₂ rise |
| Respiratory acidosis (chronic) | ↑ PaCO₂ | ↑ HCO₃⁻ | HCO₃⁻ rises 3.5 per 10 mmHg PaCO₂ rise |
| Respiratory alkalosis (acute) | ↓ PaCO₂ | ↓ HCO₃⁻ | HCO₃⁻ drops 2 per 10 mmHg PaCO₂ drop |
| Respiratory alkalosis (chronic) | ↓ PaCO₂ | ↓ HCO₃⁻ | HCO₃⁻ drops 5 per 10 mmHg PaCO₂ drop |

🟢 **Key principle:** Compensation NEVER overcompensates. If the pH has "crossed" to the other side, there is a mixed disorder.

### Anion Gap

🟢 **Anion Gap (AG) = Na⁺ – (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)**
- Normal: 12 ± 4 (or 8 ± 4 with modern ion-selective electrodes)
- **Correct for albumin:** For every 1 g/dL decrease in albumin below 4, add 2.5 to the AG

🟢 **High AG metabolic acidosis — MUDPILES:**
- **M**ethanol
- **U**remia
- **D**iabetic ketoacidosis
- **P**ropylene glycol / **P**araldehyde
- **I**soniazid / Iron
- **L**actic acidosis
- **E**thylene glycol
- **S**alicylates

🟢 **Normal AG (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis — HARDUPS:**
- **H**yperalimentation (TPN)
- **A**cetazolamide / Addison's
- **R**enal tubular acidosis
- **D**iarrhea
- **U**retero-sigmoidostomy
- **P**ancreatic fistula / drainage
- **S**aline (0.9% NS) infusion

🔵 **Delta-delta (Δ-Δ) ratio:** ΔAG / ΔHCO₃⁻. Detects mixed disorders:
- **<1:** Concurrent non-AG metabolic acidosis
- **1–2:** Pure AG metabolic acidosis
- **>2:** Concurrent metabolic alkalosis

### Surgically Relevant Acid-Base Scenarios

🟢 **Lactic acidosis** — the surgical acid-base disorder:
- **Type A** (hypoperfusion): Hemorrhagic shock, septic shock, mesenteric ischemia — surgical emergencies
- **Type B** (non-hypoperfusion): Liver failure, metformin, thiamine deficiency, malignancy
- Lactate >4 mmol/L is associated with >25% mortality in surgical patients
- Serial lactate clearance is a better prognostic marker than absolute value

🟢 **Metabolic alkalosis in the surgical patient:**
- Most common cause: **vomiting / NG suction** (loss of HCl)
- Also: diuretics, volume contraction, hypokalemia
- **Saline-responsive** (urine Cl⁻ <20): Responds to NS + KCl replacement
- **Saline-resistant** (urine Cl⁻ >20): Hyperaldosteronism, Cushing's, Bartter's — treat underlying cause

🔵 **Pyloric stenosis (pediatric surgery classic):** Persistent vomiting → loss of HCl → hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis with paradoxical aciduria. The kidney preferentially retains Na⁺ and HCO₃⁻ (because volume depletion is the bigger threat), excreting H⁺ and K⁺. Treatment: Correct volume and electrolytes (NS + KCl) BEFORE surgery.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.3; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.56*

## 11. Perioperative Fluid Management

### Preoperative Assessment

🟢 **Assess volume status clinically:**
- **Dehydration signs:** Tachycardia, hypotension, dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, oliguria, elevated BUN:creatinine ratio (>20:1)
- **Overload signs:** Peripheral edema, JVP elevation, pulmonary crackles, S3 gallop

🟢 **Correct existing deficits before elective surgery:**
- Hypokalemia → K⁺ >3.5 before induction (arrhythmia risk with anesthesia)
- Hyponatremia → correct gradually; avoid rapid changes
- Dehydration → resuscitate with balanced crystalloids

### Intraoperative Fluids

🔵 **The pendulum of perioperative fluid therapy:**

| Era | Philosophy | Problem |
|-----|-----------|---------|
| 1960s–2000s | **Liberal** (12–18 mL/kg/hr) | Edema, anastomotic leaks, pulmonary complications, ileus |
| 2000s–2010s | **Restrictive** (<4 mL/kg/hr) | AKI, hypoperfusion, tachycardia |
| 2010s–present | **Goal-directed (GDT)** | Individualized using dynamic parameters |

🔵 **Goal-directed fluid therapy (GDT):**
- Uses dynamic parameters: stroke volume variation (SVV), pulse pressure variation (PPV), cardiac output monitoring
- Fluid boluses (250 mL) guided by stroke volume response
- **Evidence:** Reduces postoperative complications, LOS, and morbidity in major abdominal surgery (multiple meta-analyses)

🟢 **ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery) fluid principles:**
- Avoid preoperative fasting dehydration (clear fluids up to 2 hrs before surgery)
- Carbohydrate loading (maltodextrin drinks) the night before and morning of surgery
- Intraoperative: balanced crystalloids, avoid >2 L unless blood loss replaced
- Target zero fluid balance by end of surgery
- Early oral intake postoperatively; discontinue IV fluids as soon as possible

🔴 **SS Pearl — The RELIEF Trial (2018):** This landmark RCT (3000 patients, major abdominal surgery) compared restrictive vs. liberal fluid regimens. The restrictive group had HIGHER rates of AKI (8.6% vs. 5.0%) with no benefit in disability-free survival. Message: being too restrictive is harmful. The sweet spot is **individualized, goal-directed** — not a one-size-fits-all mL/kg/hr.

### Fluid Resuscitation in Specific Surgical Conditions

🟢 **Hemorrhagic shock:** Balanced crystalloids + blood products. Target 1:1:1 (PRBC:FFP:platelets) in massive transfusion. Permissive hypotension (MAP 50–60) until surgical control of bleeding.

🟢 **Sepsis/Septic shock:** Initial bolus 30 mL/kg crystalloid within 3 hours (Surviving Sepsis 2021). Reassess with dynamic measures. Vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) if MAP <65 after fluids.

🔵 **Burns (Parkland formula):**
```
Total fluid in 24 hrs = 4 mL × %TBSA × body weight (kg)
Give 1/2 in first 8 hours, 1/2 in next 16 hours
Use Ringer's Lactate
Titrate to urine output 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr (adults)
```

🔵 **Intestinal obstruction:** Massive third-space losses. Aggressive balanced crystalloid resuscitation + NG decompression. Correct electrolyte derangements (hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis from vomiting; metabolic acidosis from distal obstruction with diarrhea). Monitor urine output and lactate.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.5 & Ch.4; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.3*

## 12. Colloids vs. Crystalloids

🟢 **Crystalloids** remain the fluid of choice for most surgical indications.

🔵 **The colloid evidence:**

| Colloid | Evidence | Status |
|---------|----------|--------|
| **Albumin** | SAFE trial (2004): equivalent to NS in ICU. ALBIOS (2014): no mortality benefit in sepsis | Use in specific contexts (SBP, liver transplant, post-large-volume paracentesis) |
| **Hydroxyethyl starch (HES)** | 6S trial, CHEST trial: increased AKI and need for RRT in sepsis | **CONTRAINDICATED in sepsis and critical illness.** EMA restricted use. |
| **Gelatin** | Limited evidence, anaphylaxis risk | Falling out of favor |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The only robust indications for albumin in surgery are: (1) Large-volume paracentesis (>5 L) — 6–8 g albumin per liter removed; (2) SBP prophylaxis/treatment with antibiotics; (3) Hepatorenal syndrome (with vasopressors). Outside these, crystalloids are equivalent or superior.

## 13. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **TBW = 60% body weight.** 2/3 intracellular, 1/3 extracellular. ECF = 3/4 interstitial + 1/4 plasma.
2. **Na⁺ is the dominant ECF cation; K⁺ is the dominant ICF cation.** The Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase maintains this gradient.
3. **Plasma osmolality = 2(Na⁺) + Glucose/18 + BUN/2.8.** Normal: 280–295 mOsm/kg.
4. **Hyponatremia:** Most common electrolyte disorder. Classify by osmolality → volume status. Correct slowly (≤8 mEq/L per 24 hr) to avoid ODS.
5. **Hyperkalemia** is a cardiac emergency. Treat: calcium gluconate → insulin+glucose → remove K⁺ (diuretics/dialysis).
6. **Hypocalcemia post-thyroidectomy:** Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs. Treat with IV calcium gluconate.
7. **Metabolic acidosis:** Calculate the anion gap. High AG = MUDPILES. Normal AG = HARDUPS.
8. **Vomiting causes hypokalemic, hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis** — primarily through renal K⁺ wasting.
9. **Ringer's lactate** is the preferred resuscitation crystalloid. NS causes hyperchloremic acidosis.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

10. **Balanced crystalloids** (RL/PlasmaLyte) are preferred over NS based on SMART/SALT-ED trials.
11. **RL is safe in hyperkalemia** — the acidosis from NS paradoxically worsens K⁺ more.
12. **Goal-directed fluid therapy** using dynamic parameters (SVV, PPV) reduces complications in major surgery.
13. **RELIEF trial:** Restrictive fluids cause MORE AKI than liberal — individualize, don't restrict blindly.
14. **Delta-delta ratio** detects mixed acid-base disorders hidden behind a single AG acidosis.
15. **Refeeding syndrome:** Start at 10 kcal/kg/day in malnourished patients; supplement thiamine + PO₄ + Mg²⁺ + K⁺.
16. **Hypomagnesemia** causes refractory hypokalemia and hypocalcemia — the "forgotten electrolyte."

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

17. **The glycocalyx** is the true barrier to transvascular flux — not the classical Starling forces. Surgical stress degrades it.
18. **Hospital-acquired hypernatremia** (30–50% ICU mortality) is almost always iatrogenic — a marker of care quality.
19. **Succinylcholine** can cause fatal hyperkalemia in burns/denervation injuries (upregulated extrajunctional AChRs).
20. **HES is contraindicated in critical illness** — increased AKI and RRT (6S, CHEST trials).
21. **Desmopressin clamp** rescues overcorrection of hyponatremia — a life-saving intervention.
22. **Hungry bone syndrome** after parathyroidectomy can require >10 g calcium/day — anticipate and prepare.
