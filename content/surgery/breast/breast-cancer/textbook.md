## 1. History — From Divine Punishment to Precision Medicine

Breast cancer is among the oldest documented malignancies. The Edwin Smith Papyrus (~1600 BCE, copied from texts dating to ~3000 BCE) describes eight cases of breast tumors, concluding with chilling honesty: *"There is no treatment."* Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) attributed the disease to excess black bile — the humoral theory that would dominate for two millennia.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| ~3000 BCE | **Edwin Smith Papyrus** — Earliest known description of breast tumors |
| 1757 | **Henri Le Dran** — First to propose that breast cancer spreads via lymphatics, not humoral imbalance |
| 1882 | **William Halsted** — Introduced the radical mastectomy (en bloc resection of breast, pectorals, axillary nodes) |
| 1948 | **Patey & Dyson** — Modified radical mastectomy (preserving pectoralis major) |
| 1971 | **Bernard Fisher** — NSABP B-04: proved radical mastectomy offered no survival advantage over total mastectomy |
| 1985 | **NSABP B-06** — Established breast-conserving surgery (BCS) + radiation as equivalent to mastectomy for survival |
| 2002 | **Perou et al.** — Molecular classification (Luminal A/B, HER2-enriched, Basal-like) transforms understanding |
| 2011 | **ACOSOG Z0011** — Sentinel node positive? Skip axillary dissection in select patients |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.44*

The arc of breast cancer surgery is the story of *less is more* — from Halsted's mutilating radical mastectomy to Fisher's paradigm shift recognizing breast cancer as a systemic disease from inception, culminating in today's era of de-escalation and molecular-guided therapy.

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

🟢 **Embryology:** The breast develops from the **ectodermal milk line** (mammary ridge), which extends from axilla to groin during the **6th week** of embryonic development. Most of this ridge regresses except in the pectoral region. Failure of regression → accessory nipples (polythelia) or accessory breast tissue (polymastia), most commonly along the anterior axillary fold.

🟢 **Key anatomical facts:**

- **Position:** Overlies the pectoralis major (2/3) and serratus anterior (1/3), from ribs 2–6, sternal edge to mid-axillary line
- **Structure:** 15–20 lobes arranged radially, each draining via a lactiferous duct to the nipple; separated by Cooper's ligaments (suspensory)
- **Tail of Spence:** Axillary extension of breast tissue — clinically important as a site of pathology
- **Retromammary space:** Loose areolar tissue between breast and pectoral fascia; allows mobility

🟢 **Blood supply:**
- **Medial:** Internal mammary (thoracic) artery perforators (60% of supply)
- **Lateral:** Lateral thoracic artery, thoracoacromial artery
- **Minor contributions:** Posterior intercostal arteries (3rd–5th)

🟢 **Lymphatic drainage — the surgical anatomy that matters most:**

| Drainage Route | % of Lymphatic Flow | Clinical Significance |
|----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Axillary nodes** (Levels I–III) | ~75% | Primary surgical target |
| **Internal mammary nodes** | ~25% | Medial/central tumors; rarely dissected |
| **Interpectoral (Rotter's) nodes** | Variable | Between pectoralis major and minor |

🔵 **Axillary node levels** (defined by relationship to pectoralis minor):
- **Level I:** Lateral to pec minor (low axilla)
- **Level II:** Behind pec minor (mid axilla); includes interpectoral nodes
- **Level III:** Medial to pec minor (apical axilla); infraclavicular nodes

🟢 **Key nerves at risk during surgery:**

| Nerve | Origin | Course | Injury Consequence |
|-------|--------|--------|--------------------|
| **Long thoracic** | C5–C7 | Runs on serratus anterior, medial axillary wall | **Winged scapula** |
| **Thoracodorsal** | C6–C8 | Runs with subscapular vessels to latissimus dorsi | Weakness of shoulder adduction/internal rotation |
| **Intercostobrachial** | T2 | Crosses axilla transversely | Numbness of medial upper arm (most common post-ALND sensory deficit) |
| **Medial pectoral** | C8–T1 | Lateral to pec minor, enters deep surface | Atrophy of pec major (if divided) |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.53*

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The internal mammary lymph node chain lies 2–3 cm lateral to the sternal edge within the intercostal spaces. Routine dissection of these nodes was abandoned after the NSABP B-04 trial showed no survival benefit, but modern nodal irradiation protocols (MA.20, EORTC 22922) now target this chain in high-risk patients, improving disease-free survival.

## 3. Pathophysiology — From Normal Epithelium to Invasive Cancer

### The Multistep Model

🟢 Breast cancer develops through a stepwise progression of genetic and epigenetic alterations:

```
Normal Epithelium
    → Usual Ductal Hyperplasia (UDH)
    → Atypical Ductal Hyperplasia (ADH)
    → Ductal Carcinoma In Situ (DCIS)
    → Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)
    → Metastatic Disease
```

This model is simplified — not all cancers follow this sequence, and lobular carcinomas follow a parallel pathway (ALH → LCIS → ILC).

🟢 **Risk factors** (the modifiable and the fixed):

| Risk Factor | Relative Risk | Category |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| **Age** (strongest factor) | Increases with age | Non-modifiable |
| **BRCA1/2 mutation** | 5–20× | Genetic |
| **First-degree relative with breast cancer** | 1.5–3× | Family history |
| **Prior breast biopsy showing ADH/ALH** | 4–5× | Histological |
| **LCIS** | 8–10× (bilateral risk) | Histological |
| **Prior chest wall radiation (age 10–30)** | 5–20× | Iatrogenic |
| **Early menarche / late menopause** | 1.2–1.5× | Hormonal |
| **Nulliparity / first pregnancy >30** | 1.3–2× | Reproductive |
| **HRT (combined E+P, >5 years)** | 1.2–1.4× | Hormonal |
| **Obesity (postmenopausal)** | 1.2–1.5× | Modifiable |
| **Alcohol** | 1.1× per 10 g/day | Modifiable |

🔵 **Hereditary breast cancer (5–10% of all cases):**
- **BRCA1** (Chr 17q21): Lifetime breast cancer risk 55–72%; ovarian cancer 39–44%. Tumors typically triple-negative, high-grade. DNA repair (homologous recombination) deficiency → PARP inhibitor sensitivity.
- **BRCA2** (Chr 13q12): Lifetime breast cancer risk 45–69%; ovarian cancer 11–17%. More commonly ER-positive. Also increases male breast cancer risk (6–8%).
- **Other high-penetrance genes:** TP53 (Li-Fraumeni), PTEN (Cowden), CDH1 (hereditary diffuse gastric + lobular breast cancer), STK11 (Peutz-Jeghers), PALB2.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** CDH1 germline mutations cause loss of E-cadherin — the same protein lost somatically in sporadic lobular carcinoma. Prophylactic bilateral mastectomy should be discussed in CDH1 carriers, alongside prophylactic gastrectomy for gastric cancer risk.

### Molecular Subtypes

🔵 **The Perou-Sørlie classification** revolutionized breast cancer by revealing it as not one disease but at least four:

| Subtype | ER/PR | HER2 | Ki-67 | Grade | Prognosis | ~Frequency |
|---------|-------|------|-------|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Luminal A** | + | − | Low (<20%) | Low | Best | 40% |
| **Luminal B** | + | ±  | High (≥20%) | High | Intermediate | 20% |
| **HER2-enriched** | − | + | High | High | Poor (before trastuzumab); now improved | 15% |
| **Triple-negative / Basal-like** | − | − | High | High | Worst | 15–20% |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Gene expression assays (Oncotype DX, MammaPrint, Prosigna) now serve as surrogates for molecular subtyping and predict chemotherapy benefit. The TAILORx trial proved that Luminal A tumors with low Oncotype DX recurrence scores (0–25) derive NO benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy — sparing thousands from toxic treatment.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; Robbins 10th Ed, Ch.23*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

🟢 **Most common presentation:** Painless, hard, irregular breast lump — most often in the **upper outer quadrant** (50% of cancers occur here, reflecting greater tissue volume).

🟢 **Clinical features by presentation:**

| Feature | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| **Painless lump** | Most common; hard, irregular, fixed to skin/muscle |
| **Skin dimpling / retraction** | Cooper's ligament involvement |
| **Peau d'orange** | Dermal lymphatic obstruction → skin edema with follicular prominence |
| **Nipple retraction** | Central/subareolar tumors invading ducts |
| **Bloody nipple discharge** | Unilateral, spontaneous, single-duct → investigate (10–15% malignant) |
| **Axillary lymphadenopathy** | Hard, matted, fixed nodes; may be presenting feature |
| **Skin ulceration / fungation** | Locally advanced disease |
| **Inflammatory signs** | Erythema, warmth, swelling → inflammatory breast cancer (T4d) |

🟢 **Screening-detected cancers** (mammographic abnormality without palpable lump) now constitute the majority of diagnoses in countries with established screening programs.

🔵 **Inflammatory breast cancer (IBC):**
- Clinical diagnosis: rapid onset erythema, edema (peau d'orange), warmth involving ≥1/3 of breast skin
- Pathological hallmark: dermal lymphatic tumor emboli
- Represents 2–5% of breast cancers; uniformly stage IIIB (T4d) at minimum
- Often no discrete mass on imaging
- **Must NOT be treated with upfront surgery** — neoadjuvant chemotherapy first

🔵 **Paget's disease of the nipple:**
- Eczematous, erosive lesion of the nipple
- Represents intraepidermal spread of underlying DCIS or invasive carcinoma (90–95% have underlying malignancy)
- Biopsy of the nipple confirms large, pale Paget cells (PAS-positive, HER2 often overexpressed)

🔵 **Male breast cancer:**
- 1% of all breast cancers; median age 65–70
- Usually presents as painless subareolar mass
- Higher proportion ER-positive (~90%); BRCA2 is the most significant genetic risk factor
- Stage-for-stage, outcomes mirror female breast cancer

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.53*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Triple Assessment (Gold Standard)

🟢 **Every breast lump must undergo triple assessment:**

1. **Clinical examination** — Inspection + palpation (lump characteristics, skin changes, nodal status)
2. **Imaging** — Mammography ± ultrasound (± MRI in select cases)
3. **Pathology** — Tissue sampling (core needle biopsy preferred)

*Concordance across all three = reliable diagnosis. Any discordance mandates further workup.*

### Imaging

🟢 **Mammography:**
- **BI-RADS classification** standardizes reporting (0–6)
- Findings: spiculated mass, clustered microcalcifications (especially linear/branching), architectural distortion, asymmetric density
- Sensitivity: ~85% overall; lower in dense breasts (BI-RADS C/D)
- **Screening:** Annual/biennial from age 40–50 (varies by guideline)

| BI-RADS | Assessment | Action |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| 0 | Incomplete | Additional imaging needed |
| 1 | Negative | Routine screening |
| 2 | Benign | Routine screening |
| 3 | Probably benign | Short-interval follow-up (6 months) |
| 4 | Suspicious | Biopsy recommended |
| 5 | Highly suggestive of malignancy | Biopsy required |
| 6 | Known malignancy | Staging/treatment planning |

🔵 **Ultrasound:**
- First-line in women <30 years (dense breast tissue limits mammographic sensitivity)
- Differentiates solid vs. cystic lesions
- Guides percutaneous biopsy
- Evaluates axillary nodes (cortical thickening >3 mm, loss of fatty hilum → suspicious)

🔵 **MRI:**
- Most sensitive modality (Sn ~95–100%) but lower specificity
- Indications: BRCA carriers, dense breasts with high risk, occult primary (axillary nodes positive, mammogram/US negative), assessment of treatment response in neoadjuvant therapy, implant evaluation
- Not a routine screening tool (high false-positive rate → unnecessary biopsies)

### Tissue Diagnosis

🟢 **Core needle biopsy (CNB)** is the standard — provides histological architecture, grade, ER/PR/HER2/Ki-67 status.

🟢 **FNAC** (fine needle aspiration cytology) — can confirm malignancy but cannot distinguish in situ from invasive disease, and cannot provide receptor status. Being replaced by CNB in most centers.

🔵 **Vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB):** For microcalcifications and small lesions; obtains larger tissue volume. Recommended for BI-RADS 3/4a lesions and for suspected DCIS (reduces upgrade rate at surgery).

🟢 **Excisional biopsy** — no longer appropriate as initial diagnostic step; reserved for discordant results or when CNB is non-diagnostic.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.44*

## 6. Classification & Staging

### Histological Classification

🟢 **In situ carcinomas (pre-invasive):**
- **DCIS** (Ductal carcinoma in situ): Proliferation within ducts, intact basement membrane. Grades: low/intermediate/high (comedo necrosis = high grade). Risk of progression to invasive cancer: 30–50% over 10–20 years if untreated.
- **LCIS** (Lobular carcinoma in situ): A risk marker, not an obligate precursor. Confers 8–10× bilateral risk. Management: surveillance ± chemoprevention (tamoxifen/raloxifene).

🟢 **Invasive carcinomas:**

| Type | Frequency | Key Features |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Invasive ductal (NST)** | 70–80% | No special features; variable grade |
| **Invasive lobular** | 10–15% | Single-file ("Indian file") pattern; E-cadherin loss; multifocal/bilateral; often missed on mammogram |
| **Tubular** | 2% | Excellent prognosis; well-differentiated |
| **Mucinous (colloid)** | 2% | Older women; good prognosis |
| **Medullary-like** | 1–5% | Poorly differentiated but paradoxically better prognosis; associated with BRCA1 |
| **Micropapillary** | 1–2% | High lymphovascular invasion; aggressive |

### TNM Staging (AJCC 8th Edition)

🟢 **T — Primary Tumor:**

| Stage | Size/Extent |
|-------|------------|
| Tis | In situ (DCIS, Paget's without invasion) |
| T1 | ≤2 cm (T1mi ≤0.1 cm; T1a >0.1–0.5; T1b >0.5–1; T1c >1–2) |
| T2 | >2–5 cm |
| T3 | >5 cm |
| T4a | Chest wall invasion (not pectoralis muscle alone) |
| T4b | Skin edema/ulceration, satellite skin nodules |
| T4c | Both T4a + T4b |
| T4d | Inflammatory breast cancer |

🟢 **N — Regional Nodes:**

| Stage | Description |
|-------|------------|
| N0 | No regional node metastasis |
| N1 | Movable ipsilateral Level I–II axillary nodes |
| N2 | Fixed/matted axillary nodes OR internal mammary nodes (clinically detected) |
| N3 | Infraclavicular (Level III), or IMN + axillary, or supraclavicular nodes |

🔵 **AJCC 8th Edition innovation — Prognostic Staging:** Incorporates tumor grade, ER/PR, HER2, and gene expression assays (Oncotype DX) into staging. A T2N1 ER+/HER2−/low-grade tumor may be prognostically staged as IA despite anatomical stage IIB. This better reflects actual outcomes.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Anatomical staging alone can be misleading — a triple-negative T1cN0 has worse prognosis than an ER+/grade 1 T2N1. The prognostic staging system was a paradigm shift in the 8th edition (2018), making breast cancer the first solid tumor to incorporate biomarkers into AJCC staging.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; AJCC Cancer Staging Manual 8th Ed*

### A. Surgery — Local Control

#### Breast-Conserving Surgery (BCS) + Radiation

🟢 **The standard of care for early breast cancer.** NSABP B-06 (20-year follow-up) proved that BCS + whole-breast radiation provides **identical survival** to mastectomy.

🟢 **Requirements for BCS:**
- Tumor-to-breast size ratio allows adequate cosmesis
- Negative margins achievable ("no ink on tumor" = adequate margin per SSO/ASTRO 2014 consensus for invasive; 2 mm for DCIS)
- Patient able/willing to receive adjuvant radiation
- No multicentric disease (multiple quadrants)
- No contraindication to radiation (prior chest RT, pregnancy, connective tissue disease)

🔵 **Oncoplastic techniques:** Combine cancer resection with plastic surgery principles (volume displacement/replacement) to allow wider excisions while maintaining cosmesis. Enables BCS for larger tumors that previously required mastectomy.

#### Mastectomy

🟢 **Indications:**
- Patient preference
- Multicentric disease
- Large tumor-to-breast ratio (BCS not feasible)
- Contraindication to radiation
- BRCA carriers (bilateral mastectomy may be discussed)
- Inflammatory breast cancer (after neoadjuvant therapy)

🔵 **Types:**
- **Simple (total) mastectomy:** Breast + nipple-areolar complex; no axillary dissection
- **Modified radical mastectomy (MRM):** Breast + NAC + Level I–II axillary dissection; pectoralis major preserved
- **Skin-sparing mastectomy:** Preserves skin envelope for immediate reconstruction
- **Nipple-sparing mastectomy:** Preserves skin + NAC; oncologically safe if tumor >2 cm from nipple and subareolar tissue negative on frozen section

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The NSABP B-04 trial (1971) was the death knell for the Halsted radical mastectomy. No survival difference was seen among radical mastectomy, total mastectomy + RT, and total mastectomy alone — even at 25-year follow-up. This proved Fisher's hypothesis: breast cancer is a systemic disease at inception, and extent of local surgery does not alter survival.

#### Axillary Management

🟢 **Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB)** is the standard for clinically node-negative patients.
- Identifies the first 1–3 nodes receiving lymphatic drainage from the tumor
- Dual tracer technique: radiocolloid (Tc-99m) + blue dye (isosulfan/methylene blue) or indocyanine green (ICG)
- False-negative rate: 5–8%; identification rate: >95%

🟢 **If SLNB is negative** → No further axillary surgery.

🔵 **If SLNB is positive — the de-escalation revolution:**

| Trial | Scenario | Finding |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **ACOSOG Z0011** (2011) | BCS + RT, 1–2 positive SLNs, cT1–T2 | ALND can be omitted — no survival or regional recurrence difference |
| **AMAROS** (2014) | 1–2 positive SLNs | Axillary RT equivalent to ALND with less morbidity |
| **IBCSG 23-01** | Micrometastases in SLN | ALND adds no benefit |

🟢 **Axillary lymph node dissection (ALND)** — Level I–II clearance. Now reserved for:
- Clinically node-positive disease (confirmed by CNB/FNAC)
- ≥3 positive sentinel nodes
- Patients not meeting Z0011 criteria
- After neoadjuvant chemotherapy with prior biopsy-proven nodal disease (targeted axillary dissection emerging)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The ACOSOG Z0011 trial changed practice more than any other modern breast cancer trial. Thousands of women are now spared the morbidity of ALND (lymphedema rate: 20–30% with ALND vs. 5–7% with SLNB alone).

### B. Radiation Therapy

🟢 **After BCS:** Whole-breast radiation is mandatory (reduces local recurrence from 35% to 10%). Tumor bed boost further reduces recurrence in high-risk patients.

🔵 **Hypofractionation** is now standard (UK START trials, Canadian trial): 40–42.5 Gy in 15–16 fractions over 3 weeks — equivalent to conventional 50 Gy in 25 fractions with less toxicity.

🔵 **Post-mastectomy radiation (PMRT):** Indicated for:
- T3/T4 tumors
- ≥4 positive axillary nodes (strong indication)
- 1–3 positive nodes (now supported by EBCTCG meta-analysis and MA.20)
- Positive margins

🔵 **Regional nodal irradiation (RNI):** Includes supraclavicular ± internal mammary nodes. MA.20 and EORTC 22922 showed DFS benefit for node-positive and high-risk node-negative patients.

🔴 **Accelerated partial breast irradiation (APBI):** Treats only the tumor bed. RAPID, NSABP B-39, and IMPORT LOW trials support its use in low-risk patients (>50 years, ER+, node-negative, unifocal ≤3 cm).

### C. Systemic Therapy

#### Endocrine Therapy (ER+ Disease)

🟢 **Premenopausal:** Tamoxifen 20 mg daily × 5–10 years (ATLAS/aTTom trials: 10 years superior).
- Ovarian function suppression (OFS) + aromatase inhibitor (AI) for high-risk premenopausal patients (SOFT/TEXT trials).

🟢 **Postmenopausal:** Aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole, exemestane) × 5 years. Superior to tamoxifen in DFS.
- Extended adjuvant: MA.17 showed benefit of letrozole after 5 years of tamoxifen.

🔵 **CDK4/6 inhibitors** (ribociclib, abemaciclib, palbociclib):
- MonarchE: Abemaciclib + endocrine therapy in high-risk ER+/HER2− (≥4 nodes, or 1–3 nodes with high grade/Ki-67 ≥20%) — 5% absolute iDFS benefit at 4 years.
- Now standard adjuvant therapy for high-risk ER+/HER2− disease.

#### Chemotherapy

🟢 **Standard regimens:**
- **AC-T:** Doxorubicin/cyclophosphamide × 4 → paclitaxel × 12 weekly (or docetaxel × 4)
- **TC:** Docetaxel/cyclophosphamide × 4 (anthracycline-free option)
- **Dose-dense AC-T** (q2-weekly with G-CSF): Superior to q3-weekly (CALGB 9741)

🔵 **Who needs chemotherapy?**
- Triple-negative: Almost always (except T1aN0)
- HER2+: Almost always (with anti-HER2 therapy)
- ER+/HER2−: Guided by genomic assays
  - **Oncotype DX RS ≤25** (TAILORx) or **MammaPrint low-risk** (MINDACT): Endocrine therapy alone
  - **Oncotype DX RS ≥26** or high-risk genomic profile: Add chemotherapy

#### Anti-HER2 Therapy

🟢 **Trastuzumab** (Herceptin): Monoclonal antibody targeting HER2. Reduces recurrence by 50% and death by 33% in HER2+ disease. Given for 1 year (HERA trial; 6 months noninferior per PERSEPHONE).

🔵 **Pertuzumab:** Added to trastuzumab in the neoadjuvant (NeoSphere) and adjuvant (APHINITY) settings for high-risk HER2+ disease.

🔵 **T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab emtansine):** For patients with residual disease after neoadjuvant anti-HER2 therapy (KATHERINE trial — 50% reduction in recurrence vs. trastuzumab alone).

🔴 **T-DXd (trastuzumab deruxtecan):** Game-changing antibody-drug conjugate effective even in HER2-low (IHC 1+/2+ FISH−) metastatic breast cancer (DESTINY-Breast04). Redefines HER2 as a spectrum, not binary.

#### Immunotherapy (Triple-Negative)

🔵 **Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy** (KEYNOTE-522): Neoadjuvant pembrolizumab added to chemo → higher pCR rates (64.8% vs. 51.2%); continued adjuvant pembrolizumab improves event-free survival. Now standard for stage II–III TNBC.

🔵 **PARP inhibitors** (olaparib): OlympiA trial — adjuvant olaparib for 1 year in BRCA-mutated HER2− breast cancer (high-risk) → 8.8% absolute iDFS benefit. Standard of care.

### D. Neoadjuvant Therapy

🔵 **Rationale:** Downstage tumor to enable BCS; test chemosensitivity in vivo; pathological complete response (pCR) is a prognostic biomarker.

🔵 **Best candidates:** Locally advanced, HER2+, triple-negative (highest pCR rates: 50–70% for HER2+, 40–60% for TNBC).

🔵 **Response-adapted adjuvant therapy:**
- **HER2+ with residual disease** → T-DM1 (KATHERINE)
- **TNBC with residual disease** → Capecitabine (CREATE-X)
- **Achieving pCR** → Excellent prognosis regardless of subtype

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The neoadjuvant approach has transformed clinical trial design. pCR serves as an accelerated endpoint, enabling faster drug approvals. More importantly, it personalizes adjuvant therapy — the same regimen is not given to everyone; treatment is escalated for non-responders and potentially de-escalated for complete responders.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.34; DeVita 12th Ed*

## 8. Special Situations

### DCIS (Ductal Carcinoma In Situ)

🟢 **Treatment:** BCS + radiation (standard) or mastectomy (for extensive disease).
- Low-risk DCIS (low grade, small, wide margins): Active surveillance may be appropriate (LORD trial, COMET trial — ongoing).
- Tamoxifen/AI reduces ipsilateral and contralateral recurrence (NSABP B-24).
- **No role for SLNB** with BCS for DCIS (exception: large DCIS requiring mastectomy — upstaging to invasive cancer occurs in 10–20%).

### Phyllodes Tumors

🔵 **Classification:** Benign / Borderline / Malignant (WHO).
- Wide local excision with ≥1 cm margins (no axillary surgery — spread is hematogenous, not lymphatic).
- Malignant phyllodes: consider adjuvant RT; chemotherapy role unclear.
- Recurrence managed with re-excision or mastectomy.

### Breast Cancer in Pregnancy

🔵 **Principles:**
- Surgery safe in all trimesters
- Chemotherapy safe in 2nd/3rd trimester (AC regimen); avoid in 1st trimester
- Radiation, endocrine therapy, and anti-HER2 agents: contraindicated during pregnancy
- Delivery timing: planned after completion of neoadjuvant chemo, ideally ≥35 weeks

## 9. Complications

### Surgical Complications

🟢 **Post-BCS/mastectomy:**

| Complication | Notes |
|-------------|-------|
| **Seroma** | Most common (up to 30–50%); usually self-resolving; aspiration if symptomatic |
| **Wound infection** | 3–10%; higher with immediate reconstruction, obesity, diabetes |
| **Hematoma** | 2–5%; may require surgical evacuation |
| **Flap necrosis** | Skin-sparing/nipple-sparing mastectomy risk; smoking is major risk factor |
| **Lymphedema** | 5–7% (SLNB) vs. 20–30% (ALND); lifelong risk; compression, physiotherapy |
| **Chronic pain / PMPS** | Post-mastectomy pain syndrome: 20–50%; neuropathic; intercostobrachial nerve injury |
| **Winged scapula** | Long thoracic nerve injury — rare with modern technique |
| **Reduced arm mobility** | More common after ALND; early physiotherapy essential |

### Systemic Therapy Complications

🔵 **Key toxicities to know:**

| Agent | Major Toxicity |
|-------|---------------|
| **Anthracyclines** (doxorubicin) | Cardiotoxicity (dose-dependent, cumulative limit 450 mg/m²) |
| **Trastuzumab** | Cardiotoxicity (reversible, non-cumulative; monitor LVEF q3 months) |
| **Taxanes** | Peripheral neuropathy, neutropenia |
| **Tamoxifen** | Endometrial cancer (2–7×), VTE, hot flashes |
| **Aromatase inhibitors** | Osteoporosis, arthralgias, fractures |
| **CDK4/6 inhibitors** | Neutropenia (palbociclib/ribociclib), diarrhea (abemaciclib) |
| **Pembrolizumab** | Immune-mediated: thyroiditis, pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis |

## 10. Reconstruction — Brief Overview

🔵 **Timing:** Immediate (at time of mastectomy) or delayed.

🔵 **Options:**
- **Implant-based:** Tissue expander → permanent implant. Simplest; not suitable post-radiation (capsular contracture risk).
- **Autologous flap:**
  - TRAM (transverse rectus abdominis myocutaneous) — pedicled or free
  - DIEP (deep inferior epigastric perforator) — muscle-sparing, gold standard for autologous reconstruction
  - Latissimus dorsi flap ± implant
- **Fat grafting:** Adjunctive; for contour refinement

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Radiation significantly impacts reconstruction outcomes. For patients requiring PMRT, delayed reconstruction or autologous flap (rather than implant) is preferred. The timing debate (pre-reconstruction RT vs. post) remains an active area of investigation.

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women worldwide.** Lifetime risk: ~1 in 8.
2. **Triple assessment** = Clinical exam + Imaging + Core biopsy. Mandatory for every breast lump.
3. **Painless, hard, irregular lump in the upper outer quadrant** is the classic presentation.
4. **BCS + radiation = mastectomy** in survival for early breast cancer (NSABP B-06).
5. **Sentinel lymph node biopsy** is standard for clinically node-negative patients. ALND only if indicated.
6. **ER/PR/HER2 status** determines systemic therapy. Always obtain on biopsy.
7. **Long thoracic nerve** → winged scapula; **intercostobrachial nerve** → medial arm numbness. Know these surgical anatomy points.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. ACOSOG Z0011: 1–2 positive SLNs with BCS + RT → ALND can be omitted.
9. Molecular subtypes (Luminal A/B, HER2, TNBC) drive prognosis and therapy more than TNM stage alone.
10. AJCC 8th edition incorporates biomarkers into prognostic staging — a first for solid tumors.
11. Oncotype DX RS ≤25 → endocrine therapy alone; no chemotherapy benefit (TAILORx).
12. Neoadjuvant therapy for LABC, HER2+, TNBC — pCR guides adjuvant escalation/de-escalation.
13. Inflammatory breast cancer: neoadjuvant chemo FIRST → modified radical mastectomy → RT. Never upfront surgery.
14. PMRT indicated for ≥4 positive nodes, T3/T4, positive margins; increasingly for 1–3 positive nodes.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

15. T-DXd (DESTINY-Breast04) redefines HER2-low as a targetable entity — paradigm shift.
16. CDH1 mutations → hereditary lobular breast + diffuse gastric cancer; discuss prophylactic mastectomy + gastrectomy.
17. KATHERINE trial: residual disease after neoadjuvant anti-HER2 → switch to T-DM1 (50% recurrence reduction).
18. OlympiA: adjuvant olaparib for BRCA-mutated, HER2− high-risk breast cancer → 8.8% iDFS benefit.
19. Fisher's hypothesis (breast cancer as systemic disease from inception) remains the intellectual foundation of modern breast cancer management — extent of local surgery does not determine survival.
