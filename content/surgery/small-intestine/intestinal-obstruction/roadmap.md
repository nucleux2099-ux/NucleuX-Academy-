## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Intestinal Obstruction:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Small bowel mesenteric anatomy | — | Adhesions at the mesenteric root, internal hernias, and band adhesions cause mechanical SBO |
| Physiology | Intestinal peristalsis & obstruction physiology | — | Obstruction causes proximal dilation, increased secretion, and bacterial overgrowth — explaining the vomiting, distension, and sepsis |
| Physiology | Third-space fluid losses | — | Massive fluid sequestration in bowel wall and lumen causes hypovolemia — aggressive resuscitation is essential |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Differentiate simple from strangulated obstruction using clinical and radiological features
- Apply the conservative management algorithm — NG decompression, IV fluids, serial examination
- Identify indications for surgery — strangulation, closed-loop, failure of conservative management

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Connection |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| Radiology | X-ray: air-fluid levels, CT for transition point | — | CT abdomen identifies the transition point, cause (adhesion, hernia, tumor), and strangulation signs |
| Pathology | Strangulation & bowel ischemia | — | Closed-loop obstruction compromises mesenteric blood supply → bowel necrosis → perforation — surgical emergency |

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Intestinal obstruction integrates anatomy (adhesion sites, mesenteric vessels), physiology (fluid shifts, electrolyte derangements), pathology (ischemia, necrosis), and radiology (CT transition point). The decision between conservative management and surgery is one of the most critical in general surgery — getting it wrong in either direction has consequences.
