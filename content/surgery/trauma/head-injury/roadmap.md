## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Head Injury:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Meninges — epidural, subdural, subarachnoid spaces | — | EDH = middle meningeal artery (lenticular, lucid interval); SDH = bridging veins (crescent, elderly/anticoagulated) |
| Anatomy | Skull vault & base fracture sites | — | Base of skull fractures cause specific signs — raccoon eyes (anterior fossa), Battle sign (middle fossa), CSF otorrhea |
| Anatomy | Anterior, middle, posterior cranial fossa | — | Impact location determines which fossa is involved and predicts the type of intracranial injury |
| Physiology | Cerebral blood flow & autoregulation | — | Monroe-Kellie doctrine explains raised ICP — brain, blood, CSF in a fixed volume; one increases, others must decrease |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Apply the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) accurately and use it to classify head injury severity
- Differentiate EDH, SDH, SAH, and intracerebral hemorrhage on CT and clinically
- Manage raised ICP — head elevation, osmotherapy (mannitol/hypertonic saline), surgical decompression

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Head injury integrates neuroanatomy (meninges, cranial fossae, vascular anatomy), physiology (Monroe-Kellie doctrine, cerebral autoregulation), radiology (CT pattern recognition), and neurosurgery (craniotomy, decompressive craniectomy). The GCS, developed by Teasdale and Jennett, remains one of medicine's most widely used assessment tools.
