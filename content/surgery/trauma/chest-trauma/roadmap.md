## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Chest Trauma:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Pleural space anatomy | — | Understanding pleural anatomy explains pneumothorax, hemothorax, and the rationale for ICD placement |
| Anatomy | Pericardium — cardiac tamponade | — | The rigid pericardium means small volumes of blood cause tamponade — Beck's triad |
| Anatomy | Rib fracture anatomy | — | Rib fractures at different levels have different associated injuries — upper ribs (great vessels), lower ribs (liver, spleen) |
| Physiology | Flail chest & paradoxical breathing | — | Flail segment causes paradoxical chest wall movement — the underlying pulmonary contusion is the real problem |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Identify the 6 immediately life-threatening chest injuries — tension pneumothorax, open pneumothorax, massive hemothorax, flail chest, cardiac tamponade, tracheobronchial injury
- Perform emergency procedures — needle decompression, ICD insertion, pericardiocentesis
- Determine indications for emergency thoracotomy — massive hemothorax (>1500ml), cardiac tamponade

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **Pneumothorax/Hemothorax** — Specific management of air and blood in the pleural space
2. **Chest Tube Insertion** — ICD insertion is the most common procedure in chest trauma

### Prerequisites

- **Polytrauma Assessment** — Chest trauma assessment is part of the ATLS primary survey

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Chest trauma integrates thoracic anatomy (pleura, pericardium, great vessels), respiratory physiology (flail chest, tension pneumothorax), and ATLS principles. The primary survey approach — treating life-threats as they are found — demonstrates how systematic assessment saves lives. Chest trauma management is primarily non-operative, with ~85% managed by ICD alone.
