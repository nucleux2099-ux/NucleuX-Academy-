## 1. History — From Billroth's Bold Knife to Molecular Subtyping

Gastric cancer has shaped the very identity of abdominal surgery. For centuries, surgeons could only watch as patients wasted away — "cancer ventriculi" was a death sentence. The stomach was considered inviolable territory until the late 19th century shattered that dogma.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1879 | **Jules Péan** — First attempted gastrectomy (patient died on day 5) |
| 1881 | **Theodor Billroth** — First successful distal gastrectomy (Billroth I reconstruction) for pyloric cancer |
| 1885 | **Billroth** — First Billroth II (gastrojejunostomy) reconstruction |
| 1897 | **Karl Schlatter** — First successful total gastrectomy |
| 1962 | **Jinnai & Takahashi** — Codified extended (D2) lymphadenectomy in Japan |
| 1998 | **Kitano** — First laparoscopic-assisted distal gastrectomy |
| 2014 | **TCGA** — Molecular classification of gastric cancer into 4 subtypes |

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47–50; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49*

The divergence between Eastern and Western approaches to gastric cancer — aggressive screening and D2 dissection in Japan/Korea versus late presentation and D1 surgery in the West — remains one of the great fault lines in surgical oncology, and understanding why is essential to mastering this topic.

## 2. Epidemiology

🟢 **Global burden:**
- **5th most common cancer** worldwide; **4th leading cause of cancer death**
- ~1.09 million new cases annually (GLOBOCAN 2020)
- Male:Female ratio approximately **2:1**
- Highest incidence: East Asia (Japan, Korea, China), Eastern Europe, South America
- Declining incidence of distal (intestinal-type) gastric cancer in developed nations over the past century
- **Rising incidence** of proximal (cardia/GEJ) and diffuse-type cancer in young adults — a worrying trend

🟢 **Risk factors:**

| Factor | Association |
|--------|-------------|
| ***H. pylori* infection** | Strongest risk factor; WHO Class I carcinogen; 3–6× risk |
| Dietary (salt, smoked/preserved foods, nitrosamines) | Intestinal type predominantly |
| Smoking | 1.5–2× risk |
| Chronic atrophic gastritis / intestinal metaplasia | Correa cascade |
| Pernicious anemia | 3× risk |
| Prior gastric surgery (Billroth II) | Bile reflux → chronic inflammation; risk peaks 15–20 years post-op |
| Family history (first-degree relative) | 2–3× risk |
| Blood group A | Slightly increased risk (diffuse type) |
| EBV infection | ~10% of gastric cancers globally |
| Obesity | Primarily cardia cancers |

🔵 **Hereditary syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Gene | Gastric Cancer Risk | Key Feature |
|----------|------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Hereditary Diffuse Gastric Cancer (HDGC)** | *CDH1* | 70% lifetime | Prophylactic total gastrectomy recommended |
| **Lynch syndrome (HNPCC)** | MMR genes | 5–10% | Microsatellite instability |
| **FAP / GAPPS** | *APC* | Variable | Fundic gland polyps → dysplasia |
| **Peutz-Jeghers** | *STK11* | 29% | Hamartomatous polyps |
| **Li-Fraumeni** | *TP53* | Increased | Multiple cancer predisposition |

🔴 **SS Pearl — CDH1 and Prophylactic Gastrectomy:** Pathogenic *CDH1* germline mutations carry up to 70% lifetime risk of diffuse gastric cancer. Current IGCLC guidelines recommend **prophylactic total gastrectomy** between ages 20–30 in confirmed carriers. At surgery, ~95% of macroscopically normal stomachs harbor occult signet-ring cell foci on meticulous histological examination — validating the aggressive approach.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47*

## 3. Pathology & Molecular Classification

### Anatomical Distribution

🟢 The stomach is divided into cardia, fundus, body, antrum, and pylorus. Gastric cancers are classified by location:
- **Proximal (cardia/GEJ):** Rising in incidence, behaves more like esophageal adenocarcinoma
- **Body/Fundus:** Often diffuse type
- **Distal (antrum/pylorus):** Most common overall; often intestinal type; declining incidence

### Histological Classification

🟢 **Laurén Classification (1965)** — still the most clinically relevant:

| Feature | Intestinal Type | Diffuse Type |
|---------|----------------|--------------|
| **Histology** | Gland-forming, cohesive | Poorly cohesive, signet-ring cells |
| **Gross appearance** | Polypoid or ulcerative | Infiltrative (linitis plastica) |
| **Precursor** | Correa cascade (chronic gastritis → atrophy → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia) | No known precursor; *de novo* |
| **Risk factors** | *H. pylori*, environmental, dietary | Genetic (*CDH1*), younger age |
| **Epidemiology** | Older males, declining incidence | Younger, equal sex ratio, stable/rising |
| **Prognosis** | Better (stage-for-stage) | Worse; early peritoneal dissemination |

🟢 **Borrmann Classification (macroscopic appearance):**
- **Type I:** Polypoid/fungating
- **Type II:** Ulcerating with raised margins
- **Type III:** Ulcero-infiltrative (most common)
- **Type IV:** Diffusely infiltrative (**linitis plastica**)
- Type V: Unclassifiable

🔵 **WHO Classification:** Papillary, tubular, mucinous, poorly cohesive (including signet-ring cell), mixed, and other rare variants.

### The Correa Cascade

🟢 **The stepwise progression to intestinal-type gastric cancer:**

```
Normal mucosa
  → Chronic active gastritis (H. pylori)
    → Chronic atrophic gastritis
      → Intestinal metaplasia (complete → incomplete)
        → Low-grade dysplasia
          → High-grade dysplasia
            → Invasive adenocarcinoma
```

This cascade takes **decades** and provides a window for screening and intervention. Eradication of *H. pylori* at the gastritis/atrophy stage can reduce cancer risk by ~35–50%.

### Molecular Classification (TCGA 2014)

🔵 **The Cancer Genome Atlas identified 4 molecular subtypes:**

| Subtype | Frequency | Key Features | Therapeutic Implication |
|---------|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **EBV-positive** | 9% | PIK3CA mutations, PD-L1 amplification, extreme CpG island methylation | Best immunotherapy candidates |
| **MSI-high** | 22% | Hypermutation, MLH1 silencing | Excellent response to immunotherapy; better prognosis |
| **Genomically stable (GS)** | 20% | *CDH1*, *RHOA* mutations; diffuse histology | Worst prognosis; poor chemo response |
| **Chromosomal instability (CIN)** | 50% | TP53 mutations, RTK-RAS amplification, intestinal histology | HER2 amplification → trastuzumab |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The EBV and MSI-high subtypes are the "immunotherapy sweet spots" — they have the highest tumor mutational burden and PD-L1 expression. The CheckMate 649 and KEYNOTE-859 trials have confirmed survival benefits of adding nivolumab or pembrolizumab to chemotherapy, with CPS ≥5 as the practical biomarker cutoff. Molecular subtyping is moving from research curiosity to clinical decision-making.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

🟢 **The tragedy of gastric cancer is its silence.** Early gastric cancer (EGC) is nearly always asymptomatic — this is why screening programs in Japan/Korea detect 50–60% of cancers at an early stage, while in the West, >65% present at advanced stages.

🟢 **Symptoms (by frequency in advanced disease):**

| Symptom | Frequency | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Weight loss | 60–70% | Most common presenting symptom |
| Abdominal pain (epigastric) | 50–60% | Vague, persistent, often post-prandial |
| Nausea/vomiting | 30–40% | Especially with distal obstruction |
| Dysphagia | 20–30% | Proximal/cardia tumors |
| Early satiety | 20% | Especially linitis plastica |
| GI bleeding (melena/hematemesis) | 10–15% | Rarely massive |
| Anorexia | Common | |

🟢 **Alarm features (mandate urgent endoscopy):**
- Unintentional weight loss + dyspepsia in patient >55 years
- Dysphagia at any age
- GI bleeding with dyspepsia
- Persistent vomiting
- Iron deficiency anemia (unexplained)

### Physical Examination — Signs of Advanced Disease

🟢 **Eponymous signs (all indicate incurable/metastatic disease):**

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Virchow's node (Troisier's sign)** | Left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy |
| **Sister Mary Joseph nodule** | Periumbilical nodule (peritoneal metastasis) |
| **Krukenberg tumor** | Bilateral ovarian metastasis (signet-ring cell, transcoelomic spread) |
| **Irish's node** | Left axillary lymphadenopathy |
| **Blumer's shelf** | Hard mass in the pouch of Douglas (rectal shelf) on DRE |
| **Ascites** | Peritoneal carcinomatosis |

🔵 **Paraneoplastic presentations:** Acanthosis nigricans (sign of Leser-Trélat), dermatomyositis, membranous nephropathy, Trousseau syndrome (migratory thrombophlebitis), microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.18*

## 5. Diagnosis & Staging

### Endoscopy — The Cornerstone

🟢 **Upper GI endoscopy (OGD) with biopsy** is the gold standard for diagnosis.
- Minimum **6–8 biopsies** from suspicious lesion margins and base
- Sensitivity: 95% with adequate biopsies
- EGC may appear as subtle mucosal irregularity, discoloration, or shallow ulceration

🔵 **Advanced endoscopic techniques:**
- **Chromoendoscopy** (indigo carmine, methylene blue): Enhances mucosal pattern recognition
- **NBI (Narrow Band Imaging):** Highlights vascular and surface patterns without dye
- **EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound):** Essential for T-staging accuracy (80–90%) and nodal assessment; differentiates T1a (mucosal) from T1b (submucosal) — critical for determining eligibility for endoscopic resection

### Laboratory

🟢 **Routine labs:** CBC (anemia), LFTs (liver metastases), albumin (nutritional status), renal function.

🔵 **Tumor markers:** CEA and CA 19-9 are elevated in 30–40% of advanced cases but lack sensitivity/specificity for screening. Useful for monitoring treatment response and recurrence.

### Imaging for Staging

🟢 **CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis (contrast-enhanced):** The standard staging investigation.
- Assesses: primary tumor extent, lymphadenopathy, distant metastases (liver, peritoneum, lung)
- Accuracy for T-staging: 50–70%; for N-staging: 60–70%

🔵 **PET-CT:** Useful for detecting occult distant metastases not seen on CT. Less sensitive for signet-ring/mucinous tumors (low FDG avidity) and peritoneal disease.

🔵 **Diagnostic laparoscopy ± peritoneal cytology:**
- **Recommended for all cT3/T4 or node-positive disease** before committing to major resection
- Detects occult peritoneal disease in **20–30%** of patients staged as M0 on CT
- Positive peritoneal cytology (even without visible deposits) = M1 disease (AJCC 8th edition)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The combination of staging laparoscopy + peritoneal cytology changes management in ~25% of patients who would otherwise undergo futile laparotomy. It is arguably the single most cost-effective staging intervention in gastric cancer. Some centers also use intraperitoneal fluorescence (ICG) during staging laparoscopy for enhanced detection.

### TNM Staging (AJCC 8th Edition)

🟢 **T-staging (depth of invasion):**

| Stage | Depth |
|-------|-------|
| Tis | Carcinoma in situ (intraepithelial, no lamina propria invasion) |
| T1a | Lamina propria or muscularis mucosae |
| T1b | Submucosa |
| T2 | Muscularis propria |
| T3 | Subserosa (without serosal penetration) |
| T4a | Serosa (visceral peritoneum) |
| T4b | Adjacent structures |

🟢 **N-staging:** N0 (0 nodes), N1 (1–2), N2 (3–6), N3a (7–15), N3b (≥16). **Minimum 16 lymph nodes** must be examined for adequate staging.

🟢 **Stage groupings (simplified):**

| Stage | Description | 5-Year Survival |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| IA | T1N0 | 90–95% |
| IB–IIA | T1N1, T2N0 | 70–80% |
| IIB–IIIA | T2N2, T3N1, T4aN0 | 40–60% |
| IIIB–IIIC | T3N3, T4aN2–3, T4b | 10–25% |
| IV | Any T, Any N, M1 | <5% |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.48*

## 6. Treatment — Early Gastric Cancer

🟢 **Definition of EGC:** Tumor confined to mucosa or submucosa (T1), regardless of nodal status.

### Endoscopic Resection

🔵 **Endoscopic Mucosal Resection (EMR)** and **Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD)** are curative options for carefully selected EGC.

🔵 **Absolute indications for ESD (Japanese Gastric Cancer Treatment Guidelines):**
- Differentiated (intestinal) type
- T1a (mucosal), ≤2 cm
- No ulceration
- No lymphovascular invasion

🔵 **Expanded indications:**
- T1a, differentiated, >2 cm without ulceration
- T1a, differentiated, ≤3 cm with ulceration
- T1a, undifferentiated, ≤2 cm without ulceration

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The risk of lymph node metastasis drives the decision. T1a mucosal cancers meeting absolute criteria have **<1% nodal metastasis risk** — making ESD curative. T1b (submucosal invasion) carries 15–25% nodal risk, mandating surgical resection with lymphadenectomy. The "submucosal invasion depth" (SM1 ≤500μm vs. SM2 >500μm) further stratifies risk.

🔵 **After ESD:** Meticulous histological assessment of the resected specimen for:
- Depth of invasion, differentiation, lymphovascular invasion
- Horizontal and vertical margin status
- If non-curative resection → additional surgery required

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.49; Japanese Gastric Cancer Treatment Guidelines (6th Ed)*

## 7. Treatment — Resectable Gastric Cancer

### Surgical Principles

🟢 **Goals of curative surgery:**
1. R0 resection (negative microscopic margins)
2. Adequate lymphadenectomy (≥16 nodes; D2 recommended)
3. Safe reconstruction with acceptable functional outcome

### Extent of Gastrectomy

🟢 **Choice depends on tumor location:**

| Tumor Location | Operation | Reconstruction |
|----------------|-----------|---------------|
| Distal third (antrum/pylorus) | **Subtotal (distal) gastrectomy** | Billroth I, Billroth II, or Roux-en-Y |
| Middle third (body) | Subtotal or total gastrectomy | Roux-en-Y |
| Proximal third (fundus/cardia) | **Total gastrectomy** | Roux-en-Y esophagojejunostomy |
| Linitis plastica | **Total gastrectomy** | Roux-en-Y |

🟢 **Margin requirements:**
- **Intestinal type:** ≥4 cm proximal margin (≥3 cm for T1/T2)
- **Diffuse type:** ≥8 cm proximal margin (higher submucosal spread)
- **Frozen section** of proximal margin intraoperatively is mandatory

🔵 **Subtotal vs. Total gastrectomy:** When oncologically equivalent (i.e., adequate margins achievable), subtotal gastrectomy is **preferred** — equivalent survival with better nutritional outcomes and quality of life.

### Lymphadenectomy — The D-Number System

🟢 **Lymph node stations and dissection extent:**

| Dissection | Nodes Removed | Indication |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **D1** | Perigastric nodes (stations 1–6) | EGC with low risk; Western historical standard |
| **D1+** | D1 + stations 7, 8a, 9, 11p | Reasonable minimum for advanced disease |
| **D2** | D1 + stations 7, 8a, 9, 10, 11p, 11d, 12a | **Standard of care for curable gastric cancer** |
| D3/Para-aortic | D2 + para-aortic nodes (station 16) | No survival benefit (JCOG9501 trial); not recommended |

🔵 **The D2 debate — resolved:**

| Trial | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| **Dutch D1D2 (1999, 15-yr follow-up 2010)** | D2: higher morbidity initially, but **significantly lower gastric-cancer-related death** at 15 years |
| **UK MRC (1999)** | D2: higher morbidity/mortality; no survival benefit — but high rates of splenectomy/pancreatectomy |
| **Italian IGCSG** | D2 safe in experienced centers; trend toward survival benefit |
| **Japanese JCOG9501** | D2 + para-aortic (D3) = no benefit over D2 alone |

🟢 **Current consensus:** D2 lymphadenectomy **without routine splenectomy or distal pancreatectomy** is the global standard for resectable gastric cancer. The early Western trials were confounded by high morbidity from organ resection (spleen, pancreas tail) — when these are spared, D2 is safe with mortality <2% in experienced centers.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The minimum harvest of **16 lymph nodes** is necessary for adequate staging (AJCC). However, Japanese guidelines recommend examining **≥25 nodes** after D2 dissection. Stage migration (Will Rogers phenomenon) must be considered when comparing survival data between centers performing D1 vs. D2 — higher node counts "upstage" patients, artificially improving survival in each stage group.

### Reconstruction

🔵 **After distal gastrectomy:**
- **Roux-en-Y gastrojejunostomy** — Preferred (least bile reflux)
- **Billroth I** (gastroduodenostomy) — Physiological but technically demanding; limited reach
- **Billroth II** (gastrojejunostomy with afferent loop) — Higher bile reflux, afferent loop syndrome risk

🔵 **After total gastrectomy:**
- **Roux-en-Y esophagojejunostomy** — Standard
- Roux limb length: 40–60 cm (prevents bile reflux esophagitis)
- Hunt-Lawrence jejunal pouch may improve quality of life but is not routinely performed

### Minimally Invasive Surgery

🔵 **Laparoscopic gastrectomy:**

| Trial | Conclusion |
|-------|-----------|
| **KLASS-01 (Korea, 2010)** | Lap distal gastrectomy noninferior for EGC (stage I) |
| **JCOG0912 (Japan, 2019)** | Lap distal gastrectomy noninferior for stage I |
| **KLASS-02 (Korea, 2020)** | Lap distal gastrectomy noninferior for locally advanced (stage II/III) |
| **CLASS-01 (China, 2019)** | Lap distal gastrectomy noninferior for advanced disease |

🟢 **Current position:** Laparoscopic distal gastrectomy with D2 lymphadenectomy is an accepted standard in experienced centers. Laparoscopic total gastrectomy is technically more demanding, and trials (KLASS-03, CLASS-02) are ongoing. Robotic gastrectomy offers ergonomic advantages but without proven oncological superiority.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.49–50; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.18*

## 8. Perioperative Therapy — Chemotherapy & Chemoradiation

### Neoadjuvant/Perioperative Chemotherapy

🟢 **The standard of care for locally advanced resectable gastric cancer (≥T2 or N+) is perioperative chemotherapy** — surgery alone is insufficient.

🔵 **Landmark trials:**

| Trial | Regimen | Key Result |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| **MAGIC (2006)** | ECF (epirubicin, cisplatin, 5-FU) peri-op vs. surgery alone | 5-yr OS: 36% vs. 23% — **paradigm-shifting** |
| **FNCLCC/FFCD (2011)** | CF peri-op vs. surgery alone | 5-yr OS: 38% vs. 24% |
| **FLOT4 (2019)** | FLOT (5-FU, leucovorin, oxaliplatin, docetaxel) vs. ECF/ECX | **FLOT superior**: median OS 50 vs. 35 months |

🟢 **FLOT is the current gold-standard perioperative regimen:** 4 cycles pre-op + 4 cycles post-op.

### Adjuvant Therapy

🔵 **When neoadjuvant therapy was NOT given (e.g., surgery-first approach):**

| Trial | Approach | Result |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **ACTS-GC (Japan)** | Adjuvant S-1 for 1 year (after D2) | 5-yr OS: 71.7% vs. 61.1% |
| **CLASSIC (Korea)** | Adjuvant CAPOX (after D2) | 5-yr OS: 78% vs. 69% |
| **INT-0116 (MacDonald, 2001)** | Adjuvant chemoradiation (5-FU/leucovorin + 45 Gy) | Survival benefit — but criticized for inadequate surgery (54% had <D1) |

🔵 **Adjuvant chemoradiation** — role is controversial:
- INT-0116 showed benefit, but many argue it compensated for inadequate lymphadenectomy
- **ARTIST** and **CRITICS** trials: adding RT to adjuvant chemo did not improve survival after D2 dissection
- Current practice: adjuvant RT considered only if D1 was performed or margins are close/positive

🔴 **SS Pearl — Immunotherapy enters the picture:**
- **CheckMate 649:** Nivolumab + chemotherapy improved OS vs. chemo alone in advanced/metastatic disease (CPS ≥5)
- **KEYNOTE-585 & MATTERHORN:** Perioperative pembrolizumab/durvalumab + FLOT in resectable disease — early results show improved pathological complete response
- **Adjuvant nivolumab** after neoadjuvant chemo + surgery (ATTRACTION-5): promising for MSI-H and EBV+ tumors
- We are entering the era of **biomarker-selected perioperative immunochemotherapy**

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.50; DeVita 12th Ed*

## 9. Treatment — Advanced/Metastatic Disease

🟢 **Stage IV gastric cancer is incurable in most cases.** Median survival with best systemic therapy: 12–18 months. Goals shift to palliation and quality of life.

### Systemic Therapy

🔵 **First-line backbone:** Fluoropyrimidine (5-FU or capecitabine) + platinum (cisplatin or oxaliplatin).

🔵 **Targeted/biomarker additions:**
- **HER2-positive (~15–20%):** Add **trastuzumab** (ToGA trial: OS 13.8 vs. 11.1 months)
- **PD-L1 CPS ≥5:** Add **nivolumab** (CheckMate 649) or **pembrolizumab** (KEYNOTE-859)
- **MSI-H/dMMR (~5% of metastatic):** Immunotherapy monotherapy may suffice (pembrolizumab; exceptional responders)
- **CLDN18.2-positive:** **Zolbetuximab** + chemo (SPOTLIGHT/GLOW trials — first-in-class)

🔴 **SS Pearl — Biomarker testing is now mandatory at diagnosis:**
- HER2 (IHC ± FISH)
- PD-L1 (CPS score)
- MSI/MMR status
- *CLDN18.2* expression (emerging)
- *NTRK* fusions, *FGFR2* amplification (rare but actionable)

### Palliative Interventions

🟢 **For obstruction:** Endoscopic stenting (GOO), surgical gastrojejunostomy, or percutaneous gastrostomy.

🟢 **For bleeding:** Endoscopic hemostasis, angiographic embolization, palliative radiotherapy, or palliative resection in selected cases.

🔵 **Palliative gastrectomy:** The REGATTA trial showed **no survival benefit** from palliative gastrectomy followed by chemo vs. chemo alone in stage IV disease. Not recommended routinely.

🔴 **Conversion surgery:** In patients who respond exceptionally to systemic therapy (e.g., disappearance of peritoneal/liver metastases), "conversion gastrectomy" with curative intent is being explored — particularly in Japan/Korea. Not standard but increasingly reported.

## 10. Special Topics

### Gastric Stump Cancer

🔵 Cancer arising in the gastric remnant ≥5 years after partial gastrectomy (usually Billroth II) for benign disease. Mechanism: chronic bile reflux → atrophic gastritis → intestinal metaplasia. Treatment follows standard principles; prognosis is worse due to delayed diagnosis and challenging re-operative surgery.

### Linitis Plastica

🟢 Diffuse infiltration of the stomach wall by poorly cohesive/signet-ring cell carcinoma → thick, rigid, "leather bottle" stomach. Diagnosis often delayed (submucosal spread → normal-appearing mucosa on endoscopy). CT shows diffuse wall thickening. Prognosis is dismal; peritoneal carcinomatosis is nearly universal. Total gastrectomy with wide margins required if attempted; many present at unresectable stage.

### Gastric GIST

🔵 **Not an adenocarcinoma** — arises from interstitial cells of Cajal. Most common mesenchymal tumor of the GI tract; stomach is the most common site (60%).
- **Diagnosis:** Submucosal mass on endoscopy; EUS-guided biopsy; **c-KIT (CD117)** or **DOG1** positive on IHC
- **Surgery:** Wedge resection with negative margins; lymphadenectomy NOT required (unlike adenocarcinoma)
- **Imatinib:** For unresectable, metastatic, or high-risk resected GIST (adjuvant for 3 years per SSGXVIII/AIO trial)
- Risk stratification: **Modified NIH criteria** (size, mitotic count, site, rupture)

### CRS + HIPEC for Peritoneal Metastases

🔴 **Cytoreductive surgery + HIPEC** is investigated for gastric cancer with isolated peritoneal metastases. Unlike colorectal peritoneal disease, evidence in gastric cancer is limited. The **GYMSSA** and **GASTRIPEC** trials showed no clear survival benefit. Patient selection (low PCI score, complete cytoreduction achievable) is critical. Not standard of care but offered at select centers in research settings.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47–50; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49*

## 11. Screening

🔵 **Mass screening is only justified in high-incidence countries:**
- **Japan:** Biennial photofluorography (barium swallow) or endoscopy for all adults ≥50 years — has reduced gastric cancer mortality by ~40%
- **Korea:** Biennial endoscopy or barium study for all adults ≥40 years
- **Not cost-effective** in low-incidence regions (North America, Western Europe)

🔵 **Surveillance for high-risk individuals (worldwide):**
- Extensive atrophic gastritis / intestinal metaplasia: Endoscopy every 3 years (MAPS II guidelines)
- *CDH1* mutation carriers: Prophylactic total gastrectomy or annual chromoendoscopy with random biopsies (Cambridge protocol)
- FAP with fundic gland polyps: Annual endoscopy if dysplasia detected
- Post-gastrectomy (Billroth II for benign disease): Endoscopy starting 15 years post-op

🔴 **SS Pearl — H. pylori eradication as cancer prevention:** The landmark meta-analysis by Ford et al. (2020) confirmed that *H. pylori* eradication reduces gastric cancer incidence by ~46% in asymptomatic infected individuals. This is arguably the most impactful cancer prevention strategy available for gastric cancer — yet global implementation remains poor.

## 12. Postgastrectomy Syndromes

🟢 **Long-term complications after gastrectomy — important for exams and clinical practice:**

| Syndrome | Mechanism | Management |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| **Dumping syndrome (early)** | Rapid gastric emptying → osmotic fluid shift → vasomotor symptoms | Small, frequent meals; low-carb diet; acarbose |
| **Dumping syndrome (late)** | Reactive hypoglycemia (insulin overshoot) | Similar dietary measures; octreotide for refractory |
| **Bile reflux gastritis/esophagitis** | Loss of pyloric mechanism (Billroth II) | Roux-en-Y conversion |
| **Afferent loop syndrome** | Obstruction of afferent limb (Billroth II) | Surgical revision (Roux-en-Y) |
| **Efferent loop syndrome** | Obstruction of efferent limb | Surgical adhesiolysis/revision |
| **Roux stasis syndrome** | Disruption of jejunal pacemaker → functional obstruction | Prokinetics; surgical revision |
| **Nutritional deficiencies** | Iron (loss of acid), B12 (loss of IF), calcium/vitamin D, folate | Lifelong supplementation |
| **Alkaline reflux gastritis** | Bile reflux into gastric remnant | Roux-en-Y conversion |
| **Marginal ulcer** | Anastomotic ulceration | PPIs; exclude *H. pylori*; revise if refractory |

🔵 **Key exam fact:** Dumping syndrome is the most common postgastrectomy syndrome. Early dumping (within 30 minutes) involves vasomotor symptoms (flushing, tachycardia, dizziness) + GI symptoms (cramping, diarrhea). Late dumping (2–3 hours post-meal) presents primarily as hypoglycemia.

🔵 **Lifelong monitoring after total gastrectomy:** B12 injections (mandatory — no intrinsic factor), iron and calcium supplementation, nutritional counseling, surveillance for recurrence.

## 13. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Gastric cancer is the 5th most common cancer globally; *H. pylori* is the strongest modifiable risk factor** (WHO Class I carcinogen).
2. **Laurén classification:** Intestinal type (Correa cascade, gland-forming, better prognosis) vs. Diffuse type (signet-ring, linitis plastica, worse prognosis).
3. **Diagnosis:** OGD with ≥6 biopsies. CT chest/abdomen/pelvis for staging.
4. **Alarm features** (weight loss + dyspepsia >55, dysphagia, GI bleeding, anemia) mandate urgent endoscopy.
5. **Eponymous signs of metastasis:** Virchow's node, Sister Mary Joseph nodule, Krukenberg tumor, Blumer's shelf — all indicate incurable disease.
6. **Curative surgery = gastrectomy with D2 lymphadenectomy** (≥16 nodes examined). Subtotal preferred when adequate margins achievable.
7. **Perioperative FLOT chemotherapy** is standard for locally advanced resectable disease (MAGIC → FLOT4).
8. **Dumping syndrome** is the most common postgastrectomy complication.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. **Staging laparoscopy + peritoneal cytology** changes management in ~25% of patients; recommended for all ≥cT3/N+.
10. **ESD** is curative for selected T1a EGC (differentiated, ≤2 cm, no LVI, no ulcer).
11. **D2 lymphadenectomy without splenectomy/pancreatectomy** is safe in experienced centers (Dutch trial 15-year follow-up).
12. FLOT > ECF/ECX for perioperative chemotherapy (FLOT4 trial).
13. Adjuvant chemoradiation compensates for inadequate surgery — not needed after D2 (ARTIST, CRITICS).
14. **TCGA molecular subtypes** (EBV, MSI-H, GS, CIN) guide emerging therapeutic decisions.
15. **REGATTA trial:** Palliative gastrectomy offers NO survival benefit.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

16. **CDH1 carriers:** Prophylactic total gastrectomy at age 20–30; occult signet-ring foci found in ~95%.
17. **Biomarker-driven therapy:** HER2 (trastuzumab), PD-L1/CPS ≥5 (nivolumab/pembrolizumab), MSI-H (immunotherapy), CLDN18.2 (zolbetuximab).
18. **EBV+ and MSI-H** subtypes are the best immunotherapy candidates.
19. **Stage migration (Will Rogers phenomenon)** confounds East-West survival comparisons.
20. *H. pylori* eradication reduces gastric cancer incidence by ~46% — the most impactful prevention strategy available.
