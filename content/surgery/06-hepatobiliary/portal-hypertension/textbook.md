## 1. History — From Bleeding Veins to TIPS

Portal hypertension has fascinated surgeons and physicians for centuries. Gilbert and Carnot first measured portal pressure in 1902 by direct cannulation during surgery. The devastating clinical consequences — torrential variceal hemorrhage — drove some of the most creative surgical innovations of the 20th century.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1877 | **Nicolai Eck** — First portacaval shunt in dogs (Eck's fistula) |
| 1902 | **Gilbert & Carnot** — First direct portal pressure measurement |
| 1945 | **Blakemore & Lord** — Balloon tamponade (Sengstaken–Blakemore tube) |
| 1945 | **Whipple** — Portacaval shunt for variceal bleeding in humans |
| 1967 | **Warren** — Distal splenorenal shunt (DSRS) — selective decompression |
| 1969 | **Rösch, Hanafee & Snow** — Concept of TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) |
| 1988 | **Richter** — First clinical TIPS placement |
| 1989 | **Starzl** — Liver transplantation established as definitive treatment |

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.79–82; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.130–134*

The evolution from emergency portacaval shunts (with high encephalopathy rates) to selective shunts, then to TIPS, endoscopic therapy, and ultimately transplantation reflects the maturation of hepatobiliary surgery as a discipline.

## 2. Anatomy of the Portal Venous System

🟢 **Portal vein formation:**
The portal vein forms behind the neck of the pancreas by the confluence of the **superior mesenteric vein (SMV)** and the **splenic vein**. It is approximately 8 cm long, runs in the hepatoduodenal ligament (within the free edge of the lesser omentum), posterior to the bile duct and hepatic artery, and enters the liver at the porta hepatis where it divides into right and left branches.

🟢 **Key tributaries:**
- Superior mesenteric vein (drains midgut)
- Splenic vein (drains spleen, receives inferior mesenteric vein and short gastric veins)
- Left gastric (coronary) vein — drains into the portal vein or splenic vein
- Cystic vein, paraumbilical veins

🟢 **Portal-systemic anastomoses** — these are the sites that form collaterals in portal hypertension:

| Site | Portal Tributary | Systemic Tributary | Clinical Consequence |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Gastroesophageal junction** | Left gastric vein, short gastric veins | Esophageal veins → azygos system | **Esophageal/gastric varices** |
| **Rectum** | Superior rectal vein (IMV) | Middle and inferior rectal veins (internal iliac) | **Rectal varices** (NOT hemorrhoids) |
| **Anterior abdominal wall** | Paraumbilical veins | Superior/inferior epigastric veins | **Caput medusae** |
| **Retroperitoneum** | Colic/duodenal veins | Renal, lumbar, gonadal veins | Retroperitoneal varices |
| **Bare area of liver** | Portal branches | Diaphragmatic veins → IVC | Rarely clinically significant |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The left gastric (coronary) vein is the principal feeder of gastroesophageal varices. Its anatomy is variable — it drains into the portal vein in ~80% and the splenic vein in ~20%. In DSRS surgery, ligation of the coronary vein is essential to prevent persistent variceal filling. Preoperative imaging (CT angiography or venous-phase MRI) to map this anatomy is now standard.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.1–2; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54*

## 3. Pathophysiology

### Definition

🟢 **Portal hypertension** is defined as a **hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) ≥6 mmHg**. Normal portal pressure is 5–10 mmHg; normal HVPG is 1–5 mmHg.

🟢 **Clinically significant portal hypertension (CSPH):** HVPG **≥10 mmHg** — the threshold at which varices form and complications develop.

🟢 **Variceal bleeding threshold:** HVPG **≥12 mmHg**.

### The "Backward" and "Forward" Flow Theory

🟢 Portal hypertension results from the interplay of two mechanisms:

```
BACKWARD FLOW (Increased Resistance)
    → Structural: fibrosis, nodules, sinusoidal distortion
    → Functional: sinusoidal vasoconstriction (↓ NO, ↑ endothelin, thromboxane)
    → Accounts for ~70% of intrahepatic resistance

FORWARD FLOW (Increased Portal Inflow)
    → Splanchnic vasodilation (↑ NO, prostacyclins, glucagon)
    → Hyperdynamic circulation
    → ↑ Cardiac output, ↓ SVR
    → Maintains and worsens portal hypertension even after collateral formation
```

🔵 **PG Depth — The NO Paradox:**
In cirrhosis, there is a paradoxical imbalance in nitric oxide (NO) distribution:
- **Intrahepatic:** ↓ NO production (endothelial dysfunction in sinusoids → vasoconstriction → ↑ resistance)
- **Splanchnic/systemic:** ↑ NO production (excess eNOS activation → vasodilation → ↑ portal inflow)

This "NO paradox" is the basis for developing targeted therapies: intrahepatic NO donors and splanchnic NO inhibitors.

🔵 **Hepatic stellate cells (HSCs):** In cirrhosis, quiescent HSCs transform into activated myofibroblasts that contract around sinusoids, increasing vascular resistance. This is the key cellular mediator of the "functional" component of intrahepatic resistance and is the target of anti-fibrotic therapies.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Sinusoidal endothelial dysfunction:** Before fibrosis even develops, loss of sinusoidal endothelial cell fenestrations ("capillarization") and endothelial dysfunction contribute to early portal hypertension. This is why HVPG can be elevated even in early fibrosis (F1–F2) before cirrhosis is established. It opens the door for early pharmacological intervention.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.79; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.130; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.92*

## 4. Classification (Etiology)

🟢 **The anatomical classification remains the most clinically useful:**

### Prehepatic

| Cause | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Portal vein thrombosis** | Most common prehepatic cause; associated with myeloproliferative disorders, cirrhosis, intra-abdominal sepsis, pancreatitis |
| **Splenic vein thrombosis** | "Sinistral" (left-sided) portal hypertension → isolated gastric varices; often due to chronic pancreatitis; cured by splenectomy |
| **Congenital portal vein atresia/stenosis** | Rare; leads to cavernous transformation (portal cavernoma) |

### Intrahepatic

🟢 **Further subdivided into:**

**Presinusoidal:**
- Schistosomiasis (most common cause of portal hypertension worldwide)
- Primary biliary cholangitis, sarcoidosis, congenital hepatic fibrosis
- Idiopathic portal hypertension (non-cirrhotic portal fibrosis)

**Sinusoidal:**
- **Cirrhosis** (most common cause in the West — accounts for >90% of cases)
- Alcoholic hepatitis, MASLD/MASH

**Postsinusoidal:**
- Sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (veno-occlusive disease) — post-bone marrow transplant, pyrrolizidine alkaloid exposure

### Posthepatic

| Cause | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Budd-Chiari syndrome** | Hepatic vein thrombosis; associated with myeloproliferative disorders (JAK2 mutation in 40–50%), PNH, OCP, pregnancy |
| **IVC obstruction/web** | More common in Asia/Africa; membranous obstruction of IVC |
| **Right heart failure** | Constrictive pericarditis, severe TR, restrictive cardiomyopathy |

🔵 **Clinical relevance of classification:**
- **HVPG** (wedged hepatic venous pressure – free hepatic venous pressure) only detects **sinusoidal and postsinusoidal** intrahepatic causes. In presinusoidal and prehepatic causes, HVPG may be normal despite elevated portal pressure.
- **Direct portal pressure measurement** (at surgery or via transhepatic approach) is needed for prehepatic/presinusoidal causes.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Sinistral portal hypertension:** Isolated splenic vein thrombosis (from pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer) causes isolated gastric varices via short gastric veins while the rest of the portal system is normal. The cure is **splenectomy** — not endoscopic therapy, not shunts. Missing this diagnosis is a classic surgical pitfall.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.79–80; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.130*

## 5. Clinical Presentation

🟢 **The major clinical consequences of portal hypertension:**

1. **Variceal hemorrhage** — The most dramatic and lethal complication
2. **Ascites** — Most common complication of cirrhosis
3. **Splenomegaly and hypersplenism**
4. **Hepatic encephalopathy**
5. **Hepatorenal syndrome**
6. **Hepatopulmonary syndrome / Portopulmonary hypertension**

### Variceal Hemorrhage

🟢 **Key facts:**
- 50% of cirrhotics have varices at diagnosis; 5–15% develop new varices annually
- Annual risk of first bleed from large varices: **15–20%**
- 6-week mortality from variceal bleeding: **15–25%** (down from >40% in the 1980s)
- Rebleeding rate without secondary prophylaxis: **60% within 1–2 years**

🟢 **Predictors of variceal bleeding:**
- Variceal size (large > small)
- Red wale marks/cherry red spots on endoscopy
- Severity of liver disease (Child-Pugh C > A)
- HVPG ≥12 mmHg
- Active alcohol use

### Ascites

🟢 **Pathophysiology ("underfill" → "overflow" → peripheral arterial vasodilation hypothesis):**
- Splanchnic vasodilation → ↓ effective arterial blood volume → activation of RAAS, ADH, sympathetic nervous system → renal sodium and water retention → ascites
- SAAG (serum-ascites albumin gradient) **≥1.1 g/dL** indicates portal hypertension as the cause

🔵 **Refractory ascites** (10% of cirrhotics with ascites): Does not respond to maximum diuretic therapy or recurs rapidly after large-volume paracentesis. Options: serial paracentesis, TIPS, or liver transplantation.

### Splenomegaly & Hypersplenism

🟢 Present in up to 70% of cirrhotics. Results from passive congestion → splenic hypertrophy. Leads to sequestration: thrombocytopenia (most common), leukopenia, anemia. **Thrombocytopenia <150,000/μL** is often the earliest laboratory clue to portal hypertension.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.79–82; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Harrison's 22nd Ed, Ch.337*

## 6. Diagnosis & Assessment

### Measuring Portal Pressure

🟢 **HVPG measurement** — the gold standard:
- Performed by hepatic vein catheterization via jugular/femoral approach
- Balloon catheter inflated to measure wedged hepatic venous pressure (WHVP)
- HVPG = WHVP − free hepatic venous pressure (FHVP)
- Normal: 1–5 mmHg
- CSPH: ≥10 mmHg
- Bleeding threshold: ≥12 mmHg
- Target for pharmacotherapy: ≤12 mmHg or ≥20% reduction from baseline

🔵 **Limitations:** Invasive, not universally available, requires expertise. Does not detect prehepatic or presinusoidal causes. Not routinely performed in clinical practice — used mainly in trials and specialized centers.

### Non-invasive Assessment

🟢 **Upper GI endoscopy** — essential for all patients with cirrhosis at diagnosis:
- Grades varices as small (<5 mm) or large (≥5 mm)
- Identifies red wale marks, cherry red spots (high-risk stigmata)
- Classifies gastric varices (Sarin classification — GOV1, GOV2, IGV1, IGV2)

🔵 **Non-invasive surrogates for CSPH:**

| Method | Finding | Interpretation |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Liver stiffness (transient elastography/FibroScan)** | >20–25 kPa | Rules in CSPH (>90% PPV) |
| | <15 kPa | Rules out CSPH |
| **Platelet count** | <150,000/μL | Suggests portal hypertension |
| **Platelet count/spleen diameter ratio** | <909 | Predicts varices (sensitivity ~95%) |
| **CT/MRI** | Varices, splenomegaly, collaterals, ascites | Anatomic assessment |

🔵 **The Baveno VII criteria (2022):** Liver stiffness <15 kPa AND platelet count >150,000/μL → can safely avoid screening endoscopy (risk of varices needing treatment <5%). This "rule of 5" has significantly reduced unnecessary endoscopies.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Liver stiffness >25 kPa by FibroScan is now considered sufficient to diagnose CSPH non-invasively (Baveno VII consensus). Combined with platelet count, it allows risk stratification without HVPG measurement or endoscopy in many patients — a paradigm shift from the invasive-first approach.

### Imaging

🟢 **Doppler ultrasound** — first-line:
- Portal vein diameter >13 mm suggests portal hypertension
- Flow direction: hepatofugal (away from liver) = advanced portal hypertension
- Detects thrombosis, splenomegaly, ascites, collaterals

🔵 **CT angiography (portal venous phase):**
- Maps collateral anatomy (critical before shunt surgery or TIPS)
- Detects portal/splenic/mesenteric vein thrombosis
- Identifies HCC (which may cause portal vein tumor thrombus)
- Assesses liver morphology (caudate hypertrophy, atrophic right lobe — signs of cirrhosis)

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.15, 79; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.130*

### A. Resuscitation

🟢 **Critical initial steps:**
- **Airway:** Endotracheal intubation if massive bleeding, encephalopathy, or hemodynamic instability (protects against aspiration)
- **Volume:** Crystalloid resuscitation; avoid over-resuscitation (raises portal pressure)
- **Restrictive transfusion:** Target Hb **7–8 g/dL** — liberal transfusion worsens portal pressure and increases rebleeding/mortality (Villanueva et al., NEJM 2013)
- **Antibiotics:** Start empirically **before endoscopy** — ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily × 7 days. Reduces infection, rebleeding, and mortality (NNT = 22 for mortality). This is one of the highest-impact interventions.

🟢 **Vasoactive drugs — start IMMEDIATELY upon suspicion:**

| Drug | Mechanism | Dose | Duration |
|------|-----------|------|----------|
| **Terlipressin** | V1 agonist → splanchnic vasoconstriction | 2 mg IV q4h → taper to 1 mg q4h | 2–5 days |
| **Octreotide** | Somatostatin analogue → ↓ splanchnic blood flow | 50 μg bolus, then 50 μg/hr infusion | 2–5 days |
| **Somatostatin** | Direct splanchnic vasoconstriction | 250 μg bolus, then 250 μg/hr | 2–5 days |

🔵 **Terlipressin** is the only vasoactive drug shown to reduce mortality (vs. placebo) in individual RCTs. However, it carries risk of ischemic complications (cardiac, peripheral, intestinal) — contraindicated in IHD, PVD, pregnancy.

### B. Endoscopic Therapy

🟢 **Endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL/banding)** — first-line for esophageal varices:
- Superior to sclerotherapy (lower rebleeding, fewer complications, less stricture formation)
- Bands placed starting at the GEJ, working proximally
- Repeat sessions every 2–4 weeks until variceal obliteration

🟢 **Cyanoacrylate glue injection** — first-line for **gastric varices** (GOV2, IGV1):
- N-butyl-2-cyanoacrylate mixed with Lipiodol (to slow polymerization)
- Achieves hemostasis in >90% of gastric variceal bleeds
- EVL is ineffective for fundal varices (too large, too deep)

🔵 **Sclerotherapy:** Historically important but now largely replaced by EVL. Complications: ulceration, stricture, perforation, mediastinitis. Reserved for settings where banding is unavailable.

### C. Rescue Therapies

🟢 **Balloon tamponade (Sengstaken–Blakemore / Minnesota tube):**
- Achieves hemostasis in >80% — but temporary (bridge to definitive therapy)
- **Maximum inflation time: 24 hours** (esophageal balloon) to prevent pressure necrosis
- Complications: esophageal perforation (2–5%), aspiration, rebleeding on deflation
- Must be placed by experienced operators; keep scissors at bedside for emergency deflation

🔵 **Self-expanding metal stent (SX-Ella Danis stent):**
- Alternative to balloon tamponade; better tolerated, can remain for up to 7 days
- No esophageal balloon → lower perforation risk
- Achieves hemostasis in >90%

🔵 **Rescue TIPS:** If endoscopic therapy fails (failure to control bleeding after 2 endoscopic sessions), **TIPS** is the rescue intervention of choice. Achieves hemostasis in >90% of refractory bleeding.

### D. Preemptive (Early) TIPS

🔴 **The game-changer — preemptive TIPS:**

The landmark trial by García-Pagán et al. (2010, NEJM) demonstrated that **early TIPS within 72 hours** (ideally <24 hours) in **high-risk patients** (Child B with active bleeding at endoscopy, or Child C 10–13) reduced 1-year mortality from 33% to 14%.

**Criteria for preemptive TIPS:**
- Child-Pugh B with active bleeding at index endoscopy
- Child-Pugh C (score 10–13, not >13)
- HVPG ≥20 mmHg (if measured)

This is now a **strong recommendation** in Baveno VII guidelines and represents one of the most significant advances in portal hypertension management.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.81–82; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.131–134*

## 8. Prevention of Variceal Bleeding

### Primary Prophylaxis (Never Bled)

🟢 **Non-selective beta-blockers (NSBBs):**
- **Propranolol** (20–40 mg BID, titrate to resting HR 55–60) or **Nadolol** (40 mg daily)
- Reduce first bleed risk from ~25% to ~15% (40% relative risk reduction)
- Work by ↓ cardiac output (β1) and splanchnic vasoconstriction (β2 blockade → unopposed α1)
- Effective even for small varices with red signs or Child C

🔵 **Carvedilol** (6.25–12.5 mg daily):
- Combined α1 + non-selective β-blocker → additional intrahepatic vasodilation (via α1-blockade → ↑ NO release)
- Superior to propranolol in reducing HVPG
- Better tolerated (fewer dose-limiting side effects)
- Now considered **first-line NSBB** per Baveno VII
- **Caution:** Greater hypotensive effect — avoid if systolic BP <90 mmHg

🟢 **EVL** (banding) as alternative primary prophylaxis:
- For patients intolerant of beta-blockers
- Equally effective as NSBBs for preventing first bleed
- Does NOT reduce mortality (unlike NSBBs which improve overall survival)

🔴 **SS Pearl — The "window hypothesis" for NSBBs:** In advanced decompensated cirrhosis (refractory ascites, SBP, very low MAP), NSBBs may become harmful — reducing cardiac output in patients who are already maximally vasodilated and dependent on cardiac output to maintain perfusion ("too sick for beta-blockers"). The therapeutic window for NSBBs may close in very advanced disease. Baveno VII recommends dose reduction or discontinuation if systolic BP <90 mmHg or MAP <65 mmHg.

### Secondary Prophylaxis (After First Bleed)

🟢 **Combination therapy is mandatory:**
- **EVL + NSBB** — reduces rebleeding from 60% to ~30%
- EVL repeated every 2–4 weeks until obliteration, then surveillance endoscopy every 6–12 months
- NSBB continued indefinitely

🔵 **TIPS** for secondary prophylaxis:
- Reserved for patients who rebleed despite combination EVL + NSBB
- Covered stents (ePTFE-covered) — primary patency >80% at 2 years (vs. 50% for bare metal)
- Reduces rebleeding to <10% but does not improve survival (encephalopathy trade-off)

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.81; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.131*

## 9. TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt)

🟢 **Principle:** Creates a shunt between the hepatic vein and the portal vein through the liver parenchyma, decompressing the portal system.

🟢 **Indications:**
1. Refractory variceal bleeding (rescue or preemptive)
2. Refractory ascites
3. Budd-Chiari syndrome
4. Hepatic hydrothorax
5. Portal vein thrombosis (selected cases)
6. Hepatorenal syndrome (bridge to transplant)

🟢 **Contraindications:**
- Absolute: Right heart failure, severe pulmonary hypertension, uncontrolled sepsis, severe hepatic encephalopathy
- Relative: Child-Pugh >13, MELD >18 (without transplant option), portal vein thrombosis with cavernous transformation

🔵 **Technical aspects:**
- Access via right internal jugular vein → hepatic vein → parenchymal tract → portal vein
- Stent dilated to 8–10 mm diameter
- Target portal pressure gradient <12 mmHg (or >50% reduction)
- **Covered stents (ePTFE)** now standard — significantly reduced stenosis/occlusion rates

🔵 **Complications:**

| Complication | Frequency | Management |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| **Hepatic encephalopathy** | 25–45% | Lactulose, rifaximin; stent reduction if refractory |
| **Shunt stenosis/occlusion** | 15–20% (covered) | Surveillance Doppler; revision |
| **Liver failure** | 5–10% | Shunt reduction; transplant evaluation |
| **Hemolysis** | Rare | Usually self-limited |

🔴 **SS Pearl — Small-diameter TIPS:** Using 8 mm rather than 10 mm stents provides adequate decompression for ascites/bleeding while significantly reducing encephalopathy rates (from ~45% to ~18%). The era of "one-size-fits-all" TIPS is over — tailoring stent diameter to the clinical scenario is now standard practice.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.82; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.134*

## 10. Surgical Shunts — Historical Context & Current Role

🔵 **Largely replaced by TIPS but still relevant in specific situations:**

### Classification of Surgical Shunts

| Type | Examples | Effect on Portal Flow | Encephalopathy Risk |
|------|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Total (non-selective)** | End-to-side portacaval, side-to-side portacaval, mesocaval (Drapanas) | Completely diverts portal flow away from liver | **High (30–50%)** |
| **Partial** | Small-diameter interposition portacaval (8 mm H-graft) | Partially decompresses; maintains some hepatopetal flow | Moderate |
| **Selective** | **Distal splenorenal shunt (DSRS/Warren)** | Decompresses gastrosplenic compartment while maintaining portal perfusion | **Low (10–15%)** |

🔵 **DSRS (Warren shunt):**
- Anastomoses the distal splenic vein to the left renal vein
- Selectively decompresses esophagogastric varices via the short gastric → splenic vein pathway
- Preserves portal perfusion → lower encephalopathy
- Technically demanding; requires experienced surgeon
- Best results in **non-alcoholic cirrhotics with good hepatic reserve (Child A/B)**
- Over time, "pancreatic siphon effect" — collaterals through the pancreas gradually convert it to a non-selective shunt

🟢 **Modern indications for surgical shunts:**
- TIPS unavailable or technically impossible (e.g., complete PV thrombosis with cavernoma)
- Failed/recurrent TIPS with poor anatomy for revision
- Good hepatic reserve (Child A) with recurrent bleeding — particularly in areas without access to transplant
- Budd-Chiari syndrome (side-to-side portacaval or mesoatrial shunt)

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Emory experience:** Long-term data from Emory University showed that DSRS and TIPS had similar survival and rebleeding rates at 5 years, but DSRS had significantly less encephalopathy and fewer reinterventions. In centers with expertise, DSRS remains a durable option for Child A/B patients who are not transplant candidates.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.82; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.133–134*

## 11. Management of Gastric Varices

🔵 **Sarin classification:**

| Type | Description | Prevalence | Management |
|------|-------------|------------|------------|
| **GOV1** | Extension of esophageal varices along lesser curve | 75% of GV | EVL or glue |
| **GOV2** | Extension along greater curve into fundus | 20% | **Cyanoacrylate glue** |
| **IGV1** | Isolated fundal varices (without esophageal varices) | 5% | **Glue, BRTO, or TIPS** |
| **IGV2** | Isolated ectopic varices | Rare | Site-dependent |

🔵 **BRTO (Balloon-occluded Retrograde Transvenous Obliteration):**
- Elegant technique for fundal varices fed by a gastrorenal shunt
- Balloon catheter inserted via femoral/jugular vein → left renal vein → gastrorenal shunt → injects sclerosant retrograde into varices
- Obliterates gastric varices while **preserving** portal flow (unlike TIPS)
- May worsen esophageal varices and ascites (loss of the decompressing shunt)
- Particularly popular in Japan; gaining traction worldwide

🔴 **SS Pearl — IGV1 and sinistral portal hypertension:** Always check for splenic vein thrombosis when encountering isolated fundal varices. If confirmed, **splenectomy** is curative — no need for TIPS, BRTO, or lifelong endoscopic surveillance.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.81; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.131*

## 12. Ascites Management — Surgical Perspective

🟢 **Stepwise approach:**

```
Step 1: Sodium restriction (≤2 g/day) + Spironolactone (100 mg) ± Furosemide (40 mg)
    ↓ (escalate if needed)
Step 2: Maximum diuretics (Spironolactone 400 mg + Furosemide 160 mg)
    ↓ (if refractory)
Step 3: Serial large-volume paracentesis (LVP) + albumin (6-8 g per liter removed)
    ↓
Step 4: TIPS (if suitable candidate)
    ↓
Step 5: Liver transplantation
```

🔵 **TIPS for refractory ascites:**
- Reduces ascites recurrence, reduces need for paracentesis
- Individual RCTs show improved transplant-free survival vs. repeated LVP
- Risk of encephalopathy: ~30–50%
- Best for patients with MELD <18 and adequate cardiac function

🔵 **Peritoneovenous shunts (LeVeen, Denver):** Largely abandoned due to high complication rates (DIC, shunt occlusion, SBP, superior vena cava thrombosis). Only occasionally used when TIPS is impossible and transplant is not an option.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.80; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.54*

## 13. Special Situations

### Non-Cirrhotic Portal Hypertension (NCPH)

🔵 **Key entities:**
- **Extrahepatic portal vein obstruction (EHPVO):** Common in children; cavernous transformation; presents with well-preserved liver function + variceal bleeding + splenomegaly. Treatment: endoscopic therapy + NSBB. The **Rex shunt** (mesenteric-left portal vein bypass) can restore physiological portal flow in children.
- **Non-cirrhotic portal fibrosis (NCPF)/Idiopathic portal hypertension:** Seen in India, Japan. Periportal fibrosis without cirrhosis. Obliterative portal venopathy. Good hepatic reserve; main risk is variceal bleeding.
- **Schistosomiasis:** Presinusoidal fibrosis (Symmers' pipe-stem fibrosis). Preserved liver function. Massive splenomegaly. Responds well to endoscopic therapy + NSBB.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Rex shunt in children:** The meso-Rex bypass (superior mesenteric vein to left portal vein interposition graft) is the only procedure that restores physiological portal flow to the liver. It reverses hypersplenism, reduces variceal bleeding, and may improve neurocognitive outcomes (portal-systemic shunting in children causes subclinical encephalopathy). It is now the preferred definitive surgery for EHPVO in children when anatomy permits.

### Budd-Chiari Syndrome

🔵 **Stepwise management:**
1. **Anticoagulation** — for all patients (unless contraindicated)
2. **Angioplasty/stenting** — for short-segment hepatic vein or IVC stenosis
3. **TIPS** — for diffuse thrombosis with progressive liver failure
4. **Liver transplantation** — for fulminant presentation or failed TIPS

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.83; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.130*

## 14. Liver Transplantation — The Definitive Solution

🟢 **Liver transplantation is the only treatment that addresses both the portal hypertension AND the underlying liver disease.**

🟢 **When to refer for transplant:**
- MELD score ≥15
- First episode of variceal bleeding in a Child C patient
- Refractory ascites
- Hepatorenal syndrome
- Hepatocellular carcinoma within Milan criteria

🔵 **Impact on portal hypertension:**
- Immediate normalization of portal pressure after successful graft reperfusion
- Varices regress within weeks to months
- Prior TIPS does not compromise transplant (covered stents can be removed or incorporated)
- Prior surgical shunts can make transplant technically challenging (particularly portacaval shunts near the hilum — DSRS is the most transplant-friendly shunt)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** In the era of TIPS and transplant, surgical shunt surgery has been called a "dying art." But in resource-limited settings without access to TIPS or transplant programs, well-performed surgical shunts remain life-saving. The DSRS, in particular, offers durable decompression with low encephalopathy — a "transplant-friendly" shunt that remains relevant.

## 15. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Portal hypertension** = HVPG ≥6 mmHg; **clinically significant** ≥10 mmHg; **bleeding risk** ≥12 mmHg.
2. **Cirrhosis** is the most common cause in the West; **schistosomiasis** is the most common cause worldwide.
3. Pathophysiology = increased intrahepatic resistance (structural + functional) + increased portal inflow (splanchnic vasodilation).
4. Major complications: **variceal bleeding, ascites, splenomegaly, encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome.**
5. **Acute variceal bleed management:** Resuscitate (target Hb 7–8) → IV antibiotics + vasoactive drugs BEFORE endoscopy → EVL (esophageal) or glue (gastric) within 12 hours.
6. **Primary prophylaxis:** NSBBs (carvedilol preferred) or EVL for medium/large varices.
7. **Secondary prophylaxis:** EVL + NSBB combination is mandatory.
8. **TIPS** is the rescue for failed endoscopic therapy and refractory ascites.
9. **Liver transplantation** is the definitive treatment.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

10. The "NO paradox" — decreased intrahepatic NO (vasoconstriction) vs. increased splanchnic NO (vasodilation) — drives both components of portal hypertension.
11. **Baveno VII:** Liver stiffness <15 kPa + platelets >150K → can avoid screening endoscopy. Carvedilol is now first-line NSBB.
12. **Preemptive TIPS** (within 72 hours) in high-risk patients (Child B active bleed, Child C 10–13) reduces mortality by 45%.
13. **Restrictive transfusion** (Hb 7–8) in variceal bleeding reduces rebleeding and mortality vs. liberal strategy.
14. **DSRS** remains relevant for Child A/B non-transplant candidates — lower encephalopathy than total shunts or TIPS.
15. **Sinistral portal hypertension** (splenic vein thrombosis) → isolated gastric varices → cure = splenectomy.
16. HVPG only detects sinusoidal/postsinusoidal causes — normal HVPG does NOT exclude prehepatic portal hypertension.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

17. Sinusoidal endothelial dysfunction precedes fibrosis — portal hypertension begins before cirrhosis is established.
18. The "window hypothesis" for NSBBs: in very advanced cirrhosis (refractory ascites, low MAP), beta-blockers may become harmful.
19. Small-diameter TIPS (8 mm) reduces encephalopathy while maintaining efficacy — tailored approach is the new standard.
20. Rex shunt (meso-Rex bypass) in children with EHPVO restores physiological portal flow — the only truly "curative" shunt.
21. BRTO is an emerging alternative to TIPS for isolated gastric varices, preserving portal flow while obliterating varices.
22. Covered TIPS stents have transformed long-term patency (>80% at 2 years) — bare metal stents are obsolete.
