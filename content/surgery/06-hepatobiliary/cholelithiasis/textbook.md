## 1. History — From Ancient Affliction to Laparoscopic Revolution

Gallstones have tormented humans since antiquity — Egyptian mummies and Greek medical texts attest to a disease as old as civilization. For centuries, treatment was limited to herbal concoctions and prayer. The first surgical intervention came in 1743, when **Jean-Louis Petit** lanced an inflamed gallbladder through the abdominal wall, drained the stones, and allowed it to fistulize — the first cholecystostomy. In 1867, **John Stough Bobbs** of Indianapolis performed the first deliberate cholecystostomy, opening a stone-filled gallbladder during laparotomy and removing the calculi.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1743 | **Petit** — First cholecystostomy (drainage of gallbladder) |
| 1867 | **Bobbs** — First deliberate cholecystostomy |
| 1882 | **Carl Langenbuch** — First cholecystectomy (Berlin); argued the gallbladder should be removed "not because it contains stones, but because it forms them" |
| 1911 | **William Mayo** — Declared the "innocent gallstone is a myth" |
| 1932 | **Admirand & Small** (later, 1968) — Defined the physicochemical basis of cholesterol stone formation |
| 1985 | **Erich Mühe** — First laparoscopic cholecystectomy (Böblingen, Germany) |
| 1995 | **Strasberg** — Described the Critical View of Safety (CVS) |
| 2018 | **Tokyo Guidelines (TG18)** — Standardized severity grading of acute cholecystitis |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Mühe presented his laparoscopic work to the German Surgical Society in 1986 and was met with skepticism — his contribution was only formally recognized by SAGES in 1999. The French surgeons Mouret and Dubois, who performed their laparoscopic cholecystectomies slightly later, initially received the credit.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.33; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.115*

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

### Embryology

🟢 The gallbladder, cystic duct, and extrahepatic biliary tree develop from the **caudal bud (pars cystica)** of the hepatic diverticulum, which appears around **day 18** of embryonic life. By day 26, the cystic diverticulum is recognizable; by the end of the **4th week**, the cystic duct and gallbladder are formed. The intrahepatic biliary system develops later (after the 7th week) from the ductal plate surrounding the portal vein branches.

🔵 **Key embryological points:**
- The ventral pancreatic bud is closely associated with the pars cystica — this shared origin explains the anatomical intimacy at the ampulla of Vater
- Ductal plate malformations lead to Caroli disease, choledochal cysts, and polycystic liver disease
- The biliary tree is patent at all stages — biliary atresia is NOT a failure of recanalization

### Anatomy

🟢 **Gallbladder:** A pear-shaped organ on the inferior surface of the liver between segments IVB and V. Divided into **fundus, body, infundibulum, and neck**. Capacity ~50 mL. The fundus projects beyond the liver edge at the right 9th costal cartilage (tip of the 9th rib).

🟢 **Critical wall anatomy:** Five layers — epithelium, lamina propria, smooth muscle, perimuscular connective tissue, serosa. **No muscularis mucosae or submucosa** — this is why T-staging of gallbladder cancer has profound management implications (T1a = lamina propria only → cholecystectomy sufficient; T1b = muscular layer → extended resection needed).

🟢 **Triangle of Calot (Hepatocystic Triangle):** Bounded by the cystic duct inferiorly, common hepatic duct medially, and inferior liver edge superiorly. Contains the cystic artery and cystic lymph node (Lund's node).

🔵 **Arterial supply:** Cystic artery typically arises from the right hepatic artery (76%) within the triangle of Calot. Variants include origin from the left hepatic, common hepatic, gastroduodenal, or superior mesenteric arteries. A "caterpillar hump" right hepatic artery traversing the triangle occurs in 5–15% — a trap during cholecystectomy.

🔵 **Biliary variations:** The "typical" left + right hepatic duct confluence forming the common hepatic duct occurs in only 56–68% of patients. Key dangerous variants:
- Right posterior sectoral duct draining into the left hepatic duct (8–20%)
- Trifurcation at the confluence (12–18%)
- Low-inserting right posterior duct into the common hepatic duct (4–7%)

🔴 **SS Pearl — Rouvière's Sulcus:** An inconstant cleft on the right liver surface marking the plane of the right portal pedicle. Strasberg described the "line of safety" — an imaginary line from Rouvière's sulcus to the hilar plate junction — all dissection during cholecystectomy should remain **above** this line.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.112; Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.2*

## 3. Pathophysiology — How Stones Form and Gallbladders Inflame

### Gallstone Types

🟢 **Three types of gallstones:**

| Type | Composition | Frequency (West) | Associations |
|------|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Cholesterol** | >50% cholesterol monohydrate crystals | ~80% | Obesity, female sex, rapid weight loss, pregnancy, OCP, fibrates |
| **Black pigment** | Calcium bilirubinate polymer | ~15% | Hemolytic disorders (sickle cell, spherocytosis), cirrhosis, ileal disease |
| **Brown pigment** | Calcium bilirubinate + bacterial cell bodies | ~5% | Biliary stasis, infection, parasites (endemic in Asia) |

### Cholesterol Stone Pathogenesis — Admirand's Triangle

🟢 **Admirand and Small (1968)** demonstrated that cholesterol solubility in bile depends on the relative concentrations of three components: **cholesterol, bile salts, and lecithin (phospholipids)**. When plotted on a triangular coordinate system, bile that falls outside the micellar zone is **supersaturated** — cholesterol precipitates.

🟢 **The "lithogenic triad" for cholesterol stones:**
1. **Hepatic hypersecretion of cholesterol** (supersaturation)
2. **Nucleation** — mucin glycoproteins and other pronucleating factors accelerate crystal formation
3. **Gallbladder hypomotility** — stasis allows crystals to aggregate into stones

🔵 **Risk factors (the "5 Fs" and beyond):** Female, Forty, Fat, Fertile, Fair — plus rapid weight loss, bariatric surgery, TPN, octreotide, ceftriaxone (forms calcium-ceftriaxone precipitates in bile in 25–45% of patients), ileal resection/Crohn's (depletes bile salt pool).

🔴 **SS Pearl — Microbiome:** The gut microbiome influences bile acid metabolism and gallstone formation. Germ-free mice show increased cholesterol gallstone formation; fecal microbiota transplant normalizes this. The gut-liver-bile axis is an emerging frontier.

### Pathogenesis of Cholecystitis

🟢 **Acute calculous cholecystitis (90–95% of cases):**
```
Gallstone impacts in cystic duct/infundibulum
    → Gallbladder distension
    → Mucosal edema & acute inflammation
    → Venous and lymphatic congestion
    → Cystic artery thrombosis
    → Ischemia → gangrene → perforation
```
The **fundus** is most vulnerable to ischemia (farthest from the cystic artery blood supply). Secondary bacterial infection (E. coli, Klebsiella) occurs in ~20% of cases.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.33–34; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.112, 115*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Biliary Colic

🟢 Intermittent, self-limited episodes caused by transient gallstone impaction:
- **Location:** Epigastric or RUQ pain, often radiating to right scapula/shoulder
- **Character:** Constant (misnomer "colic"), crescendo pattern lasting 30 min to several hours
- **Trigger:** Fatty meals in ~50% (not reliable)
- **Resolution:** Spontaneous dislodgement of stone
- **No fever, no leukocytosis, no peritoneal signs**

### Acute Cholecystitis

🟢 Persistent RUQ pain (>6 hours) + systemic inflammation:
- **Murphy's sign:** Inspiratory arrest during subcostal palpation of the RUQ — the single most useful bedside sign (sensitivity ~65%, specificity ~87%)
- **Sonographic Murphy's sign** — direct pressure with the ultrasound probe over the gallbladder — more reliable than clinical Murphy's
- Fever, leukocytosis, elevated CRP
- Mild elevation of bilirubin and transaminases possible (doesn't necessarily indicate CBD stones)

### Chronic Cholecystitis

🔵 Recurrent episodes of biliary colic → chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and a contracted, thick-walled gallbladder with stones. Patients describe intermittent RUQ pain with "flat" periods between. Gallbladder may become shrunken and nonfunctional.

### Courvoisier's Law

🟢 **A palpable, nontender gallbladder in the presence of jaundice is unlikely due to gallstones** — it suggests malignant obstruction of the CBD (e.g., periampullary carcinoma). Gallstone disease produces a fibrotic, contracted gallbladder that cannot distend.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.55*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Laboratory

🟢 **Biliary colic:** Labs typically normal
🟢 **Acute cholecystitis:** Leukocytosis (12,000–15,000), elevated CRP; mild bilirubin and ALP elevation possible
🔵 **Red flags for complications:** Bilirubin >4 mg/dL (CBD stone), lipase elevation (gallstone pancreatitis), markedly elevated WBC >20,000 (gangrenous/emphysematous cholecystitis)

### Imaging

🟢 **Ultrasound — First-line for ALL suspected biliary pathology:**

| Finding | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Gallstones (echogenic foci + posterior acoustic shadow) | Sensitivity 98% for stones |
| Wall thickening >4 mm | Inflammation (also seen in ascites, hypoalbuminemia, hepatitis) |
| Pericholecystic fluid | Acute cholecystitis; may indicate perforation |
| Sonographic Murphy's sign | Positive = probe tenderness directly over the gallbladder |
| Dilated CBD >7 mm (>10 mm post-cholecystectomy) | Suggests choledocholithiasis |

🟢 **HIDA scan (hepatobiliary scintigraphy):** Tc-99m–labeled IDA derivatives are taken up by hepatocytes and excreted in bile. **Non-visualization of the gallbladder at 60 minutes = cystic duct obstruction** → consistent with acute cholecystitis. Sensitivity 96%, specificity 90%. Most useful when US is equivocal.

🔵 **MRCP:** Non-invasive delineation of biliary anatomy. Best for suspected CBD stones, Mirizzi syndrome, biliary anomalies. Sensitivity and specificity >95% for CBD stones >6 mm.

🔵 **CT:** Not first-line for cholecystitis but useful for complications (perforation, emphysematous cholecystitis, gallstone ileus, gallbladder cancer). Detects only ~75% of gallstones (many are isodense).

🔵 **EUS:** Best sensitivity for small CBD stones (<5 mm) and for distinguishing sludge from stones. Useful when MRCP is equivocal.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.16, 18, 34; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.113, 115*

## 6. Classification

### Tokyo Guidelines (TG18) — Severity Grading of Acute Cholecystitis

🟢 **Diagnostic Criteria (TG18):**
- **A.** Local signs: Murphy's sign / RUQ mass / RUQ tenderness
- **B.** Systemic signs: Fever >38°C, elevated CRP, elevated WBC
- **C.** Imaging: US findings of cholecystitis
- **Suspected:** One item from A + one from B
- **Definite:** One item from A + one from B + C confirmed

🟢 **Severity Grading:**

| Grade | Criteria | 30-Day Mortality | Management |
|-------|----------|------------------|------------|
| **I — Mild** | No organ dysfunction, mild inflammatory changes | 1.1% | Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy |
| **II — Moderate** | WBC >18,000, palpable RUQ mass, symptom duration >72 h, marked local inflammation (gangrenous, pericholecystic abscess, biliary peritonitis) | 0.8% | Early lap chole ± drain; consider subtotal if difficult |
| **III — Severe** | Organ dysfunction: cardiovascular (hypotension requiring vasopressors), neurological, respiratory, renal, hepatic, or hematologic | 5.4% | Urgent biliary drainage (percutaneous cholecystostomy); cholecystectomy when stable |

### Mirizzi Syndrome Classification

🔵 **Mirizzi syndrome** = bile duct obstruction from a gallstone impacted in the cystic duct or gallbladder infundibulum. Occurs in 0.3–3% of cholecystectomies.

| Type | Description | Management |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **I** | Extrinsic compression of CHD by impacted stone (no fistula) | Cholecystectomy (partial if needed) |
| **II** | Cholecystobiliary fistula involving <1/3 of CBD circumference | Cholecystectomy + closure over T-tube or primary repair |
| **III** | Fistula involving 1/3–2/3 of CBD circumference | Cholecystectomy + bilioenteric anastomosis (hepaticojejunostomy) |
| **IV** | Fistula involving >2/3 or complete destruction of CBD wall | Bilioenteric reconstruction |
| **V** | Any type + cholecystoenteric fistula (± gallstone ileus) | Address fistula + stone + biliary reconstruction |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Mirizzi syndrome is associated with gallbladder carcinoma. When unrecognized preoperatively, laparoscopic approach leads to high conversion and complication rates. If suspected, consent should include bilioenteric reconstruction.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34, 37; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.115*

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management

🟢 All patients with acute cholecystitis:
- NPO, IV fluids, parenteral analgesia
- Antibiotics covering enteric gram-negatives and anaerobes (e.g., cefazolin + metronidazole, piperacillin-tazobactam)
- Discontinue antibiotics within 24 hours post-cholecystectomy for uncomplicated disease (TG18)
- Continue 4–7 days if gangrenous, perforated, or emphysematous (TG18)

### Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy — The Gold Standard

🟢 Standard four-port technique: umbilical (camera), subxiphoid, right midclavicular, right anterior axillary.

🟢 **Critical View of Safety (CVS) — Strasberg, 1995:** The most important concept in safe cholecystectomy. Three criteria must ALL be met:
1. The hepatocystic triangle is cleared of all fat and fibrous tissue
2. Only **two structures** are seen entering the gallbladder (cystic duct + cystic artery)
3. The **lower 1/3 of the cystic plate** (gallbladder bed) is exposed

🔵 **Doublet view:** CVS must be confirmed both anteriorly AND posteriorly. A recent study of 160 online cholecystectomy videos found only **1 video** achieved a passing CVS score — highlighting how poorly it is implemented in practice.

🔵 **Bailout strategies for the difficult gallbladder:**
- **Top-around (fundus-first) mobilization:** Dissection counterclockwise starting from the medial gallbladder border → fundus → lateral border. Safer than traditional "top-down" which drives dissection toward the portal pedicle
- **Subtotal cholecystectomy:** Fenestrating (leave posterior wall open, cauterize mucosa) or reconstituting (close remnant). Fenestrating → higher bile leak but lower recurrent cholelithiasis; reconstituting → vice versa
- **Intraoperative cholecystostomy tube:** Decompress and bail out; come back later
- **Conversion to open:** Not a failure — mature surgical judgment

🔵 **Adjuncts:** Intraoperative cholangiography (IOC) when anatomy is unclear; near-infrared fluorescent cholangiography (ICG) as an emerging tool for biliary mapping.

### Timing of Surgery

🟢 **Early cholecystectomy (<72 hours) is preferred** for acute cholecystitis — multiple RCTs confirm no increase in morbidity, shorter total hospital stay, and avoidance of the 15–30% failure rate of medical management while awaiting delayed surgery.

🔵 **ACDC trial (Gutt et al., 2013):** 618 patients randomized to early (<24 hrs) vs. delayed (7–45 days) laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Early surgery: morbidity 12% vs. 34%, hospital stay 5 vs. 10 days. No difference in conversion rates or bile duct injury.

### Percutaneous Cholecystostomy

🔵 Reserved for TG18 Grade III patients unfit for surgery. US-guided, under local anesthesia. >80% show immediate clinical improvement. Tube kept ≥6 weeks before interval cholecystectomy or removal after confirmed cystic duct patency. Recurrence rate after tube removal without cholecystectomy: 10–21%.

### CBD Stone Management

🔵 **Risk stratification for choledocholithiasis:**
- **High risk** (CBD stone on US, clinical ascending cholangitis, bilirubin >4): Pre-op ERCP
- **Moderate risk** (dilated CBD, elevated LFTs): MRCP or EUS → ERCP if positive
- **Low risk:** Proceed with cholecystectomy ± IOC

🔵 **Options for CBD clearance:**
- ERCP + sphincterotomy (most common in current practice)
- Laparoscopic CBD exploration (single-stage; shorter LOS and lower cost than two-stage ERCP + cholecystectomy)
- Open CBD exploration (uncommon; for failed endoscopic/laparoscopic approaches)

🔴 **SS Pearl — CHOCOLATE Trial (2012):** RCT comparing laparoscopic CBD exploration vs. pre-op ERCP for concomitant gallstones + CBD stones. Lap CBD exploration was non-inferior with shorter hospital stay. Supports single-stage surgery in centers with expertise.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34, 36, 37, 38; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.115, 116; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.48, 52*

## 8. Complications

### Of Gallstone Disease

🟢 **Gangrenous cholecystitis:** Full-thickness necrosis, most commonly at the fundus. More common in diabetics and elderly. CT: intramural gas, absent wall enhancement, intraluminal membranes.

🟢 **Empyema:** Pus-filled gallbladder. Clinical picture of sepsis. Urgent cholecystectomy or percutaneous drainage.

🟢 **Emphysematous cholecystitis:** Gas-forming organisms (Clostridium, E. coli) in the gallbladder wall. Male predominance. Diabetics. Plain X-ray/CT shows gas in gallbladder wall/lumen. Cover Clostridium + emergent cholecystectomy.

🔵 **Gallstone ileus:** Mechanical small bowel obstruction from gallstone erosion through a cholecystoenteric fistula (usually cholecystoduodenal). Typically elderly women; stone usually impacts at the ileocecal valve.
- **Rigler's triad:** (1) Pneumobilia, (2) SBO, (3) Ectopic gallstone — seen on plain films in only ~30%; CT far more sensitive
- **Treatment:** Enterolithotomy + stone extraction; examine entire bowel for additional stones. Fistula takedown and cholecystectomy usually deferred (one-stage in fit patients only)
- **Bouveret syndrome:** Gallstone impaction in the duodenum → gastric outlet obstruction (rare variant)

### Of Cholecystectomy

🟢 **Bile duct injury (BDI):** The most feared complication. Incidence 0.3–0.5%. 30-day mortality 2%. "Classic injury" = CBD mistaken for cystic duct. **CVS and bailout strategies are the best prevention.** If identified intraoperatively: place drain, close, transfer to an HPB center.

🔵 **Bile leak:** From cystic duct stump, duct of Luschka, or accessory duct. Presents with pain, fever, bilious drain output. ERCP + stent usually sufficient.

🔵 **Postcholecystectomy diarrhea:** Continuous bile flow into the gut → bile salt malabsorption → secretory diarrhea. Treat with cholestyramine.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34, 36, 42; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.115, 120*

## 9. Special Topics

### Cholecystitis in Pregnancy

🟢 Most common non-obstetric surgical emergency. Gallstone incidence rises due to progesterone-mediated gallbladder hypomotility and estrogen-driven cholesterol supersaturation.

🔵 **SAGES guidelines:** Laparoscopic cholecystectomy can be safely performed in **any trimester**, including late third trimester. Delaying surgery leads to recurrent symptoms in 92% (first trimester presentation), 64% (second), 44% (third).

### Acute Acalculous Cholecystitis

🔵 5–15% of all acute cholecystitis. Occurs in critically ill patients (ICU, trauma, burns, sepsis, TPN). Pathogenesis: gallbladder ischemia + biliary stasis. Male predominance. High rate of gangrene and perforation (50–60%) due to delayed diagnosis. Treatment: percutaneous cholecystostomy in the unstable; cholecystectomy if fit.

### Porcelain Gallbladder

🔵 Calcification of the gallbladder wall. Historically cited as having a 25% gallbladder cancer risk — this is now considered an overestimate. Selective mucosal calcification may carry higher risk than diffuse intramural calcification. **Cholecystectomy is still recommended** given the cancer association, though absolute risk is likely 2–7%.

### Gallbladder Polyps

🟢 **Management algorithm:**
- **≥10 mm** → Cholecystectomy (risk of malignancy ~25%)
- **6–9 mm + risk factors** (age >60, sessile, PSC, Indian/South American ethnicity) → Cholecystectomy
- **6–9 mm, no risk factors** → Surveillance US at 6 months, 1 year, 2 years
- **≤5 mm, no risk factors** → No follow-up required
- **Growth ≥2 mm on surveillance or reaching 10 mm** → Cholecystectomy

### Incidental Gallbladder Cancer

🔴 Found in 0.2–3% of cholecystectomy specimens. **T-stage–based management:**

| T Stage | Depth | Management |
|---------|-------|------------|
| Tis / T1a | Mucosa / Lamina propria | Cholecystectomy alone (ADEQUATE) |
| T1b | Muscular layer | Extended (radical) cholecystectomy: liver wedge (segments IVB/V) or 2 cm margin + portal lymphadenectomy |
| T2 | Perimuscular connective tissue | Extended cholecystectomy + portal lymphadenectomy |
| T3/T4 | Serosa / liver / adjacent organs | Extended resection if R0 achievable; often poor prognosis |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Port-site excision was historically recommended for incidental gallbladder cancer but is no longer routine unless there was intraoperative gallbladder perforation or bile spillage, which significantly increases carcinomatosis risk (24% vs. 4%).

🔴 **Xanthogranulomatous cholecystitis:** A destructive chronic inflammatory condition with foamy histiocytes that can mimic gallbladder cancer on imaging (asymmetric wall thickening, elevated CA 19-9). May require intraoperative frozen section. Referral to HPB surgeon if diagnostic doubt exists.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34, 49 (Ch.119); Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.115, 118*

## 10. Other Important Topics

### Asymptomatic Gallstones — To Operate or Not?

🟢 **The consensus: observation.** Only 10–30% of asymptomatic patients develop symptoms over decades; 0–8% develop complications. Gracie & Ransohoff (1982) followed 123 patients for 24 years: 13% developed biliary symptoms, 2% had complications — "the innocent gallstone is not a myth."

🔵 **Exceptions requiring prophylactic cholecystectomy:** Porcelain gallbladder, stones >3 cm, sickle cell disease/spherocytosis, heart transplant recipients, certain high-risk populations (Native Americans).

### Gallstones and Bariatric Surgery

🔵 Rapid weight loss after bariatric surgery increases gallstone risk (~15–30%). Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) prophylaxis for 6 months reduces cholecystectomy rate from 25% to 10%. Routine concomitant cholecystectomy is NOT recommended (Choosing Wisely guidelines) unless symptomatic.

### Ceftriaxone-Induced Biliary Sludge

🔵 Ceftriaxone precipitates as calcium salt in bile in 25–45% of patients. Reversible on drug cessation. Clinically relevant: can cause biliary colic or cholecystitis, especially in children.

### Landmark Trials

| **ACDC** | 2013 | Early (<24 h) vs. delayed cholecystectomy: early = lower morbidity (12% vs. 34%), shorter LOS |
| **CHOCOLATE** | 2012 | Lap CBD exploration non-inferior to pre-op ERCP; shorter hospital stay |
| **TG18** | 2018 | Standardized diagnosis + severity grading of acute cholecystitis |

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Gallstones affect 10–15% of the Western adult population.** Cholesterol stones (80%) are the most common type.
2. **Admirand's triangle:** Cholesterol solubility depends on the ratio of cholesterol : bile salts : lecithin. Supersaturation → nucleation → stone formation.
3. **Biliary colic** = transient cystic duct obstruction (self-limited, no fever). **Acute cholecystitis** = persistent obstruction + inflammation (Murphy's sign +, fever, leukocytosis).
4. **Courvoisier's law:** Palpable, nontender gallbladder + jaundice → think malignant obstruction, not stones.
5. **Ultrasound is the first-line investigation** for all suspected biliary disease (sensitivity 98% for stones).
6. **HIDA scan** confirms acute cholecystitis when US is equivocal (non-filling of gallbladder = cystic duct obstruction).
7. **Laparoscopic cholecystectomy within 72 hours** is the standard of care for acute cholecystitis.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **Tokyo Guidelines (TG18)** classify acute cholecystitis into Grades I–III; Grade III with organ dysfunction requires percutaneous cholecystostomy first.
9. **Critical View of Safety (Strasberg)** has three criteria — clear hepatocystic triangle, only two structures entering gallbladder, lower 1/3 of cystic plate exposed. Mandatory for every cholecystectomy.
10. **Subtotal cholecystectomy** (fenestrating or reconstituting) is the recommended bailout when CVS cannot be achieved — safer than forging ahead.
11. **Mirizzi syndrome** ranges from extrinsic compression (Type I) to complete CBD destruction (Type IV) — management escalates accordingly.
12. **Gallstone ileus:** Rigler's triad (pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic stone). Enterolithotomy first; fistula takedown deferred in most patients.
13. **Pregnancy:** Lap cholecystectomy is safe in all trimesters (SAGES). Delaying surgery leads to high recurrence rates.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **Rouvière's sulcus** and the "line of safety" define the boundary below which no dissection should occur during cholecystectomy.
15. **CHOCOLATE trial** supports single-stage laparoscopic CBD exploration over two-stage ERCP + cholecystectomy in expert centers.
16. **Incidental gallbladder cancer management** is dictated by T-stage: T1a = cholecystectomy alone; T1b and beyond = extended resection + lymphadenectomy.
17. **Porcelain gallbladder cancer risk** is likely 2–7% (not 25% as historically taught) — but cholecystectomy is still recommended.
18. **Gallbladder wall has NO submucosa** — this unique histology explains rapid T-stage escalation and the management differences between T1a and T1b cancers.
