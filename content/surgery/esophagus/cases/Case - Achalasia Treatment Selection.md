# Case Scenario: Achalasia Treatment Selection

## Clinical Vignette

### Initial Presentation

**Patient:** 52-year-old male executive

**Chief Complaint:** "I've had trouble swallowing for about 3 years, but it's getting worse"

**History of Present Illness:**
- Progressive dysphagia to solids for 3 years
- Now also difficulty with liquids ("paradoxical dysphagia")
- Regurgitation of undigested food, especially at night
- Learns to eat very slowly, drink water frequently
- Occasional substernal chest discomfort, non-exertional
- Weight loss: 6 kg over past year
- Recent episode of aspiration pneumonia (2 months ago)

**Review of Systems:**
- Denies heartburn (no classic reflux)
- No odynophagia
- Occasional bad breath (halitosis)
- Sleep disrupted by coughing/regurgitation
- No joint pain, skin changes, or Raynaud's

**Past Medical History:**
- Hypertension - on lisinopril
- Hyperlipidemia - on atorvastatin
- Aspiration pneumonia (recent)
- No prior GI procedures

**Family History:**
- Father: Prostate cancer, alive
- Mother: Type 2 diabetes
- No GI malignancies

**Social History:**
- Executive at IT company (travels frequently)
- Former smoker (quit 10 years ago)
- Social alcohol (1-2 drinks/week)

## Clinical Reasoning: Phase 1

### Stop and Think

**Question 1:** What is the diagnosis and why is subtyping important?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**Diagnosis: Type II Achalasia**

Criteria met:
1. IRP ≥15 mmHg (28 mmHg) → EGJ outflow obstruction ✓
2. 100% failed peristalsis → No normal contractions ✓
3. Panesophageal pressurization ≥20% (70%) → Type II ✓

**Why subtyping matters:**

| Subtype | Success Rate | Treatment Notes |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Type I | 85-90% | All options work |
| **Type II** | **90-95%** | **Best responder - any treatment** |
| Type III | 60-90% | POEM preferred |

Type II indicates:
- Intact esophageal muscle (still generates pressure)
- Problem is only the LES (not opening)
- Once LES released → esophagus can empty
- **Excellent prognosis with any treatment**

</details>

**Question 2:** What treatment options are available for this patient?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**Treatment Options for Type II Achalasia:**

1. **Pneumatic Dilation (PD)**
   - Success: 85-90% (may need repeat)
   - Least invasive
   - 2-3% perforation risk
   - Outpatient

2. **Laparoscopic Heller Myotomy (LHM) + Dor**
   - Success: 90-95%
   - Includes anti-reflux wrap
   - 1-3 day hospital stay
   - Established (30+ years)

3. **POEM**
   - Success: 90-95%
   - No external incisions
   - 1-2 day hospital stay
   - Higher GERD (35-50%)

**For Type II: All three are equivalent in efficacy**
→ Patient factors guide selection

</details>

### Decision Analysis

**Question 3:** What additional factors should influence this patient's decision?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**Key Decision Factors:**

1. **Aspiration pneumonia history**
   - Needs definitive treatment (not observation)
   - Effective esophageal emptying critical
   - All surgical options address this

2. **Career/travel demands**
   - Quick recovery important
   - POEM: 3-7 days to work
   - LHM: 1-2 weeks recovery
   - PD: 1-2 days, but may need repeat

3. **GERD risk tolerance**
   - Currently no reflux symptoms
   - POEM: 35-50% will develop GERD
   - LHM: 15-25% with Dor wrap
   - Needs to accept long-term PPI if chooses POEM

4. **Durability**
   - At age 52, wants lasting solution
   - PD may need repeat (20-30%)
   - LHM/POEM more durable

5. **Personal preference**
   - Some prefer no external incisions (POEM)
   - Some prefer anti-reflux protection (LHM)

</details>

## Patient's Decision

After counseling, the patient states:

"I understand the GERD risk with POEM is higher. But I travel constantly for work and really can't afford 2 weeks off. I'm okay with taking a PPI if needed. I'd like to go with POEM."

## Procedure and Outcome

### POEM Operative Report

```
PERORAL ENDOSCOPIC MYOTOMY (POEM)
═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DATE: [Scheduled]
ANESTHESIA: General endotracheal

PROCEDURE:

1. INSPECTION: Dilated esophagus, cleared of debris

2. MUCOSAL INCISION:
   - Location: Posterior wall (5 o'clock), 12 cm above EGJ
   - Submucosal lift with methylene blue-saline
   - 2 cm longitudinal incision

3. SUBMUCOSAL TUNNEL:
   - Created using spray coagulation
   - Continued to 3 cm below EGJ onto gastric cardia
   - Clear blue-stained submucosal plane throughout
   - Total tunnel length: 15 cm

4. MYOTOMY:
   - Started 3 cm below mucosal entry
   - Selective circular muscle division
   - Continued to 3 cm below EGJ
   - Full-thickness circular myotomy
   - Longitudinal fibers preserved
   - Total myotomy: 12 cm

5. MUCOSAL CLOSURE:
   - Entry closed with 6 endoscopic clips
   - Watertight closure confirmed

FINDINGS:
- No mucosal perforation
- No bleeding
- Excellent separation of muscle layers

EBL: Minimal
COMPLICATIONS: None

DISPOSITION: Recovery room, stable

═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Alternative Scenario

### What if LHM was chosen?

```
LHM + DOR ALTERNATIVE SCENARIO
══════════════════════════════

Procedure:
• 5 laparoscopic ports
• Myotomy: 6 cm esophageal + 3 cm gastric
• Dor fundoplication (180° anterior wrap)
• Operative time: 90 minutes

Hospital course:
• Day 1: Clear liquids
• Day 2: Soft diet, ambulating
• Day 3: Discharge

Recovery:
• 1-2 weeks off work
• 4-6 weeks to full activity

Outcomes:
• Success rate: 90-95% (same as POEM)
• GERD rate: 15-25% (lower than POEM)
• Less PPI dependence

This patient's Type II status means either would work well.
The choice came down to recovery time and GERD tolerance.
```
