## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Esophageal Manometry:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Physiology | Esophageal motility physiology | — | Normal peristaltic patterns and LES function must be understood before interpreting manometry tracings |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Interpret key HRM parameters — integrated relaxation pressure (IRP), distal contractile integral (DCI)
- Apply the Chicago Classification v4.0 to categorize esophageal motility disorders
- Identify indications for manometry in preoperative workup (anti-reflux surgery, achalasia)

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

*Clinical connections are integrated within the core topic.*

## ➡️ What's Next

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **Achalasia** — Manometry is THE diagnostic test for achalasia — classifies subtypes
2. **Motility Disorders** — HRM identifies DES, jackhammer esophagus, and other motility disorders

### Prerequisites

- **Esophageal Anatomy** — Structural anatomy provides context for manometry probe positioning
- **LES Physiology** — Understanding normal LES pressure is essential for interpreting abnormal findings

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Esophageal manometry connects physiology (motility patterns, LES function) to clinical decision-making (surgical planning). The Chicago Classification is a prime example of how physiological measurement drives diagnosis and treatment — making this a bridge between basic science and surgical practice.
