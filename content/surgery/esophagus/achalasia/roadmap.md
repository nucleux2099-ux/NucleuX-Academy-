## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Achalasia:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Esophageal anatomy & LES | — | The LES zone and its relationship to the diaphragmatic hiatus determines surgical approach |
| Physiology | Esophageal motility & peristalsis | — | Absence of peristalsis and failed LES relaxation are the hallmarks — understanding normal motility is essential |
| Physiology | Myenteric plexus & parasympathetic innervation | — | Auerbach plexus degeneration is the pathological basis — explains why the LES fails to relax |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Classify achalasia subtypes using the Chicago Classification (Types I, II, III)
- Interpret high-resolution manometry findings — IRP, DCI, and contractile patterns
- Compare treatment options — pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM — and their indications

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Connection |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| Radiology | Barium swallow — bird-beak sign | — | Classic radiological finding; also shows dilated esophageal body and absent peristalsis |
| Pathology | Ganglion cell degeneration | — | Loss of inhibitory ganglion cells (VIP, NO-producing) causes unopposed LES contraction |

## ➡️ What's Next

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **Heller Myotomy** — Gold standard surgical treatment — laparoscopic cardiomyotomy
2. **POEM Procedure** — Emerging endoscopic alternative with equivalent short-term outcomes
3. **Achalasia: LHM vs POEM** — Comparative decision-making for surgical vs endoscopic approach
4. **Esophageal Manometry** — Essential diagnostic tool — Chicago classification defines subtypes

### Prerequisites

- **Esophageal Anatomy** — Foundation knowledge of esophageal course, blood supply, and LES
- **LES Physiology** — Understanding normal LES function clarifies what goes wrong in achalasia

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Achalasia bridges physiology (LES mechanics, enteric nervous system), pathology (ganglion cell loss), radiology (barium swallow, manometry), and surgery (myotomy). The Chicago Classification revolution shows how physiological measurement directly drives surgical decision-making — Type II responds best to intervention.
