# Retrieval Cards: Esophageal Reconstruction

## Card Set

### Card 1: Conduit Priority
**Q: What is the order of preference for esophageal replacement conduits?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Conduit Hierarchy:**

1. **Gastric conduit** (FIRST CHOICE)
2. **Colon interposition** (Second choice)
3. **Jejunal interposition** (Third/last choice)

**Memory aid: "GAS before COAL"**
- Gastric = Always preferred, Single anastomosis
- Colon = Only if stomach unavailable
- Jejunum = Last resort

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 2: Gastric Conduit Blood Supply
**Q: Which vessels are preserved and which are divided for gastric conduit creation?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**PRESERVE (Right is Right):**
- **Right gastric artery**
- **Right gastroepiploic artery**

These form the vascular arcade along the greater curve.

**DIVIDE:**
- **Left gastric artery** (allows mobilization)
- **Short gastric vessels** (mobilizes fundus)

**Memory: "Right is right, Left and Short must go"**

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 3: Colon Indications
**Q: What are the indications for colon interposition?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Colon Interposition Indications (GFCC):**

1. **G**astrectomy (previous - no stomach available)
2. **F**ailed gastric conduit
3. **C**austic injury involving stomach
4. **C**hildhood long-gap esophageal atresia

**Key point:** Only use colon when stomach is unavailable or unsuitable

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 4: Anastomosis Location
**Q: Compare cervical vs intrathoracic anastomosis.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

| Feature | Cervical | Intrathoracic |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **Leak rate** | 10-15% (higher) | 3-5% (lower) |
| **If leak occurs** | Drains to neck (safer) | Mediastinitis (devastating) |
| **Stricture** | More common | Less common |
| **Overall safety** | Safer location | Higher risk if complications |

**Memory: "Cervical leaks drain, thoracic leaks kill"**

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 5: Colon Blood Supply
**Q: What is the blood supply for colon interposition?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Blood Supply Options:**

**Left Colon (most common):**
- Left colic artery
- **Marginal artery of Drummond** (KEY)

**Right Colon:**
- Middle colic artery
- Ileocolic artery

**Key point:** Marginal artery of Drummond is the critical vessel that must be assessed preoperatively (colonoscopy ± angiography)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 6: Pyloroplasty
**Q: Why is pyloroplasty often performed with gastric conduit reconstruction?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Reason: Vagotomy effect**

During gastric conduit creation:
- Vagus nerves are often divided or damaged
- This causes **delayed gastric emptying**
- Pyloroplasty/pyloromyotomy ensures gastric drainage

**Options:**
1. Heineke-Mikulicz pyloroplasty
2. Finney pyloroplasty
3. Jaboulay pyloromyotomy
4. Botox injection (less permanent)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 7: Number of Anastomoses
**Q: How many anastomoses are required for each conduit type?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

| Conduit | Anastomoses | Details |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Gastric** | **1** | Esophago-gastric |
| **Colon** | **3** | Esophago-colic, Colo-jejunal, Colo-colic |
| **Jejunum** | **2-3** | Esophago-jejunal, Jejuno-jejunal (Roux) |

**Key point:** Single anastomosis is a major advantage of gastric conduit

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 8: Anastomotic Leak
**Q: When does anastomotic leak typically occur and what are the signs?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Timing:** Days 5-10 post-operatively

**Signs:**
- Fever
- Tachycardia
- Leukocytosis
- Neck swelling/drainage (cervical)
- Pleural effusion (thoracic)
- Sepsis

**Diagnosis:**
- Contrast swallow study
- CT with oral contrast

**Management depends on location:**
- Cervical: Open/drain, NPO, antibiotics
- Thoracic: Stent, surgery, or drainage (emergency!)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 9: Stricture
**Q: What is the most common late complication and how is it treated?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Most common late complication: ANASTOMOTIC STRICTURE**

**Incidence:** 10-30%

**Timing:** 2-6 months post-op

**Causes:**
- Ischemia
- Leak with healing
- Reflux
- Radiation

**Treatment:**
1. **Endoscopic dilation** (first-line)
2. Multiple sessions usually needed
3. Refractory: Stent or surgical revision

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 10: Surgical Approaches
**Q: Name the main surgical approaches for esophagectomy.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

| Approach | Description | Anastomosis |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Ivor Lewis** | Right thoracotomy + laparotomy | Intrathoracic |
| **McKeown (3-field)** | Neck + chest + abdomen | Cervical |
| **Transhiatal** | Abdomen + neck (no thoracotomy) | Cervical |
| **Left thoracoabdominal** | Left thoracotomy | Low thoracic |
| **MIE** | Minimally invasive | Varies |

**Selection based on:** Tumor location, patient factors, surgeon preference

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 11: Conduit Necrosis
**Q: What is conduit necrosis and how does it present?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Definition:** Complete vascular compromise of the conduit

**Incidence:** 1-3% (rare but devastating)

**Presentation:**
- Rapid clinical deterioration
- Sepsis
- Dark/necrotic drainage
- EGD shows necrotic mucosa

**Management:** EMERGENCY SURGERY
1. Takedown anastomosis
2. Cervical esophagostomy (spit fistula)
3. Remove necrotic conduit
4. Jejunostomy for feeding
5. Delayed reconstruction (months later)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 12: Routes for Colon
**Q: What routes can be used for colon interposition?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Routes:**

1. **Retrosternal** (most common for benign disease)
   - Avoids posterior mediastinum
   - Used after caustic injury
   - Separate from native esophagus

2. **Posterior mediastinal** (orthotopic)
   - In esophageal bed after esophagectomy
   - Shorter route
   - Standard for cancer

3. **Subcutaneous** (rarely used)
   - Last resort
   - Poor cosmesis

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 13: Dumping Syndrome
**Q: What is dumping syndrome and how is it managed?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Cause:** Rapid gastric emptying after reconstruction

**Types:**

| Type | Timing | Symptoms |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Early** | 15-30 min | Vasomotor (sweating, palpitations, dizziness) |
| **Late** | 1-3 hours | Hypoglycemia (weakness, confusion) |

**Management:**
- Small, frequent meals
- Avoid simple sugars/liquids with meals
- Increase protein/fiber
- Acarbose for late dumping
- Octreotide for severe cases

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 14: Gastric Conduit Advantages
**Q: What are the advantages of gastric conduit over other options?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Advantages of Gastric Conduit:**

1. **Single anastomosis** (vs 3 for colon)
2. **Reliable blood supply** (single vascular axis)
3. **Adequate length** (reaches pharynx)
4. **Technical simplicity**
5. **Lower leak rate** than colon
6. **Robust tissue** quality

**Disadvantages:**
- No sphincter (reflux)
- Loss of reservoir
- Dumping syndrome
- Aspiration risk

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 15: Caustic Injury Timing
**Q: When should reconstruction be performed after caustic injury?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Timing:**

| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| Acute | 0-2 weeks | Supportive care, assess extent |
| Subacute | 2-6 weeks | **AVOID surgery** (friable tissue) |
| Chronic | **>6 months** | Safe for reconstruction |

**Key points:**
- Wait for tissue healing/fibrosis
- Assess stomach involvement (may need colon)
- Retrosternal route often preferred
- Serial dilations may be tried first

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 16: Supercharged Conduit
**Q: What is a supercharged gastric conduit?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Definition:** Microvascular augmentation of gastric conduit blood supply

**Technique:**
- Anastomose vessels at gastric tip to:
  - Left internal mammary artery
  - Internal jugular vein
  - Cervical vessels

**Indication:** Concerns about gastric tip perfusion

**Purpose:**
- Reduces anastomotic complications
- Especially useful for long conduit pulls
- Requires microvascular expertise

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 17: Outcomes Comparison
**Q: Compare the outcomes of gastric vs colon conduits.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

| Outcome | Gastric | Colon |
|---------|---------|-------|
| **Operative mortality** | 3-5% | 5-10% |
| **Anastomotic leak** | 5-10% | 10-15% |
| **Stricture** | 10-20% | 15-25% |
| **Hospital stay** | 10-14 days | 14-21 days |
| **Technical complexity** | Easier | Complex |
| **Long-term function** | Good | Good |

**Key:** Similar long-term function, but gastric has fewer early complications

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 18: Jejunum Indications
**Q: When would you use jejunal interposition?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Jejunal Interposition Indications:**

1. **Short segment replacement** (limited reach)
2. **Pharyngeal reconstruction** (free flap)
3. **After total gastrectomy** (no stomach)
4. **When colon unavailable** (disease, previous surgery)

**Limitations:**
- Mesenteric length constraint
- Multiple anastomoses
- Free jejunal flap requires microvascular expertise

**Advantage:** No acid production, active peristalsis

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High
