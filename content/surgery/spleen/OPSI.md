# OPSI: Prevention & Emergency Management

> **Source**: Maingot's Part VII, CDC Guidelines
> **Topic System**: [[Spleen Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> OPSI is a life-threatening fulminant sepsis in asplenic/hyposplenic patients, typically caused by encapsulated organisms, with 50-70% mortality if not treated within hours of onset.

### Key Points

1. **Lifetime risk**: 0.5-1% per year (highest in first 2 years, but lifelong risk)
2. **Organisms**: Encapsulated bacteria (Strep pneumoniae > H. influenzae > N. meningitidis)
3. **Mortality**: 50-70% even with treatment; death within 24-48 hours
4. **Prevention**: Vaccinations + education + standby antibiotics + medic alert
5. **Management**: Immediate empiric antibiotics at ANY sign of fever

### Clinical Pearl
> [!danger] "Any fever is an emergency"
> Asplenic patients must seek immediate care and receive empiric antibiotics at the FIRST sign of infection. Delay can be fatal.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Asplenic Patients Are Vulnerable

**Splenic Functions Lost:**

| Function | Consequence |
|----------|-------------|
| **Filtration of bacteria** | Encapsulated organisms escape |
| **IgM production** | Poor initial antibody response |
| **Opsonization** | Can't coat bacteria for phagocytosis |
| **Complement activation** | Alternative pathway impaired |
| **Splenic macrophages** | No backup to clear bloodstream |

### At-Risk Populations

| Condition | Risk Level |
|-----------|------------|
| **Surgical splenectomy** | High |
| **Traumatic splenectomy** | High |
| **Functional asplenia** | |
| - Sickle cell disease | Very high |
| - Celiac disease | Moderate |
| - Alcoholic liver disease | Moderate |
| - Bone marrow transplant | High initially |
| **Congenital asplenia** | Very high |

### Causative Organisms

```
ENCAPSULATED ORGANISMS (SHiN)

    Streptococcus pneumoniae (50-90%)
           │
           │ Most common! Requires polysaccharide 
           │ capsule opsonization by spleen
           ▼
    Haemophilus influenzae type b (5-15%)
           │
           ▼
    Neisseria meningitidis (5-10%)

Other organisms:
- Capnocytophaga canimorsus (dog/cat bites)
- Babesia (tick-borne)
- Plasmodium (malaria)
- Salmonella
```

### Clinical Presentation

**Warning: Rapid progression!**

```
TIMELINE OF OPSI:

Hour 0:     Mild prodrome (fever, chills, myalgia)
            - May seem like "flu"
            
Hour 2-6:   Rapid deterioration
            - High fever, rigors
            - Hypotension
            - Altered mental status
            
Hour 6-12:  Fulminant sepsis
            - DIC (petechiae, purpura)
            - Multi-organ failure
            - Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome
            
Hour 12-48: Death (50-70% mortality)
```

**Key Features:**
- Often starts with non-specific symptoms
- RAPID progression (flu to death in 24h)
- DIC common (bleeding, purpura fulminans)
- Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen)

### Prevention Strategies

#### 1. Vaccinations (Critical!)

**Timing:**
- Elective splenectomy: 2 weeks BEFORE surgery
- Emergency splenectomy: 2 weeks AFTER surgery

**Vaccines Required:**

| Vaccine | Primary Series | Boosters |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| **Pneumococcal** | PCV13 then PPSV23 (8 weeks apart) | PPSV23 every 5 years |
| **H. influenzae type b** | Single dose | None needed for adults |
| **Meningococcal** | MenACWY + MenB | MenACWY every 5 years |

**Annual flu vaccine** also recommended.

#### 2. Patient Education

**Every asplenic patient must know:**
- They are at lifelong risk of overwhelming infection
- Any fever is a medical emergency
- Seek care IMMEDIATELY (don't wait to see if it improves)
- Carry standby antibiotics
- Wear medic alert identification
- Special precautions with animal bites, travel

#### 3. Standby Antibiotics

**Prescribe at discharge:**
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125mg
- Or fluoroquinolone (moxifloxacin)

**Instructions:**
- Take immediately at first sign of fever/illness
- Then proceed to emergency department
- Do NOT wait for medical evaluation to start antibiotics

#### 4. Medical Alert

- Bracelet or card
- States: "ASPLENIC - GIVE IMMEDIATE ANTIBIOTICS"

### Emergency Management of OPSI

```
SUSPECTED OPSI (fever in asplenic patient)

    IMMEDIATE (within minutes):
    ├── Blood cultures (but don't wait for results)
    ├── IV antibiotics STAT:
    │   └── Ceftriaxone 2g IV + Vancomycin
    │       (or carbapenem if penicillin allergy)
    └── Fluid resuscitation

    NEXT:
    ├── ICU admission
    ├── Monitor for DIC
    ├── Stress-dose steroids if adrenal crisis suspected
    └── Source control if identified

    DO NOT:
    ├── Wait for cultures
    ├── Wait for lab results
    └── Assume it's viral
```

### Special Situations

#### Animal Bites
- Capnocytophaga canimorsus (dog/cat bites) can cause OPSI
- Prophylactic antibiotics for ANY animal bite
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate preferred

#### Travel to Malaria-Endemic Areas
- Strict prophylaxis essential
- Malaria can be severe in asplenic patients
- Babesia also a risk (tick-borne)

### Long-term Outcomes

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Risk period** | Highest in first 2 years; lifelong risk remains |
| **Children** | Higher risk than adults |
| **Underlying disease** | Malignancy/immunocompromise = higher risk |
| **Vaccination status** | Significantly reduces risk |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What are the three main encapsulated organisms causing OPSI?
   A: Streptococcus pneumoniae (#1), Haemophilus influenzae type b, Neisseria meningitidis

2. Q: What is the mortality rate of OPSI even with treatment?
   A: 50-70% - extremely high, emphasizing need for prevention and immediate treatment

3. Q: When should asplenic patients receive vaccines relative to splenectomy?
   A: Elective: 2 weeks BEFORE; Emergency: 2 weeks AFTER (to allow immune reconstitution)
