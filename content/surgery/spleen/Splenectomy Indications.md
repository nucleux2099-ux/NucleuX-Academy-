# Splenectomy: Indications & Technique

> **Source**: Maingot's Part VII, Fischer's Mastery of Surgery
> **Topic System**: [[Spleen Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Splenectomy is indicated for hematologic disorders (ITP, spherocytosis, hemolytic anemias), malignancy (lymphoma staging, splenic tumors), and splenic pathology (cysts, abscesses), performed laparoscopically when spleen is <1000g.

### Key Points

1. **Most common indication**: ITP (immune thrombocytopenic purpura) - 70-80% response rate
2. **Best outcomes**: Hereditary spherocytosis (nearly 100% cure)
3. **Approach**: Laparoscopic preferred for normal-size spleen (<1000g)
4. **Preoperative**: Vaccinations 2 weeks before elective surgery
5. **Accessory spleen**: Present in 15-30%; search and remove in hematologic conditions

## Detailed Notes

### Splenic Functions (Why Preservation Matters)

| Function | Consequence of Loss |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Filtration** | Howell-Jolly bodies, target cells persist |
| **Phagocytosis** | OPSI risk (encapsulated organisms) |
| **Antibody production** | Reduced IgM response |
| **Immune surveillance** | Increased infection risk |
| **Blood reservoir** | Minimal clinical impact |

### Indications for Splenectomy

#### Hematologic Disorders

| Condition | Response Rate | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **ITP (Immune Thrombocytopenia)** | 70-80% | After failed medical therapy |
| **Hereditary Spherocytosis** | ~100% | Definitive cure; delay until age 6 if possible |
| **Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia** | 50-75% | Warm AIHA responds better |
| **Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP)** | 50-60% | For refractory cases |
| **Sickle Cell Disease** | Variable | For hypersplenism, sequestration |
| **Thalassemia** | Variable | For transfusion dependence |

#### Malignancy

| Condition | Indication |
|-----------|------------|
| **Hodgkin Lymphoma** | Staging (rare now with PET-CT) |
| **Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma** | Splenomegaly, cytopenia |
| **Hairy Cell Leukemia** | Symptomatic splenomegaly |
| **CLL** | Symptomatic splenomegaly |
| **Splenic marginal zone lymphoma** | Primary treatment |
| **Primary splenic tumors** | Angiosarcoma, hemangioma |

#### Other Indications

| Condition | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Splenic cyst** | Symptomatic or >5 cm |
| **Splenic abscess** | Failed antibiotics, drainage |
| **Splenic artery aneurysm** | If cannot exclude from arterial supply |
| **Gaucher's disease** | Symptomatic splenomegaly |
| **Felty's syndrome** | RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia |
| **Portal hypertension** | Rarely; with shunt surgery |

### Preoperative Preparation

**Vaccinations (2 weeks before elective surgery):**
- Pneumococcal (PPSV23 and PCV13)
- Haemophilus influenzae type b
- Meningococcal (MenACWY and MenB)

**Hematologic optimization:**
- ITP: Steroids, IVIG to boost platelets >50k
- Blood products available

**Imaging:**
- CT/US to assess size and look for accessory spleens

### Surgical Approach

#### Laparoscopic Splenectomy (Preferred)

**Indications:**
- Spleen <1000g (sometimes up to 1500g)
- Normal to moderately enlarged
- ITP, spherocytosis, small splenic lesions

**Technique:**
```
LAPAROSCOPIC SPLENECTOMY:

1. Position: Right lateral decubitus (spleen up)
2. Ports: 4 ports (12mm camera, 5-12mm working ports)
3. Divide splenocolic ligament
4. Divide gastrosplenic ligament (short gastrics)
   - Use harmonic or vessel sealer
5. Dissect splenic hilum
6. Ligate/staple splenic artery and vein separately
7. Divide splenorenal ligament
8. Bag spleen, morcellate, extract
9. Search for accessory spleens
10. Hemostasis, remove ports
```

**Advantages:**
- Less pain, shorter hospital stay
- Fewer wound complications
- Better visualization for accessory spleens

#### Open Splenectomy

**Indications:**
- Massive splenomegaly (>1500g)
- Trauma with instability
- Splenic malignancy (need intact specimen)
- Failed laparoscopic approach

**Approach:** Left subcostal or midline incision

### Accessory Spleen

**Importance:**
- Present in 15-30% of patients
- MUST be removed for hematologic indications (ITP, spherocytosis)
- Failure to remove → disease recurrence

**Common locations:**
1. Splenic hilum (most common)
2. Gastrosplenic ligament
3. Splenocolic ligament
4. Greater omentum
5. Mesentery
6. Left ovary/broad ligament

### Postoperative Care

**Immediate:**
- DVT prophylaxis (especially with thrombocytosis)
- Monitor platelets (can rise dramatically post-splenectomy)
- Watch for complications

**Long-term:**
- Vaccinations if not given preoperatively (wait 14 days post-op)
- OPSI education
- Medic alert bracelet
- Consider prophylactic antibiotics (controversial in adults)

### Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Notes |
|--------------|-----------|-------|
| **Thrombocytosis** | 75% | Usually benign; treat if >1 million |
| **OPSI** | 0.5-1% lifetime | Life-threatening; need education |
| **DVT/PE** | 5-10% | Prophylaxis essential |
| **Pancreatic injury** | 1-3% | Leak, pseudocyst |
| **Subphrenic abscess** | 2-5% | Drain if needed |
| **Portal vein thrombosis** | 5-10% | Monitor if risk factors |
