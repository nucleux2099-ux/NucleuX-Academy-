## 1. History — From Autopsy Curiosity to Surgical Staple

The appendix hid in plain sight for centuries. Leonardo da Vinci sketched it around 1500, but formal anatomical descriptions waited until da Capri (1524) and Vesalius (1543). Perhaps the earliest clinical account belongs to Fernel (1554): a 7-year-old girl treated with a large quince developed severe abdominal pain and died — autopsy revealed the quince had obstructed and perforated her appendix.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1736 | **Amyand** — First appendectomy (within an inguinal hernia; hence "Amyand's hernia") |
| 1880 | **Lawson Tait** — First successful transabdominal appendectomy |
| 1886 | **Reginald Fitz** — Coined the term "appendicitis" and described its natural history |
| 1889 | **Charles McBurney** — Described the eponymous point and incision |
| 1890s | **Sir Frederick Treves** — Advocated initial conservative management; tragically, his own daughter died of perforated appendicitis managed this way |

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.31; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

The irony of Treves' story reverberates through modern debates on nonoperative management — a discussion that has come full circle with the APPAC and CODA trials over a century later.

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

🟢 **Embryology:** The appendix and cecum develop as outpouchings of the caudal limb of the midgut loop during the **6th week** of embryonic development. By the **5th month**, the appendix elongates into its characteristic vermiform shape. At birth, the appendix sits at the cecal tip; differential growth of the lateral cecal wall shifts the adult appendix to the **posteromedial cecal wall**, caudal to the ileocecal valve.

🟢 **Key anatomical facts:**

- **Length:** Average 9 cm (range 2–20 cm)
- **Diameter:** Outer 3–8 mm; lumen 1–3 mm
- **Landmark:** The base is *always* found by following the **three taeniae coli** to their confluence at the cecal base — this is the single most reliable intraoperative finding
- **Blood supply:** Appendicular artery (branch of the ileocolic artery), running in the mesoappendix posterior to the terminal ileum
- **Lymphatic drainage:** Along the ileocolic artery nodes

🔵 **Tip position varies considerably** — and this dictates clinical presentation:

| Position | Frequency | Clinical Implication |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Retrocecal | ~65% | Flank/back pain; psoas sign; may lack classic RLQ tenderness |
| Pelvic | ~30% | Suprapubic pain; obturator sign; urinary/rectal symptoms |
| Pre/post-ileal | ~5% | Diffuse pain; may mimic small bowel pathology |
| Subhepatic | Rare | RUQ pain; mimics cholecystitis |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.31*

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The appendix is increasingly recognized as an immunological organ — a "safe house" for commensal bacteria that can repopulate the colon after diarrheal illness. This has implications for incidental appendectomy (now generally discouraged) and for understanding conditions like *C. difficile* colitis after appendectomy.

## 3. Pathophysiology — The Obstruction Debate

### The Classical Teaching (Wangensteen's Model)

🟢 The traditional sequence taught in every surgical textbook:

```
Obstruction (fecalith / lymphoid hyperplasia / tumor)
    → Closed-loop → Mucus accumulation
    → ↑ Intraluminal pressure
    → Exceeds capillary perfusion pressure
    → Mucosal ischemia
    → Bacterial overgrowth & translocation
    → Suppurative appendicitis
    → Gangrenous appendicitis
    → Perforation
```

🟢 **Common causes of obstruction:**
- **Fecaliths (appendicoliths)** — Most frequently cited
- **Lymphoid hyperplasia** — Especially in children (viral infections, IBD)
- **Neoplasms** — Particularly in older adults (>40 years)
- **Parasites** — *Enterobius vermicularis*, rare

### The Challenge to Obstruction Theory

🔵 **PG Depth:** The obstruction model, while elegant, is increasingly questioned:

1. **Most appendicitis specimens lack a fecalith.** Burkitt found fecaliths in only 52% of Canadian and 23% of South African appendicitis patients.
2. **Fecaliths exist in normal appendices** — found in 7–32% of appendectomy specimens without inflammation.
3. **In vivo intraluminal pressure measurements** (Arnbjörnsson & Bengmark) showed elevated pressure in only 8 of 27 patients with non-perforated appendicitis.
4. **Environmental and dietary factors** — The higher incidence in developed nations (low-fiber, high-refined-sugar diets) suggests luminal ecology matters beyond simple mechanical obstruction.
5. **Genetic factors** — Familial clustering has been documented.

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Two-Disease Hypothesis:** There is growing evidence that uncomplicated and complicated appendicitis may represent **distinct pathophysiological entities** rather than a continuum. Studies on appendiceal microbiomes show different bacterial profiles in uncomplicated versus complicated disease. This has profound implications: if they are different diseases, then the progression model (inflammation → gangrene → perforation) is not inevitable, and nonoperative management of uncomplicated appendicitis is biologically rational, not merely a compromise.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### The Classic Sequence

🟢 **Memorize this progression — it remains the foundation of clinical diagnosis:**

```
Periumbilical colicky pain (visceral, T10 afferents)
    → Anorexia, nausea ± vomiting
    → Pain migrates to RLQ (parietal peritoneum)
    → Character changes: dull/colicky → sharp/constant
    → Low-grade fever (≤38.3°C / 101°F)
```

**Key clinical pearls:**
- 🟢 Pain almost always precedes vomiting — if vomiting comes first, consider gastroenteritis
- 🟢 Anorexia is present in ~92% — appendicitis is unlikely with a normal appetite
- 🟢 The classic migratory pain pattern occurs in ~75% of patients
- 🔵 Higher fevers (>38.9°C) and rigors suggest perforation or an alternative diagnosis

### Physical Examination

🟢 **McBurney's point:** One-third the distance from the ASIS to the umbilicus — tenderness here is the most consistent sign of appendicitis.

🟢 **Named signs** (all indicators of localized peritonitis, not specific to appendicitis):

| Sign | Test | Indicates |
|------|------|-----------|
| **Rovsing** | RLQ pain on LLQ palpation | Localized RLQ peritonitis |
| **Psoas** | Pain on right hip extension | Retrocecal appendix (inflammation near psoas) |
| **Obturator** | Pain on internal rotation of flexed right hip | Pelvic appendix (inflammation near obturator internus) |

🔵 **Additional bedside maneuvers:** Heel-strike test, cough test, asking the patient to jump — all provoke pain from peritoneal irritation and are quick screening tools.

🔵 **Rectal exam** is typically normal unless the appendiceal tip is pelvic or an abscess has formed. Cervical motion tenderness on pelvic exam can occur in appendicitis (pelvic peritoneal irritation) and does not definitively indicate PID.

### Special Populations

🟢 **Children:**
- Classic presentation often absent, especially in young children (<5 years)
- Most common symptoms: diffuse abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting (nonspecific)
- **Perforation rates:** 50% under age 5; 66% under age 3; nearly 100% under age 1
- Reason: delayed presentation, difficulty obtaining history, broader differential (mesenteric adenitis, streptococcal pharyngitis)

🟢 **Elderly (>65 years):**
- Diminished inflammatory response → milder symptoms, less impressive exam
- Perforation rate: **40–70%** (vs. ~20% overall)
- Broader differential: diverticulitis, malignancy, ischemic colitis, perforated ulcer
- Mortality from perforated appendicitis in patients >80: up to 21%

🟢 **Pregnancy:**
- Most common nonobstetric surgical emergency in pregnancy
- Classic presentation in only 50–60% of cases
- Nausea/vomiting overlap with normal pregnancy symptoms
- Mild leukocytosis and elevated CRP may be physiological
- 🔵 Appendix displaced cephalad by gravid uterus — though RLQ pain remains most common even in third trimester
- **Fetal loss:** 2% (uncomplicated) → 6% (complicated); negative appendectomy also carries 4% fetal loss

🔵 **Immunocompromised:**
- Blunted immune response: may lack fever, leukocytosis, peritonitis
- Broad differential: typhlitis (neutropenic enterocolitis), CMV colitis, opportunistic infections
- CT imaging essential; prompt appendectomy once diagnosed

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.31; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Clinical Scoring Systems

🟢 **Alvarado Score (MANTRELS):** Migration, Anorexia, Nausea, Tenderness in RLQ, Rebound, Elevated temperature, Leukocytosis, Shift to left. Total: 10 points. Useful for ruling out appendicitis at low scores but lacks specificity at intermediate scores.

🔵 **AIR Score (Appendicitis Inflammatory Response):** Incorporates CRP and grades RLQ pain severity. Outperforms Alvarado in both sensitivity and specificity. Current guidelines (WSES, EAES) recommend AIR or the Adult Appendicitis Score (AAS) over Alvarado.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The AIR score can reduce unnecessary imaging by up to 33% (CT) and 58% (US) when used systematically in clinical pathways.

### Laboratory Studies

🟢 **WBC:** Elevated in ~90% (mild in uncomplicated; markedly elevated in perforation). **A normal WBC does not exclude appendicitis.** Up to 50% may present with normal WBC in some series.

🟢 **Left shift** (neutrophils >74%): Present in up to 87% regardless of total WBC.

🔵 **The triad of normal WBC + normal differential + normal CRP** effectively rules out appendicitis — a suggestive physical exam in this setting is quite rare.

🟢 **Urinalysis:** Sterile pyuria can occur due to proximity of inflamed appendix to ureter/bladder — does not exclude appendicitis.

🔵 **Biomarkers (CRP, procalcitonin, IL-6):** CRP is the most sensitive supplementary marker. None individually diagnostic. Procalcitonin may help differentiate uncomplicated from complicated appendicitis.

### Imaging

🟢 **Imaging hierarchy by population:**

| Population | First-line | Second-line | Notes |
|------------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Adults** | CT (low-dose, contrast-enhanced) | — | Sensitivity 94–96%, Specificity 94–95% |
| **Children** | Ultrasound | CT (if US equivocal) | Radiation risk; US sensitivity decreases with higher BMI |
| **Pregnant** | Ultrasound | MRI (without gadolinium) | CT only if MRI unavailable |

**CT Findings of Appendicitis:**
- 🟢 Dilated appendix >6–7 mm
- 🟢 Wall thickening with mural enhancement ("target sign")
- 🟢 Periappendiceal fat stranding
- 🔵 Appendicolith (not pathognomonic but increases complicated disease risk)
- 🔵 Free fluid, free air → suggests perforation

**Ultrasound Criteria:**
- 🟢 Noncompressible appendix ≥6 mm (some centers use ≥7 mm)
- 🟢 Increased wall thickness >2 mm, increased vascularity ("ring of fire")
- Sensitivity 71–94% (highly operator-dependent); Specificity 81–98%
- Inability to visualize the appendix has ~90% negative predictive value

**MRI:**
- 🔵 Sensitivity 90–100%, Specificity 94–98%
- Appendiceal diameter >7 mm, wall thickening >2 mm, T2 high-signal luminal fluid
- Visualizes normal appendix in 87% (vs. 2% for US) — particularly valuable in pregnancy
- MRI use in pregnancy reduces negative appendectomy rates by 47%

🟢 **Plain radiographs** are neither sensitive nor specific — not recommended. A calcified appendicolith is present in only ~5% of cases.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.31; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

## 6. Classification

🟢 **The Critical Distinction:**

| Feature | Uncomplicated | Complicated |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Pathology** | Inflamed appendix, intact wall | Gangrene, perforation, abscess, phlegmon |
| **CT signs** | Dilated appendix, fat stranding | Free air, appendicolith, abscess, free fluid |
| **Treatment** | Appendectomy OR antibiotics | Appendectomy (or drainage + interval approach) |
| **Natural history** | May resolve spontaneously | Will not resolve without intervention |

🔵 **Scoring systems (SAS-US, SAS-CT):** Atema et al. developed these to differentiate complicated from uncomplicated appendicitis using clinical, laboratory, and imaging variables. They perform well at ruling out complicated disease but await external validation.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The shift from viewing appendicitis as a single disease on a spectrum to recognizing two potentially distinct pathobiological entities has been the most important conceptual change in appendicitis in the last two decades. It underpins the entire rationale for nonoperative management.

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management (All Patients)

🟢 **For all patients with confirmed appendicitis:**
- IV fluid resuscitation
- Analgesia
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting GI flora (gram-negatives + anaerobes)
  - Options: Cefoxitin, ampicillin-sulbactam, or cefazolin + metronidazole
- For uncomplicated appendicitis: a single prophylactic dose suffices if proceeding to surgery

### A. Uncomplicated Appendicitis

#### Operative Management (Gold Standard)

🟢 **Laparoscopic appendectomy** is the procedure of choice — for all populations including complicated appendicitis, elderly, and pregnant patients.

**Advantages of laparoscopic over open:**
- Lower overall morbidity and wound complications
- Less postoperative pain
- Shorter recovery and earlier return to activities
- Ability to survey the entire peritoneal cavity (diagnostic advantage)
- Slightly higher (or similar) rate of postoperative intra-abdominal abscess — the one historic concern

🔵 **Technique essentials:**
- Supine position, left arm tucked
- Umbilical camera port (Hasson or Veress), LLQ and suprapubic working ports
- Trendelenburg + left tilt to clear bowel
- Mesoappendix divided with bipolar, LigaSure, harmonic, or stapler
- Appendix divided with stapler or Endoloop at base
- Specimen in retrieval bag — always send for histopathology

🟢 **Open appendectomy** — McBurney (oblique) or Rockey-Davis (transverse) incision:
- Divide mesoappendix, ligate appendiceal artery
- Crush base, double-ligate with absorbable suture
- Simple ligation preferred over stump inversion (no benefit to inversion; may increase adhesions)
- Stump should be ≤3 mm to prevent stump appendicitis

#### Nonoperative Management (Antibiotics Alone)

🔵 **The evidence — landmark trials:**

| Trial | N | Key Finding |
|-------|---|-------------|
| **APPAC** (2015) | 530 | 27% needed appendectomy within 1 year; 30–40% by 5 years |
| **CODA** (2020) | 1552 | Antibiotics noninferior at 30 days; 29% crossed to surgery by 90 days; appendicolith = higher failure |
| **COMMA** (2021) | — | 25% recurrence; lower QoL at 1 year in antibiotic group |

🟢 **Current consensus:** Antibiotics are an **acceptable first-line treatment** for uncomplicated appendicitis *without* appendicolith. The decision should involve shared decision-making considering:
- Risk of recurrence (25–40% over 5 years)
- Risk of missed neoplasm
- Patient values and preferences
- Presence of appendicolith (favors surgery)

🔵 **Antibiotic protocol:** IV for 24 hours, then oral for total of 7–10 days. Cover gram-negatives and anaerobes (e.g., fluoroquinolone or cephalosporin + metronidazole). Clear improvement expected within 48 hours; worsening mandates reassessment for surgery.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The CODA decision-aid tool (appyornot.org) was developed to facilitate shared decision-making. Over 80% of appendicitis patients may qualify for nonoperative management based on strict criteria — but one-third will ultimately need appendectomy within a few years.

### B. Complicated Appendicitis (Without Abscess/Phlegmon)

🟢 Proven or suspected complicated appendicitis **without** an organized abscess should be treated with **appendectomy** — laparoscopic when expertise is available.

🔵 **Operative considerations:**
- Gentle handling of friable, gangrenous tissue
- Suction aspiration of purulence (as effective as large-volume irrigation per recent data)
- Drains not routinely placed (may increase LOS without reducing complications)
- Intraoperative cultures generally unnecessary unless healthcare-associated exposure
- **Postoperative antibiotics:** Maximum 4 days; recent evidence supports **2 days** (STOP-IT trial; Dutch RCT in *Lancet* 2023)

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

### C. Complicated Appendicitis With Abscess or Phlegmon

🔵 **Individualized approach required:**

**If diffuse peritonitis or sepsis** → Urgent surgery regardless

**If well-contained abscess or phlegmon** (the more common scenario):
- **Nonoperative management preferred** (75% success rate):
  - IV antibiotics (4–7 days per IDSA guidelines)
  - Percutaneous drainage if drainable abscess identified
  - Close monitoring; reimaging if no improvement
- **Immediate surgery** may offer benefits in experienced hands (fewer readmissions, less bowel resection — WSES Jerusalem guidelines)
- Risk of complications with immediate surgery: failure to identify appendix, fistula, ileocecectomy

🔵 **Predictors of failure of nonoperative management:** Smoking, generalized tenderness, tachycardia, SBO, appendicolith, absence of drainable collection.

## 8. Interval Appendectomy

🔵 **The debate:**

| Argument FOR | Argument AGAINST |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Recurrence rate 8–32% | Most won't recur |
| Risk of missed neoplasm (4–29% in complicated disease) | Operative morbidity of interval procedure |
| Appendicolith predicts higher recurrence | Cost of routine surgery |

🔵 **Current recommendations (WSES 2020):**
- **Routine interval appendectomy is NOT recommended**
- **Consider interval appendectomy if:**
  - Appendicolith present (higher recurrence risk)
  - Age ≥40 (higher neoplasm risk)
  - Recurrent symptoms

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Peri-Appendicitis Acuta Trial:** This multicenter RCT comparing interval appendectomy vs. MRI follow-up after nonoperative management of complicated appendicitis with abscess was **stopped early** due to a high rate of neoplasm in the interval appendectomy group — all in patients >40 years. This fundamentally changed follow-up recommendations.

🟢 **If no interval appendectomy is performed in patients ≥40:**
- Colonoscopy + full-dose contrast-enhanced CT are mandatory follow-up

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

## 9. Appendiceal Neoplasms — Brief Overview

🟢 **Incidence:** ~6 per 1,000,000 annually; found in up to 3.7% of appendectomy specimens (higher in interval appendectomy specimens).

🟢 **Classification (PSOGI/AJCC/WHO):**

| Type | Examples | Management |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Epithelial** | Adenoma, LAMN, HAMN, mucinous adenocarcinoma, nonmucinous adenocarcinoma, goblet cell adenocarcinoma | Appendectomy → right hemicolectomy for invasive lesions |
| **Neuroendocrine (NET)** | Well-differentiated NET, NEC, MiNEN | <1 cm: appendectomy; 1–2 cm: consider hemicolectomy; >2 cm: hemicolectomy |
| **Nonepithelial** | Lymphoma, sarcoma | Rare; manage per tumor type |

🔵 **NETs (most common appendiceal tumor):**
- 88% of all appendiceal neoplasms
- Usually at appendiceal **tip** (where neuroendocrine cells concentrate)
- 10-year survival ~90% even with node-positive disease for tumors <2 cm
- Right hemicolectomy indications: >2 cm, base involvement, positive margins/nodes, mesoappendix invasion, Ki-67 >20%

🔴 **Mucocele — a descriptive term, not a diagnosis.** Can represent benign retention cyst or LAMN/HAMN/malignancy. Must not be ruptured during surgery (risk of pseudomyxoma peritonei). Always look for peritoneal mucin deposits.

🔴 **Pseudomyxoma peritonei (PMP):** Treatment is CRS + HIPEC at a specialized center. Five-year survival: 62–90% (low-grade) vs. 38% (high-grade).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.94; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.31; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.177*

## 10. Other Important Topics

### Stump Appendicitis
🔵 Incidence 0.002–0.15%. Occurs when residual appendiceal tissue (from incomplete initial appendectomy) becomes inflamed. Risk increased after complicated appendectomy with poor visualization. Presents like primary appendicitis; imaging shows cecal wall thickening. Requires completion appendectomy.

### Normal Appendix at Operation
🟢 If a grossly normal appendix is found at surgery:
1. Systematically examine: terminal ileum (Meckel's, Crohn's), mesenteric nodes, adnexa, sigmoid, gallbladder
2. **Remove the appendix** — abnormalities are sometimes found only on histology (up to 26% may show acute appendicitis microscopically); removing it eliminates appendicitis from future differentials
3. **Exception:** If Crohn's disease involves the cecum/appendiceal base — defer appendectomy (risk of stump fistula)

### Neurogenic Appendicopathy
🔴 Found in ~10% of appendectomies for "acute appendicitis." Histologic diagnosis: nerve proliferation and increased endocrine cells without acute inflammation. Supports removing a macroscopically normal appendix in patients with classic symptoms and no alternative diagnosis.

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Appendicitis is the most common surgical emergency.** Lifetime risk: 8.6% males, 6.7% females. Peak: teens and young adults.
2. **Classic progression:** Periumbilical pain → anorexia/nausea → RLQ migration → localized peritonitis. Pain precedes vomiting.
3. **McBurney's point** = 1/3 from ASIS to umbilicus. Most consistent sign: **RLQ tenderness.**
4. **CT** is the imaging gold standard in adults (Sn 95%, Sp 94%). **US first** in children and pregnant women.
5. **Laparoscopic appendectomy** is the procedure of choice for all populations.
6. **Antibiotics alone** are acceptable for uncomplicated appendicitis (shared decision-making).
7. **Always send the specimen for histopathology.**

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. AIR score outperforms Alvarado; use for risk stratification and reducing unnecessary imaging.
9. Uncomplicated and complicated appendicitis may be distinct diseases, not a continuum.
10. Postoperative antibiotics after complicated appendectomy: 2–4 days (not longer).
11. Complicated appendicitis with abscess/phlegmon: NOM with drainage preferred; surgery in experienced hands also acceptable.
12. Interval appendectomy is NOT routine — reserve for appendicolith, age ≥40, or recurrence.
13. MRI without gadolinium is the second-line imaging in pregnancy (reduces negative appendectomy by 47%).

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. The two-disease hypothesis: uncomplicated appendicitis has different microbiomics and may resolve spontaneously.
15. Neurogenic appendicopathy accounts for ~10% of "negative" appendectomies.
16. Peri-Appendicitis Acuta trial: stopped early due to high neoplasm rate in patients >40 — drives mandatory colonoscopy/CT follow-up.
17. Appendicolith is the single most important modifier: increases failure of NOM, recurrence risk, and favors operative approach.
18. PMP from appendiceal mucinous neoplasms → CRS + HIPEC at specialized centers.
