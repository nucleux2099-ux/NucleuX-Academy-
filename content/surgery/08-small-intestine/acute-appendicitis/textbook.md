## Definition

Acute appendicitis is inflammation of the vermiform appendix, representing the most common surgical emergency worldwide. The condition affects approximately 7-8% of the population during their lifetime, with peak incidence in the second and third decades of life.

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 72, p.1299*

---

## Anatomy of the Appendix

### Embryology
The appendix develops from the midgut and appears during the eighth week of gestation as an outpouching of the caecum. Initially, it projects from the apex of the caecum, but differential growth of the caecal wall results in its final medial position.

### Position
The base of the appendix is constant, located at the posteromedial aspect of the caecum, approximately 2 cm below the ileocaecal valve at the convergence of the three taeniae coli. However, the tip position is highly variable:

| Position | Frequency | Clinical Significance |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Retrocaecal/Retrocolic | 65% | Psoas sign positive, flank pain |
| Pelvic | 30% | Urinary symptoms, rectal tenderness |
| Subcaecal | 2% | Classical presentation |
| Pre-ileal | 1% | May cause small bowel symptoms |
| Post-ileal | 0.5% | May mimic mesenteric adenitis |
| Subhepatic (rare) | <1% | Mimics cholecystitis |

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 51, p.1296*

### Blood Supply
- **Arterial:** Appendicular artery, a branch of the ileocolic artery (from superior mesenteric artery). It is an end artery, making the appendix susceptible to ischemia.
- **Venous:** Appendicular vein → ileocolic vein → superior mesenteric vein → portal vein

### Histology
The appendix is a true diverticulum containing all layers of the intestinal wall:
- Mucosa with crypts of Lieberkühn
- Submucosa with abundant lymphoid tissue (peaks at 10-20 years, then involutes)
- Muscularis propria (inner circular, outer longitudinal)
- Serosa

The lymphoid tissue is abundant in childhood and adolescence, which may explain the higher incidence of appendicitis in this age group.

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 30, p.1241*

## Pathophysiology

### The Obstruction-Inflammation Sequence

The pathogenesis of acute appendicitis follows a predictable sequence:

```
OBSTRUCTION
    │
    │ (Faecolith, lymphoid hyperplasia, stricture, neoplasm, parasites)
    ▼
MUCUS ACCUMULATION
    │
    │ (Continued secretion against closed loop)
    ▼
LUMINAL DISTENSION
    │
    │ (Intraluminal pressure exceeds venous pressure)
    ▼
VENOUS CONGESTION → ARTERIAL COMPROMISE
    │
    │ (Ischemia of appendiceal wall)
    ▼
BACTERIAL INVASION
    │
    │ (Translocation through compromised mucosa)
    ▼
TRANSMURAL INFLAMMATION
    │
    ├─────────────────────────────────────┐
    ▼                                     ▼
RESOLUTION                            PROGRESSION
(Rare without intervention)               │
                                         │
                        ┌────────────────┼────────────────┐
                        ▼                ▼                ▼
                   GANGRENE         PERFORATION      ABSCESS
                        │                │           (Walled off)
                        └────────────────┴─────────────────┘
                                         │
                                         ▼
                              GENERALIZED PERITONITIS
```

### Causes of Obstruction

1. **Faecolith (Appendicolith):** 30-40% of cases
   - More common in developed countries (low-fiber diet)
   - Associated with higher perforation rates

2. **Lymphoid Hyperplasia:** Most common in children/young adults
   - Secondary to viral infections, IBD, or immunological stimulation

3. **Foreign Bodies:** Seeds, parasites (Enterobius vermicularis), barium

4. **Neoplasms:** Carcinoid (most common), adenocarcinoma, mucocele

5. **Strictures:** Post-inflammatory, Crohn's disease

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 72, p.1300*

## Bacteriology

The appendix contains a similar bacterial flora to the colon. In acute appendicitis, the predominant organisms are:

**Aerobic:**
- *Escherichia coli* (most common)
- *Streptococcus viridans*
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*
- *Klebsiella pneumoniae*

**Anaerobic:**
- *Bacteroides fragilis* (most common anaerobe)
- *Peptostreptococcus*
- *Bilophila*
- *Fusobacterium*

In gangrenous and perforated appendicitis, anaerobic organisms predominate, which has implications for antibiotic selection.

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 51, p.1298*

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

#### The Classical Sequence (Murhy's Triad - not to be confused with Murphy's sign)
1. **Periumbilical pain** → Initial visceral pain referred to T10 dermatome
2. **Anorexia, nausea, vomiting** → Autonomic response
3. **Pain migration to RIF** → Parietal peritoneal involvement

This sequence occurs in approximately 50-60% of patients. The migration of pain from periumbilical to right iliac fossa is considered pathognomonic.

#### Timeline
- Pain precedes vomiting (unlike gastroenteritis where vomiting precedes pain)
- Complete sequence typically evolves over 12-24 hours
- Symptoms >48 hours suggest complications (mass, perforation)

### Signs

#### Inspection
- Patient lies still (movement aggravates peritoneal pain)
- Flushed facies
- Foetor oris (bad breath)
- Right hip flexion (psoas spasm)

#### Palpation

| Sign | Description | Sensitivity |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **McBurney's point tenderness** | Point tenderness at junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of line from ASIS to umbilicus | 50-94% |
| **Rebound tenderness** | Pain on sudden release of pressure | 63% |
| **Guarding** | Involuntary muscle rigidity | Variable |
| **Rovsing's sign** | RIF pain on LIF palpation | 22-68% |
| **Psoas sign** | Pain on right hip extension | Retrocaecal |
| **Obturator sign** | Pain on internal rotation of flexed right hip | Pelvic |
| **Dunphy's sign** | Increased pain on coughing | Peritonitis |
| **Pointing sign** | Patient points to exact location with one finger | High specificity |

#### Rectal/Vaginal Examination
- Tenderness in pouch of Douglas (pelvic appendicitis)
- Essential to exclude pelvic pathology in females
- May reveal pelvic abscess

#### Temperature
- Low-grade fever (37.5-38.5°C) typical
- High fever (>39°C) suggests perforation or abscess

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 72, p.1302-1303*

## Clinical Scoring Systems

### Alvarado Score (MANTRELS)

| Component | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| **M**igration of pain | 1 |
| **A**norexia | 1 |
| **N**ausea/vomiting | 1 |
| **T**enderness in RIF | 2 |
| **R**ebound tenderness | 1 |
| **E**levated temperature (>37.3°C) | 1 |
| **L**eukocytosis (>10,000/mm³) | 2 |
| **S**hift to left (>75% neutrophils) | 1 |
| **Total** | **10** |

**Interpretation:**
- 1-4: Unlikely (discharge with advice)
- 5-6: Possible (observe, imaging)
- 7-8: Probable (operate)
- 9-10: Highly probable (operate)

### RIPASA Score
Developed in Asia, includes additional parameters:
- Age, sex, RIF pain, migration, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, duration of symptoms
- RIF tenderness, guarding, rebound, Rovsing's
- Fever, raised WBC, negative urinalysis
- Foreign NRIC (identifies non-residents)

**Score >7.5:** Sensitivity 98%, specificity 81.3%

📚 *Alvarado A. Ann Emerg Med 1986; Chong CF et al. Singapore Med J 2010*

## Investigations

### Laboratory

| Test | Findings | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| **WBC count** | >10,000/mm³ (70-90%) | Sensitivity 80-85% |
| **Neutrophilia** | >75% | Left shift indicates acute inflammation |
| **CRP** | Elevated (>10 mg/L) | Higher with complicated appendicitis |
| **Urinalysis** | Usually normal | Sterile pyuria may occur (pelvic appendix) |
| **Pregnancy test** | Mandatory in females | Rule out ectopic pregnancy |

### Imaging

#### Ultrasonography
- **Operator-dependent** but useful initial test
- Sensitivity: 75-90%, Specificity: 85-95%
- **Positive findings:**
  - Non-compressible appendix
  - Diameter >6 mm (outer wall to outer wall)
  - Target sign (layers visible in cross-section)
  - Appendicolith
  - Periappendiceal fluid
- **Preferred in:** Children, pregnant women, thin patients

#### CT Scan
- **Gold standard** for diagnosis
- Sensitivity: 94-98%, Specificity: 95-98%
- **Positive findings:**
  - Appendix diameter >6 mm
  - Wall thickening (>3 mm)
  - Periappendiceal fat stranding
  - Appendicolith
  - Abscess or free fluid
  - Arrowhead sign (contrast in caecum pointing to appendix)
- **Indications:**
  - Atypical presentation
  - Equivocal clinical findings
  - Suspected complications
  - Obese patients

#### MRI
- Emerging alternative, especially in pregnancy
- No radiation exposure
- Limited availability and expertise

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 51, p.1301-1302*

## Differential Diagnosis

### Surgical Conditions
1. **Mesenteric adenitis** - Often viral prodrome, less localized
2. **Meckel's diverticulum** - Rule of 2s, may mimic exactly
3. **Crohn's disease (terminal ileitis)** - Chronic symptoms, diarrhea
4. **Caecal pathology** - Diverticulitis, carcinoma
5. **Intussusception** - Children, bloody stool, sausage-shaped mass

### Gynecological Conditions
1. **Ruptured ovarian cyst** - Mid-cycle, sudden onset
2. **Ovarian torsion** - Severe pain, ovarian mass
3. **Ectopic pregnancy** - Missed period, positive β-hCG
4. **Pelvic inflammatory disease** - Bilateral pain, discharge
5. **Mittelschmerz** - Mid-cycle, self-limiting

### Medical Conditions
1. **Gastroenteritis** - Vomiting precedes pain, diarrhea prominent
2. **Urinary tract infection** - Dysuria, pyuria
3. **Right lower lobe pneumonia** - Cough, respiratory signs
4. **Diabetic ketoacidosis** - Diffuse pain, metabolic derangement
5. **Acute porphyria** - Recurrent attacks, neurological features

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 30, p.1248*

## Management

### Initial Management (Resuscitation)
1. NPO (nil per os)
2. IV access and fluid resuscitation
3. Analgesia (does NOT mask signs - studies have shown this)
4. Antibiotics (pre-operative or therapeutic)
5. DVT prophylaxis

### Antibiotic Therapy

| Situation | Regimen | Duration |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Uncomplicated** | Ceftriaxone 1g IV (single pre-op dose) | Once |
| **Complicated** | Ceftriaxone 1g IV BD + Metronidazole 500mg IV TDS | 5-7 days |
| **Penicillin allergy** | Ciprofloxacin 400mg IV BD + Metronidazole | As above |

### Surgical Management

#### Timing
- **Uncomplicated:** Surgery within 12-24 hours (no benefit to immediate surgery at night)
- **Complicated (perforation/peritonitis):** Emergency surgery
- **Appendicular mass:** See separate protocol

#### Approach Comparison

| Parameter | Laparoscopic | Open |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| Wound infection | Lower (3% vs 8%) | Higher |
| Postoperative pain | Less | More |
| Hospital stay | Shorter (1-2 days) | Longer (2-4 days) |
| Return to work | Faster | Slower |
| Cosmesis | Better | Poorer |
| Diagnostic capability | Yes | No |
| Cost | Higher equipment cost | Lower |

📚 *Sauerland S et al. Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2010*

## Complications of Appendicitis

### Pre-operative
1. **Perforation** (20-30%)
   - Increases with delay
   - More common at extremes of age
   - Associated with faecolith
   
2. **Appendicular mass**
   - Forms after 48-72 hours
   - Omentum and bowel wall off inflammation
   - Management: Ochsner-Sherren regimen

3. **Appendicular abscess**
   - Walled-off perforation
   - May be palpable
   - Management: Drainage (percutaneous or surgical)

4. **Generalized peritonitis**
   - Free perforation into peritoneal cavity
   - Requires emergency laparotomy

5. **Pylephlebitis**
   - Suppurative thrombophlebitis of portal vein
   - Rare but high mortality
   - Presents with high spiking fever, jaundice

### Post-operative
1. **Wound infection** (most common, 3-10%)
2. **Intra-abdominal abscess** (pelvic, subphrenic)
3. **Fecal fistula** (caecal injury, stump dehiscence)
4. **Adhesive intestinal obstruction**
5. **Stump appendicitis** (incomplete removal)
6. **Incisional hernia**

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 72, p.1309-1310*

## Special Situations

### Appendicular Mass (Ochsner-Sherren Regimen)

**Definition:** Inflammatory mass in RIF formed by inflamed appendix walled off by omentum and adjacent bowel loops.

**Management Protocol:**
1. Admit and monitor
2. NPO, IV fluids
3. IV antibiotics (Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole)
4. Mark mass boundary daily
5. Monitor vital signs, WBC, CRP

**Outcomes:**
- **Mass resolving:** Continue conservative management → Interval appendicectomy at 6-8 weeks
- **Mass increasing/peritonitis:** Emergency surgery

**Indications for immediate surgery:**
- Signs of peritonitis
- Mass not settling after 48-72 hours
- Sepsis
- Suspicion of malignancy

📚 *Bailey & Love, 28th Ed, Ch 72, p.1307*

### Appendicitis in Pregnancy

- Most common non-obstetric surgical emergency
- Incidence: 1 in 1500-2000 pregnancies
- **Anatomical considerations:** Appendix displaced superiorly and laterally by gravid uterus
- **Diagnosis:** More difficult; nausea/vomiting common in pregnancy
- **Imaging:** USG first, MRI if inconclusive (avoid CT)
- **Management:** Surgery at any trimester (laparoscopic safe)
- **Risks:** Higher perforation rate if delayed, fetal loss higher with complicated appendicitis

📚 *Sabiston, 21st Ed, Ch 51, p.1308*

### Appendicitis in the Elderly

- Atypical presentation common (minimal pain, afebrile)
- Higher perforation rate (>50%)
- Higher mortality (5-15% vs <1% in young adults)
- Consider malignancy (caecal carcinoma may present similarly)
- Lower threshold for imaging

## Prognosis

| Type | Mortality |
|------|-----------|
| Uncomplicated | <0.1% |
| Complicated (perforation) | 1-3% |
| Elderly | 5-15% |

**Negative appendicectomy rate:** Historically 15-25%, now <10% with CT

📚 *Schwartz, 11th Ed, Ch 30, p.1262*

---

## Key Points Summary

1. Most common surgical emergency with 7-8% lifetime risk
2. Pain migration from periumbilical to RIF is pathognomonic
3. Alvarado score ≥7 indicates high probability
4. CT is gold standard; USG for children/pregnancy
5. Laparoscopic appendicectomy is preferred approach
6. Appendicular mass: Conservative first, then interval surgery
7. Don't delay surgery—perforation risk increases with time
8. In pregnancy and elderly, maintain high index of suspicion
