# Femoral Hernia: Diagnosis & Urgent Management

> **Source**: Maingot's Part I Ch 11, Fischer's Ch 21
> **Topic System**: [[Hernia Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Femoral hernia is a protrusion through the femoral canal (below the inguinal ligament), more common in women, with HIGH risk of strangulation due to the narrow, rigid femoral ring.

### Key Points

1. **Epidemiology**: 3% of all hernias; F:M = 4:1; more common in elderly women
2. **Location**: BELOW and LATERAL to pubic tubercle (vs inguinal = above)
3. **High-risk**: 40% present with strangulation (highest of all hernias)
4. **Surgical emergency**: All femoral hernias should be repaired promptly
5. **Repair**: Must close femoral ring; preperitoneal approach preferred

### Clinical Pearl
> [!warning] "Every femoral hernia is an emergency"
> 40% of femoral hernias present with strangulation. Never watch and wait.

## Detailed Notes

### Anatomy of the Femoral Canal

```
FEMORAL CANAL BOUNDARIES:

        Inguinal ligament (anterior)
                    │
    ┌───────────────┴───────────────┐
    │                               │
Lacunar ──────┐ FEMORAL ┌───────Femoral
ligament      │  CANAL  │        vein
(medial)      │ (1-2cm) │      (lateral)
              │         │
    └─────────┴─────────┘
                    │
        Pectineal ligament (posterior)

CONTENTS: Lymph node (Cloquet's node), fat
         (normally just a potential space)
```

**Femoral Ring (superior opening):**
- Anterior: Inguinal ligament
- Posterior: Pectineal ligament (Cooper's)
- Medial: Lacunar ligament (Gimbernat's)
- Lateral: Femoral vein

### Clinical Presentation

#### Symptoms
- Small, often irreducible groin lump
- May present with bowel obstruction (40%)
- Pain (especially if incarcerated)
- Often missed or misdiagnosed

#### Signs
- Lump BELOW and LATERAL to pubic tubercle
- Small, firm, often tender
- Non-cough impulse (often incarcerated)
- May be difficult to palpate in obese patients

### Femoral vs Inguinal Hernia

| Feature | FEMORAL | INGUINAL |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Location** | Below inguinal ligament | Above inguinal ligament |
| **Relation to pubic tubercle** | Below and lateral | Above and medial |
| **Sex predominance** | Women (4:1) | Men (9:1) |
| **Strangulation risk** | HIGH (40%) | Lower (10%) |
| **Into scrotum/labia** | Never | Can descend |
| **Cough impulse** | Often absent | Usually present |

### Why High Strangulation Risk?

```
FEMORAL RING = NARROW + RIGID

    Inguinal ligament
         │ (unyielding)
         ▼
    ┌─────────┐
    │ Narrow  │ ← Cannot expand
    │  ring   │   
    │(1-2 cm) │ ← Lacunar ligament
    └─────────┘   (rigid, sharp edge)
         │
    Contents trapped → ischemia → strangulation
```

The femoral ring is bounded by rigid structures that cannot stretch, so even small hernia contents quickly become strangulated.

### Management

#### Urgency Assessment

| Presentation | Urgency | Management |
|--------------|---------|------------|
| **Reducible** | Semi-urgent | Repair soon (don't delay) |
| **Incarcerated** | Urgent | Attempt reduction → repair |
| **Strangulated** | EMERGENCY | Immediate surgery |
| **Bowel obstruction** | EMERGENCY | Resuscitation → surgery |

#### Surgical Approaches

**1. Open Infrainguinal (Low/Lockwood) Approach**
- Incision below inguinal ligament
- Direct access to femoral canal
- Limited access if bowel needs resection

**2. Open Inguinal (High) Approach**
- Inguinal incision (like for inguinal hernia)
- Can repair femoral and inguinal hernias
- Better access if bowel needs inspection

**3. Preperitoneal (Extraperitoneal) Approach**
- Through lower midline or Pfannenstiel
- Excellent view of myopectineal orifice
- Can cover femoral AND inguinal regions with mesh

**4. Laparoscopic (TAPP/TEP)**
- Preperitoneal mesh covering femoral ring
- Especially good for bilateral or recurrent
- Requires general anesthesia

#### Repair Techniques

**Suture Repair (McVay/Cooper's Ligament)**
- Suture transversalis fascia to Cooper's ligament
- Closes femoral canal
- High tension - relaxing incision needed

**Mesh Repair (Preferred)**
- Mesh plug or flat mesh
- Preperitoneal mesh (TAPP/TEP) covers all potential defects
- Lower recurrence than suture repair

### Special Situation: Richter's Hernia

> [!tip] Richter's Hernia
> Only part of the bowel wall (antimesenteric border) is caught in the hernia.
> - Can strangulate WITHOUT complete obstruction
> - May present with localized pain, no obstruction initially
> - High risk of perforation
> - More common in femoral hernias (small neck)

```
    Bowel wall
    ┌────────────────────┐
    │                    │
    │  ════════════════  │ ← Only this part herniates
    │                    │    (no obstruction initially)
    └────────────────────┘
```

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: Where is a femoral hernia located relative to the pubic tubercle?
   A: BELOW and LATERAL to the pubic tubercle (inguinal hernia is above and medial)

2. Q: Why do femoral hernias have such high strangulation rates?
   A: The femoral ring is narrow (1-2 cm) and bounded by rigid structures (inguinal ligament, lacunar ligament) that cannot expand

3. Q: What is Richter's hernia and why is it dangerous?
   A: Only the antimesenteric border of bowel herniates, can strangulate and perforate without causing complete bowel obstruction
