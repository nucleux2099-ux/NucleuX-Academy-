# Inguinal Hernia: Anatomy, Classification & Repair

> **Source**: Maingot's Part I Ch 5-10, Netter's Surgical Anatomy
> **Topic System**: [[Hernia Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Inguinal hernia is a protrusion of abdominal contents through the inguinal canal, classified as INDIRECT (through deep ring, lateral to inferior epigastric vessels) or DIRECT (through Hesselbach's triangle, medial to vessels).

### Key Points

1. **Epidemiology**: Most common hernia (75% of all); Lifetime risk: 27% men, 3% women
2. **Indirect**: Congenital (patent processus vaginalis); through deep ring; may descend into scrotum
3. **Direct**: Acquired (weakness in floor); through Hesselbach's triangle; rarely into scrotum
4. **Surgical anatomy**: Know the inguinal canal boundaries, Hesselbach's triangle, and myopectineal orifice
5. **Repair options**: Open (Lichtenstein) vs Laparoscopic (TAPP/TEP); mesh is standard

### Mnemonic: MALT for Inguinal Canal Boundaries
- **M**uscle (internal oblique, transversus) - roof
- **A**poneurosis (external oblique) - anterior wall
- **L**igament (inguinal ligament) - floor
- **T**ransversalis fascia + conjoint tendon - posterior wall

### Hesselbach's Triangle (Direct Hernia Site)

**Boundaries:**
- **Medial**: Lateral border of rectus abdominis
- **Lateral**: Inferior epigastric vessels
- **Inferior**: Inguinal ligament

```
        Rectus abdominis
              │
              │
    ┌─────────┴─────────┐
    │   HESSELBACH'S    │
    │    TRIANGLE       │
    │    (Direct        │ ← Inferior epigastric vessels
    │     hernia)       │
    └───────────────────┘
         Inguinal ligament
```

### Direct vs Indirect Inguinal Hernia

| Feature | INDIRECT | DIRECT |
|---------|----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Congenital (patent processus) | Acquired (floor weakness) |
| **Age** | Any age, more common young | Usually >40 years |
| **Path** | Through deep ring | Through Hesselbach's triangle |
| **Relation to IEV** | LATERAL to vessels | MEDIAL to vessels |
| **Into scrotum** | Yes, often | Rarely |
| **Reduction** | Reduces toward deep ring | Reduces toward Hesselbach's |
| **Deep ring occlusion** | Controls hernia | Does NOT control |
| **Strangulation risk** | Higher | Lower |

### Nyhus Classification

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **I** | Indirect, normal deep ring (pediatric) |
| **II** | Indirect, enlarged deep ring, intact posterior wall |
| **III-A** | Direct hernia |
| **III-B** | Indirect, destroyed posterior wall ± pantaloon |
| **III-C** | Femoral hernia |
| **IV** | Recurrent hernia (A=direct, B=indirect, C=femoral, D=combined) |

### Clinical Presentation

#### Symptoms
- Groin bulge (worse with standing, coughing, straining)
- Aching discomfort
- Heaviness in groin
- May extend into scrotum (indirect)

#### Signs
- Visible/palpable bulge
- Cough impulse
- Reducibility (unless incarcerated)
- Finger invagination of scrotum → feel deep ring

#### Complications

| Complication | Definition | Management |
|--------------|------------|------------|
| **Incarceration** | Cannot reduce hernia contents | Attempt reduction → urgent repair |
| **Strangulation** | Compromised blood supply | EMERGENCY surgery |
| **Obstruction** | Bowel obstruction | Resuscitation → urgent repair |

### Surgical Repair Options

#### Open Repairs

**1. Lichtenstein Tension-Free Repair (Standard)**
```
LICHTENSTEIN TECHNIQUE:
1. Inguinal incision (skin crease)
2. Open external oblique aponeurosis
3. Identify cord structures, ilioinguinal nerve
4. Dissect hernia sac (indirect: ligate at deep ring)
5. Place flat mesh over posterior wall
6. Slit mesh around cord
7. Fix mesh to pubic tubercle, inguinal ligament, internal oblique
8. Close external oblique over mesh
```

**Advantages:**
- Low recurrence (1-2%)
- Can be done under local anesthesia
- Quick recovery

**2. Shouldice Repair (Tissue Repair)**
- 4-layer repair of posterior wall
- No mesh (useful if mesh contraindicated)
- Requires expertise
- Recurrence ~1% in expert centers

#### Laparoscopic Repairs

**1. TAPP (Transabdominal Preperitoneal)**
```
TAPP TECHNIQUE:
1. General anesthesia, supine
2. Ports: Umbilical (camera), 2 lateral
3. Enter peritoneal cavity
4. Incise peritoneum above hernia
5. Reduce sac, identify structures
6. Place mesh in preperitoneal space
7. Cover myopectineal orifice
8. Close peritoneum over mesh
```

**2. TEP (Totally Extraperitoneal)**
```
TEP TECHNIQUE:
1. Infraumbilical incision, create preperitoneal space
2. Balloon dissection (optional)
3. Place ports in preperitoneal space
4. Dissect without entering peritoneum
5. Identify structures, reduce sac
6. Place mesh covering myopectineal orifice
7. No peritoneal closure needed
```

**TAPP vs TEP:**
| Feature | TAPP | TEP |
|---------|------|-----|
| **Learning curve** | Easier | Steeper |
| **Previous surgery** | Can assess adhesions | May be difficult |
| **Peritoneal entry** | Yes | No |
| **Port site hernia** | Slightly higher | Lower |
| **Intra-abdominal injury** | Possible | Less likely |

### Complications of Hernia Repair

| Complication | Incidence | Prevention/Management |
|--------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Seroma/hematoma** | 5-10% | Usually self-resolves |
| **Wound infection** | 1-2% | Prophylactic antibiotics |
| **Chronic pain** | 5-10% | Nerve identification, mesh fixation |
| **Recurrence** | 1-2% (mesh) | Adequate mesh overlap, no tension |
| **Testicular atrophy** | <1% | Avoid cord ischemia |
| **Mesh infection** | Rare | May need mesh removal |

### Triangle of Doom & Triangle of Pain

**Critical laparoscopic anatomy:**

```
        TRIANGLE OF DOOM              TRIANGLE OF PAIN
        (contains vessels)            (contains nerves)

           Deep ring                     Deep ring
              /\                            /\
             /  \                          /  \
            /    \                        /    \
     Vas   /      \ Ext iliac       Iliopubic/  \
    deferens      vessels          tract    /    \ Lateral
                                           /      \ cutaneous
                                          /        \ nerve
```

**Triangle of Doom** (medial):
- Boundaries: Vas deferens (medial), gonadal vessels (lateral)
- Contains: External iliac vessels
- **NEVER** staple or tack here

**Triangle of Pain** (lateral):
- Boundaries: Iliopubic tract (superior), gonadal vessels (medial)
- Contains: Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, femoral branch of genitofemoral nerve
- **NEVER** staple or tack here

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What distinguishes direct from indirect inguinal hernia anatomically?
   A: Direct is MEDIAL to inferior epigastric vessels (through Hesselbach's triangle); Indirect is LATERAL (through deep ring)

2. Q: What are the boundaries of Hesselbach's triangle?
   A: Medial - rectus abdominis; Lateral - inferior epigastric vessels; Inferior - inguinal ligament

3. Q: What is the Triangle of Doom and why is it important?
   A: Space between vas deferens (medial) and gonadal vessels (lateral) containing external iliac vessels. Never staple/tack here during laparoscopic repair.
