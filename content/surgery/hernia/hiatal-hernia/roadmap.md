## 📐 Foundations

Study these **before** diving into Hiatal Hernia:

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Anatomy | Esophageal hiatus & phrenoesophageal ligament | AN21.1 | The esophageal hiatus is formed by the right crus — widening allows gastric herniation; the phrenoesophageal ligament normally anchors the GEJ |

## 🎯 Core Learning Objectives

- Classify hiatal hernias — Type I (sliding, 95%), Type II (rolling/paraesophageal), Type III (mixed), Type IV (giant)
- Differentiate management — Type I managed with GERD therapy; Types II-IV often require surgical repair
- Identify emergency presentations of paraesophageal hernia — volvulus, incarceration, strangulation

## 🔬 Clinical Science Connections

| Subject | Topic | NMC Code | Connection |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| Radiology | Barium swallow: sliding vs paraesophageal | — | Type I (sliding) shows GEJ above diaphragm; Type II-IV (paraesophageal) show gastric fundus rolling through the hiatus |

## ➡️ What's Next

After mastering this topic, progress to:

1. **GERD** — Sliding hiatal hernias contribute to GERD by disrupting the anti-reflux barrier
2. **Fundoplication** — Surgical repair includes crural closure and fundoplication

### Prerequisites

- **Esophageal Anatomy** — Hiatal anatomy and the GEJ are the key anatomical foundations

## 🔗 Cross-Subject Integration Points

Hiatal hernia bridges thoracic and abdominal anatomy (diaphragmatic hiatus, phrenoesophageal ligament), esophageal physiology (anti-reflux barrier disruption), and surgery (crural repair, fundoplication). The classification system directly predicts clinical behavior — sliding hernias cause reflux, paraesophageal hernias risk volvulus.
