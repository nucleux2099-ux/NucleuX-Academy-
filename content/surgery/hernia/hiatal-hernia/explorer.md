# Hiatal Hernia: Types & Surgical Management

> **Source**: Shackelford Ch 12-14, Maingot's Part III
> **Topic System**: [[Hernia Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Hiatal hernia is a herniation of abdominal contents (usually stomach) through the esophageal hiatus of the diaphragm, classified into 4 types with different clinical implications.

### Key Points

1. **Type I (Sliding)**: GEJ migrates into chest; 95% of hiatal hernias; associated with GERD
2. **Type II-IV (Paraesophageal)**: GEJ may stay in place; fundus/other organs herniate; risk of strangulation
3. **Symptoms**: GERD, dysphagia, chest pain, anemia (Cameron lesions), or gastric volvulus
4. **Surgery for**: Symptomatic Type I with GERD; ALL paraesophageal hernias
5. **Repair**: Reduce hernia, repair crura, add fundoplication (for Type I)

### Classification of Hiatal Hernias

| Type | Description | Frequency | Key Feature |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Type I** | Sliding hernia; GEJ moves into chest | 95% | GEJ above diaphragm |
| **Type II** | True paraesophageal; GEJ stays, fundus herniates | 2% | GEJ normal position |
| **Type III** | Mixed; both GEJ and fundus herniate | 2% | Combination of I & II |
| **Type IV** | Large PEH with other organs (colon, spleen, omentum) | <1% | Organ strangulation risk |

### Clinical Presentations

#### Type I (Sliding) - Mainly GERD Symptoms
- Heartburn, regurgitation
- Dysphagia
- Chest pain
- Cough, hoarseness

#### Types II-IV (Paraesophageal) - Variable
| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Dysphagia** | Compression of esophagus |
| **Postprandial fullness** | Herniated fundus |
| **Chest pain** | Distension, ischemia |
| **Dyspnea** | Lung compression |
| **Anemia** | Cameron lesions (linear ulcers at hiatus) |
| **Acute obstruction** | Gastric volvulus (emergency!) |

#### Gastric Volvulus (Emergency)

> [!danger] Borchardt's Triad (Acute Gastric Volvulus)
> 1. Severe epigastric pain
> 2. Retching without vomiting
> 3. Inability to pass NG tube

**Types:**
- Organoaxial: Along long axis (more common)
- Mesenteroaxial: Along short axis

**Management**: Emergency surgery - reduce volvulus, repair hernia

### Diagnostic Workup

| Test | Role |
|------|------|
| **Barium swallow** | Best for anatomy; shows hernia type and size |
| **EGD** | Evaluate esophagitis, Barrett's, Cameron lesions |
| **CT chest/abdomen** | Confirm large PEH, identify other organs |
| **Manometry** | Assess esophageal motility before surgery |
| **pH study** | Quantify reflux (for Type I) |

### Indications for Surgery

#### Type I (Sliding)
- GERD refractory to medical therapy
- Patient preference over lifelong PPI
- Complications: Stricture, Barrett's, aspiration
- Large type I with significant symptoms

#### Types II-IV (Paraesophageal)
**Traditionally: All PEH should be repaired** (due to strangulation risk)

**Current approach:**
- Symptomatic PEH → Repair
- Asymptomatic small PEH → Consider watchful waiting in elderly/high-risk
- Acute gastric volvulus → Emergency repair

### Surgical Technique

#### Standard Approach: Laparoscopic Repair

```
HIATAL HERNIA REPAIR STEPS:

1. Position: Supine, split legs, reverse Trendelenburg
2. Ports: 5 ports, liver retractor
3. Reduce hernia contents into abdomen
4. Excise hernia sac (controversial - at least mobilize)
5. Mobilize esophagus (adequate intra-abdominal length)
6. Identify and protect vagus nerves
7. Close crura posteriorly (± anterior)
8. ADD FUNDOPLICATION (for Type I with GERD):
   - Nissen (360°) - most common
   - Toupet (270°) - if motility concerns
   - Dor (anterior) - less common
9. Consider gastropexy (tack stomach to abdominal wall)
10. ± Mesh reinforcement of crural repair
```

#### Key Technical Points

**Adequate Esophageal Length:**
- Need 2-3 cm tension-free intra-abdominal esophagus
- If short esophagus: Collis gastroplasty (lengthening procedure)

**Crural Closure:**
- Primary suture closure standard
- Mesh for large defects (>5 cm)
- Mesh type: Biological or absorbable preferred (to avoid erosion)

**Fundoplication:**
- Type I with GERD: Add anti-reflux procedure
- Type II-IV: Fundoplication optional (reduces recurrence)
- Gastropexy may help prevent recurrence

### Outcomes & Complications

#### Success Rates
- Type I + fundoplication: 90% symptom relief
- PEH repair: 85-95% (higher recurrence for large hernias)

#### Recurrence
| Factor | Recurrence Risk |
|--------|-----------------|
| Hernia size >8 cm | Higher |
| Tension on repair | Higher |
| No mesh | Higher for large defects |
| Inadequate crural closure | Higher |

**Radiographic recurrence**: Up to 25-40%
**Symptomatic recurrence**: 5-15%

#### Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------|
| **Dysphagia** | 10-20% early | Usually resolves; dilate if persistent |
| **Gas bloat** | 5-10% | Usually resolves |
| **Recurrence** | 5-15% symptomatic | Re-repair if symptomatic |
| **Wrap migration** | 2-5% | May need revision |
| **Vagal injury** | <1% | Gastroparesis |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What distinguishes Type I from Type II hiatal hernia?
   A: Type I (sliding): GEJ migrates into chest. Type II (paraesophageal): GEJ stays in normal position but fundus herniates alongside esophagus.

2. Q: What is Borchardt's triad?
   A: Severe epigastric pain, retching without vomiting, inability to pass NG tube - indicates acute gastric volvulus (emergency).

3. Q: When is mesh used in hiatal hernia repair?
   A: For large crural defects (>5 cm) to reinforce repair and reduce recurrence. Biological or absorbable mesh preferred.
