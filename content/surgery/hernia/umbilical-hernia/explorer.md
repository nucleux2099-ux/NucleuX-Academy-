# Umbilical Hernia: Pediatric & Adult Management

> **Source**: Maingot's Part I Ch 12, Fischer's Ch 22
> **Topic System**: [[Hernia Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Umbilical hernia is a protrusion through the umbilical ring, occurring as a congenital defect in children (usually self-resolves) or as an acquired weakness in adults (requires repair).

### Key Points

1. **Pediatric**: Congenital; 10-20% of infants; most close by age 4-5; observe initially
2. **Adult**: Acquired; risk factors: obesity, ascites, pregnancy, multiparous women
3. **True vs Para**: True = through umbilical ring; Para = through linea alba adjacent to umbilicus
4. **Repair indication**: All symptomatic; pediatric if persists >5 years or >2cm
5. **Technique**: Small defect (<2cm) = suture; Large = mesh

## Detailed Notes

### Pediatric Umbilical Hernia

**Epidemiology:**
- 10-20% of all newborns
- Higher in African descent, preterm infants
- Equal sex distribution

**Natural History:**
- 80-90% close spontaneously by age 4-5
- Larger defects less likely to close
- Rarely incarcerate in children

**Indications for Repair:**
| Indication | Timing |
|------------|--------|
| Persists >4-5 years | Elective repair |
| Defect >2 cm | Less likely to close, repair earlier |
| Symptomatic | Repair |
| Incarceration (rare) | Urgent repair |
| Proboscoid hernia | Unlikely to close, repair |

**Repair Technique:**
- Infraumbilical curvilinear incision
- Dissect sac, reduce contents
- Close fascial defect with absorbable suture
- Preserve umbilical cosmesis

### Adult Umbilical/Paraumbilical Hernia

**Risk Factors:**
- Obesity
- Ascites/cirrhosis
- Multiparity
- Chronic cough
- Heavy lifting

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Visible bulge at umbilicus
- Pain with straining, coughing
- May contain omentum or bowel
- Skin changes if large (thinning, ulceration)

**Complications:**
- Incarceration (15-20%)
- Strangulation
- Skin necrosis (in large neglected hernias)
- Rupture of ascitic fluid ("flood syndrome" in cirrhotics)

### Repair Techniques

#### Small Defect (<2 cm): Suture Repair

**Mayo Repair (Vest-over-Pants)**
- Overlapping repair of fascia
- Historical technique, higher recurrence
- Still used for small defects

**Simple Fascial Closure**
- Primary approximation
- For defects <1-2 cm
- Acceptable if no tension

#### Larger Defect (≥2 cm): Mesh Repair

**Mesh Options:**
| Location | Technique |
|----------|-----------|
| **Onlay** | Mesh on top of fascia |
| **Sublay/Retrorectus** | Mesh behind rectus muscle |
| **Underlay/IPOM** | Intraperitoneal mesh (laparoscopic) |
| **Preperitoneal** | Between peritoneum and fascia |

**Preferred**: Sublay or preperitoneal (mesh away from skin, less infection)

**Laparoscopic Repair:**
- IPOM (Intraperitoneal Onlay Mesh)
- Requires composite mesh (non-adhesive surface to bowel)
- Good for recurrent or larger hernias

### Special Situation: Cirrhosis with Ascites

> [!warning] High-Risk Group
> Umbilical hernia in cirrhosis with ascites:
> - High complication rate (skin necrosis, rupture)
> - High surgical mortality
> - Optimize liver function first (TIPS if needed)
> - Repair with mesh, drain ascites
> - Consider prophylactic repair before transplant

**Management:**
1. Control ascites (diuretics, TIPS, paracentesis)
2. Optimize nutrition and coagulation
3. Elective repair when possible
4. Emergency repair has high mortality (30%)

### Outcomes

| Repair Type | Recurrence Rate |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Suture repair (Mayo) | 10-20% |
| Mesh repair (open) | 1-5% |
| Laparoscopic IPOM | 2-5% |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: At what age should a pediatric umbilical hernia be repaired if it hasn't closed?
   A: Age 4-5 years, or earlier if defect >2cm or symptomatic

2. Q: What are the major risk factors for adult umbilical hernia?
   A: Obesity, ascites/cirrhosis, multiparity, chronic cough

3. Q: When is mesh recommended for umbilical hernia repair?
   A: Defects ≥2 cm to reduce recurrence (1-5% vs 10-20% for suture)
