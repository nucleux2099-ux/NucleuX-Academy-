# Choledocholithiasis: Diagnosis & Management

> **Source**: Blumgart Ch 35-36, ASGE Guidelines
> **Topic System**: [[Biliary Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Choledocholithiasis refers to stones in the common bile duct, which may be primary (form in ducts) or secondary (migrate from gallbladder), causing biliary obstruction and risk of cholangitis.

### Key Points

1. **Prevalence**: 10-15% of patients with gallstones have CBD stones
2. **Origin**: Secondary (90%) - migrated from GB; Primary (10%) - brown pigment, form in ducts
3. **Presentation**: May be asymptomatic, or cause jaundice, pain, elevated LFTs, or cholangitis
4. **Diagnosis**: MRCP or EUS for confirmation; ERCP is therapeutic
5. **Treatment**: ERCP + sphincterotomy → lap chole; or LCBDE during chole

### Clinical Relevance

- **When to suspect**: Jaundice, dilated CBD on US, elevated ALP/bilirubin with gallstones
- **Red flags**: Fever, rigors → cholangitis (emergency)
- **Decision point**: Pre-op ERCP vs intraoperative management (LCBDE or IOC-guided approach)

## Detailed Notes

### Risk Stratification (ASGE Guidelines)

```
CBD STONE PROBABILITY ASSESSMENT

High Risk (>50%):                     → ERCP before chole
├── CBD stone on imaging
├── Cholangitis
└── Bilirubin >4 + dilated CBD

Intermediate Risk (10-50%):           → MRCP or EUS or IOC
├── Dilated CBD (>6mm with GB)
├── Elevated LFTs (any)
└── Age >55
└── Gallstone pancreatitis

Low Risk (<10%):                      → Proceed to chole
├── No predictors
└── Normal labs and imaging
```

### Diagnostic Approach

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Role |
|------|-------------|-------------|------|
| **Transabdominal US** | 50-80% | 95% | First-line; may miss stones, shows dilation |
| **MRCP** | 95% | 95% | Non-invasive confirmation; intermediate risk |
| **EUS** | 95% | 98% | Best accuracy; intermediate risk |
| **IOC** | 90-95% | 95% | Intraoperative evaluation |
| **ERCP** | 95% | 95% | Therapeutic; high risk patients |

### Laboratory Findings

| Test | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| **ALP** | Elevated (often >2x) | First to rise |
| **GGT** | Elevated | Sensitive for biliary disease |
| **Bilirubin** | Elevated | May be normal if partial/intermittent obstruction |
| **AST/ALT** | Mildly elevated | May spike with stone passage |
| **Amylase/Lipase** | Elevated | If associated pancreatitis |

### Management Strategies

#### ERCP + Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy (Most Common)

```
CONFIRMED/SUSPECTED CBD STONE
            │
            ▼
    ┌───────────────┐
    │     ERCP      │
    │ + Sphincterotomy
    │ + Stone extraction
    └───────┬───────┘
            │
            ▼
    Lap Cholecystectomy
    (same admission or within 2 weeks)
```

**ERCP Techniques:**
- Sphincterotomy + balloon extraction (most common)
- Mechanical lithotripsy for large stones
- Cholangioscopy with laser/EHL for difficult stones
- Plastic or metal stent for incomplete clearance

#### Laparoscopic CBD Exploration (LCBDE)

**Single-stage approach during lap chole:**
- Transcystic approach (for small stones <6mm)
- Choledochotomy (for larger stones)

**Advantages:**
- Single procedure, single anesthesia
- Avoids ERCP complications
- Preserves sphincter (transcystic)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires expertise and equipment
- Longer OR time

#### Open CBD Exploration

- Rarely needed now
- Indications: Failed ERCP/LCBDE, open chole, complex anatomy

### ERCP Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Prevention/Management |
|--------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Post-ERCP pancreatitis** | 3-7% | Rectal indomethacin, pancreatic stent |
| **Bleeding** | 1-2% | Endoscopic hemostasis |
| **Perforation** | 0.5-1% | Retroperitoneal (sphincterotomy site) |
| **Cholangitis** | 1-2% | Complete drainage; failed = PTC |

### Special Situations

#### Cholangitis (Emergency)
- Charcot's triad: Fever, jaundice, RUQ pain
- Reynolds' pentad: + hypotension, altered mental status
- **Treatment**: Antibiotics + urgent ERCP (<24h)

#### Gallstone Pancreatitis
- ERCP indicated if: Cholangitis or persistent biliary obstruction
- Timing: Urgent if cholangitis; can wait if improving
- Cholecystectomy: Same admission after pancreatitis resolves

#### Large CBD Stones (>15mm)
- May need: Mechanical lithotripsy, cholangioscopy, or surgical exploration
- Consider: Biliary stent as bridge if cannot clear

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What are the high-risk predictors for CBD stones per ASGE guidelines?
   A: CBD stone on imaging, clinical cholangitis, or bilirubin >4 with dilated CBD

2. Q: What is the difference between primary and secondary CBD stones?
   A: Secondary (90%): Migrated from gallbladder (cholesterol). Primary (10%): Form in ducts (brown pigment, associated with infection/stasis)

3. Q: What is the main advantage of LCBDE over ERCP?
   A: Single-stage procedure avoiding ERCP complications (post-ERCP pancreatitis) and preserving sphincter if transcystic
