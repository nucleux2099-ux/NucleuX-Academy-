# Lesser Sac (Omental Bursa)

## Overview
The lesser sac is a large peritoneal cavity posterior to the stomach and lesser omentum. It communicates with the greater sac (general peritoneal cavity) through the epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow). It allows free movement of the stomach and is clinically significant as a site for collection of fluid (e.g., pancreatic pseudocyst).

## Key Points
- Lies behind stomach and lesser omentum
- Communicates with greater sac via epiploic foramen (of Winslow)
- Two recesses: superior (behind caudate lobe) and inferior (in greater omentum)
- Posterior wall: structures of the stomach bed
- Important in spread of peritoneal collections

## Gross Anatomy

### Boundaries

| Boundary | Structure |
|----------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | Lesser omentum, posterior wall of stomach, gastrocolic ligament |
| **Posterior** | Pancreas, left kidney, left suprarenal, transverse mesocolon, aorta, IVC |
| **Superior** | Caudate lobe of liver, diaphragm |
| **Inferior** | Transverse colon, transverse mesocolon (fusion of greater omentum layers) |
| **Left** | Spleen, gastrosplenic ligament, lienorenal (splenorenal) ligament |
| **Right** | Communicates with greater sac through epiploic foramen |

### Epiploic Foramen (Foramen of Winslow)
| Boundary | Structure |
|----------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | Free edge of lesser omentum (hepatoduodenal ligament) containing portal vein, hepatic artery proper, CBD |
| **Posterior** | IVC, right crus of diaphragm |
| **Superior** | Caudate lobe of liver |
| **Inferior** | First part of duodenum |

### Recesses
1. **Superior recess:** Behind caudate lobe of liver, up to diaphragm
2. **Inferior recess:** Extends between layers of greater omentum (obliterated in adults)
3. **Splenic recess:** Extends to the left towards the hilum of spleen

### Stomach Bed (Posterior Wall of Lesser Sac)
Structures forming the stomach bed (seen after lifting the stomach):
- Left crus of diaphragm
- Left suprarenal gland
- Upper pole of left kidney
- Splenic artery
- Body and tail of pancreas
- Transverse mesocolon
- Splenic flexure of colon

## Relations & Surface Markings
- The lesser sac extends from the diaphragm superiorly to the transverse colon inferiorly
- Accessed surgically through the gastrocolic ligament or lesser omentum

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply
- The lesser sac itself has no specific blood supply — it is a peritoneal space
- The peritoneum lining it receives supply from adjacent visceral vessels

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Pancreatic Pseudocyst
- Commonly presents in the lesser sac (posterior to stomach)
- Results from acute pancreatitis — collection of pancreatic juice
- Displaces stomach anteriorly

### Pringle Manoeuvre
- Compression of the free edge of lesser omentum (anterior boundary of epiploic foramen)
- Occludes hepatic artery and portal vein → controls hepatic bleeding
- If bleeding continues after Pringle → hepatic vein or IVC injury

### Peritoneal Fluid Collections
- Lesser sac acts as a relatively closed space
- Fluid from perforated posterior gastric ulcer collects here
- May be drained through the epiploic foramen or by opening gastrocolic ligament

### Surgical Access
- **Through gastrocolic ligament:** Most common approach (for pancreatic surgery)
- **Through lesser omentum:** For access to coeliac trunk area
- **Through transverse mesocolon:** For retroperitoneal access

### Posterior Gastric Ulcer Perforation
- Gastric contents → lesser sac → relatively contained (unlike anterior perforation → general peritoneal cavity)

## Development
- Develops from the right side of the dorsal mesogastrium
- As the stomach rotates, the space behind it expands to form the lesser sac
- Greater omentum grows down from greater curvature, enclosing part of the lesser sac
