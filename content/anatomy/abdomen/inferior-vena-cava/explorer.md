# Inferior Vena Cava (IVC)

## Overview
The inferior vena cava is the largest vein in the body, draining blood from the lower limbs, pelvis, and abdomen to the right atrium. It is formed by the union of the two common iliac veins at L5 level, ascends on the right side of the aorta, and passes through the caval opening in the diaphragm at T8 to enter the right atrium.

## Key Points
- Formed at L5 by union of right and left common iliac veins
- Ascends on the right of the aorta
- Passes through caval opening in diaphragm at T8 (tendinous part)
- No valves except at its termination (Eustachian valve in right atrium)
- Receives hepatic veins just before passing through diaphragm
- Does NOT receive left gonadal or left suprarenal veins (they drain into left renal vein)

## Gross Anatomy

### Course
- **Formation:** L5 level, right of midline, behind right common iliac artery
- **Course:** Ascends retroperitoneally on right side of vertebral column
- **Termination:** Passes through caval opening (T8) → right atrium

### Relations

| Aspect | Structures |
|--------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | Peritoneum, right common iliac artery, head of pancreas, D1, portal vein, liver (bare area), epiploic foramen |
| **Posterior** | Right psoas, right crus of diaphragm, right renal artery, right suprarenal gland, vertebral bodies |
| **Right** | Right ureter, right kidney, right lobe of liver |
| **Left** | Aorta (throughout), right crus of diaphragm (above) |

### Tributaries
#### Direct tributaries (paired):
- Common iliac veins (formation)
- Lumbar veins (3rd and 4th)
- Right gonadal vein
- Renal veins (right and left)
- Right suprarenal vein
- Inferior phrenic veins
- Hepatic veins (2–3, enter just below diaphragm)

#### Indirect tributaries:
- Left gonadal vein → left renal vein
- Left suprarenal vein → left renal vein

### Important: Left Renal Vein
- Longer than right (crosses anterior to aorta, posterior to SMA)
- Receives left gonadal vein and left suprarenal vein
- "Nutcracker" position between aorta and SMA

## Relations & Surface Markings
- To the right of midline, from L5 to T8
- Lies in a groove on the posterior surface of the liver (bare area)
- Forms anterior boundary of epiploic foramen (of Winslow)

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply
- Vasa vasorum from adjacent arteries supply the IVC wall
- Sympathetic innervation from adjacent plexuses

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### IVC Obstruction
- Causes: thrombosis, tumour invasion (renal cell carcinoma), Budd-Chiari syndrome
- Features: bilateral lower limb oedema, distended superficial veins of abdomen, ascites

### Collateral Pathways in IVC Obstruction
1. Lumbar azygos → azygos/hemiazygos system
2. Superficial epigastric → lateral thoracic (thoracoepigastric vein)
3. Paravertebral venous plexus (Batson's plexus)

### IVC Filter
- Placed below renal veins to prevent pulmonary embolism
- Indications: contraindication to anticoagulation, recurrent PE despite anticoagulation

### Renal Cell Carcinoma
- Commonly invades renal vein and extends into IVC (especially left-sided tumours)
- Left renal vein also drains left gonadal vein → left varicocele with RCC

### Budd-Chiari Syndrome
- Obstruction of hepatic veins or IVC at hepatic level
- Hepatomegaly, ascites, abdominal pain

### Epiploic Foramen (Foramen of Winslow)
- IVC forms the posterior boundary
- Pringle manoeuvre: compression of hepatoduodenal ligament (anterior boundary)

## Development
- Complex development from multiple embryonic veins:
  - Subcardinal veins (renal segment)
  - Supracardinal veins (infrarenal segment)
  - Vitelline/hepatic veins (hepatic segment)
  - Right subcardinal-supracardinal anastomosis (prerenal segment)
- Anomalies: double IVC, left-sided IVC, absent infrarenal IVC
