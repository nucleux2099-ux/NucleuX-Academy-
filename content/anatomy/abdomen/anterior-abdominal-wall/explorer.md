# Anterior Abdominal Wall

## Overview
The anterior abdominal wall extends from the xiphoid process and costal margins superiorly to the inguinal ligament, pubic crest, and pubic symphysis inferiorly. It provides protection and support to abdominal viscera, assists in respiration, micturition, defecation, parturition, and coughing.

## Key Points
- Nine-region and four-quadrant systems for surface anatomy
- Three flat muscles (external oblique, internal oblique, transversus abdominis) and two vertical muscles (rectus abdominis, pyramidalis)
- Rectus sheath formed by aponeuroses of the three flat muscles
- Blood supply: superior and inferior epigastric arteries (anastomose)
- Innervation: T7–T12, L1 (iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal)

## Gross Anatomy

### Layers (Superficial to Deep)
1. **Skin**
2. **Superficial fascia** — two layers:
   - Camper's fascia (fatty layer)
   - Scarpa's fascia (membranous layer) → continuous with Colles' fascia in perineum
3. **Deep fascia** — thin, insignificant
4. **Muscles and aponeuroses**
5. **Transversalis fascia**
6. **Extraperitoneal fat**
7. **Parietal peritoneum**

### Muscles

| Muscle | Origin | Insertion | Nerve Supply | Action |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|--------|
| External oblique | Lower 8 ribs (5–12) | Linea alba, pubic crest, iliac crest, inguinal ligament | T7–T12 | Compresses abdomen, flexion, contralateral rotation |
| Internal oblique | Thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, lateral 2/3 inguinal ligament | Lower 3 ribs, linea alba, pubic crest (conjoint tendon) | T7–T12, L1 | Compresses abdomen, ipsilateral rotation |
| Transversus abdominis | Lower 6 costal cartilages, thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, lateral 1/3 inguinal ligament | Linea alba, pubic crest (conjoint tendon) | T7–T12, L1 | Compresses abdomen |
| Rectus abdominis | Pubic crest, pubic symphysis | Xiphoid process, 5th–7th costal cartilages | T7–T12 | Flexes trunk, compresses abdomen |
| Pyramidalis | Pubic crest | Linea alba | T12 | Tenses linea alba |

### Linea Alba
- Midline fibrous raphe from xiphoid to pubic symphysis
- Formed by interlacing of aponeuroses of the three flat muscles
- Wider above the umbilicus

### Umbilicus
- Located at L3–L4 level (variable)
- Scar of attachment of umbilical cord
- Deep to it: median umbilical ligament (obliterated urachus)

## Relations & Surface Markings
- **Transpyloric plane (L1):** midpoint between jugular notch and pubic symphysis
- **Subcostal plane (L3):** lower border of 10th costal cartilage
- **Transtubercular plane (L5):** level of iliac tubercles
- **McBurney's point:** junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of line from ASIS to umbilicus

## Blood Supply
- **Superior epigastric artery** (from internal thoracic)
- **Inferior epigastric artery** (from external iliac)
- **Superficial epigastric artery** (from femoral)
- **Deep circumflex iliac artery** (from external iliac)
- **Posterior intercostal arteries** (T7–T11)
- **Subcostal artery** (T12)

### Anastomosis
- Superior and inferior epigastric arteries anastomose posterior to rectus abdominis — important collateral circulation

## Nerve Supply
- **Intercostal nerves** T7–T11
- **Subcostal nerve** T12
- **Iliohypogastric nerve** L1
- **Ilioinguinal nerve** L1

### Dermatomes
- T7: xiphoid
- T10: umbilicus
- T12/L1: inguinal region

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Surgical Incisions
- **Midline:** through linea alba — relatively bloodless
- **Paramedian:** through rectus sheath, rectus displaced laterally
- **Kocher's (subcostal):** for cholecystectomy
- **McBurney's (gridiron):** for appendicectomy — muscle-splitting
- **Pfannenstiel:** suprapubic transverse — for C-sections

### Scarpa's Fascia
- Urine extravasation in ruptured bulbar urethra spreads into anterior abdominal wall (deep to Scarpa's) but NOT into the thigh (fused to fascia lata)

### Rectus Sheath Haematoma
- Injury to superior/inferior epigastric vessels
- Positive Fothergill's sign: mass does not cross midline, more prominent on tensing rectus

## Development
- Muscles develop from myotomes of lower thoracic and upper lumbar somites
- Umbilicus: site of physiological herniation during development (weeks 6–10)
