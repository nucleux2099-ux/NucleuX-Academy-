# Stomach

## Overview
The stomach is the most dilated part of the GI tract, located in the left hypochondrium and epigastrium. It is a J-shaped muscular organ that stores food, mixes it with gastric juice, and initiates protein digestion. It extends from the cardiac orifice (T11) to the pylorus (L1). It is entirely intraperitoneal.

## Key Points
- J-shaped, entirely intraperitoneal (covered by peritoneum all around)
- Capacity: ~1500 mL (50 mL at birth)
- Two curvatures (lesser, greater), two orifices (cardiac, pyloric), two surfaces (anterior, posterior)
- Four parts: cardia, fundus, body, pylorus (antrum + canal)
- Blood supply from all three branches of coeliac trunk
- Three muscle layers (outer longitudinal, middle circular, inner oblique)

## Gross Anatomy

### Parts
1. **Cardia:** Around cardiac orifice, at T11 (left of midline)
2. **Fundus:** Dome-shaped, above cardiac orifice, related to left dome of diaphragm; contains gas bubble on X-ray
3. **Body:** Largest part, between fundus and pyloric antrum
4. **Pylorus:** Divided into antrum (dilated) and canal (narrow); pyloric sphincter at L1 (transpyloric plane)

### Curvatures
- **Lesser curvature:** Shorter, concave, right side; attached to lesser omentum; incisura angularis marks junction of body and pyloric antrum
- **Greater curvature:** Longer, convex, left side; attached to greater omentum, gastrosplenic ligament, gastrophrenic ligament

### Surfaces
- **Anterior:** Related to diaphragm, left lobe of liver, anterior abdominal wall
- **Posterior (Stomach bed):** Left crus of diaphragm, left suprarenal, upper pole of left kidney, splenic artery, pancreas body, transverse mesocolon, splenic flexure

### Peritoneal Relations
- Greater omentum from greater curvature
- Lesser omentum from lesser curvature to liver
- Gastrosplenic ligament (to spleen)
- Gastrophrenic ligament (to diaphragm)

### Histology
- Three muscle layers: outer longitudinal, middle circular, inner oblique
- Middle circular thickens at pylorus → pyloric sphincter
- Inner oblique unique to stomach (absent in rest of GIT)
- Rugae: longitudinal mucosal folds for distension

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Cardiac orifice: 2.5 cm to the left of midline at T11
- Pylorus: L1 (transpyloric plane), 1.25 cm to the right of midline
- Fundus: 5th intercostal space in left midclavicular line

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial (All from coeliac trunk)
| Artery | Origin | Area Supplied |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| Left gastric | Coeliac trunk | Lesser curvature (upper) |
| Right gastric | Hepatic artery proper | Lesser curvature (lower) |
| Left gastroepiploic | Splenic artery | Greater curvature (upper) |
| Right gastroepiploic | Gastroduodenal artery | Greater curvature (lower) |
| Short gastric (5–7) | Splenic artery | Fundus |

### Venous
- Veins correspond to arteries and drain into portal venous system
- Left gastric vein → portal vein (site of portosystemic anastomosis at lower oesophagus)

### Lymphatic
- Follows arterial supply → coeliac nodes
- Four zones draining to: left gastric, right gastric, right gastroepiploic, left gastroepiploic/splenic nodes

### Nerve Supply
- **Parasympathetic:** Vagus nerves — anterior vagal trunk (mainly left vagus) and posterior vagal trunk (mainly right vagus)
- **Sympathetic:** T6–T9 via greater splanchnic nerve → coeliac plexus
- Anterior vagal trunk: mainly to anterior surface (hepatic branch, gastric branches)
- Posterior vagal trunk: coeliac branch (large), gastric branches
- Nerve of Latarjet: continuation along lesser curvature → "crow's foot" at pyloric antrum

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Gastric Ulcer
- Most common on lesser curvature (poorest blood supply, most acid exposure)
- Anterior ulcer → perforation; Posterior → erosion of splenic artery → massive haemorrhage

### Vagotomy
- Truncal: both vagal trunks cut → drainage procedure needed
- Selective: gastric branches only
- Highly selective (parietal cell): preserves nerve of Latarjet → no drainage needed

### Hiatus Hernia
- Sliding (80%): cardia slides through oesophageal hiatus
- Rolling/paraesophageal (20%): fundus herniates beside oesophagus

### Congenital Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis
- Hypertrophy of pyloric sphincter (circular muscle)
- Projectile non-bilious vomiting in 3–6 week old infant
- Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy

### Gastrectomy
- Total or partial; knowledge of arterial arcades essential
- Billroth I (gastroduodenostomy), Billroth II (gastrojejunostomy)

## Development
- Develops from the foregut
- Rotates 90° clockwise (viewed from above)
- Left vagus → anterior; Right vagus → posterior (due to rotation)
- Greater curvature grows faster → J-shape
