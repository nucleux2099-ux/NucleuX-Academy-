# Inferior Mesenteric Artery (IMA)

## Overview
The inferior mesenteric artery is the smallest of the three unpaired ventral branches of the abdominal aorta. It supplies the hindgut derivatives — from the left 1/3 of the transverse colon to the upper part of the anal canal. It arises from the anterior surface of the aorta at L3 level.

## Key Points
- Arises from aorta at L3 level
- Supplies hindgut: left 1/3 transverse colon → rectum → upper anal canal
- Three branches: left colic, sigmoid (2–4), superior rectal
- Marginal artery of Drummond connects SMA and IMA territories
- Critical point of Sudeck: junction of sigmoid and superior rectal arteries

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin, Course & Termination
- **Origin:** Anterior surface of abdominal aorta at L3
- **Course:** Descends to the left, retroperitoneally, crosses left common iliac artery
- **Termination:** Continues as superior rectal artery after crossing left common iliac artery

### Branches
1. **Left colic artery:** Ascends retroperitoneally to supply left 1/3 of transverse colon and descending colon. Divides into ascending and descending branches.
2. **Sigmoid arteries (2–4):** Supply sigmoid colon through sigmoid mesocolon
3. **Superior rectal artery:** Terminal branch, supplies upper rectum. Descends into pelvis in mesorectum.

### Relations
| Aspect | Structures |
|--------|-----------|
| Anterior | Peritoneum, splenic vein, body of pancreas |
| Posterior | Aorta, left psoas major |
| Left | Left ureter, left gonadal vessels |

### Marginal Artery (of Drummond)
- Continuous arterial arcade along the inner margin of the colon
- Connects SMA and IMA territories
- Particularly important at splenic flexure — **watershed area**

### Arc of Riolan (Meandering Mesenteric Artery)
- Inconstant; connects SMA and IMA directly through the mesentery
- Important collateral in IMA/SMA occlusion

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Origin: L3, about 3–4 cm above aortic bifurcation
- Left of midline on posterior abdominal wall
- Crosses left common iliac artery to enter pelvis

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Supply Territory
- Left 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, upper anal canal

### Venous Drainage
- **Inferior mesenteric vein (IMV):** drains same territory
- IMV ascends to the left of IMA, passes behind the pancreas
- Joins splenic vein (most common) or SMV or at the confluence

### Nerve Supply
- Sympathetic: L1–L2 (via lumbar splanchnic nerves → inferior mesenteric plexus)
- Parasympathetic: pelvic splanchnic nerves S2–S4

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Watershed Areas
1. **Splenic flexure** (Griffith's point): junction of SMA and IMA territories — vulnerable to ischaemia in hypotension
2. **Rectosigmoid junction** (Sudeck's critical point): junction of sigmoid and superior rectal arteries

### IMA Ligation in Surgery
- Ligated in left hemicolectomy, sigmoid colectomy, anterior resection
- Adequate collateral via marginal artery usually ensures viability
- High ligation at origin necessary for lymph node clearance in cancer

### Ischaemic Colitis
- Most common at splenic flexure (watershed)
- Occurs after aortic surgery if IMA not reimplanted
- Presents with bloody diarrhoea, abdominal pain

### IMV as Surgical Landmark
- In left paraduodenal hernia, IMV lies in the anterior margin of the fossa
- During mobilization of splenic flexure, IMV identified and preserved or ligated

## Development
- Develops from the vitelline artery supplying the hindgut
- The hindgut-midgut junction is at the junction of right 2/3 and left 1/3 of transverse colon
