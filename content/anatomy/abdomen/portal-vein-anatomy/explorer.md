# Portal Vein

## Overview
The portal vein is the main venous channel of the portal venous system, carrying nutrient-rich but deoxygenated blood from the GIT, spleen, pancreas, and gallbladder to the liver. It is formed behind the neck of the pancreas by the union of the superior mesenteric vein (SMV) and splenic vein. It has no valves.

## Key Points
- Formed behind neck of pancreas at L2 by SMV + splenic vein
- Length ~8 cm, diameter ~1.2 cm
- Ascends in free edge of lesser omentum (hepatoduodenal ligament)
- Divides at porta hepatis into right and left branches
- No valves — allows bidirectional flow in pathology
- Tributaries: SMV, splenic vein, left gastric, right gastric, cystic, paraumbilical veins

## Gross Anatomy

### Formation
- Behind neck of pancreas at L2
- Union of SMV (from right) and splenic vein (from left)
- IMV usually joins splenic vein (or SMV or at confluence)

### Course
1. Ascends behind D1 of duodenum
2. Enters free edge of lesser omentum (hepatoduodenal ligament)
3. Ascends to porta hepatis
4. Divides into right and left branches within porta hepatis

### Relations
| Aspect | Structures |
|--------|-----------|
| Anterior | D1 of duodenum (below), hepatic artery proper (in free edge) |
| Posterior | IVC (separated by epiploic foramen) |
| Right | CBD (in free edge of lesser omentum) |
| Left | Hepatic artery proper |

**In the free edge (hepatoduodenal ligament):**
- Portal vein: most posterior
- Hepatic artery proper: left and anterior
- CBD: right and anterior

### Tributaries
**Direct tributaries:**
- Left gastric vein (coronary vein)
- Right gastric vein
- Cystic vein
- Paraumbilical veins (in falciform ligament)

**Forming tributaries:**
- Superior mesenteric vein
- Splenic vein (receives IMV and short gastric veins)

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Origin: behind neck of pancreas, at L2
- Lies in the hepatoduodenal ligament (free edge of lesser omentum)
- Most posterior structure in the portal triad

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply
- Vasa vasorum from adjacent arteries
- Sympathetic fibres from hepatic plexus around it

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Portal Hypertension
- Normal portal pressure: 5–10 mmHg
- Portal hypertension: >12 mmHg
- Causes: prehepatic (portal vein thrombosis), hepatic (cirrhosis), posthepatic (Budd-Chiari)

### Sites of Portosystemic Anastomosis
1. **Lower oesophagus:** Left gastric vein ↔ oesophageal veins (azygos) → oesophageal varices
2. **Rectum/anal canal:** Superior rectal vein ↔ middle/inferior rectal veins → rectal varices
3. **Umbilicus:** Paraumbilical veins ↔ superficial epigastric veins → caput medusae
4. **Retroperitoneal:** Colic/pancreatic veins ↔ renal/lumbar/phrenic veins
5. **Bare area of liver:** Portal radicles ↔ diaphragmatic veins

### Caput Medusae
- Dilated paraumbilical veins radiating from umbilicus
- Flow: away from umbilicus (portal hypertension)

### Portal Vein Thrombosis
- Prehepatic cause of portal hypertension
- May occur in neonates (umbilical sepsis → portal pyaemia)
- Causes splenomegaly with normal liver function

### Pringle Manoeuvre
- Compression of hepatoduodenal ligament (contains portal vein, hepatic artery, CBD)
- Controls hepatic haemorrhage

## Development
- Develops from vitelline veins
- Right vitelline vein → portal vein and hepatic sinusoids
- Left vitelline vein mostly degenerates
