# Appendix (Vermiform Appendix)

## Overview
The vermiform appendix is a narrow, blind-ended tube arising from the posteromedial aspect of the caecum, about 2 cm below the ileocaecal junction. It is a vestigial organ but rich in lymphoid tissue (sometimes called the "abdominal tonsil"). It is one of the most clinically important structures due to the frequency of appendicitis.

## Key Points
- Arises from caecum at convergence of three taeniae coli
- Average length: 6–9 cm (range 2–20 cm)
- Has a complete peritoneal covering and its own mesentery (mesoappendix)
- Blood supply: appendicular artery (branch of ileocolic artery) — an end artery
- Base is constant; tip varies in position

## Gross Anatomy

### Position of the Tip
| Position | Frequency |
|----------|-----------|
| Retrocaecal/retrocolic | 65% (most common) |
| Pelvic | 30% |
| Subcaecal | 2% |
| Pre-ileal | 1% |
| Post-ileal | 0.5% |

### Structure
- All four coats of the GI tract present
- Serosa: complete peritoneal covering
- Muscularis: longitudinal muscle forms a complete layer (unlike caecum)
- Submucosa: rich in lymphoid follicles (peak at puberty, atrophy in old age)
- Mucosa: columnar epithelium with goblet cells

### Mesoappendix
- Triangular fold of peritoneum
- Contains the appendicular artery, vein, nerve, and lymphatics
- Attaches appendix to the lower part of the mesentery of the ileum

## Relations & Surface Markings

### Surface Marking of Base
- **McBurney's point:** Junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of the line from right ASIS to the umbilicus
- Base is constant even when tip position varies

### Relations
- **Anterior:** Anterior abdominal wall (in McBurney's position), small intestine coils
- **Posterior:** Psoas major, right ureter (retrocaecal appendix)
- **Medial:** Terminal ileum

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial Supply
- **Appendicular artery** — branch of the lower division of the ileocolic artery
- It is an **end artery** — thrombosis leads to gangrenous appendicitis

### Venous Drainage
- Appendicular vein → ileocolic vein → superior mesenteric vein → portal vein

### Lymphatic Drainage
- Ileocolic lymph nodes → superior mesenteric nodes

### Nerve Supply
- Sympathetic: T10 (via superior mesenteric plexus) — referred pain to periumbilical region
- Parasympathetic: vagus nerve

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Appendicitis
- Most common surgical emergency of the abdomen
- **Referred pain** initially periumbilical (T10 dermatome) → localizes to RIF (somatic)
- McBurney's sign, Rovsing's sign, psoas sign (retrocaecal), obturator sign (pelvic)
- Appendicular artery is an end artery → gangrene and perforation risk

### Appendicular Abscess
- Retrocaecal appendix may lead to retroperitoneal abscess
- Pelvic appendix may irritate bladder or rectum

### Appendicectomy
- McBurney's (gridiron) incision or Lanz incision (cosmetically better)
- Taeniae coli converge to identify the base of the appendix
- Mesoappendix ligated to control appendicular artery

### Mucocele of Appendix
- Accumulation of mucus due to obstruction → if ruptures, pseudomyxoma peritonei

### Carcinoid Tumour
- Most common site of carcinoid in GIT is the appendix

## Development
- Develops as an outgrowth from the caecal bud of the midgut loop
- Differential growth of caecum pushes appendix posteromedially
- Lymphoid tissue develops after birth, peaks at puberty
