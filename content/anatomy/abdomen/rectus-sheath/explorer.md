# Rectus Sheath

## Overview
The rectus sheath is a strong, incomplete fibrous compartment enclosing the rectus abdominis and pyramidalis muscles. It is formed by the aponeuroses of the three flat muscles of the anterior abdominal wall (external oblique, internal oblique, and transversus abdominis). Its composition changes above and below the arcuate line (of Douglas).

## Key Points
- Encloses rectus abdominis and pyramidalis
- Formed by aponeuroses of EO, IO, and TA
- Composition differs above and below the arcuate line
- Arcuate line: midway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis
- Contains superior and inferior epigastric vessels, lower intercostal nerves

## Gross Anatomy

### Formation Above the Arcuate Line (Upper 3/4)
| Layer | Composition |
|-------|------------|
| **Anterior wall** | EO aponeurosis + anterior lamina of IO aponeurosis |
| **Posterior wall** | Posterior lamina of IO aponeurosis + TA aponeurosis |

### Formation Below the Arcuate Line (Lower 1/4)
| Layer | Composition |
|-------|------------|
| **Anterior wall** | ALL three aponeuroses (EO + IO + TA) pass anterior to rectus |
| **Posterior wall** | Only transversalis fascia (no aponeurotic posterior wall) |

### Arcuate Line (of Douglas)
- Located midway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis (~one-third of the way from umbilicus to pubis)
- Crescentic lower free edge of the posterior wall
- Below this, all aponeuroses shift anteriorly
- Inferior epigastric artery enters the sheath at this level

### Contents of Rectus Sheath
- Rectus abdominis muscle
- Pyramidalis muscle (in lower part)
- Superior epigastric artery (from internal thoracic)
- Inferior epigastric artery (from external iliac)
- Terminal parts of lower 6 intercostal nerves (T7–T12)
- Lymph vessels

### Linea Alba
- Midline raphe between the two rectus sheaths
- Formed by interlacing of aponeuroses from both sides
- Extends from xiphoid to pubic symphysis
- Wider above umbilicus; practically a line below umbilicus

### Linea Semilunaris
- Lateral curved line where aponeuroses of flat muscles fuse with the sheath
- Extends from tip of 9th costal cartilage to pubic tubercle

### Tendinous Intersections
- Three (sometimes four) transverse fibrous bands in rectus abdominis
- At xiphoid, umbilicus, and midway between them
- Firmly attached to anterior wall of sheath (not posterior)
- Give the "six-pack" appearance

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Linea alba: midline
- Linea semilunaris: curved lateral border of rectus
- Arcuate line: approximately midway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis
- Tendinous intersections: at xiphoid, umbilicus, midpoint

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial
- **Superior epigastric artery** (continuation of internal thoracic)
- **Inferior epigastric artery** (from external iliac, enters sheath at arcuate line)
- These anastomose within the sheath — important collateral in aortic coarctation

### Nerve Supply
- Lower 6 intercostal nerves (T7–T12) enter rectus sheath laterally
- Pierce posterior wall of sheath, pass between rectus and posterior wall
- Supply rectus abdominis and end as anterior cutaneous branches

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Rectus Sheath Haematoma
- Bleeding from superior or inferior epigastric vessels
- Fothergill's sign: swelling does not cross midline, becomes more prominent on contracting rectus
- Carnett's sign: tenderness increases on tensing abdominal muscles (vs decreases in visceral pathology)

### Surgical Incisions
- **Midline incision:** Through linea alba — avascular, no muscle/nerve damage
- **Paramedian incision:** Through anterior rectus sheath, rectus retracted laterally, posterior sheath opened
- Nerves enter from lateral side — paramedian incision may damage them

### Arcuate Line Significance
- Below it, all aponeuroses anterior → posterior wall is weak (only transversalis fascia)
- Inferior epigastric vessels enter here — vulnerable to injury
- Spigelian hernia occurs along linea semilunaris, typically near arcuate line

### Tendinous Intersections
- Adhere to anterior wall only → haematoma tracks posteriorly but not across intersections anteriorly
- Important in TRAM flap (transverse rectus abdominis myocutaneous flap)

## Development
- Aponeuroses develop from myotomes of thoracic and lumbar somites
- Rectus abdominis from thoracic myotomes
