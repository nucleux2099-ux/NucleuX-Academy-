# Greater Omentum

## Overview
The greater omentum is the largest peritoneal fold in the abdomen. It hangs like an apron from the greater curvature of the stomach and the first part of the duodenum, drapes over the transverse colon and coils of small intestine, then folds back on itself to attach to the transverse colon. It is often called the "policeman of the abdomen" due to its ability to migrate to sites of infection and inflammation.

## Key Points
- Four-layered peritoneal fold (two layers descend, fold back as two layers)
- Hangs from greater curvature of stomach and first part of duodenum
- Attached to transverse colon
- Contains fat, blood vessels, lymphatics, and macrophages
- Acts as immune barrier — walls off infections

## Gross Anatomy

### Attachments
- **Superior:** Greater curvature of stomach and first part of duodenum
- **Posterior:** Anterior surface of transverse colon and transverse mesocolon
- **Inferior:** Free, hanging edge (variable length)

### Structure
- Four peritoneal layers that are fused in adults
- Contains the right and left gastroepiploic vessels in its upper part
- Abundant adipose tissue — can be massive in obese individuals
- Contains macrophages and lymphoid aggregates ("milky spots")

### Parts
1. **Gastrocolic ligament:** Between greater curvature and transverse colon — contains gastroepiploic vessels
2. **Gastrosplenic ligament:** Between stomach and spleen — contains short gastric and left gastroepiploic vessels
3. **Gastrophrenic ligament:** Between stomach and diaphragm

### Relations
- **Anterior:** Anterior abdominal wall
- **Posterior:** Transverse colon, coils of small intestine
- **Superior:** Greater curvature of stomach

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Hangs from the greater curvature, draping over the intestines
- Lies between the anterior abdominal wall and intestines
- In children, it is thin and lace-like; in adults, it accumulates fat

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial Supply
- **Right gastroepiploic artery** (from gastroduodenal artery)
- **Left gastroepiploic artery** (from splenic artery)
- These anastomose along the greater curvature within the omentum
- **Omental branches** from both gastroepiploic arteries

### Venous Drainage
- Right and left gastroepiploic veins → portal venous system

### Lymphatic Drainage
- Gastroepiploic lymph nodes → coeliac nodes

### Nerve Supply
- Sympathetic and parasympathetic from the coeliac plexus

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### "Policeman of the Abdomen"
- Migrates to areas of infection/perforation and walls them off
- In appendicitis, the omentum wraps around the inflamed appendix → omental cake/appendicular mass
- Limits peritonitis by localizing infection

### Omental Patch Repair (Graham Patch)
- Used to seal perforated duodenal ulcers
- A pedicle of omentum is sutured over the perforation

### Omental Infarction
- Rare cause of acute abdomen
- Torsion of omental pedicle → ischaemia

### Omentectomy
- Performed in ovarian cancer surgery (omentum is common site of metastatic deposits)
- Also in gastric cancer (lymph node clearance)

### Omental Transplant
- Used as a vascularized flap in reconstructive surgery
- Can be used to wrap around vascular anastomoses

### Peritoneal Dialysis
- Greater omentum can obstruct peritoneal dialysis catheter → may require omentectomy

## Development
- Develops from the dorsal mesogastrium
- As stomach rotates, the dorsal mesogastrium elongates and hangs down
- Originally contains the lesser sac between its layers (obliterated in adults)
