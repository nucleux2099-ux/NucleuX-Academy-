# Inguinal Region

## Overview
The inguinal region is the area of the anterior abdominal wall between the ASIS and the pubic tubercle, centered on the inguinal canal. It is the most common site of abdominal hernias. The inguinal canal transmits the spermatic cord in males and the round ligament of the uterus in females.

## Key Points
- Inguinal canal: 4 cm long, oblique passage through anterior abdominal wall
- Deep ring: transversalis fascia; Superficial ring: external oblique aponeurosis
- Hesselbach's triangle defines direct inguinal hernia site
- Midpoint of inguinal ligament → femoral artery; Mid-inguinal point → deep ring

## Gross Anatomy

### Inguinal Ligament
- Lower rolled edge of external oblique aponeurosis
- Extends from ASIS to pubic tubercle
- Forms the floor of the inguinal canal

### Inguinal Canal

| Wall | Structure |
|------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | External oblique aponeurosis (entire length) + internal oblique (lateral 1/3) |
| **Posterior** | Transversalis fascia (entire length) + conjoint tendon (medial 1/3) |
| **Roof** | Arching fibres of internal oblique and transversus abdominis |
| **Floor** | Inguinal ligament + lacunar ligament (medially) |

### Deep (Internal) Inguinal Ring
- Opening in transversalis fascia
- Located at midpoint of inguinal ligament (1.25 cm above)
- Lateral to inferior epigastric artery

### Superficial (External) Inguinal Ring
- Triangular opening in external oblique aponeurosis
- Located above and medial to pubic tubercle

### Contents of Inguinal Canal
**Males:** Spermatic cord containing:
- 3 coverings: external spermatic fascia, cremasteric fascia, internal spermatic fascia
- 3 arteries: testicular, cremasteric, artery to vas
- 3 nerves: ilioinguinal (not in cord, on it), genital branch of genitofemoral, sympathetic fibres
- 3 others: vas deferens, pampiniform plexus, lymphatics

**Females:** Round ligament of uterus, ilioinguinal nerve

### Hesselbach's Triangle (Inguinal Triangle)
| Border | Structure |
|--------|-----------|
| Lateral | Inferior epigastric artery |
| Medial | Lateral border of rectus abdominis |
| Inferior | Inguinal ligament |

## Relations & Surface Markings
- Inguinal ligament: ASIS to pubic tubercle
- Deep ring: midpoint of inguinal ligament
- Superficial ring: above and medial to pubic tubercle
- Femoral artery: mid-inguinal point (midpoint between ASIS and pubic symphysis)

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply
- **Inferior epigastric artery** — crucial landmark (deep ring lateral to it)
- **Cremasteric artery** — from inferior epigastric
- **Ilioinguinal nerve (L1)** — sensory to inguinal region and scrotum
- **Genitofemoral nerve (L1, L2):** genital branch enters canal through deep ring

### Cremasteric Reflex
- Afferent: femoral branch of genitofemoral (L1, L2)
- Efferent: genital branch of genitofemoral (L1, L2)
- Stroking medial thigh → cremasteric contraction → testicular elevation

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Indirect Inguinal Hernia
- Through deep ring → along inguinal canal → may exit superficial ring → may descend into scrotum
- Lateral to inferior epigastric artery
- More common; congenital (patent processus vaginalis) or acquired
- Covered by all three spermatic cord coverings

### Direct Inguinal Hernia
- Through Hesselbach's triangle
- Medial to inferior epigastric artery
- Acquired only; weakness of posterior wall (transversalis fascia)
- Covered only by external spermatic fascia
- Rarely descends into scrotum

### Distinguishing Indirect from Direct
- Relationship to inferior epigastric artery (lateral = indirect; medial = direct)
- Deep ring occlusion test: control indirect hernia by pressing over deep ring

### Strangulation
- Indirect hernia more prone to strangulation (narrow deep ring)
- Constriction at deep ring, superficial ring, or neck of hernial sac

### Femoral vs Inguinal Hernia
- Femoral hernia: below and lateral to pubic tubercle (through femoral canal)
- Inguinal hernia: above and medial to pubic tubercle

## Development
- Processus vaginalis: peritoneal diverticulum through inguinal canal during testicular descent
- Normally obliterated after birth
- Patent processus vaginalis → indirect inguinal hernia or hydrocele
