# Hypoglossal Nerve (CN XII)

## Overview

The hypoglossal nerve is the motor nerve of the tongue. It is a purely somatic motor nerve (GSE) that supplies all intrinsic and most extrinsic muscles of the tongue (except palatoglossus, which is supplied by the vagus/pharyngeal plexus). It emerges from the anterolateral sulcus of the medulla (between the pyramid and olive), exits the skull through the hypoglossal canal, and descends in the neck to reach the tongue.

## Key Points

- **Type:** Pure motor (GSE)
- **Nucleus:** Hypoglossal nucleus in the medulla, beneath the hypoglossal trigone in the floor of the 4th ventricle
- **Exit from skull:** Hypoglossal canal (in the occipital bone)
- **Supplies:** All intrinsic muscles + all extrinsic muscles of tongue EXCEPT palatoglossus
- **Extrinsic muscles:** Genioglossus (protrudes tongue), hyoglossus, styloglossus, (not palatoglossus)
- **C1 fibres travel with it** (to thyrohyoid and geniohyoid) but are NOT part of CN XII

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin
- Hypoglossal nucleus in the medulla oblongata
- Multiple rootlets emerge from the anterolateral sulcus (between pyramid and olive)

### Course
1. Rootlets converge and exit through the **hypoglossal canal**
2. Descends between the internal carotid artery and internal jugular vein
3. Loops around the occipital artery and the origin of external carotid artery
4. Crosses the internal and external carotid arteries, the loop of lingual artery
5. Passes superficial to hyoglossus muscle (lateral to it)
6. Passes deep to mylohyoid to reach the tongue
7. Lies on hyoglossus, then on genioglossus, distributing branches to tongue muscles

### Relations
- In the neck: between ICA and IJV; deep to posterior belly of digastric
- Hooks around the occipital artery near the angle of mandible
- Crosses superficial to hyoglossus (key relation — structures deep to hyoglossus include lingual artery and glossopharyngeal nerve)
- C1 fibres join it temporarily → give off superior root of ansa cervicalis, nerve to thyrohyoid, nerve to geniohyoid

### Muscles Supplied
**Intrinsic muscles (4):**
- Superior longitudinal, inferior longitudinal, transverse, vertical

**Extrinsic muscles (3 of 4):**
- Genioglossus (most important — protrudes tongue to opposite side)
- Hyoglossus (depresses and retracts tongue)
- Styloglossus (retracts and elevates tongue)
- Palatoglossus — supplied by vagus (pharyngeal plexus), NOT hypoglossal

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Hypoglossal canal: above the anterolateral margin of foramen magnum
- In the neck: crosses the carotid triangle superficially
- On hyoglossus: between the lingual nerve (above) and the lingual artery (below and deep)
- Accompanied by the vena comitans of the hypoglossal nerve (connects lingual vein to facial vein)

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

- Meningeal branches of ascending pharyngeal artery supply the nerve intracranially
- Vasa nervorum from the lingual artery in the tongue
- The nerve itself carries C1 fibres for a short distance (hitchhiker fibres)

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Hypoglossal Nerve Palsy
- **Unilateral LMN lesion:** Tongue deviates TO the side of lesion on protrusion (due to unopposed genioglossus on the normal side pushing tongue to paralysed side)
- **Ipsilateral atrophy and fasciculations** of the tongue
- Causes: tumour at skull base, surgical injury (carotid endarterectomy, neck dissection), trauma

### Bilateral LMN Lesion
- Tongue cannot be protruded
- Severe dysarthria and dysphagia
- Causes: motor neuron disease, bilateral hypoglossal canal fractures

### UMN Lesion
- Tongue deviates to OPPOSITE side of cortical lesion (contralateral to the lesion)
- No atrophy or fasciculations
- Usually part of a hemispheric stroke

### Surgical Importance
- At risk during carotid endarterectomy (runs near carotid bifurcation)
- During submandibular gland excision (runs deep to posterior belly of digastric)
- During neck dissection

### Tongue Deviation Rule
- "The tongue licks the wound" — deviates toward the side of LMN lesion
