# Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)

## Overview

The oculomotor nerve is the chief motor nerve of the eye. It supplies most of the extraocular muscles (superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique), the levator palpebrae superioris, and carries parasympathetic fibres to the sphincter pupillae and ciliary muscle. It arises from the midbrain and traverses the cavernous sinus before entering the orbit through the superior orbital fissure.

## Key Points

- **Type:** Motor (GSE) + Parasympathetic (GVE)
- **Nuclei:** Oculomotor nucleus (midbrain, at level of superior colliculus) + Edinger-Westphal nucleus (parasympathetic)
- **Emerges:** Interpeduncular fossa (between cerebral peduncles) of midbrain
- **Course:** Between posterior cerebral and superior cerebellar arteries → lateral wall of cavernous sinus → superior orbital fissure → orbit
- **Supplies:** SR, IR, MR, IO, LPS (all except LR and SO)
- **Parasympathetic:** Edinger-Westphal → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae (miosis) + ciliary muscle (accommodation)

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin
- Oculomotor nucleus complex: group of subnuclei in midbrain at superior colliculus level
  - Each subnucleus supplies a specific muscle
  - Superior rectus subnucleus supplies contralateral SR (fibres decussate)
  - Levator palpebrae superioris: single midline subnucleus (central caudal nucleus) — supplies both sides
- Edinger-Westphal nucleus: dorsal to main nucleus; parasympathetic

### Course
1. Fibres pass through red nucleus and exit at interpeduncular fossa
2. Passes between posterior cerebral artery (above) and superior cerebellar artery (below) — aneurysm compression site
3. Runs in lateral wall of cavernous sinus (most superior nerve in the wall)
4. Divides into superior and inferior divisions before entering orbit

### Divisions
- **Superior division:** Supplies superior rectus and levator palpebrae superioris
- **Inferior division:** Supplies medial rectus, inferior rectus, inferior oblique
  - Branch to inferior oblique carries parasympathetic fibres → ciliary ganglion

### Parasympathetic Pathway
Edinger-Westphal nucleus → preganglionic fibres in CN III (inferior division) → ciliary ganglion (synapse) → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae + ciliary muscle

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Passes between PCA and SCA — vulnerable to aneurysm at this site
- In cavernous sinus: most superior in lateral wall (above CN IV)
- Order in cavernous sinus wall (superior to inferior): III, IV, V₁, V₂ (VI is within the sinus, lateral to ICA)
- Enters orbit through superior orbital fissure (within tendinous ring of Zinn)
- Parasympathetic fibres are superficial (peripheral) in the nerve trunk — compressed first by external pressure

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

- Branches from posterior cerebral artery and superior cerebellar artery
- Inferolateral trunk from intracavernous ICA

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Complete CN III Palsy
- **Ptosis** (LPS paralysis — eye closed)
- **Eye "down and out"** (unopposed LR and SO)
- **Fixed dilated pupil** (parasympathetic loss → mydriasis, no light reflex)
- **Loss of accommodation** (ciliary muscle paralysis)

### Surgical vs Medical CN III Palsy
- **Surgical (compressive):** Pupil involved EARLY (parasympathetic fibres are peripheral → compressed first)
  - Causes: PCA/PComm aneurysm, uncal herniation
- **Medical (ischaemic):** Pupil SPARED (ischaemia affects core motor fibres; parasympathetic on surface receive blood from vasa nervorum)
  - Causes: diabetes, hypertension

### Uncal Herniation
- Temporal lobe uncus herniates through tentorial notch
- Compresses ipsilateral CN III → dilated pupil (first sign) → complete CN III palsy → coma

### Weber's Syndrome
- Midbrain lesion (usually vascular) involving CN III nucleus/fibres + cerebral peduncle
- Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia

### Pupillary Light Reflex
- Afferent: CN II → pretectal nucleus → bilateral Edinger-Westphal nuclei
- Efferent: CN III → ciliary ganglion → sphincter pupillae
- Tests both direct and consensual responses
