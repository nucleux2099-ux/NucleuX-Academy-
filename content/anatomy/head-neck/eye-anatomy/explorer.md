# Eye Anatomy

## Overview

The eye (eyeball/globe) is the organ of vision, approximately 2.5 cm in diameter, situated in the orbit. It consists of three coats: outer fibrous (sclera and cornea), middle vascular (uveal tract — choroid, ciliary body, iris), and inner nervous (retina). The eyeball contains three chambers: anterior chamber (between cornea and iris), posterior chamber (between iris and lens), and vitreous chamber (behind lens). The eye functions as a camera — cornea and lens refract light onto the retina.

## Key Points

- **Outer coat:** Sclera (posterior 5/6, opaque) + cornea (anterior 1/6, transparent, avascular)
- **Middle coat (uvea):** Choroid, ciliary body, iris
- **Inner coat:** Retina — contains photoreceptors (rods and cones)
- **Chambers:** Anterior and posterior (contain aqueous humour), vitreous (vitreous humour)
- **Lens:** Biconvex, transparent, avascular; held by suspensory ligament (zonule of Zinn) from ciliary body
- **Optic disc:** Blind spot; exit point of optic nerve; no photoreceptors
- **Macula lutea:** Central area for acute vision; fovea centralis at its centre (cones only)

## Gross Anatomy

### Fibrous Coat
- **Sclera:** Dense white connective tissue; maintains shape; attachment for extraocular muscles
  - Lamina cribrosa: sieve-like area where optic nerve fibres exit
- **Cornea:** Transparent, avascular; 5 layers (epithelium, Bowman's, stroma, Descemet's, endothelium)
  - Most powerful refracting medium of the eye (~43 dioptres)
  - Nourished by aqueous humour, tears, and limbal vessels
  - Nerve supply: nasociliary nerve (V₁) → ophthalmic division → long ciliary nerves

### Vascular Coat (Uvea)
- **Choroid:** Vascular layer between sclera and retina; nourishes outer retinal layers
- **Ciliary body:** Produces aqueous humour; contains ciliary muscle (accommodation)
  - Parasympathetic supply (CN III → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves) contracts ciliary muscle → lens becomes more convex
- **Iris:** Coloured diaphragm with central pupil
  - Sphincter pupillae: parasympathetic (III) → constricts pupil (miosis)
  - Dilator pupillae: sympathetic → dilates pupil (mydriasis)

### Retina (Neural Coat)
- **10 layers** including pigment epithelium, photoreceptor layer, ganglion cell layer, nerve fibre layer
- **Rods:** ~120 million; peripheral retina; scotopic (dim light) vision; rhodopsin
- **Cones:** ~6 million; concentrated at macula/fovea; photopic (bright light/colour) vision
- **Fovea centralis:** Thinnest part of retina; only cones; area of maximum visual acuity
- **Optic disc:** Nasal to macula; no photoreceptors → physiological blind spot
  - Site where central retinal artery enters and central retinal vein exits

### Aqueous Humour
- Produced by ciliary epithelium → posterior chamber → through pupil → anterior chamber
- Drained through trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm → aqueous veins → episcleral veins
- Maintains intraocular pressure (IOP): normal 10–21 mmHg

### Vitreous Humour
- Transparent gel filling vitreous chamber (80% of eye volume)
- Hyaloid canal: vestige of hyaloid artery (embryological)

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Eyeball occupies anterior 1/3 of the orbit
- Optic nerve exits posteriorly through optic foramen
- Extraocular muscles: 4 recti (superior, inferior, medial, lateral) + 2 obliques (superior, inferior)
- Tenon's capsule (fascia bulbi) envelops the eyeball posterior to the limbus

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial Supply
- **Ophthalmic artery** (branch of internal carotid) →
  - **Central retinal artery:** Enters optic nerve, supplies inner retinal layers — END ARTERY
  - **Short posterior ciliary arteries:** Supply choroid
  - **Long posterior ciliary arteries:** Supply ciliary body and iris
  - **Anterior ciliary arteries:** From muscular branches

### Venous Drainage
- Central retinal vein → cavernous sinus
- Vortex veins (4–5) drain choroid → superior and inferior ophthalmic veins → cavernous sinus

### Nerve Supply
- **Sensory:** Optic nerve (CN II) — retina; nasociliary (V₁) — cornea
- **Motor:** CN III (SR, IR, MR, IO, levator), CN IV (SO), CN VI (LR)
- **Parasympathetic:** CN III → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae + ciliary muscle
- **Sympathetic:** Superior cervical ganglion → long ciliary nerves → dilator pupillae

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Glaucoma
- Raised IOP due to impaired aqueous drainage → optic nerve damage → visual field loss
- Open-angle (chronic) vs closed-angle (acute — emergency)

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
- End artery → sudden painless loss of vision
- Fundus: pale retina with cherry-red spot at macula (choroidal blood supply visible)

### Papilloedema
- Optic disc swelling due to raised intracranial pressure
- Central retinal vein compressed within the optic nerve sheath

### Retinal Detachment
- Separation of neurosensory retina from pigment epithelium
- Presents with flashes, floaters, curtain-like visual field loss

### Pupillary Light Reflex
- Afferent: CN II; Efferent: CN III (parasympathetic)
- Direct and consensual responses
