# Olfactory Nerve (CN I)

## Overview

The olfactory nerve (CN I) is the nerve of smell. It is a purely sensory nerve and is unique among cranial nerves — it is not a single nerve trunk but a collection of ~20 fine olfactory fila (nerve bundles) that pass through the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone. CN I is actually a tract of the brain (telencephalon) rather than a true peripheral nerve. The olfactory neurons are bipolar and are the only neurons in the body that are replaced throughout life (neuronal regeneration).

## Key Points

- **Type:** Pure sensory (SVA — special visceral afferent for smell)
- **Not a true peripheral nerve:** Extension of the brain (telencephalon)
- **Olfactory neurons:** Bipolar; in olfactory mucosa of nasal cavity roof
- **Olfactory area:** Upper part of nasal cavity — roof, superior concha, upper nasal septum (~2.5 cm²)
- **Pathway:** Olfactory mucosa → olfactory fila → cribriform plate → olfactory bulb → olfactory tract → primary olfactory cortex
- **Unique:** Only cranial nerve that does NOT relay through thalamus; only neurons that regenerate

## Gross Anatomy

### Olfactory Mucosa
- Located in the roof of the nasal cavity, upper part of nasal septum, and superior concha
- Contains bipolar olfactory receptor neurons (first-order neurons)
- Dendrites project into the mucus layer as olfactory cilia
- Axons form ~20 olfactory fila (unmyelinated) — collectively called CN I

### Olfactory Pathway
1. **Olfactory fila:** Pass through cribriform plate of ethmoid
2. **Olfactory bulb:** Lies on the cribriform plate in the anterior cranial fossa; contains mitral cells (second-order neurons); synapse in glomeruli
3. **Olfactory tract:** Runs on the inferior surface of the frontal lobe in the olfactory sulcus
4. **Olfactory trigone:** Tract divides into lateral and medial olfactory striae
5. **Lateral olfactory stria → primary olfactory cortex** (uncus, piriform cortex, entorhinal area of temporal lobe)
6. **Medial olfactory stria → septal area and contralateral olfactory bulb** (via anterior commissure)

### Key Features
- No thalamic relay (unique among sensory systems) — goes directly to cortex
- Primary olfactory cortex: in the temporal lobe (uncus, piriform area, entorhinal cortex)
- Close connections to limbic system → smell evokes strong memories and emotions

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Olfactory bulb: lies on the cribriform plate, inferior to the frontal lobe
- Olfactory tract: in the olfactory sulcus on the inferior surface of the frontal lobe, medial to the orbital gyri
- Gyrus rectus: medial to the olfactory sulcus
- Cribriform plate: between the anterior cranial fossa and the nasal cavity roof

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Blood Supply
- Olfactory mucosa: anterior and posterior ethmoidal arteries (from ophthalmic artery), sphenopalatine artery
- Olfactory bulb and tract: anterior cerebral artery branches

### Note
- The olfactory nerve itself IS the sensory supply for smell — no separate nerve supply

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Anosmia (Loss of Smell)
- **Causes:**
  - Fracture of cribriform plate (anterior cranial fossa fracture) — tears olfactory fila
  - Meningioma of olfactory groove — compresses olfactory bulb/tract
  - Upper respiratory infections (viral)
  - Nasal polyps (obstruction)
  - Ageing (gradual decline)
  - COVID-19 (olfactory epithelium damage)

### Foster Kennedy Syndrome
- Olfactory groove meningioma →
  - Ipsilateral anosmia + optic atrophy (direct compression)
  - Contralateral papilloedema (raised ICP)

### CSF Rhinorrhoea
- Fracture of cribriform plate → CSF leaks into nasal cavity
- Risk of ascending meningitis
- Associated with anosmia and raccoon eyes

### Olfactory Neuroblastoma (Esthesioneuroblastoma)
- Rare malignant tumour arising from olfactory epithelium
- Presents with nasal obstruction, epistaxis, anosmia

### Uncinate Fits
- Temporal lobe epilepsy involving the uncus → olfactory hallucinations (often unpleasant smells)
- May be associated with dreamy states, déjà vu
