# Meninges

## Overview

The meninges are three protective membranes that surround the brain and spinal cord: dura mater (outermost, tough), arachnoid mater (middle, web-like), and pia mater (innermost, delicate, adherent to brain surface). They provide mechanical protection, contain CSF in the subarachnoid space, and house the dural venous sinuses. The spaces between the meninges are of immense clinical significance in intracranial haemorrhage.

## Key Points

- **Three layers:** Dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater
- **Cranial dura:** Two layers — periosteal (outer, adherent to skull) and meningeal (inner); separate to form dural venous sinuses
- **Dural folds:** Falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli, falx cerebelli, diaphragma sellae
- **Spaces:** Epidural (potential, between skull and dura), subdural (potential, between dura and arachnoid), subarachnoid (real, contains CSF)
- **CSF:** Produced by choroid plexus; flows in subarachnoid space; absorbed by arachnoid granulations into superior sagittal sinus

## Gross Anatomy

### Dura Mater (Cranial)
- **Periosteal layer:** Acts as periosteum of inner skull surface; does not continue through foramen magnum
- **Meningeal layer:** Continues as spinal dura; forms dural folds and venous sinuses

#### Dural Folds
1. **Falx cerebri:** Sickle-shaped; lies in the longitudinal fissure between cerebral hemispheres
   - Attachment: crista galli (anteriorly) → internal occipital protuberance (posteriorly)
   - Contains: superior sagittal sinus (upper border), inferior sagittal sinus (lower free border)
2. **Tentorium cerebelli:** Tent-like roof over posterior cranial fossa; separates cerebrum from cerebellum
   - Attached border: along transverse sinuses, petrous temporal ridge
   - Free border: tentorial notch (incisura) — midbrain passes through it
   - Contains: transverse sinus (attached border), superior petrosal sinus (petrous attachment), straight sinus (junction with falx)
3. **Falx cerebelli:** Small, below tentorium, between cerebellar hemispheres; contains occipital sinus
4. **Diaphragma sellae:** Covers pituitary fossa; pierced by pituitary stalk

### Arachnoid Mater
- Thin, transparent membrane; separated from dura by potential subdural space
- Does NOT dip into sulci (unlike pia)
- **Arachnoid granulations (Pacchionian bodies):** Project into superior sagittal sinus; absorb CSF into venous blood
- **Subarachnoid cisterns:** Enlargements where arachnoid bridges over sulci/fossae
  - Cisterna magna (cerebellomedullary), pontine cistern, interpeduncular cistern, cistern of lateral fossa (Sylvian)

### Pia Mater
- Thin, highly vascular; closely adherent to brain surface, dipping into every sulcus
- Contains the superficial cerebral blood vessels
- Forms tela choroidea (part of the roof of 3rd and 4th ventricles)
- Forms choroid plexus (with ependyma)

### Meningeal Spaces
- **Epidural (extradural) space:** Potential space between skull and periosteal dura; site of epidural haematoma (middle meningeal artery rupture)
- **Subdural space:** Potential space between dura and arachnoid; site of subdural haematoma (bridging veins rupture)
- **Subarachnoid space:** Real space between arachnoid and pia; contains CSF, cerebral arteries, cranial nerves

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Superior sagittal sinus: runs along midline in upper border of falx cerebri from crista galli to internal occipital protuberance
- Tentorium cerebelli: attached along the transverse sinus groove
- Tentorial notch: CN III runs along its free edge → compressed in uncal herniation
- Middle meningeal artery enters skull via foramen spinosum; runs in epidural space at pterion

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial Supply to Meninges
- **Anterior cranial fossa:** Anterior meningeal artery (from anterior ethmoidal)
- **Middle cranial fossa:** Middle meningeal artery (from maxillary artery) — most important
- **Posterior cranial fossa:** Posterior meningeal artery (from vertebral artery)

### Nerve Supply (Dura is pain-sensitive)
- **Supratentorial dura:** Trigeminal nerve (V₁, V₂, V₃ branches)
- **Infratentorial dura:** C1, C2, C3 (upper cervical nerves), vagus (X)
- Headache in meningitis = from dural irritation

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Epidural (Extradural) Haematoma
- Rupture of **middle meningeal artery** (at pterion)
- Biconvex (lens-shaped) haematoma on CT
- "Lucid interval" followed by rapid deterioration → surgical emergency (craniotomy)

### Subdural Haematoma
- Rupture of **bridging veins** (between cerebral cortex and dural sinuses)
- Crescent-shaped haematoma on CT; crosses suture lines
- Acute (trauma) or chronic (elderly, brain atrophy → veins stretched)

### Subarachnoid Haemorrhage
- Rupture of **berry aneurysm** (circle of Willis, commonly anterior communicating artery)
- "Thunderclap headache"; blood in CSF; xanthochromia on LP

### Meningitis
- Infection of pia-arachnoid → pus in subarachnoid space
- Headache, neck stiffness, Kernig's sign, Brudzinski's sign

### Tentorial Herniation (Uncal Herniation)
- Raised ICP → temporal uncus herniates through tentorial notch
- Compresses CN III → ipsilateral dilated pupil → coma → death if untreated
