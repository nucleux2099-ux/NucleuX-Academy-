# Facial Nerve (CN VII)

## Overview

The facial nerve (CN VII) is the nerve of the second pharyngeal arch. It is a mixed cranial nerve containing motor fibres to the muscles of facial expression and other second-arch derivatives, parasympathetic secretomotor fibres to glands, taste fibres from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, and a small somatic sensory component. Its complex course through the petrous temporal bone makes it vulnerable to injury from middle ear pathology and surgery.

## Key Points

- **Type:** Mixed — motor, sensory (taste), parasympathetic
- **Pharyngeal arch:** Second (hyoid)
- **Motor supply:** Muscles of facial expression, stapedius, stylohyoid, posterior belly of digastric
- **Taste:** Anterior 2/3 of tongue (chorda tympani)
- **Parasympathetic:** Submandibular, sublingual glands (via chorda tympani → submandibular ganglion); lacrimal, nasal, palatal glands (via greater petrosal nerve → pterygopalatine ganglion)
- **Exit foramen:** Stylomastoid foramen

## Gross Anatomy

### Two Roots
1. **Motor root:** Larger; from facial motor nucleus in pons
2. **Nervus intermedius:** Smaller; carries taste (SVA) and parasympathetic (GVE) fibres

### Intracranial Course
- Emerges at the cerebellopontine angle (pontomedullary junction)
- Enters internal acoustic meatus with CN VIII and labyrinthine artery
- In the meatus: CN VII lies anterosuperior to CN VIII

### Facial Canal (3 Segments)
1. **Labyrinthine segment:** Above cochlea; ends at geniculate ganglion
   - Greater petrosal nerve given off → joins deep petrosal nerve → nerve of pterygoid canal → pterygopalatine ganglion
2. **Tympanic (horizontal) segment:** Along medial wall of tympanic cavity, above oval window
3. **Mastoid (vertical) segment:** Descends in posterior wall of middle ear
   - Nerve to stapedius
   - Chorda tympani (6 mm above stylomastoid foramen)

### Extracranial Course
- Exits stylomastoid foramen
- Gives off: posterior auricular nerve, nerve to digastric (posterior belly), nerve to stylohyoid
- Enters parotid gland → divides into temporofacial and cervicofacial divisions → 5 terminal branches

### Terminal Branches
- Temporal, Zygomatic, Buccal, Marginal mandibular, Cervical
- Supply all muscles of facial expression + platysma

### Associated Parasympathetic Ganglia
1. **Pterygopalatine ganglion:** Relay for greater petrosal nerve fibres → lacrimal gland, nasal and palatal glands
2. **Submandibular ganglion:** Relay for chorda tympani fibres → submandibular and sublingual salivary glands

## Relations & Surface Markings

- **In IAM:** Anterosuperior quadrant (Bill's bar landmark for surgery)
- **Tympanic segment:** Above oval window; dehiscent in 50% (no bony covering) — risk in middle ear surgery
- **Mastoid segment:** Between middle ear and mastoid process
- **In parotid:** Divides the gland into superficial and deep lobes
- **Marginal mandibular branch:** Crosses below mandible superficial to the facial artery

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

- **Labyrinthine artery** (AICA/basilar): supplies nerve in IAM
- **Petrosal branch of middle meningeal artery:** supplies geniculate ganglion region
- **Stylomastoid branch of posterior auricular artery:** supplies mastoid segment and extracranial part

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Bell's Palsy
- Most common cause of unilateral LMN facial palsy
- Idiopathic (possibly viral — HSV reactivation)
- Entire ipsilateral face paralysed; eye cannot close; drooping of mouth angle
- Taste loss anterior 2/3 tongue; hyperacusis (stapedius paralysis)
- Usually recovers spontaneously (85%)

### Ramsay Hunt Syndrome
- Herpes zoster of geniculate ganglion
- Facial palsy + vesicles in ear (external ear, concha) + severe ear pain
- May have sensorineural deafness

### Middle Ear Surgery Complications
- Facial nerve injury during mastoidectomy or tympanoplasty
- Tympanic segment most vulnerable (often dehiscent)

### Parotid Surgery
- Facial nerve identified using tragal pointer landmark
- Nerve divides gland into superficial and deep lobes
- Superficial parotidectomy preserves the nerve

### Cerebellopontine Angle Tumour
- Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) compresses CN VII and VIII
- Progressive sensorineural hearing loss, facial weakness, loss of corneal reflex
