# Brachial Plexus

## Overview

The brachial plexus is a somatic nerve plexus formed by the ventral rami of C5–T1 spinal nerves, with variable contributions from C4 and T2. It provides the entire motor and most of the sensory innervation to the upper limb. The plexus is organised into roots, trunks, divisions, and cords, and is located in the posterior triangle of the neck and the axilla.

## Key Points

- **Formation:** Ventral rami of C5, C6, C7, C8, T1
- **Parts:** Roots → Trunks → Divisions → Cords → Branches
- **Location:** Posterior triangle (supraclavicular part) and axilla (infraclavicular part)
- **Trunks:** Upper (C5, C6), Middle (C7), Lower (C8, T1)
- **Cords:** Named by their relation to the 2nd part of the axillary artery — Lateral, Posterior, Medial
- **Key Relations:** Between scalenus anterior and scalenus medius; enclosed in the axillary sheath

## Gross Anatomy

### Roots (C5–T1)
- Emerge between scalenus anterior and scalenus medius
- **Dorsal scapular nerve** (C5): to rhomboids and levator scapulae
- **Long thoracic nerve** (C5, C6, C7): to serratus anterior
- **Nerve to subclavius** (C5, C6): to subclavius muscle

### Trunks
- **Upper trunk** (C5+C6): Erb's point — junction of C5 and C6
  - Suprascapular nerve → supraspinatus and infraspinatus
  - Nerve to subclavius
- **Middle trunk** (C7): No branches
- **Lower trunk** (C8+T1): No named branches (but T1 gives grey rami communicantes to stellate ganglion)

### Divisions
- Each trunk divides into anterior and posterior divisions behind the clavicle
- Anterior divisions supply flexor compartments; posterior divisions supply extensor compartments

### Cords
- **Lateral cord** (anterior divisions of upper + middle trunks): lateral to 2nd part of axillary artery
  - Lateral pectoral nerve, musculocutaneous nerve, lateral root of median nerve
- **Posterior cord** (all 3 posterior divisions): posterior to 2nd part of axillary artery
  - Upper subscapular, thoracodorsal, lower subscapular, axillary, radial nerves
- **Medial cord** (anterior division of lower trunk): medial to 2nd part of axillary artery
  - Medial pectoral nerve, medial cutaneous nerve of arm, medial cutaneous nerve of forearm, ulnar nerve, medial root of median nerve

### Terminal Branches
- Musculocutaneous, median, ulnar, radial, axillary nerves

## Relations & Surface Markings

### Surface Marking
- A line from the middle of the posterior border of SCM to the midpoint of the clavicle represents the supraclavicular part
- Infraclavicular part: around the axillary artery in the axilla

### Key Relations
- Roots: between scalenus anterior and medius
- Trunks: in the posterior triangle, above and behind subclavian artery
- Cords: surround the 2nd part of axillary artery in the axilla
- Upper trunk: crossed by the inferior belly of omohyoid and transverse cervical artery

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Blood Supply to the Plexus
- Branches from the subclavian artery (transverse cervical, suprascapular arteries)
- Axillary artery branches in the infraclavicular part
- Vasa nervorum from adjacent arteries

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Erb-Duchenne Palsy (Upper Trunk Injury — C5, C6)
- **Cause:** Forceful widening of angle between neck and shoulder (birth injury, motorcycle accidents)
- **Erb's point:** Union of C5 and C6
- **Position:** Arm hangs by side, medially rotated, forearm pronated — "policeman's tip" / "porter's tip"
- **Muscles paralysed:** Deltoid, biceps, brachialis, brachioradialis, supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor

### Klumpke's Palsy (Lower Trunk Injury — C8, T1)
- **Cause:** Forceful abduction of arm above head (clutching to prevent fall)
- **Effects:** Claw hand (paralysis of intrinsic muscles of hand), loss of sensation in medial forearm and hand
- **May have Horner's syndrome** if T1 root avulsed (loss of sympathetic fibres)

### Winged Scapula
- Long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7) injury → serratus anterior paralysis → medial winging

### Crutch Palsy / Saturday Night Palsy
- Radial nerve injury (posterior cord territory)
