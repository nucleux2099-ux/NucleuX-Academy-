# Facial Nerve (CN VII) — Detailed Anatomy

## Overview

The facial nerve is the nerve of the second pharyngeal arch. It is a mixed nerve with a large motor root (supplying muscles of facial expression, stapedius, stylohyoid, posterior belly of digastric) and a smaller sensory and parasympathetic root called the nervus intermedius. It has the most complex intracranial and intratemporal course of any cranial nerve.

## Key Points

- **Type:** Mixed — motor (SVE), sensory/taste (SVA), parasympathetic (GVE)
- **Motor nucleus:** Facial motor nucleus in pons
- **Parasympathetic:** Superior salivatory nucleus → nervus intermedius
- **Taste:** Anterior 2/3 of tongue via chorda tympani → nervus intermedius → nucleus of tractus solitarius
- **Course:** Intracranial → internal acoustic meatus → facial canal (labyrinthine, tympanic, mastoid segments) → stylomastoid foramen → parotid gland → terminal branches

## Gross Anatomy

### Nuclei
- **Motor nucleus:** Lower pons (SVE) — muscles of facial expression
- **Superior salivatory nucleus:** Pons (GVE) — submandibular, sublingual, lacrimal glands, nasal and palatal glands
- **Nucleus of tractus solitarius:** Medulla (SVA) — taste from anterior 2/3 tongue
- **Spinal nucleus of trigeminal:** (GSA) — small sensory area of ear

### Course

#### 1. Intracranial Part
- Motor root loops around the abducens nucleus (internal genu/facial colliculus) in the floor of 4th ventricle
- Nervus intermedius lies between motor root and CN VIII
- Both roots enter the internal acoustic meatus

#### 2. Intratemporal Part (Facial Canal)
- **Labyrinthine segment:** Shortest, narrowest; above cochlea; ends at geniculate ganglion
  - **Geniculate ganglion:** Contains cell bodies of taste and somatic sensory neurons
  - **Greater petrosal nerve** arises here → pterygopalatine ganglion → lacrimal, nasal, palatal glands
- **Tympanic segment:** Runs along medial wall of middle ear above oval window; horizontal
- **Mastoid segment:** Descends in posterior wall of middle ear
  - **Nerve to stapedius** arises here
  - **Chorda tympani** arises 6mm above stylomastoid foramen; enters middle ear, crosses tympanic membrane (between handle of malleus and long process of incus)

#### 3. Extracranial Part
- Exits at **stylomastoid foramen**
- Gives off: posterior auricular nerve, nerve to posterior belly of digastric, nerve to stylohyoid
- Enters parotid gland — divides into 5 terminal branches within the gland (but does NOT supply the parotid — parotid is supplied by CN IX via otic ganglion)

### Terminal Branches (within parotid)
1. **Temporal:** Frontalis, orbicularis oculi (upper), corrugator supercilii
2. **Zygomatic:** Orbicularis oculi (lower), zygomaticus
3. **Buccal:** Buccinator, orbicularis oris, upper lip muscles
4. **Marginal mandibular:** Lower lip muscles, mentalis
5. **Cervical:** Platysma

### Chorda Tympani
- Carries taste (SVA) from anterior 2/3 of tongue
- Carries parasympathetic (GVE) preganglionic fibres → submandibular ganglion → submandibular and sublingual glands
- Joins the lingual nerve (V₃) in the infratemporal fossa

## Relations & Surface Markings

- Facial canal: within petrous temporal bone
- Geniculate ganglion: at the bend between labyrinthine and tympanic segments
- In parotid: most superficial structure in the gland (but deep to the gland itself)
- Marginal mandibular branch: below the angle of mandible, superficial to facial artery and vein

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

- Labyrinthine artery (from basilar/AICA) supplies the nerve in the internal acoustic meatus
- Stylomastoid artery (from posterior auricular) supplies the nerve in the facial canal
- Superficial temporal and facial arteries in the extracranial course

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Bell's Palsy
- Idiopathic LMN facial palsy; most common cause of facial nerve paralysis
- Entire ipsilateral face affected; inability to close eye, wrinkle forehead, whistle
- Loss of taste anterior 2/3 tongue, hyperacusis (stapedius paralysis)
- Labyrinthine segment most commonly affected (narrowest — ischaemia/oedema)

### Localisation of Lesion
- **Above geniculate ganglion:** Loss of lacrimation + taste + motor + hyperacusis
- **Between geniculate ganglion and nerve to stapedius:** Loss of taste + motor + hyperacusis (lacrimation preserved)
- **Below nerve to stapedius:** Loss of taste + motor (no hyperacusis)
- **Below chorda tympani:** Motor loss only
- **After stylomastoid foramen:** Motor loss only (branches affected depend on level)

### Crocodile Tears (Bogorad's Syndrome)
- Aberrant regeneration after facial nerve injury
- Parasympathetic fibres meant for salivary glands regenerate to lacrimal gland → tearing while eating

### Parotid Surgery
- Facial nerve must be identified and preserved during parotidectomy
- Landmark: nerve found at the pointer of the tragal cartilage, ~1 cm deep and inferior
