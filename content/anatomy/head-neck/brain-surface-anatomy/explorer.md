# Brain — Surface Anatomy

## Overview

The brain is the largest part of the central nervous system, weighing approximately 1400 g in males and 1250 g in females. It is divided into the cerebrum (two cerebral hemispheres), cerebellum, and brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata). The cerebral hemispheres have three surfaces (superolateral, medial, inferior), three poles (frontal, occipital, temporal), and three borders. The surface is thrown into folds (gyri) separated by grooves (sulci), which increase the cortical surface area.

## Key Points

- **Cerebral hemispheres:** Superolateral, medial, and inferior surfaces
- **Poles:** Frontal, occipital, temporal
- **Major sulci:** Central (of Rolando), lateral (of Sylvius), parieto-occipital, calcarine, cingulate
- **Major lobes:** Frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, limbic (+ insula — hidden)
- **Important gyri:** Precentral (motor), postcentral (sensory), superior temporal (auditory)
- **Functional areas:** Primary motor (area 4), primary sensory (areas 3,1,2), visual (area 17), auditory (areas 41,42), Broca's (44,45), Wernicke's (22)

## Gross Anatomy

### Superolateral Surface
- **Central sulcus (Rolando):** Separates frontal from parietal lobe; between precentral and postcentral gyri
- **Lateral sulcus (Sylvius):** Separates temporal lobe below from frontal and parietal above; has anterior, ascending, and posterior rami
- **Frontal lobe:** Precentral gyrus, superior/middle/inferior frontal gyri
- **Parietal lobe:** Postcentral gyrus, superior and inferior parietal lobules (supramarginal and angular gyri)
- **Temporal lobe:** Superior/middle/inferior temporal gyri; transverse temporal gyri of Heschl (primary auditory cortex)
- **Occipital lobe:** Lateral occipital gyri

### Medial Surface
- **Corpus callosum:** Rostrum, genu, body, splenium
- **Cingulate sulcus and gyrus:** Part of limbic lobe
- **Calcarine sulcus:** Contains primary visual cortex (area 17) on its walls
- **Parieto-occipital sulcus:** Boundary between parietal and occipital lobes
- **Paracentral lobule:** Continuation of pre- and postcentral gyri; controls lower limb
- **Precuneus and cuneus:** Medial parietal and occipital regions

### Inferior Surface
- **Frontal lobe:** Orbital gyri, gyrus rectus, olfactory sulcus (with olfactory bulb and tract)
- **Temporal lobe:** Parahippocampal gyrus, uncus, occipitotemporal gyri
- **Uncus:** Hook-shaped; related to tentorial herniation

### Insula (Island of Reil)
- Hidden deep to the lateral sulcus
- Covered by opercula (frontal, parietal, temporal)
- Functions: gustatory cortex, visceral sensation

## Relations & Surface Markings

### Surface Landmarks (on Skull)
- **Central sulcus:** Upper end — 1 cm behind midpoint of nasion-inion line; lower end — at midpoint of zygomatic arch
- **Lateral sulcus:** Pterion to a point 2 cm below parietal eminence (posterior ramus)
- **Superior sagittal sinus:** Along the midline from nasion to inion

### Important Relations
- Frontal lobe: overlies anterior cranial fossa and orbit
- Temporal lobe: lies in middle cranial fossa
- Cerebellum: lies in posterior cranial fossa, under tentorium cerebelli
- Brainstem: passes through tentorial notch

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

### Arterial Supply
- **Anterior cerebral artery:** Medial surface of hemisphere (up to parieto-occipital sulcus)
- **Middle cerebral artery:** Superolateral surface (largest territory)
- **Posterior cerebral artery:** Inferior surface of temporal and occipital lobes, visual cortex
- **Circle of Willis:** Interconnects anterior and posterior circulations

### Venous Drainage
- Superior cerebral veins → superior sagittal sinus
- Superficial middle cerebral vein → cavernous sinus
- Deep cerebral veins (internal cerebral veins + basal veins of Rosenthal) → great cerebral vein of Galen → straight sinus

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Motor and Sensory Homunculus
- Topographic representation on pre- and postcentral gyri
- Lower limb — paracentral lobule (medial surface)
- Upper limb and trunk — lateral surface
- Face — lowest part, near lateral sulcus
- Disproportionate representation: hand and face areas are large

### Broca's Aphasia (Expressive)
- Lesion of Broca's area (areas 44, 45) in the inferior frontal gyrus of dominant hemisphere
- Patient understands but cannot speak fluently

### Wernicke's Aphasia (Receptive)
- Lesion of area 22 in superior temporal gyrus of dominant hemisphere
- Patient speaks fluently but speech is meaningless; cannot comprehend

### Uncal Herniation
- Raised intracranial pressure → temporal uncus herniates through tentorial notch
- Compresses CN III → ipsilateral pupil dilation, contralateral hemiplegia

### Lesions of Paracentral Lobule
- Loss of voluntary control of bladder and lower limb on opposite side
