# Cranial Nerves — Overview

## Overview

There are 12 pairs of cranial nerves (CN I–XII) that arise from the brain and brainstem. They exit the skull through various foramina and supply structures predominantly in the head and neck, with the notable exception of the vagus nerve (CN X) which extends into the thorax and abdomen. Cranial nerves may be sensory, motor, or mixed, and some carry parasympathetic fibres.

## Key Points

- **12 pairs:** Olfactory (I), Optic (II), Oculomotor (III), Trochlear (IV), Trigeminal (V), Abducens (VI), Facial (VII), Vestibulocochlear (VIII), Glossopharyngeal (IX), Vagus (X), Accessory (XI), Hypoglossal (XII)
- **Purely sensory:** I, II, VIII
- **Purely motor:** III, IV, VI, XI, XII
- **Mixed:** V, VII, IX, X
- **Parasympathetic:** III (Edinger-Westphal), VII (superior salivatory), IX (inferior salivatory), X (dorsal motor nucleus)
- **Attached to brainstem:** III–XII; CN I and II are not true nerves (tracts of brain)

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin from Brain/Brainstem
| CN | Name | Attachment |
|----|------|------------|
| I | Olfactory | Olfactory bulb (telencephalon) |
| II | Optic | Optic chiasma (diencephalon) |
| III | Oculomotor | Interpeduncular fossa (midbrain) |
| IV | Trochlear | Posterior surface of midbrain (only CN to emerge dorsally) |
| V | Trigeminal | Lateral surface of pons |
| VI | Abducens | Pontomedullary junction (anterior) |
| VII | Facial | Pontomedullary junction (cerebellopontine angle) |
| VIII | Vestibulocochlear | Pontomedullary junction (lateral to VII) |
| IX | Glossopharyngeal | Posterolateral sulcus of medulla (upper) |
| X | Vagus | Posterolateral sulcus of medulla (below IX) |
| XI | Accessory | Posterolateral sulcus (medulla) + lateral spinal cord (C1–C5) |
| XII | Hypoglossal | Anterolateral sulcus of medulla (between pyramid and olive) |

### Functional Components
- **GSA (General Somatic Afferent):** Skin sensation — V, VII, IX, X
- **GVA (General Visceral Afferent):** Visceral sensation — IX, X
- **SSA (Special Somatic Afferent):** Vision, hearing, balance — II, VIII
- **SVA (Special Visceral Afferent):** Taste, smell — I, VII, IX, X
- **GSE (General Somatic Efferent):** Skeletal muscle from somites — III, IV, VI, XII
- **GVE (General Visceral Efferent):** Parasympathetic — III, VII, IX, X
- **SVE (Special Visceral Efferent/Branchiomotor):** Muscles from pharyngeal arches — V, VII, IX, X, XI

### Parasympathetic Ganglia
| CN | Nucleus | Ganglion | Target |
|----|---------|----------|--------|
| III | Edinger-Westphal | Ciliary | Sphincter pupillae, ciliary muscle |
| VII | Superior salivatory | Pterygopalatine, Submandibular | Lacrimal, nasal, palatal glands; submandibular, sublingual glands |
| IX | Inferior salivatory | Otic | Parotid gland |
| X | Dorsal motor nucleus | Ganglia in/near organs | Heart, lungs, GI tract (to splenic flexure) |

## Relations & Surface Markings

- CN I: beneath the frontal lobe on the cribriform plate
- CN II: converges at the optic chiasma above the pituitary
- CN III–VI: traverse the cavernous sinus (III, IV in lateral wall; VI within the sinus)
- CN VII, VIII: enter the internal acoustic meatus together
- CN IX, X, XI: exit through the jugular foramen together
- CN XII: exits through the hypoglossal canal

## Blood Supply & Nerve Supply

- Cranial nerves are supplied by vasa nervorum from adjacent arteries
- CN III: may be compressed by posterior cerebral artery or superior cerebellar artery aneurysm
- CN VI: longest intracranial course — vulnerable to raised ICP (false localising sign)

## Applied / Clinical Anatomy

### Testing Cranial Nerves (Clinical Examination)
- **I:** Smell test (each nostril)
- **II:** Visual acuity, fields, fundoscopy, pupillary reflexes
- **III, IV, VI:** Eye movements, pupil size, ptosis
- **V:** Facial sensation (3 divisions), jaw muscles, corneal reflex (afferent)
- **VII:** Facial movements, taste (anterior 2/3 tongue), corneal reflex (efferent)
- **VIII:** Hearing (Weber, Rinne), balance
- **IX, X:** Gag reflex, palate movement, voice
- **XI:** SCM and trapezius power
- **XII:** Tongue protrusion (deviates to side of lesion in LMN)

### Important Clinical Correlations
- **CN III palsy:** Ptosis, eye "down and out," dilated pupil (if parasympathetic involved)
- **CN IV palsy:** Difficulty looking down and in; head tilt to opposite side
- **CN VI palsy:** Convergent squint; false localising sign in raised ICP
- **CN VII palsy:** UMN (forehead spared) vs LMN (entire face affected)
- **Cerebellopontine angle tumour:** CN VII, VIII involvement → facial palsy, deafness
