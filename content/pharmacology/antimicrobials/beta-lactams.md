# Beta-Lactam Antibiotics

> 📚 **Sources:** KD Tripathi Essentials of Medical Pharmacology 8th Ed, Goodman & Gilman's The Pharmacological Basis of Therapeutics 13th Ed, Katzung Basic & Clinical Pharmacology
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Beta-lactam antibiotics contain a **β-lactam ring** essential for antibacterial activity. They are **bactericidal** and work by inhibiting **cell wall synthesis** through irreversible binding to **Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs)**. This class includes penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, and monobactams.

## Key Points

- **Mechanism**: Inhibit transpeptidase (PBP) → block peptidoglycan cross-linking → cell lysis
- **Bactericidal** - kill actively dividing bacteria
- **Time-dependent killing** - efficacy depends on time above MIC (T > MIC)
- **Resistance mechanisms**: β-lactamase production (most common), altered PBPs, decreased permeability
- **β-lactamase inhibitors**: Clavulanic acid, sulbactam, tazobactam, avibactam

## Detailed Content

### Mechanism of Action

```
Peptidoglycan synthesis:
NAM-NAG chains → cross-linked by transpeptidase (PBP)
                              ↑
                    β-lactams INHIBIT
                              ↓
              ↓ Cross-linking → Weak cell wall → Osmotic lysis
```

**Steps:**
1. β-lactam enters periplasmic space (via porins in Gram-negative)
2. Binds to PBPs (transpeptidases, carboxypeptidases)
3. Inhibits transpeptidation (cross-linking of peptidoglycan)
4. Activates autolysins
5. Cell wall weakening → osmotic lysis

### PENICILLINS

#### Natural Penicillins

| Drug | Spectrum | Route | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|-------|
| **Penicillin G** | Strep, Syphilis, Meningococcus, Actinomyces | IV, IM | Acid-labile (destroyed by gastric acid) |
| **Penicillin V** | Same as Pen G | PO | Acid-stable, oral use |
| **Benzathine Penicillin G** | Same | IM depot | Long-acting (3-4 weeks), rheumatic fever prophylaxis |
| **Procaine Penicillin G** | Same | IM depot | Intermediate duration (1-2 days) |

**Spectrum:** Strep pyogenes, Strep pneumoniae, Treponema pallidum, Neisseria meningitidis, Clostridium, Actinomyces

**Clinical uses:**
- **Syphilis**: Benzathine Penicillin G (drug of choice)
- **Rheumatic fever prophylaxis**: Benzathine Pen G monthly
- **Strep pharyngitis**: Pen V oral
- **Meningococcal meningitis**: High-dose Pen G IV

#### Penicillinase-Resistant Penicillins (Anti-staphylococcal)

| Drug | Route | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Methicillin** | IV | Prototype (no longer used - interstitial nephritis) |
| **Nafcillin** | IV | Preferred parenteral |
| **Oxacillin** | IV | Alternative to nafcillin |
| **Cloxacillin** | PO | Oral anti-staph |
| **Dicloxacillin** | PO | Best oral absorption |

**Spectrum:** MSSA (Methicillin-sensitive Staph aureus)
**NOT effective against:** MRSA (has altered PBP2a)

#### Aminopenicillins (Extended spectrum)

| Drug | Route | Spectrum Extension |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| **Ampicillin** | IV, PO | + Enterococcus, H. influenzae, E. coli, Listeria |
| **Amoxicillin** | PO | Same, better oral absorption |

**Key uses:**
- **Listeria meningitis**: Ampicillin (drug of choice)
- **Enterococcal endocarditis**: Ampicillin + Gentamicin
- **H. pylori**: Amoxicillin (triple/quadruple therapy)
- **Otitis media**: Amoxicillin first-line
- **UTI**: Ampicillin (but resistance increasing)

**Amoxicillin + Clavulanic acid (Augmentin):**
- Adds coverage for β-lactamase producers
- Animal bites, sinusitis, diabetic foot infections

#### Antipseudomonal Penicillins

| Drug | Route | Spectrum |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Piperacillin** | IV | Pseudomonas, Enterobacteriaceae, anaerobes |
| **Ticarcillin** | IV | Pseudomonas (less potent than piperacillin) |

**Piperacillin-Tazobactam (Zosyn):**
- Broadest spectrum penicillin
- Covers: Pseudomonas, ESBL producers, anaerobes
- **"Kitchen sink" antibiotic for severe infections**
- Nosocomial pneumonia, intra-abdominal infections, febrile neutropenia

### CEPHALOSPORINS

**Mnemonic for generations: "1st generation Kills GP, 5th generation Kills MRSA"**

#### First Generation

| Drug | Route | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Cefazolin** | IV | Surgical prophylaxis (DOC) |
| **Cephalexin** | PO | Skin/soft tissue infections |
| **Cefadroxil** | PO | UTI, pharyngitis |

**Spectrum:** Excellent **Gram-positive** (Staph, Strep), limited Gram-negative

**Clinical uses:**
- **Surgical prophylaxis**: Cefazolin (most common)
- **Skin infections**: Cephalexin
- **UTI**: Cephalexin

#### Second Generation

| Drug | Route | Special Coverage |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **Cefuroxime** | IV/PO | H. influenzae, Moraxella |
| **Cefaclor** | PO | Respiratory pathogens |
| **Cefoxitin** | IV | **Anaerobes** (B. fragilis) |
| **Cefotetan** | IV | Anaerobes, surgical prophylaxis |

**"Cefoxitin and Cefotetan = Cephamycins" → Anaerobic coverage**

**Clinical uses:**
- **Community-acquired pneumonia**: Cefuroxime
- **Intra-abdominal infections**: Cefoxitin (anaerobe coverage)
- **PID**: Cefoxitin + Doxycycline

#### Third Generation

| Drug | Route | Special Features |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **Ceftriaxone** | IV/IM | Long half-life (once daily), biliary excretion |
| **Cefotaxime** | IV | Safer in neonates (no biliary sludge) |
| **Ceftazidime** | IV | **Anti-pseudomonal** |
| **Cefixime** | PO | Gonorrhea (if ceftriaxone unavailable) |
| **Cefpodoxime** | PO | Oral step-down |

**Spectrum:** Excellent **Gram-negative**, good Gram-positive, **crosses BBB**

**Clinical uses:**
- **Bacterial meningitis**: Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin (empiric)
- **Gonorrhea**: Ceftriaxone 500mg IM (DOC)
- **Hospital-acquired Gram-negative infections**: Ceftazidime
- **Lyme disease**: Ceftriaxone (neurologic/cardiac)
- **Typhoid fever**: Ceftriaxone

**Ceftriaxone warnings:**
- Avoid in neonates (bilirubin displacement, biliary sludge)
- Do NOT mix with calcium-containing solutions
- Use cefotaxime in neonates instead

#### Fourth Generation

| Drug | Route | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Cefepime** | IV | Pseudomonas + better Gram-positive than 3rd gen |

**Spectrum:** Gram-positive + Gram-negative + Pseudomonas
**Resistant to many β-lactamases** (but not ESBL)

**Clinical uses:**
- Febrile neutropenia (monotherapy)
- Nosocomial infections
- Pseudomonal infections

#### Fifth Generation

| Drug | Route | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Ceftaroline** | IV | **MRSA coverage** |
| **Ceftobiprole** | IV | MRSA + Pseudomonas |

**Ceftaroline:** Only β-lactam effective against MRSA (binds PBP2a)
- Community-acquired pneumonia
- Skin/soft tissue infections (including MRSA)

### Cephalosporin Adverse Effects

| Effect | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Hypersensitivity** | Cross-reactivity with penicillins (~1-2%) |
| **Disulfiram reaction** | Cefotetan, cefoperazone (MTT side chain) |
| **Vitamin K deficiency** | Cefotetan, cefoperazone (↓ gut flora) |
| **Biliary sludge** | Ceftriaxone (avoid in neonates) |
| **Seizures** | High doses, renal failure |

### CARBAPENEMS

| Drug | Route | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Imipenem-Cilastatin** | IV | Broadest spectrum, needs cilastatin (DHP-1 inhibitor) |
| **Meropenem** | IV | Less seizure risk, meningitis-approved |
| **Ertapenem** | IV/IM | Once daily, NO Pseudomonas coverage |
| **Doripenem** | IV | Similar to meropenem |

**Spectrum:** Broadest of all β-lactams
- Gram-positive, Gram-negative, anaerobes, ESBL producers
- **NOT effective against:** MRSA, Enterococcus faecium, Stenotrophomonas

**Clinical uses:**
- Severe nosocomial infections
- Multi-drug resistant Gram-negatives
- Polymicrobial infections (intra-abdominal, diabetic foot)
- Meningitis (meropenem only)

**Imipenem + Cilastatin:**
- Cilastatin inhibits renal dehydropeptidase-1 (DHP-1)
- Prevents imipenem degradation in kidney
- ↓ Seizure threshold (avoid in CNS infections)

---

### MONOBACTAMS

| Drug | Route | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Aztreonam** | IV | Only Gram-negative coverage |

**Unique features:**
- **NO Gram-positive or anaerobic activity**
- **NO cross-allergenicity with penicillins** (safe in penicillin allergy)
- Covers Pseudomonas

**Clinical use:** Gram-negative infections in penicillin-allergic patients

---

### β-LACTAMASE INHIBITORS

| Inhibitor | Paired With | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Clavulanic acid** | Amoxicillin, Ticarcillin | Suicidal inhibitor |
| **Sulbactam** | Ampicillin | Also has activity vs Acinetobacter |
| **Tazobactam** | Piperacillin | Broadest combination |
| **Avibactam** | Ceftazidime | Covers KPC (CRE), some ESBL |
| **Vaborbactam** | Meropenem | Covers KPC |

**Mechanism:** Bind irreversibly to β-lactamases → protect the β-lactam

**Important combinations:**
- **Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin):** Respiratory, bites, sinusitis
- **Ampicillin-Sulbactam (Unasyn):** Acinetobacter, mixed infections
- **Piperacillin-Tazobactam (Zosyn):** Broad nosocomial coverage
- **Ceftazidime-Avibactam:** CRE (carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae)

---

### RESISTANCE MECHANISMS

| Mechanism | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **β-lactamase production** | ESBL, KPC, AmpC, NDM-1 (most common) |
| **Altered PBPs** | MRSA (PBP2a), penicillin-resistant Strep pneumoniae |
| **Decreased permeability** | Porin mutations (Pseudomonas, Enterobacter) |
| **Efflux pumps** | Pseudomonas |

**ESBL (Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamases):**
- Hydrolyze 3rd generation cephalosporins
- Treat with: Carbapenems, Ceftazidime-avibactam

**CRE (Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacteriaceae):**
- KPC, NDM-1, OXA-48
- Treat with: Ceftazidime-avibactam, Meropenem-vaborbactam, polymyxins

---

### PHARMACOKINETICS

| Parameter | Clinical Relevance |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Time-dependent killing** | T > MIC most important (prolonged/continuous infusion) |
| **Renal excretion** | Dose adjust in renal failure (except nafcillin, ceftriaxone) |
| **CSF penetration** | 3rd gen cephalosporins, meropenem, high-dose penicillin |
| **Protein binding** | Ceftriaxone highly bound (avoid in neonates - bilirubin displacement) |

## Clinical Applications

1. **Empiric meningitis**: Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin (± Ampicillin if Listeria risk)
2. **Surgical prophylaxis**: Cefazolin (1st gen)
3. **MSSA bacteremia**: Nafcillin or cefazolin
4. **Pseudomonas infection**: Piperacillin-tazobactam, ceftazidime, cefepime, meropenem
5. **ESBL infections**: Carbapenems (meropenem, ertapenem)
6. **Penicillin allergy**: Aztreonam (Gram-negative), or carefully selected cephalosporin

## High-Yield for Exams

1. **Mechanism of β-lactams** → Inhibit transpeptidase (PBP) → ↓ cell wall synthesis
2. **Drug of choice for syphilis** → Benzathine Penicillin G
3. **Drug of choice for Listeria** → Ampicillin
4. **Surgical prophylaxis DOC** → Cefazolin
5. **Only cephalosporin for MRSA** → Ceftaroline
6. **Cephalosporins with anaerobic coverage** → Cefoxitin, Cefotetan (cephamycins)
7. **Anti-pseudomonal cephalosporins** → Ceftazidime (3rd), Cefepime (4th)
8. **Carbapenem requiring cilastatin** → Imipenem (DHP-1 inhibitor)
9. **Carbapenem safe in meningitis** → Meropenem (less seizure risk)
10. **No Pseudomonas coverage** → Ertapenem
11. **Safe in penicillin allergy** → Aztreonam (no cross-reactivity)
12. **Disulfiram-like reaction** → Cefotetan, Cefoperazone (MTT side chain)
13. **Time-dependent killing** → Efficacy = Time > MIC
14. **ESBL treatment** → Carbapenems
15. **Penicillin + Aminoglycoside synergy** → Enterococcal endocarditis
