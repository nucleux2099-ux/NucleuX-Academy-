# Aminoglycoside Antibiotics

> 📚 **Sources:** KD Tripathi Essentials of Medical Pharmacology 8th Ed, Goodman & Gilman's The Pharmacological Basis of Therapeutics 13th Ed, Katzung Basic & Clinical Pharmacology
> **Level:** UG | PG

## Core Concept

Aminoglycosides are **bactericidal** antibiotics that inhibit bacterial protein synthesis by irreversibly binding to the **30S ribosomal subunit**. They exhibit **concentration-dependent killing** and a significant **post-antibiotic effect (PAE)**. They are highly effective against aerobic **Gram-negative bacilli** but carry significant risks of **nephrotoxicity** and **ototoxicity**.

## Key Points

- **Mechanism**: Irreversible binding to 30S ribosome → misreading of mRNA → defective proteins
- **Bactericidal** - one of few protein synthesis inhibitors that kills (not just inhibits)
- **Concentration-dependent killing** - efficacy depends on Cmax/MIC ratio
- **Post-antibiotic effect (PAE)** - bacterial suppression continues after drug cleared
- **Toxicity**: Nephrotoxicity (reversible), Ototoxicity (irreversible - vestibular/cochlear)
- **Poor oral absorption** - must give IV/IM for systemic infections

## Detailed Content

### Drugs in Class

| Drug | Primary Use | Special Features |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Gentamicin** | Serious Gram-negative infections | Most commonly used, synergy with β-lactams |
| **Tobramycin** | Pseudomonas (especially lung) | Inhaled for CF patients |
| **Amikacin** | Resistant Gram-negatives, TB | Most resistant to aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes |
| **Streptomycin** | TB, Plague, Tularemia | First aminoglycoside, mainly for mycobacteria now |
| **Neomycin** | Topical, bowel prep | Too toxic for systemic use |
| **Paromomycin** | Intestinal amebiasis, Cryptosporidium | Not absorbed orally |
| **Plazomicin** | CRE infections | Newest, resists most modifying enzymes |
| **Kanamycin** | MDR-TB | Limited systemic use |

### Mechanism of Action

**Target:** Bacterial **30S ribosomal subunit** (specifically 16S rRNA)

```
Aminoglycoside → Binds 30S ribosome
                        ↓
        Irreversible binding (bactericidal)
                        ↓
              Multiple effects:
              1. Blocks initiation complex
              2. Misreading of mRNA codons
              3. Premature termination
              4. Defective proteins insert into membrane → cell death
```

**Unique features:**
- **Irreversible binding** (unlike macrolides, tetracyclines)
- **Misreading** produces defective proteins → membrane damage
- Requires **oxygen** for uptake (ineffective against anaerobes)
- Requires **active transport** into bacteria (energy-dependent)

**Why bactericidal?**
- Defective proteins insert into cell membrane
- Membrane damage → cell lysis
- This distinguishes aminoglycosides from other protein synthesis inhibitors

---

### Spectrum of Activity

| Coverage | Organisms |
|----------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Aerobic Gram-negative bacilli (E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus, Pseudomonas, Serratia, Enterobacter) |
| **Good** | Staphylococci (synergy with β-lactams) |
| **Synergistic** | Enterococci, Streptococci (with cell wall agents) |
| **Poor/None** | Anaerobes (require O₂ for uptake), Streptococci (alone), intracellular organisms |

**Key point:** Aminoglycosides do NOT work against:
- Anaerobes (no oxygen-dependent uptake)
- Streptococci (low-level intrinsic resistance)
- Intracellular pathogens (poor cell penetration)

---

### Pharmacokinetics

| Parameter | Characteristic | Clinical Relevance |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Absorption** | Poor oral (< 1%) | Must use IV/IM for systemic; PO for gut decontamination |
| **Distribution** | Hydrophilic, low Vd | Poor CNS/eye/prostate penetration |
| **Protein binding** | Low (< 10%) | Not affected by hypoalbuminemia |
| **Metabolism** | None (not metabolized) | Excreted unchanged |
| **Elimination** | 100% renal (glomerular filtration) | Dose adjust in renal impairment |
| **Half-life** | 2-3 hours (normal renal function) | Extended in renal failure |

**Special considerations:**
- **Obesity**: Use adjusted body weight (ABW) for dosing
- **Pregnancy**: Category D (fetal ototoxicity)
- **CSF penetration**: Poor (only ~20% even with inflamed meninges)
- **Accumulates in**: Renal cortex (nephrotoxicity), inner ear (ototoxicity)

---

### Pharmacodynamics

**Concentration-dependent killing:**
- Higher peak concentrations → more bacterial killing
- Goal: **Cmax/MIC ratio of 8-10**

**Post-antibiotic effect (PAE):**
- Bacterial suppression continues 2-8 hours after drug levels fall below MIC
- Allows once-daily dosing

**Dosing strategies:**

| Strategy | Dose | Frequency | Advantages |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|
| **Extended-interval (Once daily)** | Higher | Every 24h | ↑ Efficacy, ↓ toxicity, convenient |
| **Traditional (Multiple daily)** | Lower | Every 8h | Used in endocarditis synergy, pregnancy, burns, CF |

**Once-daily dosing rationale:**
1. Maximizes Cmax/MIC (concentration-dependent killing)
2. Allows drug-free interval for renal/ear recovery
3. Equivalent/better efficacy, less nephrotoxicity
4. Exception: Enterococcal endocarditis (traditional dosing for synergy)

### Toxicity

#### Nephrotoxicity (5-25%)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Mechanism** | Accumulates in proximal tubular cells → ATN |
| **Risk factors** | Duration > 7 days, trough levels, dehydration, other nephrotoxins |
| **Presentation** | ↑ Creatinine, non-oliguric renal failure, cylindruria |
| **Reversibility** | Usually reversible after discontinuation |
| **Prevention** | Once-daily dosing, monitor troughs, avoid nephrotoxins, hydration |
| **Relative risk** | Streptomycin < Tobramycin < Gentamicin < Amikacin < Neomycin |

#### Ototoxicity (2-10%)

| Type | Mechanism | Symptoms | Reversibility |
|------|-----------|----------|---------------|
| **Vestibular** | Hair cell damage in vestibule | Vertigo, nystagmus, ataxia | Usually irreversible |
| **Cochlear** | Hair cell damage in cochlea | Tinnitus, hearing loss (high-frequency first) | Usually irreversible |

| Drug | Predominant Toxicity |
|------|---------------------|
| **Streptomycin, Gentamicin, Tobramycin** | Vestibular |
| **Amikacin, Neomycin, Kanamycin** | Cochlear |

**Risk factors:** Duration, dose, renal impairment, concurrent ototoxins (loop diuretics, cisplatin), genetic susceptibility (MT-RNR1 mutation)

#### Neuromuscular Blockade

- **Mechanism:** Block presynaptic ACh release + postsynaptic receptor block
- **Risk:** Rapid IV infusion, myasthenia gravis, concurrent neuromuscular blockers
- **Presentation:** Respiratory paralysis
- **Treatment:** Calcium gluconate, neostigmine

#### Other Toxicities

| Effect | Drug | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| **Contact dermatitis** | Neomycin (topical) | Common cause of contact allergy |
| **Malabsorption** | Neomycin (oral) | Prolonged use |
| **Optic neuritis** | Streptomycin | Rare |

---

### Resistance Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Details | Clinical Impact |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| **Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (AMEs)** | Acetylation, adenylation, phosphorylation | Most common; Amikacin most resistant |
| **Ribosomal mutations** | 16S rRNA mutations | Single-step high-level resistance |
| **Decreased uptake** | Altered transport, anaerobic conditions | Pseudomonas |
| **Efflux pumps** | Active drug extrusion | Less common |

**Amikacin advantage:** Most resistant to AMEs (only 1 modifying enzyme affects it vs multiple for gentamicin)

### Clinical Applications

#### 1. Severe Gram-Negative Infections (Usually combination therapy)

| Infection | Regimen | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Sepsis/Bacteremia** | Aminoglycoside + β-lactam | Synergy, empiric coverage |
| **Hospital-acquired pneumonia** | Combination for Pseudomonas | Double coverage |
| **Pyelonephritis** | Gentamicin | Severe cases |
| **Intra-abdominal** | Part of combination | With β-lactam + metronidazole |

#### 2. Synergy with Cell Wall Agents

| Infection | Regimen | Mechanism |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Enterococcal endocarditis** | Ampicillin + Gentamicin | β-lactam opens cell wall → ↑ aminoglycoside entry |
| **Viridans streptococcal endocarditis** | Penicillin + Gentamicin | Shorter duration possible |
| **Staphylococcal endocarditis** | Nafcillin + Gentamicin | First 3-5 days (controversial) |

**High-level aminoglycoside resistance (HLAR):** If MIC > 500 (gentamicin) or > 1000 (streptomycin), synergy is lost → use alternative

#### 3. Specific Infections

| Infection | Drug of Choice | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **Plague (Yersinia pestis)** | Streptomycin or Gentamicin | Alternatives: doxycycline, ciprofloxacin |
| **Tularemia** | Streptomycin or Gentamicin | DOC |
| **Brucellosis** | Streptomycin + Doxycycline | 6 weeks |
| **MDR-TB** | Streptomycin, Amikacin, Kanamycin | Second-line agents |
| **Pseudomonas in CF** | Inhaled Tobramycin | TOBI - chronic suppressive |

#### 4. Local/Topical Uses

| Use | Drug | Notes |
|-----|------|-------|
| **Bowel prep (hepatic encephalopathy)** | Neomycin oral | ↓ Ammonia-producing bacteria |
| **Surgical bowel prep** | Neomycin + Erythromycin | Before colorectal surgery |
| **Topical skin infections** | Neomycin, Gentamicin cream | Part of triple antibiotic |
| **Eye infections** | Gentamicin, Tobramycin drops | Bacterial conjunctivitis |
| **Intestinal amebiasis** | Paromomycin | Luminal cysts |

---

### Drug Interactions

| Interaction | Drugs | Effect |
|-------------|-------|--------|
| **↑ Nephrotoxicity** | Vancomycin, amphotericin B, cisplatin, NSAIDs, contrast | Additive renal damage |
| **↑ Ototoxicity** | Loop diuretics (furosemide), cisplatin | Additive ear damage |
| **↑ Neuromuscular blockade** | Succinylcholine, vecuronium, botulinum toxin | Prolonged paralysis |
| **Synergy** | β-lactams | Enhanced killing of Gram-positives |
| **Antagonism** | Tetracyclines, chloramphenicol | Bacteriostatic agents reduce aminoglycoside uptake |

---

### Monitoring

| Parameter | Target | When |
|-----------|--------|------|
| **Peak level** | Gentamicin/Tobramycin: 5-10 µg/mL | 30 min after infusion |
| | Amikacin: 20-30 µg/mL | |
| **Trough level** | Gentamicin/Tobramycin: < 2 µg/mL | Just before next dose |
| | Amikacin: < 5-10 µg/mL | |
| **Creatinine** | Baseline, then every 2-3 days | Throughout therapy |
| **Audiometry** | Baseline if prolonged use | Weekly if symptomatic |

**For once-daily dosing:** Use nomograms (Hartford nomogram) rather than peak/trough

## Clinical Applications

1. **Gram-negative sepsis**: Empiric combination with β-lactam
2. **Enterococcal endocarditis**: Ampicillin + Gentamicin synergy
3. **Plague/Tularemia**: Streptomycin or Gentamicin first-line
4. **CF Pseudomonas**: Inhaled tobramycin (TOBI)
5. **Hepatic encephalopathy**: Oral neomycin (↓ ammonia)
