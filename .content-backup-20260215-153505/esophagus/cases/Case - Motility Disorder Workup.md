# Case Scenario: Motility Disorder Workup

## Clinical Vignette

### Initial Presentation

**Patient:** 42-year-old female software engineer

**Chief Complaint:** "Food gets stuck in my chest for the past 2 years"

**History of Present Illness:**
- Progressive dysphagia initially to solids, now also with liquids
- Describes food "getting stuck" at the lower chest level
- Regurgitation of undigested food, especially when lying down
- Has learned to eat slowly and drink water after each bite
- Occasional non-cardiac chest discomfort
- Weight loss: 8 kg over 2 years
- No odynophagia, no heartburn
- No nausea or vomiting (other than regurgitation)

**Review of Systems:**
- No joint pain or skin changes
- No Raynaud's phenomenon
- No respiratory symptoms
- No changes in voice or speech

**Past Medical History:**
- Hypothyroidism on levothyroxine
- No prior surgeries
- No known allergies

**Family History:**
- Mother: Type 2 diabetes
- Father: Hypertension
- No family history of GI malignancy

**Social History:**
- Non-smoker
- Occasional alcohol
- No recent travel
- No drug use

## Clinical Reasoning: Phase 1

### Stop and Think

**Question 1:** Based on the history, what type of dysphagia is this?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**Analysis: Esophageal Dysphagia**

Features pointing to esophageal (not oropharyngeal):
- Food "stuck" in chest (transfer complete, obstruction downstream)
- Progressive course
- No difficulty initiating swallow
- No coughing during swallowing
- No nasal regurgitation

**Key observation:** Dysphagia to both solids AND liquids
- Suggests motility disorder (not mechanical obstruction)
- Mechanical obstruction typically affects solids first, then liquids late
- "Paradoxical dysphagia" (liquids worse than solids) = classic for achalasia

</details>

**Question 2:** What is your differential diagnosis?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Differential</summary>

**Primary Differential:**

1. **Achalasia** (most likely)
   - Progressive dysphagia solids → liquids
   - Regurgitation of undigested food
   - Weight loss
   - Classic presentation

2. **Eosinophilic Esophagitis**
   - Typically intermittent
   - Food impaction common
   - Usually younger males with atopy

3. **Esophageal stricture** (peptic, post-caustic)
   - Usually solids > liquids
   - History of GERD or ingestion

4. **Distal Esophageal Spasm**
   - Usually chest pain predominant
   - Intermittent symptoms

5. **Esophageal malignancy** (less likely)
   - Age 42 without alarm features
   - But must rule out with EGD

</details>

## Clinical Reasoning: Phase 2

### Stop and Think

**Question 3:** What do the EGD findings suggest and what is the next step?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**EGD Interpretation:**

The findings strongly suggest achalasia:
- Dilated esophagus (from chronic obstruction)
- Retained food/saliva (poor emptying)
- Tight EGJ (non-relaxing LES)
- No mechanical obstruction

**Next Step: High-Resolution Manometry (HRM)**
- Gold standard for motility disorder diagnosis
- Will confirm achalasia and determine subtype
- Essential for treatment planning

**Why not treat based on EGD alone?**
- Need to confirm diagnosis
- Need to determine achalasia subtype (I, II, or III)
- Treatment selection depends on subtype

</details>

## Clinical Reasoning: Phase 3

### Stop and Think

**Question 4:** How do you interpret these HRM findings and what is the final diagnosis?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**HRM Analysis per Chicago Classification v4.0:**

Step 1: Assess IRP
- IRP = 24 mmHg → ≥15 mmHg = **EGJ outflow obstruction present**

Step 2: Assess Peristalsis
- 0/10 normal propagating contractions = **100% failed peristalsis**

Step 3: Determine Achalasia Subtype
- Panesophageal pressurization in 80% swallows (≥20%)
- No premature contractions

**Diagnosis: TYPE II ACHALASIA**

**Why Type II?**
- IRP elevated ✓
- 100% failed peristalsis ✓
- Panesophageal pressurization ≥20% ✓ (this makes it Type II)
- No premature/spastic contractions (would make it Type III)

</details>

**Question 5:** What is the clinical significance of Type II vs other subtypes?

<details>
<summary>Reveal Analysis</summary>

**Type II Achalasia - Clinical Significance:**

**Best prognosis among subtypes:**
- ~90-95% response to treatment
- Panesophageal pressurization indicates preserved muscle function
- Represents 50% of all achalasia cases

**Why best response?**
- The esophageal muscle is intact and contractile
- Just needs LES obstruction relieved
- Once myotomy done, pressurization helps empty

**Treatment options (all equivalent for Type II):**
1. Pneumatic dilation (PD)
2. Laparoscopic Heller myotomy + Dor fundoplication
3. POEM

**Compare to other subtypes:**
- Type I: Good response but less than II (no pressure to empty)
- Type III: Worst response (spastic muscle, POEM preferred)

</details>

## Treatment and Follow-up

### Patient's Choice and Procedure

The patient chose **POEM** after discussion, preferring the endoscopic approach with no external incisions.

**POEM Procedure Report:**

```
PERORAL ENDOSCOPIC MYOTOMY (POEM)
═════════════════════════════════

Date: [Scheduled]
Anesthesia: General

PROCEDURE:
1. Mucosal incision: 10 cm above EGJ, posterior approach
2. Submucosal tunnel created down to 2 cm below EGJ
3. Selective circular myotomy performed
   - From 2 cm above mucosal entry to 2 cm onto gastric cardia
   - Total myotomy length: 12 cm
4. Hemostasis achieved
5. Mucosal incision closed with endoscopic clips

FINDINGS:
- Thick circular muscle layer
- No perforation
- Good muscle separation

OUTCOME: Successful POEM
```

### Post-Procedure Course

**Day 1:**
- Gastrografin swallow: No leak
- Started clear liquids
- Mild chest discomfort (expected)

**Day 2:**
- Advanced to soft diet
- Discharged home
- Prescriptions: PPI (pantoprazole 40mg daily), soft diet 2 weeks

### Follow-up at 3 Months

```
FOLLOW-UP ASSESSMENT
════════════════════

Symptoms:
- Dysphagia: Resolved (Eckardt score 1, pre-procedure 8)
- No regurgitation
- Eating regular diet
- Weight gained 3 kg
- Mild heartburn (controlled with PPI)

Timed Barium Esophagogram:
- 1 minute: 3 cm column
- 5 minutes: Complete emptying
- Improved from pre-procedure

Plan:
- Continue PPI indefinitely
- Annual EGD for surveillance (GERD risk)
- Return if symptoms recur
```
