# Case: Achalasia - New Diagnosis & Management

> **Type**: Elective (Diagnostic → Treatment Planning)
> **Difficulty**: Moderate
> **Primary System**: [[Esophagus Index]]

## Clinical Presentation

### Chief Complaint

> **Patient**: 34-year-old female presents with...
>
> **Duration**: 18 months, progressive
>
> **Main symptoms**:
> - Difficulty swallowing both solid food and liquids
> - Regurgitation of undigested food, especially at night

### History of Present Illness

Ms. Sharma is a 34-year-old software engineer who presents with an 18-month history of progressive difficulty swallowing. She reports that the problem began insidiously and has gradually worsened.

**Dysphagia characteristics**:
- Affects **both solids and liquids equally** from the start
- Does not get stuck at one level; feels like food "sits" in her chest
- Takes 20-30 minutes to finish a meal (previously 10 minutes)
- Has learned to eat slowly and take small bites

**Associated symptoms**:
- **Regurgitation** of undigested food, particularly when lying down at night
- Wakes up coughing 2-3 times per week
- Occasional retrosternal chest discomfort after eating
- Reports "heartburn" but describes it as more of a heaviness than burning
- Has lost 6 kg over 18 months (unintentional)

**Alleviating factors**:
- Drinking **carbonated beverages** helps food go down
- Raising arms overhead
- Walking around after meals
- Avoiding eating within 3 hours of bedtime

She denies odynophagia, hematemesis, melena, or hoarseness. No fevers or night sweats.

- [ ] Hypertension
- [ ] Cardiac disease
- [ ] Previous surgeries: Appendectomy age 16
- [x] Medications: None regular
- [ ] Allergies: NKDA

### Social History

- Alcohol: Occasional (1-2 drinks/month)
- Occupation: Software engineer (sedentary)

## Stop & Think #1: Initial Assessment

> [!question] What is your differential diagnosis?
> List in order of likelihood:
> 1. **Achalasia** - solids AND liquids from onset, regurgitation, maneuvers help, gradual weight loss
> 2. **Other esophageal motility disorder** - DES, jackhammer esophagus
> 3. **Eosinophilic esophagitis** - though typically intermittent, food impaction
> 4. **Esophageal stricture** - usually solids first
> 5. **Esophageal cancer** - less likely given age, duration, and pattern

> [!question] What key features point toward achalasia?
> - Dysphagia to **solids AND liquids equally from onset** (motility, not mechanical)
> - Regurgitation of **undigested** food (not digested = not GERD)
> - Specific maneuvers help (carbonated drinks, raising arms)
> - Nocturnal symptoms (supine position worsens stasis)
> - Gradual weight loss over long duration

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal teaching points</summary>

**Key considerations**:
- "Solids AND liquids from onset" is the hallmark of achalasia
- Mechanical obstruction causes "solids first, then liquids"
- Undigested food regurgitation = not reaching stomach
- Young age makes cancer less likely but doesn't exclude it

**Red flags NOT present in this case**:
- Age <55 (not >55)
- Duration 18 months (not <1 year)
- Gradual weight loss (not rapid)
- No signs of malignancy

</details>

## Investigations

### Upper GI Endoscopy (EGD)

**Findings**:
- Esophagus: Dilated with moderate amount of retained saliva and food debris despite overnight fasting
- Mucosa: Normal appearance, no ulceration or mass
- GEJ: "Puckered" or "rosette" appearance; scope passed with **mild resistance** but no obstruction
- Stomach: Normal
- Duodenum: Normal

**Interpretation**: Findings consistent with achalasia. No evidence of malignancy.

**Findings**:

## Stop & Think #3: Interpretation

> [!question] What is the confirmed diagnosis?
> **Achalasia Type II** (Chicago Classification v4.0)

> [!question] Why is Type II the best prognosis?
> In Type II, the esophageal body generates **uniform pressurization** (panesophageal compression) with swallows. This indicates preserved muscle function. When the LES is disrupted by treatment, this residual contractility helps push food through the opened sphincter.
>
> Type II has **>90% success rate** with any treatment modality.

**Confirmed Diagnosis**: Achalasia Type II

**Diagnostic criteria met**:
- Clinical: Dysphagia solids + liquids, regurgitation, maneuvers
- Barium: Bird's beak, dilated esophagus
- HRM: IRP 24 mmHg (>15), 100% failed peristalsis, panesophageal pressurization 55%

**Classification**: Type II (compression pattern) - Best prognosis

**Staging**: Not advanced - no sigmoid configuration, moderate dilation only

### Patient Counseling

**Discussed with patient**:
1. Achalasia is a chronic condition - **cannot restore peristalsis**
2. All treatments aim to **disrupt the LES** to allow food passage
3. Lifelong follow-up needed
4. Cancer surveillance (SCC risk) after 10-15 years

**Patient preference**: Prefers definitive surgical treatment over repeated dilations. Interested in POEM due to no incisions but concerned about GERD.

## Stop & Think #4: Management

> [!question] What would you recommend and why?
> **Laparoscopic Heller Myotomy + Dor fundoplication**
>
> For a young patient with Type II achalasia concerned about GERD, Heller + Dor offers:
> - Excellent long-term durability (20+ years data)
> - Fundoplication reduces GERD to 10-20%
> - Single definitive procedure
> - Type II responds well to any treatment

> [!question] What are the key operative steps for Heller myotomy?
> 1. Supine, split legs, reverse Trendelenburg
> 2. 5 ports, liver retractor
> 3. Open gastrohepatic ligament, identify anterior vagus
> 4. Mobilize distal esophagus (preserve vagus)
> 5. **Myotomy**: 6-8 cm esophageal + **2-3 cm onto gastric cardia**
> 6. Divide circular muscle, spare longitudinal, expose mucosa
> 7. Test with air insufflation (check for leak)
> 8. **Dor fundoplication** (anterior 180°)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal management discussion</summary>

**Why not POEM for this patient?**
- Higher GERD rates (20-50%) without fundoplication
- Patient specifically concerned about reflux
- For Type II, Heller and POEM have similar efficacy
- POEM would be preferred for Type III (needs longer myotomy)

**Why not Pneumatic Dilation?**
- Young patient → higher lifetime risk of repeat procedures
- 5-year success only 60-85%
- Surgery has better durability
- Appropriate for older patients or those declining surgery

</details>

## Operative Details

### Procedure: Laparoscopic Heller Myotomy + Dor Fundoplication

**Pre-operative preparation**:
- NPO after midnight
- Clear liquid diet for 48 hours pre-op (reduce residual food)
- Consent: Myotomy, fundoplication, possible conversion to open, risks
- DVT prophylaxis

**Position**: Supine, split legs, steep reverse Trendelenburg (30°)

**Port placement**:
- 10mm umbilical (camera)
- 5mm left upper quadrant (surgeon's left hand)
- 5mm right upper quadrant (surgeon's right hand)
- 5mm subxiphoid (liver retractor)
- 5mm left lateral (assistant)

**Key operative steps**:

1. **Exposure**:
   - Insert liver retractor, elevate left lobe
   - Open gastrohepatic ligament (pars flaccida)
   - Identify right crus, anterior vagus nerve

2. **Esophageal mobilization**:
   - Open phrenoesophageal ligament
   - Mobilize distal esophagus circumferentially
   - Preserve anterior and posterior vagus
   - Identify GEJ (fat pad landmark)

3. **Myotomy**:
   - Start on anterior esophagus, 6-8 cm above GEJ
   - Use hook cautery or ultrasonic device
   - Divide **circular muscle fibers** longitudinally
   - **Preserve longitudinal muscle layer**
   - Mucosa should bulge through (indicate complete myotomy)
   - **Extend distally 2-3 cm onto gastric cardia** (CRITICAL)
   - Sling fibers at GEJ must be completely divided

4. **Leak test**:
   - Submerge in saline
   - Insufflate air via NG tube
   - No bubbles = no perforation

5. **Dor fundoplication** (anterior 180°):
   - Suture left edge of fundus to left edge of myotomy
   - Fold fundus over exposed mucosa
   - Suture right edge of fundus to right edge of myotomy
   - Suture apex to diaphragm
   - Creates 2-row repair covering myotomy site

6. **Closure**:
   - Remove liver retractor
   - Hemostasis check
   - No drain required if dry
   - Close ports

**Specimen**: None (no tissue removed)

**EBL**: <50 mL

**Duration**: 90-120 minutes

## Intraoperative Considerations

### Critical Structures to Identify/Preserve

| Structure | Location | Consequence if Injured |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Anterior vagus** | On esophagus, right of myotomy | Delayed gastric emptying |
| **Posterior vagus** | Behind esophagus | Delayed gastric emptying |
| **Esophageal mucosa** | Deep to circular muscle | Perforation, leak |
| **Short gastric vessels** | If fundus mobilized | Bleeding (rarely needed) |

### Intraoperative Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------|
| **Mucosal perforation** | 5-10% | Primary repair + cover with Dor |
| **Bleeding** | <5% | Cautery, clips |
| **Vagus injury** | <5% | Supportive, may need pyloroplasty |
| **Pleural entry** | Rare | Evacuate pneumothorax, close defect |

## Post-operative Care

### Immediate Post-op (Day 0-1)

- **Diet**: NPO overnight
- **Gastrografin swallow**: POD 1 - confirm no leak
- **If no leak**: Start clear liquids, advance as tolerated
- **Pain**: Minimal (laparoscopic), oral analgesics
- **DVT prophylaxis**: Continue until mobile
- **Discharge**: POD 1-2 if tolerating diet

### Diet Progression

| Phase | Duration | Foods |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Clear liquids** | 1-2 days | Water, broth, juice |
| **Full liquids** | 3-5 days | Smoothies, soups, pudding |
| **Soft diet** | 2-4 weeks | Mashed foods, scrambled eggs |
| **Regular diet** | After 4 weeks | Gradual reintroduction |

- Eat slowly, chew thoroughly
- Small frequent meals
- Sit upright during and after meals
- Avoid carbonated beverages initially

## Complications to Anticipate

| Complication | Incidence | Prevention | Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------|------------|
| **Mucosal leak** | 1-2% | Intraop leak test, Dor coverage | NPO, CT, drainage ± reoperation |
| **Persistent dysphagia** | 5-10% | Adequate distal extension | Dilation, redo myotomy, POEM |
| **GERD** | 10-20% | Dor fundoplication | PPI therapy |
| **Wound infection** | <2% | Standard prophylaxis | Antibiotics, drainage |
| **Gastroparesis** | Rare | Preserve vagus nerves | Prokinetics |

## Follow-up & Prognosis

### Expected Outcomes for This Patient

- **Symptom relief**: >90% (Type II has best outcomes)
- **Long-term durability**: 85-90% at 10 years
- **GERD development**: 10-20% (reduced by Dor)
- **Need for reoperation**: <10%

### Timed Barium Esophagram (TBE)

**At 3 months post-op**:
- Objective measure of esophageal emptying
- Compare to pre-op baseline
- Barium column height at 1, 2, 5 minutes

**Expected improvement**:
- Faster emptying time
- Less residual barium at 5 minutes
- Reduced esophageal diameter over time

### Long-term Surveillance

**Cancer risk**:
- Squamous cell carcinoma risk: 0.5-1% per year after 15-20 years
- Due to chronic stasis, irritation, bacterial overgrowth
- Begin EGD surveillance at 10-15 years from diagnosis
- Repeat every 3-5 years

## Case Summary

### Final Diagnosis
**Achalasia Type II** (Chicago Classification v4.0)
- 34-year-old female
- 18-month history
- Classic presentation
- HRM: IRP 24 mmHg, 100% failed peristalsis, panesophageal pressurization 55%

### Treatment
**Laparoscopic Heller Myotomy + Dor Fundoplication**

### Key Learning Points

1. **Clinical pearl**: Dysphagia to solids AND liquids equally from onset = motility disorder (achalasia), not mechanical obstruction

2. **Diagnostic insight**: HRM is gold standard; Chicago Classification guides prognosis and treatment selection

3. **Management principle**: Type II has best outcomes (>90%) with ANY treatment modality; choice depends on patient factors and GERD concern

4. **Surgical technique**: Myotomy MUST extend 2-3 cm onto gastric cardia; incomplete distal myotomy is #1 cause of failure

5. **Complication prevention**: Dor fundoplication covers myotomy site (protects if mucosal injury) AND reduces GERD

### Take-Home Message

> **For Type II achalasia in a young patient**: Heller myotomy + Dor fundoplication offers excellent long-term durability with low GERD rates. The key technical point is ensuring the myotomy extends onto the gastric cardia. Type II's preserved esophageal compression provides the best treatment response of all subtypes.
