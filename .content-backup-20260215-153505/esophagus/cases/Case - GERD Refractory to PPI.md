# Case Scenario: GERD Refractory to Medical Management

> **Linked Concept**: [[GERD]]
> **Linked Cards**: [[Retrieval Cards - GERD]]
> **Source**: Shackelford Ch 7, 8, 10
> **Difficulty**: Intermediate

---

## Learning Objectives

By working through this case, you will be able to:
1. Evaluate a patient with symptoms refractory to PPI therapy
2. Order and interpret appropriate diagnostic studies
3. Determine candidacy for anti-reflux surgery
4. Select the appropriate surgical procedure based on manometric findings
5. Counsel patients on expected outcomes and complications

---

## Part 1: Initial Presentation

### Clinical Vignette

A **52-year-old accountant** presents to your clinic with persistent heartburn and regurgitation despite being on omeprazole 20 mg twice daily for the past 6 months. His symptoms began 3 years ago and initially responded well to once-daily PPI. Over the past year, symptoms have progressively worsened.

**Chief Complaint**: "The burning in my chest wakes me up at night, and I'm constantly clearing my throat."

### History of Present Illness

- Heartburn: Retrosternal burning, worse after meals and when lying down
- Regurgitation: Bitter/sour taste, especially at night
- Chronic throat clearing and hoarseness (noticed by colleagues)
- Occasional chest pain (evaluated by cardiology - cleared)
- Denies dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, or GI bleeding
- No food impaction episodes

### Medications

- Omeprazole 20 mg BID (takes 30 minutes before meals)
- Lisinopril 10 mg daily (hypertension)
- Aspirin 81 mg daily

### Social History

- BMI: 31 kg/m²
- Alcohol: 2-3 glasses of wine with dinner
- Smoking: Quit 5 years ago (20 pack-years)
- Diet: Late dinners due to work schedule (often 9 PM)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal differential diagnosis</summary>

**Differential Diagnosis**:

1. **Refractory GERD** (most likely)
   - Classic symptoms
   - Extraesophageal manifestations (throat clearing, hoarseness)
   - Risk factors present

2. **Non-acid reflux**
   - Would explain persistent symptoms on PPI
   - PPI blocks acid but not volume

3. **Functional heartburn**
   - Normal acid exposure, symptoms unrelated to reflux
   - Would need pH-impedance to distinguish

4. **Achalasia**
   - Can present with "reflux" symptoms
   - Less likely without dysphagia
   - MUST rule out before surgery

5. **Barrett's esophagus with/without dysplasia**
   - Family history of esophageal cancer
   - Long-standing GERD
   - Requires endoscopic evaluation

</details>

- Confirms chronic GERD (complication)
- Cancer risk: 0.5% per year
- Requires surveillance EGD every 3-5 years
- Supports consideration of surgery (may stabilize Barrett's)

**LA Grade B Esophagitis**:
- Confirms pathologic acid exposure
- Mucosal breaks >5mm (single fold)
- Demonstrates failure of current PPI regimen
- No need for pH study to confirm GERD

**3 cm Hiatal Hernia**:
- Contributes to barrier dysfunction
- "Acid pocket" phenomenon
- Separates LES from crural diaphragm
- Surgery would include crural repair

## Part 3: Further Workup for Surgery

### Clinical Decision Point

Given the confirmed GERD with Barrett's, ongoing symptoms despite maximal medical therapy, and a documented hiatal hernia, the patient expresses interest in surgical options.

---

```

║  _______________________________________________                 ║
║  _______________________________________________                 ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**MANDATORY**: **High-Resolution Manometry (HRM)**

**Why Essential**:

1. **Rule out achalasia**
   - Can present with "reflux" symptoms
   - Fundoplication on achalasia = complete obstruction
   - Look for: IRP >15 mmHg, absent peristalsis

2. **Assess esophageal body function**
   - Guides selection of wrap type
   - Normal motility → Nissen (360°)
   - Impaired motility → Partial wrap (Toupet 270°)

3. **Medicolegal requirement**
   - Standard of care before anti-reflux surgery

</details>

---

### Manometry Results

```

• Resting pressure: 8 mmHg (low normal: 10-30)
• Integrated Relaxation Pressure (IRP): 6 mmHg (normal <15)
• LES length: 3 cm

• Peristalsis: Present in 90% of swallows
```

**1. Achalasia Ruled Out**:
- IRP = 6 mmHg (normal <15, achalasia requires >15)
- Complete LES relaxation
- Peristalsis present (achalasia = absent/spastic)

**2. IRP of 6 mmHg indicates**:
- Normal LES relaxation
- The sphincter OPENS appropriately
- Problem is LOW resting pressure, not failure to relax

**3. Appropriate Fundoplication**:
- **Nissen (360°)** is appropriate
- Peristalsis present in 90% of swallows
- DCI of 1800 is in normal range
- No significant motility disorder that would require partial wrap

*Note: If DCI <450 or <50% peristalsis → would choose Toupet (270°)*

</details>

## Part 4: Surgical Planning

### Pre-operative Discussion

You discuss the findings with the patient:

- Confirmed GERD with Barrett's esophagus (no dysplasia)
- LA Grade B esophagitis despite BID PPI
- 3 cm hiatal hernia contributing to symptoms
- Manometry shows hypotensive LES but normal relaxation and peristalsis
- Good surgical candidate

**Expected Benefits**:
- 85-90% long-term symptom control
- 70-80% chance of being off PPIs completely
- May stabilize Barrett's (prevent progression)
- Repair of hiatal hernia
- Resolution of extraesophageal symptoms likely

**Complications to Discuss**:
- **Dysphagia** (10-50% early, 3-5% persistent)
- **Gas-bloat syndrome** (inability to belch/vomit)
- **Recurrent GERD** (5-10% failure)
- **Need for redo surgery** (5-10% lifetime)
- Splenic injury (<1%), esophageal perforation (<1%)

**Long-term Expectations**:
- Diet advancement over 4-6 weeks
- Small bites, chew well initially
- May not be able to vomit
- Barrett's surveillance continues (every 3-5 years)
- Some patients need occasional PPI

</details>

### Operative Details

The patient undergoes **laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication with hiatal hernia repair**.

```
OPERATIVE REPORT SUMMARY
════════════════════════

PROCEDURE: Laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication with
           crural repair

FINDINGS:
• 3 cm sliding hiatal hernia confirmed
• No evidence of short esophagus
• Normal appearing stomach and spleen

PROCEDURE DETAILS:
• 5-port technique
• Gastrohepatic ligament divided
• Both crura identified and dissected
• Esophagus mobilized, adequate intra-abdominal length (3 cm)
• Short gastric vessels divided (tension-free wrap)
• Posterior crural closure with 3 interrupted 0-Ethibond sutures
• 360° floppy Nissen fundoplication (2.5 cm)
• 56 Fr bougie passed easily through wrap
• Wrap secured with 3 sutures incorporating anterior esophageal wall

ESTIMATED BLOOD LOSS: 25 mL
COMPLICATIONS: None
```

## Test Your Understanding

### Final Questions

1. **If the patient's IRP had been 18 mmHg with absent peristalsis, what would be the diagnosis and management?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Diagnosis**: Achalasia (IRP >15 + absent peristalsis)

**Management**:
- DO NOT perform fundoplication
- Would cause complete obstruction
- Treatment: Heller myotomy with partial fundoplication (Dor)
- Or POEM procedure

</details>

2. **If manometry showed DCI <450 with 40% failed swallows, what wrap would you choose?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Toupet (270° posterior)** or **Dor (180° anterior)**

**Rationale**:
- Impaired esophageal motility
- Cannot generate enough force to overcome 360° wrap
- Partial wrap provides reflux control with less dysphagia risk

</details>

3. **What is the surveillance protocol for this patient's Barrett's esophagus going forward?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Non-dysplastic Barrett's**: EGD every **3-5 years**

- Continue surveillance even after fundoplication
- Surgery may stabilize but does not reverse Barrett's
- If dysplasia develops: more frequent surveillance or ablation

</details>

## Clinical Pearls

> [!tip] Pearl 1: Pre-surgical Manometry
> **NEVER skip manometry before fundoplication** - achalasia and GERD can present similarly, but treatments are opposite. Fundoplication on achalasia = disaster.

> [!tip] Pearl 2: Barrett's and Surgery
> Surgery may **stabilize** Barrett's but does not reverse it. Surveillance must continue. Consider surgery in Barrett's patients to prevent progression.

> [!tip] Pearl 3: Early Dysphagia
> **Early dysphagia is normal** (10-50%). Only worry if: persists >3 months, unable to eat, weight loss, or complete obstruction.

> [!tip] Pearl 4: The "Floppy" Nissen
> Dysphagia usually results from a wrap that is too **tight** or too **long**. A proper floppy Nissen: 2-2.5 cm, fundus-to-fundus, 56-60 Fr bougie passes easily.
