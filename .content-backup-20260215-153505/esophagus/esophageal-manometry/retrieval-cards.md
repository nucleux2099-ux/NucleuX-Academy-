## Card Set

### Card 1: Gold Standard
**Q: What is the gold standard for diagnosing esophageal motility disorders?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**High-Resolution Manometry (HRM)**

**Features:**
- 36 circumferential pressure sensors
- Spaced 1 cm apart
- Spans pharynx to stomach
- Generates topographic pressure plots (color Cleland plots)
- Classified using Chicago Classification v4.0

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 2: Indications
**Q: List 5 primary indications for esophageal manometry.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

1. **Dysphagia with normal EGD** - Rule out motility disorder
2. **Pre-fundoplication** - MANDATORY to rule out achalasia
3. **Suspected achalasia** - Gold standard diagnosis
4. **Refractory GERD** - Assess LES function
5. **Non-cardiac chest pain** - Spastic disorders

**When NOT to do:**
- Active esophagitis
- Esophageal obstruction
- Recent esophageal surgery
- Severe coagulopathy

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 3: IRP Definition
**Q: Define IRP and state the cutoff value for impaired relaxation.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**IRP = Integrated Relaxation Pressure**

**Definition:** Mean of lowest 4 seconds in relaxation window after swallow

**Cutoff:**
- Normal: <15 mmHg
- **Abnormal: >15 mmHg** = Impaired LES relaxation

**Significance:**
- Most important single parameter
- Entry point for Chicago Classification
- IRP >15 → Think achalasia or EGJOO

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 4: DCI Definition
**Q: What is DCI, what are the values, and what does each range mean?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**DCI = Distal Contractile Integral**

**Definition:** Amplitude × Duration × Length of contraction

**Units:** mmHg·cm·s

| DCI Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| <100 | **Failed** (absent contraction) |
| 100-450 | **Weak** (ineffective) |
| 450-8000 | **Normal** |
| >8000 | **Hypercontractile** (Jackhammer) |

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 5: DL Definition
**Q: What is Distal Latency (DL) and what does an abnormal value indicate?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**DL = Distal Latency**

**Definition:** Time from UES relaxation to contractile deceleration point

**Values:**
- Normal: >4.5 seconds
- **Abnormal: <4.5 seconds** = Premature (spastic)

**Clinical significance:**
- DL <4.5s indicates:
  - **Type III achalasia** (spastic)
  - **Distal Esophageal Spasm (DES)**
- Distinguishes "timing problem" from "strength problem"

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 6: Achalasia Criteria
**Q: What are the two manometric criteria required to diagnose achalasia?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Both criteria must be present:**

1. **IRP >15 mmHg** (impaired LES relaxation)
2. **100% failed peristalsis** (no normal propagating contractions)

**Important:**
- If some peristalsis preserved with elevated IRP → **EGJOO, not achalasia**
- Must have BOTH features

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 7: Achalasia Type I
**Q: Describe the manometric features of Achalasia Type I (Classic).**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Type I (Classic) - 20-40% of cases**

**Features:**
- IRP >15 mmHg
- 100% failed peristalsis
- **NO esophageal pressurization**
- Aperistaltic, "quiet" esophageal body

**HRM pattern:** Flat, no activity in body

**Treatment response:** Good

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 8: Achalasia Type II
**Q: Describe Type II achalasia and why it has the best treatment outcomes.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Type II (Compression) - 50-70% of cases - BEST outcomes**

**Features:**
- IRP >15 mmHg
- 100% failed peristalsis
- **Panesophageal pressurization ≥20% swallows** (>30 mmHg)

**Why BEST outcomes (>90% success):**
- The uniform compression helps with emptying
- Even without LES relaxation, compression pushes food through
- Paradoxically, the "pan-esophageal squeeze" is beneficial

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 9: Achalasia Type III
**Q: Describe Type III achalasia and its treatment implications.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Type III (Spastic) - 10-15% of cases - WORST outcomes**

**Features:**
- IRP >15 mmHg
- 100% failed peristalsis
- **≥20% premature contractions (DL <4.5s)**
- DCI >450 (not failed, just spastic)

**Treatment implications:**
- WORST response to treatment (70-80% with Heller)
- **POEM preferred** - can do longer myotomy (15-20 cm)
- Need to address entire spastic segment
- Heller limited to ~8 cm

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 10: EGJOO
**Q: What is EGJ Outflow Obstruction and how does it differ from achalasia?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**EGJOO = EGJ Outflow Obstruction**

**Criteria:**
- IRP >15 mmHg
- **Some preserved peristalsis** (NOT 100% failed)

**Difference from achalasia:**
- Achalasia: 100% failed peristalsis
- EGJOO: Some peristalsis preserved

**Must rule out:**
- Mechanical obstruction (CT, EUS)
- May be early achalasia
- Opioid effect (functional)
- Idiopathic

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 11: Absent Contractility
**Q: What is Absent Contractility and what condition is it classically associated with?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Absent Contractility:**

**Criteria:**
- IRP **≤15 mmHg** (normal - LES relaxes fine)
- 100% failed peristalsis

**Classic association: SCLERODERMA**

**Scleroderma pattern:**
- IRP normal (LES relaxes)
- 100% failed peristalsis
- Often LOW LES pressure
- Smooth muscle fibrosis

**Key distinction from achalasia:**
IRP is NORMAL in absent contractility

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 12: DES Criteria
**Q: What are the manometric criteria for Distal Esophageal Spasm (DES)?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Distal Esophageal Spasm (DES):**

**Criteria:**
- IRP ≤15 mmHg (normal)
- **>20% premature contractions (DL <4.5s)**
- DCI >450 (not failed, present but premature)

**Clinical features:**
- Chest pain (can mimic cardiac)
- Dysphagia
- "Corkscrew esophagus" on barium

**Differs from Type III achalasia:**
- DES has normal IRP
- Type III has elevated IRP

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 13: Jackhammer Esophagus
**Q: What is Hypercontractile (Jackhammer) esophagus?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Jackhammer Esophagus (Hypercontractile):**

**Criteria:**
- IRP ≤15 mmHg (normal)
- **>20% swallows with DCI >8000 mmHg·cm·s**
- Extreme contractile vigor

**Clinical features:**
- Chest pain
- Dysphagia
- Often associated with GERD (reflex)

**Treatment:**
- Treat underlying GERD first
- Smooth muscle relaxants
- Botox injection (if refractory)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 14: IEM Criteria
**Q: What is Ineffective Esophageal Motility (IEM)?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**IEM = Ineffective Esophageal Motility**

**Criteria:**
- IRP ≤15 mmHg (normal)
- **>70% ineffective swallows** (failed DCI<100 or weak DCI 100-450)

**Clinical significance:**
- Associated with GERD
- Poor bolus clearance
- Pre-fundoplication consideration

**Surgical implication:**
- Consider **partial wrap** (Toupet/Dor) instead of Nissen
- Complete wrap + poor motility = dysphagia risk

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 15: Chicago v4.0 First Step
**Q: What is the FIRST step in the Chicago Classification v4.0 algorithm?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**STEP 1: Assess IRP (LES relaxation)**

```
IRP >15 mmHg → EGJ OUTFLOW DISORDERS
              │
              ├── 100% failed → ACHALASIA (I, II, III)
              └── Some preserved → EGJOO

IRP ≤15 mmHg → Proceed to Step 2 (assess peristalsis)
```

**Key point:** IRP is the entry point - it determines which branch of the algorithm you follow

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 16: Pre-Fundoplication Manometry
**Q: Why is manometry MANDATORY before fundoplication and how do findings affect surgical decision?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Reasons:**
1. **Rule out achalasia** - Would need myotomy, not fundoplication!
2. **Assess peristalsis** - Determines wrap type
3. **Document baseline** - For comparison if symptoms recur

**Surgical implications:**

| Finding | Wrap Type |
|---------|-----------|
| Normal motility | Nissen (360°) OK |
| IEM (>70% ineffective) | Partial wrap (Toupet/Dor) |
| Absent contractility | Very partial, proceed with caution |
| **Achalasia** | DO NOT DO FUNDO - needs myotomy! |

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 17: Type II Best Outcomes Paradox
**Q: Why does Type II achalasia have the best treatment outcomes despite having panesophageal pressurization?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Paradox explanation:**

Type II has **panesophageal pressurization** (uniform compression)

**Why this HELPS:**
- The esophagus acts like a "pump"
- Uniform pressure compresses the bolus
- Even with non-relaxing LES, compression pushes food through
- Better emptying than Type I (no pressure) or Type III (chaotic spastic)

**Treatment success:**
- Type II: >90%
- Type I: 85-90%
- Type III: 70-80% (needs longer myotomy)

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 18: EndoFLIP
**Q: What is EndoFLIP and how is it used in achalasia?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**EndoFLIP = Functional Lumen Imaging Probe**

**What it measures:**
- GEJ distensibility
- Cross-sectional area at different pressures
- Distensibility Index (DI) = CSA / pressure

**Normal DI:** >2.8 mm²/mmHg
**Impaired:** <2.8 mm²/mmHg

**Uses:**
1. **Intraoperative** - Assess myotomy adequacy during Heller/POEM
2. **Diagnosis** - Supplement to manometry
3. **Follow-up** - Assess treatment response

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

---

### Card 19: Timed Barium Esophagram
**Q: What is Timed Barium Esophagram (TBE) and when is it used?**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**TBE = Timed Barium Esophagram**

**Technique:**
- Patient drinks barium
- Images at 1, 2, and 5 minutes
- Measures column height of retained barium

**What it assesses:**
- Esophageal emptying
- Functional obstruction

**Uses:**
- **Follow-up after achalasia treatment**
- Assess treatment response
- Compare before/after myotomy/POEM
- Objective measure of symptom improvement

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High

### Card 20: HRM Procedure
**Q: Describe the standard HRM procedure protocol.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>

**Preparation:**
- NPO 6 hours
- Stop prokinetics, PPIs (if assessing LES)

**Procedure:**
1. Transnasal catheter insertion
2. Position tip in stomach
3. **Baseline recording:** 30 seconds at rest (LES pressure)
4. **Test swallows:** 10 wet swallows (5 mL water)
5. 30 seconds between swallows
6. Software analysis generates topographic plots

**What's assessed:**
- LES basal pressure
- IRP (relaxation)
- Peristaltic function (DCI, DL)
- UES function

</details>

Confidence: ⬜ Low | ⬜ Medium | ⬜ High
