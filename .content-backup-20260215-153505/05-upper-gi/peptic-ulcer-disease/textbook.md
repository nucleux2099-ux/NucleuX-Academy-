## 1. History — From "Acid Disease" to Bacterial Revolution

Few diseases have undergone as dramatic a paradigm shift as peptic ulcer disease (PUD). For over a century, surgeons bore the burden of treating what turned out to be, in most cases, a curable infection.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1586 | **Marcello Donati** — First autopsy description of a gastric ulcer |
| 1857 | **Brinton** — Classic monograph on gastric ulcers; first systematic clinical description |
| 1881 | **Billroth** — First successful gastrectomy for gastric cancer (later adapted for ulcer disease) |
| 1884 | **Billroth** — Introduces Billroth I (gastroduodenostomy) and Billroth II (gastrojejunostomy) reconstructions |
| 1943 | **Dragstedt** — Introduces truncal vagotomy for duodenal ulcer, based on the neural phase of acid secretion |
| 1957 | **Griffith & Harkins** — Selective vagotomy (preserving hepatic and celiac branches) |
| 1970 | **Johnston & Amdrup** — Highly selective vagotomy (parietal cell vagotomy), independently described |
| 1976 | **Black et al.** — Development of cimetidine (first H₂ receptor antagonist) — Nobel Prize 1988 |
| 1982 | **Marshall & Warren** — Discovery of *Helicobacter pylori*; self-experimentation by Marshall — Nobel Prize 2005 |
| 1989 | **Omeprazole** — First proton pump inhibitor (PPI) approved |

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.18; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49*

The discovery of *H. pylori* by Marshall and Warren in Perth, Australia, is one of the great stories in medical history. Marshall, frustrated by skepticism, drank a broth of cultured bacteria, developed gastritis, and proved Koch's postulates. The subsequent shift from surgical to medical management of PUD stands as one of the most transformative changes in the history of surgery — vagotomy, once a core general surgery operation, has essentially vanished from practice.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology

### Gastric Anatomy Relevant to PUD

🟢 **Regions of the stomach:**
- **Cardia** — Surrounds the esophagogastric junction; mucus-secreting glands
- **Fundus** — Superior, dome-shaped; contains parietal and chief cells
- **Body (corpus)** — Major acid-secreting region; oxyntic (parietal) cells concentrated here
- **Antrum** — Distal stomach; G-cells (gastrin) and D-cells (somatostatin)
- **Pylorus** — Muscular sphincter; regulates gastric emptying

🟢 **Blood supply:**
- **Lesser curvature:** Left gastric artery (from celiac trunk) and right gastric artery (from proper hepatic) — ulcers on the lesser curvature or posterior duodenum can erode into the left gastric or gastroduodenal artery
- **Greater curvature:** Left gastroepiploic (from splenic) and right gastroepiploic (from GDA)
- **Gastroduodenal artery (GDA):** Runs behind the first part of the duodenum — **posterior duodenal ulcers classically erode into the GDA**, causing massive hemorrhage

🟢 **Innervation:**
- **Vagus nerves:** Left (anterior) and right (posterior) vagal trunks
- **"LARP" mnemonic:** Left vagus = Anterior; Right vagus = Posterior
- Anterior trunk → hepatic branch (to liver/gallbladder) + anterior nerve of Latarjet (along lesser curvature)
- Posterior trunk → celiac branch (to celiac plexus) + posterior nerve of Latarjet
- **Criminal nerve of Grassi:** The first branch of the posterior vagal trunk to the fundus — failure to divide this during vagotomy is a cause of recurrent ulceration

🔵 **Duodenal anatomy:**
- First part (D1, ~5 cm) — Most common site of duodenal ulcers, especially the anterior and posterior walls of the duodenal bulb
- The anterior wall perforates freely into the peritoneal cavity
- The posterior wall erodes into the GDA or pancreas (bleeding > perforation)

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.46*

### Physiology of Acid Secretion

🟢 **Three phases of acid secretion:**

| Phase | Stimulus | Mediator | Contribution |
|-------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Cephalic** | Sight, smell, thought of food | Vagus → acetylcholine | ~30% |
| **Gastric** | Gastric distension, peptides | Gastrin (from G-cells) | ~60% |
| **Intestinal** | Amino acids in duodenum | Entero-oxyntin (minor) | ~10% |

🟢 **Parietal cell stimulation** — three pathways converge on the H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump):
1. **Acetylcholine** (vagal, M₃ receptor)
2. **Gastrin** (CCK-B receptor)
3. **Histamine** (ECL cells, H₂ receptor) — the final common pathway; amplifies responses from both ACh and gastrin

🟢 **Defensive mechanisms (mucosal barrier):**
- Mucus-bicarbonate layer (unstirred layer)
- Surface epithelial cell integrity (tight junctions, rapid restitution)
- Mucosal blood flow (washes away back-diffused H⁺)
- Prostaglandins (PGE₂ — stimulate mucus, bicarbonate, blood flow; inhibit acid)
- Epithelial renewal (every 3–5 days)

🔵 **PUD = imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and defensive mechanisms.** Duodenal ulcers are predominantly acid-driven (hyperacidity); gastric ulcers more often reflect mucosal defense failure.

## 3. Pathophysiology — Acid, Bacteria, and NSAIDs

### The Dictum: "No Acid, No Ulcer" (Schwarz, 1910)

🟢 While oversimplified, this principle remains foundational. Peptic ulcers do not develop without acid exposure — they are not found in achlorhydric patients.

### *Helicobacter pylori*

🟢 **Key facts:**
- Gram-negative, spiral, microaerophilic, flagellated bacterium
- Infects ~50% of the world's population (higher in developing countries)
- Present in **90–95% of duodenal ulcers** and **70–80% of gastric ulcers**
- Produces **urease** → converts urea to ammonia + CO₂ → creates alkaline microenvironment → protects itself from acid
- Also produces CagA and VacA virulence factors → mucosal inflammation and injury

🔵 **Pathogenic mechanisms:**
- **Duodenal ulcer:** H. pylori antral gastritis → increased gastrin release → acid hypersecretion → acid injury to duodenal bulb where gastric metaplasia develops → H. pylori colonizes metaplastic epithelium → duodenitis → ulceration
- **Gastric ulcer:** Pangastritis or corpus-predominant gastritis → mucosal atrophy → weakened mucosal defense → ulceration despite normal/low acid

🟢 **Diagnosis of H. pylori:**

| Test | Type | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|------|------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| **Urea breath test** | Non-invasive | 95% | 95% | Gold standard non-invasive; test of cure |
| **Stool antigen** | Non-invasive | 95% | 95% | Comparable to UBT |
| **Serology (IgG)** | Non-invasive | 85% | 79% | Cannot distinguish active vs past; NOT for test of cure |
| **Rapid urease test (CLO)** | Invasive | 90% | 95% | At endoscopy; from antral biopsy |
| **Histology** | Invasive | >95% | >95% | Gold standard; Giemsa or immunohistochemistry |
| **Culture** | Invasive | 70–80% | 100% | For antibiotic sensitivity; not routine |

🔵 **Important:** PPIs must be stopped 2 weeks before and antibiotics 4 weeks before testing (except serology) to avoid false negatives.

### NSAIDs

🟢 **The second great cause of PUD:**
- NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 → decrease prostaglandin synthesis → reduce mucus, bicarbonate, mucosal blood flow
- Risk is dose-dependent and increases with duration of use
- NSAIDs cause both gastric and duodenal ulcers (gastric > duodenal)
- Annual risk of clinically significant ulcer complications in chronic NSAID users: **1–4%**
- **Aspirin** is the most common offender globally

🔵 **Risk factors for NSAID ulceration:**
- Age >60, prior ulcer history, concurrent corticosteroids or anticoagulants, dual antiplatelet therapy, *H. pylori* co-infection, high-dose NSAID use

🔴 **SS Pearl:** *H. pylori* and NSAIDs are **synergistic** risk factors — together they increase ulcer risk 6-fold compared to either alone. Current guidelines recommend testing and eradicating *H. pylori* before starting long-term NSAID therapy.

### Other Causes

🔵 **Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (ZES):**
- Gastrin-secreting tumor (gastrinoma), usually in the "gastrinoma triangle" (junction of cystic duct, D2-D3, and neck/body of pancreas)
- Suspect when: multiple ulcers, unusual locations (distal duodenum, jejunum), refractory to standard PPI therapy, diarrhea, MEN-1 association (20–30%)
- Diagnosis: Fasting serum gastrin >1000 pg/mL with gastric pH <2 is virtually diagnostic; secretin stimulation test for equivocal cases
- ~60% are malignant

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.18*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Uncomplicated PUD

🟢 **Classic symptoms:**

| Feature | Duodenal Ulcer | Gastric Ulcer |
|---------|---------------|--------------|
| **Pain location** | Epigastric | Epigastric |
| **Timing** | 2–3 hours after meals; nocturnal (midnight–3 AM) | Within 30 min of eating |
| **Relation to food** | Relieved by food/antacids ("hunger pain") | Aggravated by food |
| **Weight** | Maintained or increased (eats to relieve pain) | Weight loss (food avoidance) |
| **Periodicity** | Seasonal exacerbations (spring/autumn) | Less predictable |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Less common | More common |

🟢 **Important caveat:** Up to **70% of NSAID-related ulcers** are asymptomatic — patients may present with a complication (bleeding, perforation) as the first manifestation.

🔵 **Physical examination** in uncomplicated PUD is often unremarkable — mild epigastric tenderness at most. Significant physical findings suggest complications.

### Complications of PUD

🟢 **The four major complications:**

1. **Hemorrhage** — Most common complication (~15–20% of ulcer patients); posterior DU eroding into GDA
2. **Perforation** — Second most common (~5–10%); anterior DU perforating freely
3. **Gastric outlet obstruction (GOO)** — Due to edema, spasm, fibrosis, or combination (~2%)
4. **Penetration** — Ulcer erodes into adjacent organ (pancreas, liver, omentum) without free perforation

## 5. Diagnosis

### Upper GI Endoscopy (EGD)

🟢 **Gold standard** for diagnosis:
- Allows direct visualization, biopsy, and therapeutic intervention
- **All gastric ulcers must be biopsied** (4–6 biopsies from ulcer margin) — to exclude malignancy (5–10% of gastric ulcers are malignant)
- **Duodenal ulcers** do not routinely require biopsy (duodenal cancer is exceedingly rare)
- Biopsy from antrum for H. pylori (rapid urease test + histology)

🔵 **Endoscopic features suggesting malignancy:**
- Irregular, heaped-up margins
- Nodularity within the ulcer crater
- Absent mucosal folds converging on the ulcer
- Location on greater curvature (higher malignancy risk)

🟢 **Follow-up endoscopy** is recommended for gastric ulcers at 8–12 weeks to confirm healing and re-biopsy if not healed — persistent ulceration despite adequate therapy mandates malignancy exclusion.

### Imaging

🟢 **CT abdomen** — Not for diagnosing uncomplicated PUD, but critical in complications:
- **Perforation:** Pneumoperitoneum (free air under diaphragm on erect CXR; CT is more sensitive)
- **GOO:** Dilated stomach with transition point at pylorus/duodenal bulb
- **Bleeding:** CT angiography for localization when endoscopy fails

🔵 **Barium meal** — Largely superseded by endoscopy; may show ulcer crater, niche sign, edematous folds. Historical significance: Hampton's line (thin lucent line = benign), Carman meniscus sign (concave toward lumen = malignant).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.47*

## 6. Classification

### Johnson Classification of Gastric Ulcers

🟢 **The most important classification for surgical decision-making:**

| Type | Location | Acid Level | Association | Surgical Approach |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **I** | Lesser curvature (body) | Normal/Low | Mucosal defense failure | Distal gastrectomy (Billroth I) |
| **II** | Body + duodenal ulcer | High | Acid hypersecretion | Distal gastrectomy + vagotomy |
| **III** | Prepyloric (<3 cm from pylorus) | High | Behaves like DU | Distal gastrectomy + vagotomy |
| **IV** | High on lesser curvature (near GEJ) | Normal/Low | Mucosal defense failure | Subtotal gastrectomy (Csendes procedure) |
| **V** | Anywhere (medication-induced) | Variable | NSAIDs | Medical management |

🔵 **Clinical importance:** Types II and III are associated with acid hypersecretion and require a vagotomy in addition to resection if surgery is needed. Types I and IV have normal or low acid — resection alone suffices.

### Forrest Classification (Bleeding Ulcers)

🔵 **Used to stratify rebleeding risk and guide endoscopic therapy:**

| Class | Description | Rebleed Risk | Endoscopic Rx |
|-------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Ia** | Spurting hemorrhage | 90% | Yes |
| **Ib** | Oozing hemorrhage | 50% | Yes |
| **IIa** | Visible vessel (non-bleeding) | 43% | Yes |
| **IIb** | Adherent clot | 22% | Controversial (lift/irrigate → treat if vessel underneath) |
| **IIc** | Flat pigmented spot | 10% | No |
| **III** | Clean base | 5% | No |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.18*

## 7. Treatment

### Medical Management

🟢 **The mainstay of modern PUD treatment:**

**A. H. pylori eradication (when positive):**
- First-line: **PPI-based triple therapy** × 14 days
  - PPI (standard dose BID) + Clarithromycin 500 mg BID + Amoxicillin 1 g BID
  - Or: PPI + Clarithromycin + Metronidazole (if penicillin-allergic)
- 🔵 **Bismuth quadruple therapy** (in areas of high clarithromycin resistance >15%):
  - PPI + Bismuth subsalicylate + Metronidazole + Tetracycline × 14 days
- **Confirm eradication** with urea breath test or stool antigen ≥4 weeks after completing antibiotics and ≥2 weeks off PPI

**B. Acid suppression:**
- 🟢 **PPIs** are the drugs of choice: Omeprazole 20 mg, Pantoprazole 40 mg, etc.
  - DU: 4 weeks; GU: 8 weeks
- 🟢 **H₂ receptor antagonists** (ranitidine, famotidine): Less potent; second-line
- 🔵 **Sucralfate:** Mucosal protectant; binds to ulcer base in acidic pH; useful adjunct

**C. NSAID-related ulcers:**
- Discontinue NSAID if possible
- PPI therapy for 8 weeks
- If NSAID must be continued: PPI co-therapy + switch to COX-2 selective inhibitor
- Test and treat *H. pylori*

### Surgical Management — Indications

🟢 **Surgery for PUD has become rare but remains critical for complications:**

**Absolute indications:**
1. **Perforation** (most common surgical indication today)
2. **Hemorrhage** uncontrolled by endoscopic/radiological means
3. **Gastric outlet obstruction** refractory to medical/endoscopic therapy
4. **Suspected malignancy** (non-healing gastric ulcer despite adequate therapy)

**Relative indications:**
- Recurrent ulceration despite optimal medical therapy
- Non-compliance with medical therapy
- 🔵 Giant ulcers (>2 cm DU, >3 cm GU) — slower healing, higher complication rates

### Perforated Peptic Ulcer

🟢 **The most common indication for emergency surgery in PUD today.**

**Epidemiology:**
- Incidence: 3–14 per 100,000/year
- Anterior DU is the most common site of perforation
- Mortality: 5–25% (higher in elderly, delayed presentation, comorbidities)
- **Pneumoperitoneum** is present in 75–80% on erect CXR

🟢 **Clinical presentation — the classic triad:**
1. **Sudden-onset severe epigastric pain** ("like a knife/thunderclap") — the most dramatic presentation in abdominal surgery
2. **Board-like rigidity** (generalized peritonitis from chemical peritonitis → bacterial peritonitis)
3. **Free air on imaging**

🟢 **Three-stage progression (Borrmann):**
1. **Chemical peritonitis** (0–2 hours) — Pain, shock, board-like rigidity
2. **Intermediate stage** (2–12 hours) — Pain may temporarily improve ("period of relief" — dangerous, may falsely reassure clinician)
3. **Bacterial peritonitis** (>12 hours) — Worsening sepsis, abdominal distension, multi-organ dysfunction

🔵 **Boey score** — predicts mortality after perforated peptic ulcer:

| Factor | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Major medical illness | 1 |
| Preoperative shock (SBP <90) | 1 |
| Duration of perforation >24 hours | 1 |

- Score 0: ~1% mortality; Score 1: ~10%; Score 2: ~45%; Score 3: ~100%

🟢 **Graham patch (omental plug repair):**
- The workhorse operation for perforated DU
- Technique: Three full-thickness sutures placed across the perforation; a tongue of omentum is drawn over the defect and the sutures tied — the omentum seals the perforation
- NOT a "closure" — the sutures hold the omentum in place; the omentum provides the seal
- Can be performed open or laparoscopically

🔵 **Laparoscopic repair:**
- Increasingly the approach of choice in experienced centers
- Same principles as open Graham patch
- Advantages: less postoperative pain, shorter stay, better cosmesis
- Contraindications: hemodynamic instability, large perforation (>2 cm), posterior perforation, lack of expertise
- 🔴 The LAMA trial and DULOP trial showed equivalent outcomes to open repair with shorter recovery

🔵 **Taylor's method (conservative/non-operative management):**
- NPO + NGT + IV antibiotics + IV PPI + serial clinical reassessment
- Water-soluble contrast study to confirm sealed perforation
- Strict criteria: sealed perforation, improving clinically, no gastric ulcer (cannot exclude malignancy)
- Success rate ~70–80% in selected patients; 15–25% require conversion to surgery
- 🔴 Generally reserved for poor operative candidates or sealed perforations; not standard of care

🔴 **SS Pearl — Definitive surgery for perforation:**
In the pre-PPI era, perforated DU was often treated with definitive acid-reducing surgery (vagotomy + drainage or vagotomy + antrectomy) at the time of emergency laparotomy. Today, with reliable H. pylori eradication and PPIs, **simple omental patch + postoperative PPI + H. pylori eradication** is standard. Definitive ulcer surgery at the time of perforation is reserved for rare circumstances: known refractory ulcer disease, inability to take medications, or geographic inaccessibility to follow-up.

🔵 **Surgical technique for bleeding posterior DU:**
- Longitudinal duodenotomy across pylorus
- Three-point suture ligation of the GDA: proximal, distal, and transverse pancreatic branch
- **U-stitch** through the ulcer base to control the vessel
- The duodenotomy is then closed as a pyloroplasty (Heineke-Mikulicz)

🔵 **Rockall score** — post-endoscopy rebleeding and mortality prediction (incorporates age, shock, comorbidity, endoscopic diagnosis, stigmata of hemorrhage).

### Gastric Outlet Obstruction

🔵 **Etiology:** Acute (edema/spasm around active ulcer — reversible) or chronic (fibrotic scarring — irreversible).

🟢 **Classic presentation:**
- Non-bilious projectile vomiting of undigested food eaten hours/days earlier
- Succession splash on examination
- **Metabolic alkalosis:** Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis (loss of HCl in vomitus → kidneys retain H⁺/excrete K⁺ → paradoxical aciduria)

🟢 **Management:**
- **Initial:** NPO, NGT decompression, IV fluids (correct metabolic derangements), IV PPI
- **Assess reversibility:** Medical therapy for 48–72 hours
  - If relieved → continue medical management
  - If persistent → endoscopic balloon dilation (success 76–85%) or surgery
- **Surgery:** Vagotomy + antrectomy (most effective) or vagotomy + gastrojejunostomy (drainage procedure)

🔵 **Endoscopic balloon dilation** (TTS balloon, 12–20 mm): Graded dilations; ~50% require repeat sessions; perforation risk 3–5%.

## 8. Surgical Procedures for PUD — A Historical and Practical Guide

🔵 **Though rarely performed today, knowledge of ulcer operations remains essential for PG exams and managing complications.**

### Vagotomy Types

| Procedure | What Is Divided | Drainage Needed? | Ulcer Recurrence | Side Effects |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Truncal vagotomy** | Both vagal trunks at esophageal hiatus | Yes (pyloroplasty or gastrojejunostomy) | 10–15% | Dumping, diarrhea (20–30%) |
| **Selective vagotomy** | Gastric branches only (preserves hepatic + celiac) | Yes | 5–10% | Less diarrhea than truncal |
| **Highly selective (parietal cell)** | Only branches to parietal cell mass (preserves antral innervation + crow's foot) | No | 10–15% | Minimal (<5% dumping/diarrhea) |

🟢 **Pyloroplasty types:**
- **Heineke-Mikulicz:** Longitudinal incision across pylorus → transverse closure (most common)
- **Finney:** Inverted U-shaped gastroduodenostomy (for scarred/large ulcers)
- **Jaboulay:** Side-to-side gastroduodenostomy (for very large or posterior ulcers; does not open the ulcer)

### Gastrectomy and Reconstruction

🟢 **Billroth I** (gastroduodenostomy): Distal gastrectomy with direct gastroduodenal anastomosis. More physiological (duodenal passage maintained). Preferred for Type I gastric ulcers.

🟢 **Billroth II** (gastrojejunostomy): Distal gastrectomy with closure of duodenal stump and gastrojejunal anastomosis. Used when Billroth I is not technically feasible (short duodenum, scarring).

🔵 **Roux-en-Y gastrojejunostomy:** Reduces bile reflux; preferred reconstruction after gastrectomy in modern practice.

## 9. Complications of Surgery (Post-Gastrectomy Syndromes)

🔵 **Essential PG knowledge — these remain examinable though operations are rare:**

| Syndrome | Mechanism | Presentation | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Dumping (early)** | Rapid hyperosmolar load into small bowel → fluid shift | 15–30 min postprandial: nausea, cramping, diarrhea, vasomotor symptoms (flushing, tachycardia, diaphoresis) | Dietary: small, frequent, low-carb meals; lie down after eating; octreotide for refractory |
| **Dumping (late)** | Reactive hypoglycemia (rapid glucose absorption → insulin surge) | 1–3 hours postprandial: diaphoresis, tremor, confusion | Dietary modification; acarbose |
| **Alkaline reflux gastritis** | Bile reflux into gastric remnant | Constant epigastric burning, bilious vomiting, NOT relieved by antacids | Roux-en-Y conversion (most effective) |
| **Afferent loop syndrome** | Partial obstruction of afferent limb (Billroth II) | Postprandial bloating → sudden projectile bilious vomiting (relieves pain) | Roux-en-Y conversion |
| **Efferent loop syndrome** | Obstruction of efferent limb | Bilious vomiting that does NOT relieve pain | Adhesiolysis or revision |
| **Marginal (stomal) ulcer** | Acid exposure at anastomosis | Recurrent ulcer symptoms | PPIs; R/O retained antrum, incomplete vagotomy, ZES |
| **Gastric remnant carcinoma** | Chronic bile reflux → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia | >15 years post-gastrectomy | Surveillance endoscopy; completion gastrectomy |

🔴 **SS Pearl — Retained antrum syndrome:** After Billroth II, if antral mucosa is inadvertently left on the duodenal stump, it is bathed in alkaline duodenal secretions → continuous gastrin release (no acid feedback inhibition) → severe recurrent ulceration. Diagnosis: technetium-99m pertechnetate scan. Treatment: excision of the retained antral mucosa.

## 10. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **PUD = imbalance** between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and mucosal defense mechanisms.
2. **H. pylori** causes 90–95% of DU and 70–80% of GU. **NSAIDs** are the second major cause.
3. **"No acid, no ulcer"** — Schwarz's dictum remains valid. PUD does not occur without acid.
4. **DU pain** is relieved by food; **GU pain** is worsened by food.
5. **All gastric ulcers must be biopsied** — 5–10% harbor malignancy.
6. **Johnson classification:** Types II and III have high acid → need vagotomy if operated. Types I and IV have normal/low acid.
7. **Perforated DU:** Sudden epigastric pain + board-like rigidity + pneumoperitoneum → **Graham omental patch** is the standard operation.
8. **Posterior DU bleeds** (GDA erosion); **anterior DU perforates.**
9. **GOO** causes hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis with paradoxical aciduria.
10. **H. pylori eradication + PPI** has virtually eliminated elective ulcer surgery.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

11. **Forrest classification** guides endoscopic therapy for bleeding ulcers — Ia/Ib/IIa require intervention.
12. **Boey score** predicts mortality in perforated PUD (medical illness, shock, delay >24h).
13. **Laparoscopic repair** of perforated DU is equivalent to open in experienced hands.
14. **Bleeding DU surgery:** Duodenotomy + three-point GDA ligation + vagotomy + pyloroplasty.
15. **Post-gastrectomy syndromes** (dumping, afferent loop, alkaline reflux) — know mechanisms and treatments.
16. **Highly selective vagotomy** has fewest side effects but highest recurrence.
17. **ZES:** Suspect with multiple/refractory ulcers; fasting gastrin >1000 with pH <2.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

18. **H. pylori + NSAID synergy:** 6× ulcer risk — eradicate before long-term NSAID therapy.
19. **Retained antrum syndrome:** Continuous gastrin release from alkaline-bathed antral remnant post-Billroth II → refractory ulceration.
20. **Taylor's method** (conservative management of perforation) — 70–80% success in selected patients but not standard of care.
21. **Definitive ulcer surgery at time of perforation** is now reserved for exceptional circumstances given reliable PPI + H. pylori eradication.
22. **Gastric remnant carcinoma** develops >15 years after gastrectomy — long-term surveillance indicated.
