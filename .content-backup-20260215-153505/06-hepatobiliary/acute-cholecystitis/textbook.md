## 1. History — From Bilious Temperaments to Surgical Innovation

The gallbladder has occupied a unique place in surgical history. Ancient civilizations attributed temperament to bile — the Hippocratic "choleric" personality stemmed from yellow bile (chole). Yet the surgical treatment of gallbladder disease is surprisingly modern.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1743 | **Jean-Louis Petit** — First reported drainage of a gallbladder abscess |
| 1867 | **John Stough Bobbs** — First planned cholecystostomy (Indianapolis); patient survived |
| 1882 | **Carl Langenbuch** — First successful cholecystectomy (Berlin); established that the gallbladder should be removed, not merely drained ("the gallbladder must be removed not because it contains stones, but because it forms them") |
| 1932 | **Graham & Cole** — Developed oral cholecystography (for which Graham received the AMA Distinguished Service Award) |
| 1985 | **Erich Mühe** — First laparoscopic cholecystectomy (Böblingen, Germany); initially ridiculed by peers |
| 1987 | **Philippe Mouret** — Performed the procedure that popularized laparoscopic cholecystectomy worldwide (Lyon, France) |
| 2018 | **Tokyo Guidelines (TG18)** — Established the current global standard for severity grading and management |

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.55*

Langenbuch's dictum remains one of the most quoted aphorisms in surgery — and his insight that the diseased organ, not merely its contents, must be addressed presaged the modern understanding of gallbladder dysmotility, chronic inflammation, and carcinogenesis.

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

🟢 **Embryology:** The gallbladder, cystic duct, and extrahepatic biliary tree arise from the **hepatic diverticulum** (an outgrowth of the ventral foregut endoderm) during the **4th week** of embryonic development. The gallbladder bud initially forms as a solid cord of cells that subsequently canalizes by the **12th week**. The hepatic diverticulum also gives rise to the liver parenchyma and the ventral pancreatic bud.

🟢 **Key anatomical facts:**

- **Location:** Fossa on the inferior surface of the liver at the junction of the right and left hepatic lobes (Cantlie's line)
- **Size:** 7–10 cm long, capacity 30–50 mL (can distend to >300 mL in obstruction)
- **Parts:** Fundus (projects beyond the liver edge at the tip of the 9th costal cartilage), body, infundibulum (Hartmann's pouch), and neck
- **Blood supply:** Cystic artery — classically a branch of the **right hepatic artery** (found within Calot's triangle)
- **Venous drainage:** Small veins from the gallbladder bed drain directly into the liver (segments IV and V); the cystic vein drains the free peritoneal surface
- **Nerve supply:** Sympathetic via the celiac plexus (T7–T9); parasympathetic via the vagus; referred pain to the right shoulder/scapula (C3–C5 via phrenic nerve)
- **Lymphatic drainage:** Cystic duct node (Lund's node) → hepatic nodes → celiac nodes

🔵 **Calot's Triangle (Hepatobiliary Triangle):**

| Border | Structure |
|--------|-----------|
| Superior | Inferior surface of the liver (segment V) |
| Medial | Common hepatic duct |
| Inferior | Cystic duct |

The cystic artery, right hepatic artery (in some variants), and the cystic duct node of Lund are found within this triangle. **Calot originally described the triangle bordered by the cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and cystic artery** — the modern definition replaces the cystic artery with the liver edge.

🔵 **Anatomical variants — the surgeon's minefield:**

| Variant | Frequency | Surgical Risk |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Short cystic duct (<5 mm) or absent | 1–2% | CBD injury during division |
| Cystic duct joining right hepatic duct | 1–2% | CBD injury if mistaken |
| Replaced right hepatic artery (from SMA) | 15–20% | Runs behind or through Calot's triangle |
| Accessory hepatic ducts (ducts of Luschka) | 2–5% | Post-cholecystectomy bile leak |
| Low medial insertion of cystic duct | 5–10% | CBD injury; parallel course |
| Double cystic artery | 15–25% | Bleeding if one missed |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **Critical View of Safety (CVS)**, described by Strasberg in 1995, was developed specifically to prevent bile duct injuries caused by these variants. It requires: (1) clearance of Calot's triangle of fat and fibrous tissue, (2) the lower third of the gallbladder separated from the liver (cystic plate), and (3) only two structures entering the gallbladder. This must be achieved **before any structure is divided**.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.1–2; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.57; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.92*

## 3. Pathophysiology

### The Classical Sequence

🟢 **Acute cholecystitis is an inflammatory condition of the gallbladder, most commonly triggered by obstruction of the cystic duct by a gallstone.**

```
Gallstone impacts in cystic duct / Hartmann's pouch
    → Gallbladder distension
    → ↑ Intraluminal pressure (>30 cmH₂O)
    → Mucosal ischemia + prostaglandin release
    → Chemical inflammation (bile salts, lysolecithin,
       phospholipase A₂)
    → Bacterial colonization (secondary, in ~50%)
    → Suppuration → Empyema
    → Wall necrosis → Gangrenous cholecystitis
    → Perforation (localized or free)
```

🟢 **Causative agents (calculous cholecystitis — 90–95% of cases):**
- Gallstones are present in **90–95%** of acute cholecystitis cases
- The stone obstructs the cystic duct, triggering the cascade above
- **Not all gallstones cause cholecystitis** — 80% of gallstone carriers remain asymptomatic throughout life

🔵 **The inflammatory cascade in detail:**
1. **Mechanical:** Distension → venous and lymphatic congestion → edema
2. **Chemical:** Trapped bile is hydrolyzed by phospholipase A₂ into lysolecithin and other toxic mediators → direct mucosal injury
3. **Bacterial:** Secondary infection occurs in **50–75%** of cases; organisms isolated include *E. coli* (most common), *Klebsiella*, *Enterococcus*, and *Bacteroides*. Bacteria arrive via hematogenous, lymphatic, or ascending routes.
4. **Prostaglandins:** PGE₁ and PGI₂ mediate mucosal inflammation; this is the rationale for indomethacin (NSAID) in biliary colic and early cholecystitis

### Acalculous Cholecystitis

🔵 **Accounts for 5–10% of acute cholecystitis** — but carries **much higher morbidity and mortality** (up to 30–50%).

**Risk factors and mechanisms:**
- **Critical illness:** ICU patients, post-major surgery, trauma, burns, TPN
- **Mechanism:** Gallbladder stasis + ischemia → mucosal injury → inflammation without stone impaction
- **Additional factors:** Positive pressure ventilation, opioid use, splanchnic hypoperfusion
- **Other associations:** HIV/AIDS (CMV, Cryptosporidium cholecystitis), diabetes, atherosclerosis

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Acalculous cholecystitis in the ICU patient is a diagnostic and therapeutic emergency. The classic clinical signs are often masked by sedation, mechanical ventilation, and multiorgan dysfunction. A high index of suspicion is critical. Bedside percutaneous cholecystostomy is often the initial treatment in unstable patients.

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.57; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.92*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### The Classic Presentation

🟢 **Distinguish biliary colic from acute cholecystitis — this is fundamental:**

| Feature | Biliary Colic | Acute Cholecystitis |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Duration** | <6 hours (resolves as stone disimpacts) | >6 hours, persistent |
| **Fever** | Absent | Present (low-grade initially) |
| **Murphy's sign** | Negative | Positive |
| **WBC** | Normal | Elevated |
| **Peritonitis** | Absent | RUQ guarding, rebound |

🟢 **Classic symptoms:**
- **RUQ pain** — constant, severe, radiating to the right shoulder or infrascapular area (referred via phrenic nerve, C3–C5)
- Often precipitated by a **fatty meal** (CCK release → gallbladder contraction against obstructing stone)
- **Nausea and vomiting** — nearly universal
- **Fever** — typically 37.5–38.5°C; high fever (>39°C) suggests empyema, gangrene, or perforation
- **Anorexia**

🟢 **Physical examination:**
- **Murphy's sign:** Inspiratory arrest during deep palpation of the RUQ — the single most useful bedside sign
  - Sensitivity: ~65%; Specificity: ~87% when positive
  - 🔵 **Sonographic Murphy's sign** (tenderness with ultrasound probe pressure over the gallbladder) has higher sensitivity (~86%) and specificity (~35%)
- **RUQ tenderness** with voluntary/involuntary guarding
- Palpable gallbladder in ~25–33% (more common in empyema or mucocele)
- 🔵 **Boas' sign:** Hyperesthesia below the right scapula — historically described but rarely elicited clinically

### Special Populations

🟢 **Elderly (>65 years):**
- Diminished pain perception and blunted inflammatory response
- May present with vague malaise, confusion, or sepsis
- Gangrenous cholecystitis is more common (up to 30%) — often without proportional clinical signs
- Higher incidence of acalculous cholecystitis
- Mortality: 5–10% (vs. <1% in younger patients)

🟢 **Diabetics:**
- Higher risk of gangrenous cholecystitis (possibly due to microangiopathy)
- Gas-forming organisms → **emphysematous cholecystitis** — a surgical emergency
- Empyema more common

🔵 **Pregnancy:**
- Second most common nonobstetric surgical emergency (after appendicitis)
- Biliary colic and acute cholecystitis are both common due to progesterone-mediated gallbladder hypomotility and estrogen-induced cholesterol supersaturation
- Laparoscopic cholecystectomy safe in all trimesters (second trimester traditionally preferred)

🔴 **SS Pearl — Emphysematous Cholecystitis:**
- Gas-forming organisms (*Clostridium*, *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*) infect a poorly perfused gallbladder wall
- Male predominance (3:1); 40–50% are diabetic
- CT shows intramural or intraluminal gas
- **30–75% are acalculous**; gangrene rate 60–75%; perforation rate 15–20%
- **Mortality: 15–25%** — demands urgent cholecystectomy

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.55; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.57*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Laboratory Studies

🟢 **Standard workup:**
- **WBC:** Elevated (typically 12,000–18,000/μL) with left shift
- **CRP:** Elevated; useful for monitoring response
- **LFTs:** Mild elevation of AST/ALT/ALP is common (reactive hepatitis or compression of bile ducts by gallbladder edema)
  - 🔵 Significantly elevated bilirubin (>4 mg/dL) or markedly elevated ALP → suspect choledocholithiasis or Mirizzi syndrome
- **Amylase/lipase:** Check to exclude pancreatitis (gallstone pancreatitis coexists in 5–10%)
- **Blood cultures:** If sepsis suspected (TG18 Grade III)

### Imaging

🟢 **Imaging hierarchy:**

| Modality | Role | Key Findings |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Ultrasound** | First-line (always) | Gallstones + GB wall thickening (>3 mm) + pericholecystic fluid + sonographic Murphy's sign |
| **CT scan** | Second-line / complications | Wall thickening, pericholecystic fat stranding, gas (emphysematous), perforation, abscess |
| **HIDA scan** | Equivocal cases | Non-visualization of GB at 4 hours (cystic duct obstruction); Sn 97%, Sp 90% |
| **MRI/MRCP** | Suspected CBD stones | Delineates biliary anatomy and choledocholithiasis |

🟢 **Ultrasound findings (the "4 signs"):**
1. Gallstones (echogenic focus with posterior acoustic shadow)
2. Gallbladder wall thickening >3 mm (or >5 mm if edematous — wall edema is nonspecific)
3. Pericholecystic fluid
4. Positive sonographic Murphy's sign

**Sensitivity of US for acute cholecystitis: 81–88%; Specificity: 80–83%**

🔵 **HIDA (hepatobiliary iminodiacetic acid) scan:**
- Gold standard for cystic duct obstruction
- Non-filling of gallbladder after 4 hours (or 30 min after morphine augmentation) = positive
- False positives: chronic cholecystitis, TPN, prolonged fasting, severe liver disease
- Best used when US is equivocal and clinical suspicion remains high

🔵 **CT findings suggesting complicated cholecystitis:**
- Irregular or absent gallbladder wall → gangrene
- Intramural gas → emphysematous cholecystitis
- Pericholecystic abscess
- Intraluminal membrane (mucosal sloughing)
- Lack of wall enhancement → mural ischemia

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.57; Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.34*

## 6. Classification — Tokyo Guidelines (TG18)

🟢 **The TG18 severity grading is the current international standard:**

| Grade | Severity | Criteria | Treatment Implication |
|-------|----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Grade I (Mild)** | No organ dysfunction; no severe local inflammation | Acute cholecystitis in a healthy patient without severe findings | Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy |
| **Grade II (Moderate)** | Associated with any of: WBC >18,000, palpable RUQ mass, duration >72 h, marked local inflammation (gangrene, emphysema, pericholecystic/hepatic abscess, biliary peritonitis) | Local complications but no organ dysfunction | Early lap chole by experienced surgeon; consider drainage if high surgical risk |
| **Grade III (Severe)** | Organ dysfunction in any system | CV: hypotension requiring vasopressors; Neuro: decreased consciousness; Resp: PaO₂/FiO₂ <300; Renal: Cr >2.0; Hepatic: PT-INR >1.5; Heme: Plt <100,000 | Urgent gallbladder drainage (cholecystostomy); delayed cholecystectomy after stabilization |

🔵 **TG18 also provides a Predictive Scoring System** using entry criteria (local signs + systemic signs + imaging confirmation) to confirm diagnosis:
- **A:** Local signs of inflammation (Murphy's sign, RUQ mass/pain/tenderness)
- **B:** Systemic signs of inflammation (fever, elevated CRP, elevated WBC)
- **C:** Imaging findings consistent with acute cholecystitis
- **Definite diagnosis:** One item from A + one from B + C

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The Tokyo Guidelines have been validated across multiple international cohorts. However, critics note that the severity grading does not account for the **Charlson Comorbidity Index** or **frailty scores**, which may be more predictive of outcomes than organ dysfunction in isolation. The **AAST (American Association for the Surgery of Trauma) grading system** provides an anatomic severity scale (Grade I–V) that correlates with morbidity independently of TG18.

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management (All Patients)

🟢 **For all patients with confirmed acute cholecystitis:**
- NPO, IV fluid resuscitation
- Analgesia: **NSAIDs first-line** (diclofenac/indomethacin — reduce prostaglandin-mediated inflammation AND provide analgesia); opioids as rescue
- IV antibiotics targeting biliary flora:
  - Options: Cefuroxime + metronidazole, piperacillin-tazobactam, or ampicillin-sulbactam
  - 🔵 For healthcare-associated: consider carbapenems or fluoroquinolone + metronidazole
- Nasogastric decompression only if vomiting is intractable

### A. Early Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy (The Standard of Care)

🟢 **Early cholecystectomy (within 72 hours of symptom onset, ideally during the index admission) is the definitive treatment for acute cholecystitis.**

**The evidence is overwhelming:**

| Trial / Meta-analysis | Key Finding |
|----------------------|-------------|
| **Cochrane Review (2013)** | Early (within 7 days) vs. delayed (>6 weeks): no difference in complications; early reduces total hospital stay |
| **ACDC Trial (2013)** | Early (<24 h) vs. delayed (7–45 days): lower morbidity and costs with early; no increase in bile duct injury |
| **CHOCOLATE Trial (2015)** | Early lap chole superior to percutaneous cholecystostomy in high-risk patients for complication rates |

🟢 **Key operative principles:**
- Establish pneumoperitoneum; 4-port technique (10 mm umbilical, 10 mm epigastric, two 5 mm subcostal)
- Fundus-first approach if anatomy obscured by inflammation
- **Critical View of Safety (CVS)** — mandatory before clipping/dividing any structure
- Irrigate and aspirate; drain placement individualized (not routine)
- Always retrieve specimen in a bag; send for histopathology

🔵 **Conversion to open:** Rate 5–15% in acute setting (vs. 2–5% elective). Conversion is a **sign of good judgment, not failure**. Indications:
- Inability to achieve CVS
- Unclear anatomy despite dissection
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Suspected bile duct injury
- Dense adhesions (frozen Calot's triangle)

### B. Bail-Out Strategies — When Cholecystectomy Becomes Hazardous

🔵 **The concept of "bail-out" procedures has transformed how surgeons manage the difficult gallbladder:**

| Strategy | Technique | When to Use |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Subtotal cholecystectomy** | Leave posterior wall (reconstituting or fenestrating technique); remove all stones; close/drain remnant | Frozen Calot's; unclear anatomy; dense inflammation |
| **Fundus-first (top-down)** | Dissect from fundus toward infundibulum | Cannot access Calot's triangle initially |
| **Cholecystostomy tube** | Drain gallbladder (open or percutaneous) | Unstable patient; extreme inflammation |
| **Open conversion** | Convert to open approach | Laparoscopic approach not progressing safely |

🔴 **SS Pearl — The SAGES Safe Cholecystectomy Program (2014):**
SAGES codified a "Culture of Safety in Cholecystectomy" emphasizing:
1. CVS should be documented photographically
2. If CVS cannot be achieved → STOP and employ a bail-out strategy
3. Intraoperative cholangiography (IOC) or near-infrared fluorescent cholangiography (ICG) should be used liberally
4. "The most dangerous operation in surgery is the one where the surgeon doesn't recognize danger"

### C. Percutaneous Cholecystostomy

🔵 **Indications:**
- TG18 Grade III (severe) with organ dysfunction — temporizing measure
- Unfit for general anesthesia (ASA IV/V)
- Critically ill ICU patients with acalculous cholecystitis

**Technique:** US-guided transperitoneal or transhepatic (preferred — lower risk of bile leak) placement of 8–10 Fr pigtail catheter into the gallbladder.

**Outcomes:**
- Clinical improvement in 85–90% within 72 hours
- 30-day mortality: 15–20% (reflects the sick population, not procedure failure)
- Interval cholecystectomy performed 6–8 weeks later if fit

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The CHOCOLATE trial challenged the widespread use of percutaneous cholecystostomy in high-risk patients, demonstrating that **early laparoscopic cholecystectomy had better outcomes even in high-risk patients** — suggesting that the procedure should be reserved for those who truly cannot tolerate any general anesthesia.

### D. Timing: The Early vs. Delayed Debate — Settled

🟢 **Summary of the timing evidence:**

```
    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │     TIMING OF CHOLECYSTECTOMY             │
    │                                           │
    │  EARLY (index admission, <72h)            │
    │  ✓ Shorter total hospital stay            │
    │  ✓ Lower overall morbidity                │
    │  ✓ Lower cost                             │
    │  ✓ No increase in bile duct injury        │
    │  ✓ 20% of "delayed" patients return       │
    │    emergently before planned surgery       │
    │                                           │
    │  DELAYED (>6 weeks — "cool down")         │
    │  ✗ Longer total hospital stay             │
    │  ✗ 20% emergency readmission              │
    │  ✗ Same complication rate                  │
    │  ✗ Patient anxiety during wait            │
    │                                           │
    │  VERDICT: Early is STANDARD OF CARE       │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

🔵 **The "golden window":** Evidence suggests that cholecystectomy within **72 hours** of symptom onset is technically easier due to edematous planes that facilitate dissection. Beyond 72 hours, fibrotic adhesions develop, making dissection harder. However, recent data (including the Tokyo Guidelines) supports early surgery even beyond 72 hours — **"better late than never; better now than later."**

## 8. Complications

### A. Complications of Acute Cholecystitis Itself

🟢 **Natural history without intervention — progressive complications:**

| Complication | Features | Management |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| **Empyema** | Purulent gallbladder; high fever, rigors, toxicity; WBC often >20,000 | Urgent cholecystectomy or cholecystostomy |
| **Gangrenous cholecystitis** | Wall necrosis; ~15–25% of acute cholecystitis; paradoxically may have LESS pain (nerve destruction) | Urgent cholecystectomy |
| **Perforation** | Type I (free, 1%): generalized peritonitis; Type II (localized, 85%): pericholecystic abscess; Type III (cholecystenteric fistula) | Type I: emergency surgery; Type II: drainage ± surgery; Type III: manage fistula |
| **Mirizzi syndrome** | External compression of CHD by impacted stone in Hartmann's pouch/cystic duct | Type I: cholecystectomy; Type II–IV: biliary reconstruction (hepaticojejunostomy) |
| **Gallstone ileus** | Stone erodes into duodenum (cholecystoduodenal fistula) → bowel obstruction (usually at ileocecal valve; stone >2.5 cm) | Enterolithotomy ± fistula repair (one-stage vs. two-stage) |
| **Cholecystoenteric fistula** | Result of chronic inflammation/perforation into adjacent bowel | May be managed at cholecystectomy or as separate procedure |

🔵 **Mirizzi Syndrome Classification (Csendes, modified by Beltrán):**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| I | External compression of CHD (no fistula) |
| II | Cholecystobiliary fistula involving <1/3 of CHD circumference |
| III | Fistula involving 1/3 to 2/3 of CHD circumference |
| IV | Fistula involving >2/3 or complete destruction of CHD wall |
| V (added) | Type I–IV + cholecystoenteric fistula ± gallstone ileus |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Gallstone ileus classically presents with the **Rigler's triad** on imaging: (1) small bowel obstruction, (2) pneumobilia, and (3) ectopic gallstone. However, the complete triad is present in only 40–50% of cases. It accounts for 1–4% of all mechanical SBO but up to **25% of SBO in patients >65 years**.

### B. Complications of Cholecystectomy

🟢 **Bile duct injury (BDI):**
- Incidence: **0.3–0.5%** (laparoscopic) vs. 0.1–0.2% (open)
- **Most devastating complication** — can lead to recurrent cholangitis, biliary cirrhosis, need for liver transplant
- 🔵 **Strasberg Classification (Types A–E)** is the standard for describing BDI
- 🔵 **Mechanism:** Misidentification of CBD as cystic duct (the "classic laparoscopic injury") — often involves excision of a segment of CBD
- **Prevention:** CVS, IOC, ICG fluorescent cholangiography, experienced surgeon

🔵 **Other operative complications:**
- Bile leak (cystic duct stump, duct of Luschka): 0.5–1.5%
- Bleeding (cystic artery, liver bed): 0.1–0.5%
- Retained CBD stones: 1–4%
- Port-site hernia: 0.5–1%
- Post-cholecystectomy syndrome (persistent pain, often sphincter of Oddi dysfunction or missed CBD stones)

> 📚 *See Blumgart 7th Ed, Ch.35–40; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.57; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.93*

## 9. Special Topics

### Incidental Gallbladder Carcinoma

🔵 **Found in 0.2–3% of cholecystectomy specimens** — hence the mandate to ALWAYS send the gallbladder for histopathology.

| T Stage | Management |
|---------|------------|
| Tis / T1a | Cholecystectomy alone (curative) |
| T1b | Controversial — some advocate radical re-resection |
| T2–T4 | Radical re-resection: hepatic segments IVb/V + portal lymphadenectomy + excision of port sites |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** If gallbladder cancer is suspected intraoperatively (mass, mucosal irregularity, porcelain gallbladder), **do NOT perform cholecystectomy** — instead, take a biopsy and refer to an HPB center. An oncologically inadequate cholecystectomy with gallbladder perforation dramatically worsens prognosis.

### Porcelain Gallbladder

🔵 Previously considered an absolute indication for cholecystectomy due to "25% GBC risk." Recent evidence suggests only **selective intimal calcification** (not complete mural calcification) carries elevated cancer risk. Current recommendation: cholecystectomy for symptomatic or incompletely calcified porcelain gallbladder.

## 10. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Acute cholecystitis is calculous in 90–95% of cases.** Gallstones are present in 10–15% of the adult population; 20% of stone carriers develop symptoms.
2. **Classic presentation:** RUQ pain >6 hours + fever + positive Murphy's sign. Pain after a fatty meal is classic. **Murphy's sign** is the most useful bedside test.
3. **Ultrasound is first-line imaging:** Stones + wall thickening (>3 mm) + pericholecystic fluid + sonographic Murphy's sign.
4. **HIDA scan** is the most accurate test for cystic duct obstruction (Sn 97%, Sp 90%).
5. **Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy** (within 72 hours / index admission) is the standard of care. Delay increases total hospital stay and readmission rates without reducing complications.
6. **Critical View of Safety** must be achieved before dividing any structure.
7. **Always send the gallbladder for histopathology** — incidental GBC in 0.2–3%.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **TG18 severity grading** (I/II/III) guides management: Grade I → early lap chole; Grade II → experienced surgeon or drainage; Grade III → urgent drainage, delayed surgery.
9. **Bail-out strategies** (subtotal cholecystectomy, fundus-first, cholecystostomy) are alternatives when CVS cannot be achieved — never persist in hazardous dissection.
10. **Conversion to open is good judgment, not failure.** Rate 5–15% in acute cholecystitis.
11. **Acalculous cholecystitis** (5–10% of cases): high mortality (30–50%), occurs in critically ill patients; percutaneous cholecystostomy is often the initial treatment.
12. **Gangrenous cholecystitis** occurs in 15–25% — paradoxically may present with reduced pain. CT findings: irregular wall, mural non-enhancement, intraluminal membranes.
13. **Mirizzi syndrome:** Classified I–V; Types II+ require biliary reconstruction.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **The CHOCOLATE trial** showed that early lap chole outperforms percutaneous cholecystostomy even in high-risk patients — reserve cholecystostomy for the truly inoperable.
15. **Emphysematous cholecystitis:** Gas-forming organisms, diabetic males, 30–75% acalculous, mortality 15–25% — surgical emergency.
16. **Gallstone ileus:** Rigler's triad (SBO + pneumobilia + ectopic stone) present in only 40–50%; accounts for 25% of SBO in the elderly.
17. **SAGES Culture of Safety:** CVS documentation, liberal use of IOC/ICG, bail-out strategies, and recognition of dangerous anatomy prevent bile duct injuries.
18. **Incidental GBC management:** T1a = cholecystectomy alone (curative); T2+ = radical re-resection at HPB center. Never perforate a suspicious gallbladder.
