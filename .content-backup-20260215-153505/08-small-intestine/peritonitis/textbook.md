## 1. History — From "Inflammation of the Belly" to Evidence-Based Protocols

Peritonitis has been recognized since antiquity. Hippocrates described fatal abdominal suppuration, and for millennia, peritonitis was essentially a death sentence. The term "peritonitis" was coined by **Cullen** in 1776. Before the era of asepsis and surgery, the condition carried near-100% mortality.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1776 | **Cullen** — Coined "peritonitis" |
| 1884 | **Mikulicz** — Advocated early operative intervention with peritoneal lavage |
| 1886 | **Fitz** — Linked appendicitis to peritonitis; advocated early surgery |
| 1926 | **Kirschner** — Proposed radical peritoneal debridement and drainage |
| 1970s | **Wittmann** — Introduced planned relaparotomy and open abdomen concepts |
| 2002 | **Solomkin et al.** — IDSA guidelines for intra-abdominal infections formalized antibiotic protocols |

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.36; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46*

The modern era has shifted focus from heroic surgical exploration to source control, damage control surgery, and the open abdomen — a philosophical pivot from "do everything now" to "do enough now and come back."

## 2. Anatomy of the Peritoneum

🟢 **Basic structure:**
- The peritoneum is the **largest serous membrane** in the body, with a surface area approximating skin (~1.7–2.0 m²)
- Composed of a single layer of **mesothelium** resting on a basement membrane and connective tissue layer
- Divided into **parietal** (lines abdominal wall, diaphragm, pelvis) and **visceral** (covers abdominal organs) peritoneum

🟢 **Key anatomical compartments:**

| Compartment | Boundaries | Clinical Significance |
|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Right subphrenic** | Liver dome, diaphragm | Subphrenic abscess post-appendicitis/cholecystitis |
| **Left subphrenic** | Spleen, diaphragm | Post-splenectomy, gastric perforation |
| **Subhepatic (Morison's pouch)** | Liver, right kidney | Most dependent space in supine position; fluid collects here first |
| **Lesser sac (omental bursa)** | Stomach, pancreas, transverse mesocolon | Pancreatic collections, posterior gastric perforation |
| **Right paracolic gutter** | Ascending colon to pelvis | Conduit for flow from RUQ ↔ pelvis |
| **Left paracolic gutter** | Descending colon (partially blocked by phrenicocolic ligament) | Less free communication to LUQ |
| **Pelvis (Pouch of Douglas / rectovesical)** | Most dependent in upright position | Pelvic abscess accumulation |

🔵 **The phrenicocolic ligament** partially blocks flow from the left paracolic gutter to the left subphrenic space — this is why **right subphrenic abscesses** are far more common than left.

🔵 **Peritoneal physiology:**
- Can absorb up to **500 mL/hour** of fluid
- Diaphragmatic lymphatics (stomata) drain directly to the thoracic duct
- This explains why peritonitis causes **pleural effusions** and **diaphragmatic splinting**
- Normal peritoneal fluid: ~50 mL, straw-colored, <300 cells/mm³

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The peritoneum is an active immunological organ, not a passive lining. Mesothelial cells express TLRs, produce cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α), and recruit neutrophils and macrophages. Peritoneal macrophages represent the largest population of tissue-resident macrophages in the body. Understanding this has implications for intraperitoneal drug delivery and HIPEC pharmacokinetics.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.36*

## 3. Classification

🟢 **Primary classification — the essential framework:**

### A. Primary Peritonitis (Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis — SBP)

- Infection **without** a surgical source (no perforation, no abscess)
- Occurs by **hematogenous or lymphatic spread** or **translocation** of bacteria across the bowel wall
- 🟢 **Classic setting:** Cirrhosis with ascites (most common), nephrotic syndrome, SLE, peritoneal dialysis
- **Microbiology:** Usually **monomicrobial** — E. coli, Klebsiella, Streptococcus pneumoniae
- **NOT a surgical disease** — treated with antibiotics alone

### B. Secondary Peritonitis

- 🟢 **The most common form** — results from contamination of the peritoneal cavity due to:
  - **Perforation** of a hollow viscus (peptic ulcer, appendicitis, diverticulitis, typhoid, trauma)
  - **Anastomotic leak** postoperatively
  - **Ischemia/necrosis** (strangulated hernia, mesenteric ischemia)
  - **Inflammation** (pancreatitis, cholecystitis with perforation)
- **Microbiology:** **Polymicrobial** — E. coli, Bacteroides fragilis, Enterococcus, Klebsiella
- **Treatment:** Source control (surgery) + antibiotics

### C. Tertiary Peritonitis

- 🔵 **Persistent or recurrent infection** >48 hours after apparently adequate treatment of secondary peritonitis
- Represents a failure of host defenses and/or source control
- **Microbiology:** Resistant organisms — Enterococcus, Candida, Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, coagulase-negative Staphylococci
- **Mortality:** 30–64%
- 🔴 **SS Pearl:** Tertiary peritonitis is increasingly recognized as a distinct entity reflecting immune exhaustion and organ dysfunction rather than simply "ongoing infection." Its management crosses into ICU immunomodulation territory.

🟢 **Other important classifications:**

| Type | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Chemical peritonitis** | Bile, gastric acid, blood, barium, meconium — initial reaction is sterile |
| **Sclerosing encapsulating peritonitis** | Chronic; cocoons bowel; seen in peritoneal dialysis, β-blocker (practolol) |
| **Granulomatous peritonitis** | TB, foreign body (starch, talc), Crohn's |
| **Periodic (Familial Mediterranean Fever)** | Recurrent self-limiting sterile peritonitis; MEFV gene mutation |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.36; Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60*

## 4. Pathophysiology

### The Peritoneal Response to Contamination

🟢 **Sequential events after peritoneal contamination:**

```
Bacterial contamination / chemical irritation
    → Mesothelial cell activation → cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)
    → ↑ Vascular permeability → protein-rich exudate floods peritoneum
    → Neutrophil influx (within hours)
    → Fibrin deposition → attempts to wall off infection
    → If contained → Abscess formation
    → If NOT contained → Diffuse peritonitis → SIRS → Sepsis → MOF → Death
```

🟢 **Key pathophysiological consequences:**

1. **Third-space fluid loss:** Massive exudation into the peritoneal cavity → hypovolemia, hypotension
2. **Paralytic ileus:** Peritoneal inflammation → bowel wall edema → ileus → distension → further fluid sequestration
3. **Diaphragmatic splinting:** Pain + irritation → ↓ respiratory excursion → atelectasis → hypoxia
4. **Metabolic derangements:** Acidosis (lactic), electrolyte losses (K⁺, Na⁺), catabolism

🔵 **The fibrin paradox:**
- Fibrin deposition is the body's attempt to **contain** infection (abscess formation = relatively good outcome)
- But fibrin also **shields bacteria** from phagocytes and antibiotics
- B. fragilis uniquely promotes abscess formation through its capsular polysaccharide (synergy with E. coli)
- This explains the classic **E. coli + B. fragilis** synergy: E. coli causes the early septicemia, B. fragilis causes late abscesses

🔴 **SS Pearl — The Abdominal Compartment Syndrome connection:** Severe peritonitis with massive fluid sequestration, bowel edema, and aggressive resuscitation can lead to intra-abdominal hypertension (IAP >12 mmHg) and abdominal compartment syndrome (IAP >20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction). This is the pathophysiological basis for the open abdomen approach.

### Bacterial Count and Peritoneal Defense

🔵 **Inoculum matters:**
- The peritoneum can clear **small** bacterial loads effectively
- Overwhelming contamination (colon perforation: 10⁹–10¹¹ bacteria/mL) exceeds defenses
- **Adjuvants** worsen infection: hemoglobin, bile salts, barium, necrotic tissue, foreign bodies

🔵 **Bacterial gradient along the GI tract:**

| Location | Bacterial Count (CFU/mL) | Predominant Flora |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Stomach | 10¹–10³ | Streptococci, Lactobacillus |
| Jejunum | 10³–10⁴ | Streptococci, Enterococci |
| Ileum | 10⁵–10⁸ | Mixed |
| Colon | 10¹⁰–10¹² | Bacteroides, E. coli, Enterococci |

This gradient explains why **gastric perforations** (initially chemical, low bacterial load) have better prognosis than **colonic perforations** (massive polymicrobial contamination).

## 5. Clinical Presentation

🟢 **Cardinal features of peritonitis:**

### Symptoms
- **Abdominal pain** — initially localized (to the site of pathology), becomes diffuse with generalized peritonitis
- **Nausea and vomiting**
- **Anorexia**
- **Inability to pass flatus/stool** (ileus)
- **Fever** (may be absent in elderly, immunosuppressed, or early presentation)

### Signs
🟢 **The "peritonitic abdomen":**
- **Board-like rigidity** — involuntary guarding (hallmark of generalized peritonitis)
- **Rebound tenderness** — pain on sudden release of palpation pressure
- **Absent bowel sounds** — "silent abdomen" (paralytic ileus)
- **Tenderness on percussion** — more reliable and less painful than rebound
- **Lying still** — patients avoid movement (unlike colicky pain where patients writhe)

🔵 **Additional signs:**
- **Cullen's sign** (periumbilical ecchymosis) — hemorrhagic pancreatitis, ruptured ectopic
- **Grey Turner's sign** (flank ecchymosis) — retroperitoneal hemorrhage
- **Kehr's sign** (shoulder-tip pain) — diaphragmatic irritation by blood/bile/gas
- **Tachycardia, hypotension** — late signs indicating sepsis/hypovolemia

### Special Considerations

🟢 **Elderly patients:**
- Diminished pain perception and immune response
- May lack fever, guarding, or rigidity
- Often present late with established sepsis
- "Any elderly patient with unexplained deterioration — think peritonitis"

🟢 **Immunocompromised patients:**
- Absent inflammatory signs
- High index of suspicion required
- CT imaging is essential

🔵 **Peritoneal dialysis patients:**
- Cloudy effluent is the earliest and most sensitive sign
- Cell count >100/mm³ with >50% neutrophils is diagnostic
- Most commonly: coagulase-negative Staphylococci (Gram-positive)
- Differs from SBP of cirrhosis (Gram-negative predominant)

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46*

## 6. Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

🟢 **Peritonitis is primarily a clinical diagnosis.** In patients with an acute abdomen, board-like rigidity, and absent bowel sounds, further investigation serves to identify the **cause**, not to confirm peritonitis itself.

### Laboratory Studies

🟢 **Essential labs:**
- **CBC:** Leukocytosis (or leukopenia in severe sepsis — worse prognosis)
- **Metabolic panel:** Acidosis, renal function, electrolytes
- **Lactate:** ↑ indicates tissue hypoperfusion; >4 mmol/L = poor prognosis
- **Amylase/lipase:** If pancreatitis suspected
- **Blood cultures:** Before antibiotics if possible
- **ABG:** Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

🔵 **Serum procalcitonin:**
- Helps differentiate bacterial peritonitis from sterile causes
- Useful for monitoring treatment response and antibiotic de-escalation

### Imaging

🟢 **Erect chest X-ray — the most important initial investigation:**
- **Pneumoperitoneum** (free air under diaphragm) — present in ~80% of gastroduodenal perforations but only ~50% of colonic perforations
- Absent in 20–50% of perforations — **a normal CXR does not exclude perforation**
- **Left lateral decubitus** can detect as little as 1 mL of free air

🟢 **Abdominal X-ray:**
- Dilated loops (ileus)
- Ground-glass appearance (free fluid)
- Rigler's sign (double-wall sign) — free air outlines both sides of bowel wall

🔵 **CT abdomen with IV contrast — the investigation of choice:**
- Sensitivity 95% for detecting peritonitis and identifying the source
- Free fluid, free air, bowel wall thickening, fat stranding
- Can guide percutaneous drainage of localized collections
- **Oral contrast** is often unnecessary and delays imaging in emergencies

🔵 **Ultrasound:**
- Useful for free fluid detection (especially in trauma — FAST scan)
- Guidance for paracentesis
- Limited by ileus, obesity, and operator dependence

🔵 **Diagnostic peritoneal lavage (DPL) / paracentesis:**
- **SBP diagnosis:** Ascitic fluid WBC >500/mm³ or PMN >250/mm³
- **Secondary peritonitis:** Fluid amylase, bilirubin, food particles
- Peritoneal aspirate with bacteria on Gram stain = peritonitis

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.36*

## 7. Etiology — Common Causes by Region

🟢 **In developed countries:**

| Cause | Frequency | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Perforated appendicitis** | Most common overall | Peak in young adults |
| **Perforated peptic ulcer** | Common | ↑ NSAID use; ↑ in elderly |
| **Perforated diverticulitis** | Common in elderly | Left-sided predominance (Western) |
| **Anastomotic leak** | Postoperative | Typically POD 5-7 |
| **Cholecystitis/perforation** | Less common | Empyema, gangrenous |
| **Trauma** | Variable | Penetrating > blunt for hollow viscus |

🟢 **In developing countries / Indian subcontinent:**

| Cause | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Perforated duodenal ulcer** | Most common cause in India |
| **Typhoid perforation** | Ileal perforation; endemic areas |
| **Tuberculous peritonitis** | Often presents as chronic/subacute |
| **Perforated appendicitis** | Common globally |
| **Intestinal obstruction with gangrene** | Strangulated hernia, volvulus |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** In India, duodenal ulcer perforation remains the leading cause of peritonitis, unlike the West where appendicitis and diverticulitis dominate. Typhoid perforation (terminal ileum, antimesenteric border) carries a mortality of 10–30% and requires careful surgical technique (simple closure in two layers, avoid resection if possible, thorough peritoneal lavage).

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; SRB Manual of Surgery*

## 8. Treatment

### ⚡ Clinical Decision Framework

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│            PERITONITIS - DECISION MAP              │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                    │
│  Step 1: RESUSCITATE (ABCs)                        │
│  • IV access (2 large bore), fluid resuscitation   │
│  • Urinary catheter, NG tube                       │
│  • Analgesia (DO NOT withhold)                     │
│  • Oxygen, monitor vitals                          │
│                                                    │
│  Step 2: CLASSIFY                                  │
│  ┌──────────────┐ ┌────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐ │
│  │   PRIMARY     │ │  SECONDARY │ │   TERTIARY   │ │

│  (No surgery)           │                          │
│                   ┌─────┴──────┐                   │
│                   │            │                    │
│                LOCALIZED   GENERALIZED              │
│                   │            │                    │
│            Percutaneous    Exploratory              │
│            drainage ±      laparotomy               │
│            antibiotics                              │
│                                                    │
│  Step 3: SOURCE CONTROL (within 6-12 hours)        │
│  • Repair perforation / resect necrotic bowel      │
│  • Drain collections                               │
│  • ± Damage control surgery if unstable            │
│                                                    │
│  Step 4: POST-OP                                   │
│  • ICU care if severe sepsis                       │
│  • Antibiotics: 4-7 days (shorter is better)       │
│  • Monitor for complications                       │
│  • Relook if needed (Day 2-3)                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### A. Initial Resuscitation

🟢 **Simultaneous with diagnosis — do NOT delay for investigations:**
- **IV crystalloids** — target MAP ≥65 mmHg, UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
- **Nasogastric decompression** — relieves distension, prevents aspiration
- **Urinary catheter** — monitor output as resuscitation endpoint
- **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** — within 1 hour of recognition of sepsis
- **Analgesia** — opioids are appropriate; withholding analgesia is unethical and does NOT improve diagnostic accuracy (multiple RCTs)

### B. Antibiotic Therapy

🟢 **Empiric regimens (IDSA / WSES guidelines):**

| Severity | Regimen Options |
|----------|----------------|
| **Community-acquired, mild-moderate** | Cefoxitin; Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole; Ampicillin-Sulbactam |
| **Community-acquired, severe** | Piperacillin-Tazobactam; Meropenem; Imipenem |
| **Hospital-acquired / tertiary** | Meropenem ± Vancomycin; add antifungal (Fluconazole) if Candida risk |

🔵 **Duration:**
- **Adequate source control achieved:** 4 days maximum (STOP-IT trial — no benefit beyond 4 days)
- **Inadequate source control:** Continue until source controlled or clinical improvement
- 🔴 **SS Pearl:** The DURAPOP trial confirmed that 8 days was noninferior to 15 days even for complicated cases. The trend is toward **shorter antibiotic courses** with adequate source control.

### C. Surgical Management — Source Control

🟢 **Principles of source control (the "4 Ds"):**
1. **Drainage** — of abscesses and collections
2. **Debridement** — of necrotic tissue
3. **Definitive repair** — repair/resect the source
4. **Diversion** — stoma if primary repair is unsafe

🟢 **Timing:** Source control should be achieved within **6–12 hours** of diagnosis. Delays beyond 24 hours increase mortality significantly.

🟢 **Surgical approach by etiology:**

| Cause | Procedure |
|-------|-----------|
| **Perforated DU** | Graham patch (omental plug) ± definitive ulcer surgery |
| **Perforated appendicitis** | Appendectomy |
| **Perforated diverticulitis** | Hartmann's procedure or resection + primary anastomosis ± diverting loop ileostomy |
| **Small bowel perforation (typhoid)** | Simple closure in 2 layers + peritoneal lavage |
| **Colonic perforation (trauma)** | Resection ± primary anastomosis or stoma |
| **Anastomotic leak** | Drainage ± diversion; takedown only if localized |

🔵 **Peritoneal lavage:**
- **Warm saline lavage** until effluent is clear
- No evidence for antiseptic solutions (betadine, hydrogen peroxide) — may be harmful
- Lavage with antibiotics shows no benefit over saline in most studies
- **"The solution to pollution is dilution"**

🔵 **Damage Control Surgery (DCS):**
- For the physiologically deranged patient ("lethal triad": hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy)
- Abbreviated surgery: source control only, temporary abdominal closure, pack and close
- Transfer to ICU for resuscitation
- Planned relaparotomy in 24-48 hours

🔴 **SS Pearl — Open Abdomen:**
- Indications: abdominal compartment syndrome, planned relook, inability to close
- **Negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT)** with the ABThera or Barker's vacuum pack is the preferred temporary closure technique
- Fascial closure rate >65% if closed within 7 days; drops significantly after
- The COOL trial (2022) showed no benefit of routine open abdomen over closed abdomen after source control for severe peritonitis — selective use only

### D. Management of SBP (Primary Peritonitis)

🟢 **Diagnosis:** Ascitic PMN >250/mm³ (culture often negative)

🟢 **Treatment:**
- **Cefotaxime** 2g IV q8h × 5 days (first-line)
- **IV albumin** 1.5 g/kg on Day 1, 1 g/kg on Day 3 → reduces mortality (Sort trial)
- **SBP prophylaxis** (secondary): Norfloxacin 400 mg daily long-term

🔵 **Distinguish from secondary peritonitis in cirrhotics:**
- Suspect if: ascitic fluid shows ≥2 of: glucose <50 mg/dL, protein >10 g/L, LDH > serum LDH
- Runyon's criteria — if positive, CT imaging and surgical consultation mandatory

## 9. Prognosis & Scoring Systems

🔵 **Mannheim Peritonitis Index (MPI):**

| Factor | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Age >50 | 5 |
| Female | 5 |
| Organ failure | 7 |
| Malignancy | 4 |
| Duration of peritonitis >24h | 4 |
| Origin not colonic | 4 |
| Diffuse peritonitis | 6 |
| Exudate: clear | 0 |
| Exudate: cloudy/purulent | 6 |
| Exudate: fecal | 12 |

- **MPI <21:** Mortality ~2.3%
- **MPI 21–29:** Mortality ~22.5%
- **MPI >29:** Mortality ~59.1%

🔵 **WSES Sepsis Severity Score** and **APACHE II** are also used in ICU settings for prognostication.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The most important modifiable factor in peritonitis outcomes is **time to source control.** Every 6-hour delay in surgery for diffuse peritonitis increases mortality by approximately 10%. The non-modifiable factors (age, comorbidity, colonic source) determine baseline risk.

## 10. Specific Types — Brief Notes

### Tuberculous Peritonitis

🔵 **Three forms:**
1. **Wet** (ascitic) — most common; straw-colored, high-protein ascites; SAAG <1.1
2. **Dry** (adhesive) — caseous nodules, matted bowel, "doughy abdomen"
3. **Encysted** (loculated) — loculated collections mimicking abscess

🔵 **Diagnosis:**
- Ascitic fluid: lymphocyte-predominant, ADA >39 IU/L (sensitivity ~100%)
- Laparoscopy with biopsy: "violin-string" adhesions, white tubercles on peritoneum → **gold standard**
- **Treatment:** ATT (standard 6-month regimen); surgery only for complications (obstruction, perforation)

### Sclerosing Encapsulating Peritonitis (Abdominal Cocoon)

🔴 **Types:**
- **Primary (idiopathic):** Young females in tropical countries; thick membrane encasing small bowel
- **Secondary:** Long-term peritoneal dialysis (most common cause), β-blockers (practolol — historical)
- **Treatment:** Careful surgical peeling of the membrane; high recurrence rate

### Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF)

🔵 Autosomal recessive; **MEFV gene** mutation; affects Mediterranean populations.
- Recurrent episodes of sterile peritonitis with fever (12-72 hours, self-limiting)
- **Danger:** Can lead to unnecessary laparotomies (up to 30% of FMF patients have "negative explorations")
- **Prophylaxis:** Colchicine (prevents attacks and AA amyloidosis)

> 📚 *See Bailey & Love 28th Ed, Ch.60; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46*

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Peritonitis = inflammation of the peritoneum.** Classified as primary (SBP), secondary (perforation/contamination), or tertiary (persistent after treatment).
2. **Secondary peritonitis is the most common type** and requires surgical source control.
3. **Board-like rigidity + absent bowel sounds** = hallmarks of generalized peritonitis.
4. **Erect CXR** is the most important initial investigation — look for free air under the diaphragm.
5. **CT abdomen** is the investigation of choice to identify the source.
6. **Resuscitate first** — IV fluids, NG tube, catheter, antibiotics within 1 hour, analgesia (never withhold).
7. **Source control within 6–12 hours** — the single most important intervention.
8. **SBP** is treated with antibiotics alone (Cefotaxime + IV albumin); NOT a surgical disease.
9. **Perforated DU** = most common cause of peritonitis in India; appendicitis in the West.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

10. The E. coli–B. fragilis synergy: E. coli causes early septicemia, B. fragilis causes late abscesses.
11. MPI score >29 carries ~60% mortality — identifies high-risk patients for ICU admission.
12. Antibiotics after adequate source control: **4 days maximum** (STOP-IT trial).
13. Peritoneal lavage with saline only — no antiseptics, no antibiotic solutions.
14. Damage control surgery for the "lethal triad" — abbreviated source control, ICU resuscitation, planned relook.
15. TB peritonitis: ADA >39 in ascitic fluid; laparoscopy is gold standard; treat with ATT, not surgery.
16. Distinguish SBP from secondary peritonitis in cirrhotics using Runyon's criteria.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

17. Tertiary peritonitis reflects immune exhaustion — Candida and MDR organisms predominate.
18. Open abdomen: COOL trial showed no benefit of routine use; reserve for ACS and planned relook.
19. Abdominal compartment syndrome (IAP >20 + organ dysfunction) can complicate severe peritonitis.
20. Every 6-hour delay in source control increases mortality by ~10%.
21. FMF causes recurrent sterile peritonitis — 30% undergo unnecessary laparotomy before diagnosis.
