## 1. History — From Ancient Enigma to Modern Algorithm

Intestinal obstruction has tormented humanity since antiquity. The Ebers Papyrus (1550 BCE) describes abdominal distension and inability to pass stool — likely the earliest written account. Hippocrates recognized volvulus and attempted reduction by insufflating air per rectum. Praxagoras of Cos (circa 350 BCE) is credited with the first enterotomy for obstruction, creating what may be the earliest recorded intestinal fistula.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 350 BCE | **Praxagoras** — First recorded enterotomy for obstruction |
| 1674 | **Thomas Sydenham** — Differentiated mechanical obstruction from "ileus" (functional) |
| 1872 | **Jonathan Hutchinson** — Described intussusception and attempted operative reduction |
| 1889 | **Frederick Treves** — Published landmark classification of intestinal obstruction |
| 1912 | **Hartwell & Hoguet** — Demonstrated IV saline resuscitation reverses mortality in experimental obstruction |
| 1932 | **Owen Wangensteen** — Introduced nasogastric suction decompression, transforming nonoperative management |
| 2017 | **WSES Bologna Guidelines** — First international consensus on adhesive SBO management |

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.26; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49*

Wangensteen's introduction of nasogastric decompression was transformative — preoperative mortality from SBO dropped from over 50% to under 10%. The modern era has refined the question from *whether* to decompress to *how long* before declaring nonoperative failure.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology

🟢 **Functional anatomy relevant to obstruction:**

The small bowel extends approximately **6 metres** from the duodenojejunal flexure (ligament of Treitz) to the ileocecal valve. The large bowel spans **1.5 metres** from cecum to rectum. Understanding their differences is essential because small bowel and large bowel obstructions are fundamentally different diseases.

🟢 **Key differences affecting obstruction behaviour:**

| Feature | Small Bowel | Large Bowel |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Diameter** | 3–4 cm | 5–8 cm (cecum largest) |
| **Wall thickness** | Thinner | Thicker (taeniae + haustra) |
| **Fluid handling** | 6–8 L/day passes through | 1–1.5 L enters; 100 mL excreted |
| **Bacterial load** | 10⁴/mL (jejunum) → 10⁸/mL (ileum) | 10¹¹–10¹²/mL |
| **Ileocecal valve** | — | Competent in 50–75%: creates closed-loop |

🔵 **The ileocecal valve is the pivotal structure.** When competent, a distal colonic obstruction creates a closed loop between the valve and the point of obstruction. The cecum — with the largest diameter — is the site of maximal wall tension (Law of Laplace: T = P × r) and therefore the most vulnerable to perforation.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The critical cecal diameter is classically taught as **12 cm** on plain radiograph, beyond which perforation risk rises sharply. However, the rate of dilation matters more than absolute diameter — a rapidly distending cecum at 10 cm is more dangerous than a chronically dilated one at 14 cm.

🟢 **Normal intestinal motility:** Coordinated peristalsis requires an intact myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus, submucosal (Meissner's) plexus, interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells), and intact autonomic innervation. Disruption at any level produces functional obstruction (ileus or pseudo-obstruction).

## 3. Pathophysiology — The Obstruction Cascade

### Mechanical Obstruction Sequence

🟢 **The universal sequence once mechanical obstruction occurs:**

```
Mechanical obstruction
    → Proximal bowel dilation (gas + fluid accumulation)
    → ↑ Intraluminal pressure
    → Impaired venous → then arterial flow
    → Mucosal ischemia & breakdown
    → Bacterial translocation (to mesenteric nodes, portal blood, peritoneum)
    → Third-spacing (massive fluid sequestration into bowel wall & lumen)
    → Hypovolemia, electrolyte derangement, metabolic derangement
    → Sepsis → Multiorgan failure → Death (if untreated)
```

🟢 **Fluid shifts are enormous.** The GI tract normally secretes 6–8 litres daily (saliva, gastric, biliary, pancreatic, intestinal). In obstruction, this fluid cannot be reabsorbed distally. Combined with inflammatory exudate into the bowel wall and peritoneal cavity, patients can sequester **4–6 litres** within 24–48 hours — producing profound hypovolemic shock.

🟢 **Electrolyte derangements depend on level of obstruction:**

| Level | Dominant Loss | Metabolic Result |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| Proximal (gastric/duodenal) | HCl, K⁺ | Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic **alkalosis** |
| Distal SBO / LBO | Alkaline secretions, third-spacing | Metabolic **acidosis** (lactic acid from ischemia) |

### Strangulation — The Lethal Escalation

🟢 **Strangulation** = obstruction with compromised blood supply. Occurs in approximately **25–30%** of SBO cases. The sequence: venous congestion → hemorrhagic infarction → arterial insufficiency → transmural necrosis → perforation.

🔵 **Closed-loop obstruction** (both ends of a bowel segment occluded) is the most dangerous configuration because:
1. No proximal decompression is possible
2. Progressive distension is relentless
3. Strangulation develops rapidly
4. NGT decompression is ineffective (the closed segment cannot communicate proximally)

🔵 **Causes of closed-loop:** Internal hernia, adhesive band across a loop, volvulus, incarcerated external hernia.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The classic teaching that "no clinical or laboratory parameter reliably distinguishes simple from strangulated obstruction" remains true. CT findings of reduced wall enhancement, mesenteric haziness, free fluid, and pneumatosis are the best available indicators — but even CT has only ~83% sensitivity for strangulation. This is why serial clinical examination remains critical.

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Cardinal Symptoms

🟢 **The classic tetrad of intestinal obstruction:**
1. **Colicky abdominal pain** — intermittent, crampy, coinciding with peristaltic waves
2. **Vomiting**
3. **Abdominal distension**
4. **Absolute constipation (obstipation)** — failure to pass flatus or stool

🟢 **The pattern varies by level of obstruction:**

| Feature | Proximal SBO | Distal SBO | Large Bowel |
|---------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| **Vomiting** | Early, profuse, bilious | Late, may be feculent | Very late or absent |
| **Distension** | Minimal | Moderate–marked | Marked |
| **Pain** | Upper abdominal, frequent cramps | Periumbilical, intermittent | Lower abdominal, less frequent |
| **Obstipation** | Late (distal bowel empties first) | Early–moderate | Early and complete |
| **Dehydration** | Severe, rapid | Moderate | Gradual |

🟢 **Key clinical pearl:** A change in pain character from **colicky to constant** suggests transition from simple obstruction to strangulation — this is the single most important symptom evolution to detect.

### Physical Examination

🟢 **Inspection:** Distension (more marked in distal obstruction); visible peristalsis (especially in thin patients with SBO); scars from previous surgery (adhesions!).

🟢 **Auscultation:** High-pitched, tinkling bowel sounds with rushes in mechanical obstruction. Absent bowel sounds suggest ileus, late obstruction, or strangulation with peritonitis.

🟢 **Palpation:** Diffuse tenderness (mild in simple obstruction). Localized tenderness, guarding, or rebound = strangulation until proven otherwise. **Always examine all hernia orifices** — inguinal, femoral, umbilical, incisional. A missed incarcerated hernia is a preventable catastrophe.

🔵 **Per rectal examination:** Empty rectum in complete obstruction. May reveal a rectal mass (cause of LBO). "Ballooning" of the rectum suggests distal obstruction with air trapping.

🔵 **Signs of strangulation (mandate urgent surgery):**
- Constant (non-colicky) pain
- Fever, tachycardia
- Peritonitis (guarding, rigidity, rebound)
- Hemodynamic instability disproportionate to apparent fluid loss
- Bloody aspirate via NGT or bloody stool

## 5. Diagnosis

### Laboratory Studies

🟢 **Baseline labs** (none are diagnostic; all guide resuscitation and risk assessment):
- **CBC:** Leukocytosis with left shift suggests strangulation/sepsis
- **BMP:** Assess dehydration (elevated BUN/creatinine), electrolyte derangements
- **Lactate:** Elevation correlates with ischemia but is a late finding; normal lactate does NOT exclude strangulation
- **ABG/VBG:** Metabolic alkalosis (proximal) or acidosis (distal/ischemic)
- **LDH, CK, phosphate:** Elevated in bowel necrosis — poor sensitivity, reasonable specificity

### Imaging

🟢 **Plain abdominal X-ray (supine + erect):**

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Multiple air-fluid levels** (step-ladder pattern) | SBO |
| **Dilated small bowel** (>3 cm) with **valvulae conniventes** visible | SBO |
| **Dilated colon** (>6 cm; cecum >9 cm) with **haustral markings** | LBO |
| **Coffee-bean sign** | Sigmoid volvulus (inverted U from left pelvis) |
| **Absence of distal gas** | Complete obstruction |
| **Pneumatosis intestinalis** | Bowel wall ischemia/necrosis |
| **Pneumoperitoneum** | Perforation — surgical emergency |

🟢 Plain X-ray has sensitivity of only **60–70%** for SBO and is often indeterminate. It remains a reasonable **first-line screening test** but should not delay CT when obstruction is suspected clinically.

🟢 **CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast** — the gold standard:

| CT Finding | Significance |
|------------|-------------|
| **Transition point** | Identifies site and often cause of obstruction |
| **Small bowel feces sign** | Particulate matter in dilated ileum — suggests distal/prolonged SBO |
| **Whirl sign** | Mesenteric vessels spiraling — pathognomonic for volvulus |
| **Beak sign** | Tapered narrowing at obstruction point |
| **Reduced wall enhancement** | Ischemia/strangulation |
| **Mesenteric haziness/fluid** | Strangulation, venous congestion |
| **Pneumatosis + portal venous gas** | Bowel necrosis — ominous |
| **Closed-loop** (C or U-shaped dilated loop with convergence of mesentery) | Surgical emergency |

🔵 **CT sensitivity for SBO:** 92–95%. **For strangulation:** ~83%. CT also identifies the cause in up to 85% of cases (adhesive band, hernia, tumor, etc.).

🔵 **Oral contrast is generally not needed** for diagnosis and delays imaging. IV contrast is essential for assessing wall enhancement (ischemia).

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **small bowel feces sign** — particulate matter resembling fecal content in dilated small bowel — was first described by Mayo-Smith in 1999. Present in ~7% of SBO cases, it indicates prolonged obstruction with bacterial overgrowth and stasis. It localizes the transition point to the distal ileum and correlates with a higher likelihood of requiring surgery.

### Water-Soluble Contrast (Gastrografin) Challenge

🔵 **Dual role — both diagnostic and therapeutic:**

- **Diagnostic:** If contrast reaches the colon on follow-up X-ray at **4–24 hours** (most use 6–8 hours), adhesive SBO will likely resolve with nonoperative management (sensitivity 96%, specificity 98%).
- **Therapeutic:** Gastrografin's hyperosmolarity (2150 mOsm/L) draws fluid into the bowel lumen, reduces edema at the obstruction point, and stimulates peristalsis. Meta-analyses show it **reduces need for surgery** and **shortens hospital stay** by 1–2 days.

🔵 **Protocol:** 50–100 mL Gastrografin via NGT after decompression. Abdominal X-ray at 6–8 hours. Contrast in cecum/colon = likely resolution. No contrast past obstruction = likely operative.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Gastrografin should **not** be used if perforation is suspected (risk of chemical peritonitis) or in complete high-grade obstruction where aspiration risk is high. It is most valuable in **partial adhesive SBO** where the decision between operative and nonoperative management is unclear.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.25; WSES 2017 Guidelines*

## 6. Classification

🟢 **Primary Classification:**

```
INTESTINAL OBSTRUCTION
├── MECHANICAL (physical barrier)
│   ├── Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
│   │   ├── Adhesions (60–75% in developed world)
│   │   ├── Hernias (#1 worldwide; incarcerated inguinal, femoral, internal)
│   │   ├── Neoplasms (primary, metastatic, carcinomatosis)
│   │   ├── Crohn's disease (strictures)
│   │   ├── Intussusception
│   │   ├── Gallstone ileus
│   │   ├── Meckel's diverticulum
│   │   └── Foreign bodies, bezoars
│   └── Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
│       ├── Colorectal carcinoma (#1, ~60%)
│       ├── Volvulus (sigmoid >> cecal)
│       ├── Diverticular stricture
│       ├── Fecal impaction
│       └── Extrinsic compression
└── FUNCTIONAL (no physical barrier)
    ├── Paralytic ileus (postoperative, metabolic, drugs)
    └── Pseudo-obstruction
        ├── Acute (Ogilvie's syndrome)
        └── Chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction (CIPO)
```

🟢 **Further subclassification:**

| Parameter | Categories |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Completeness** | Complete vs. Partial |
| **Vascular compromise** | Simple vs. Strangulated |
| **Loop configuration** | Open-loop vs. Closed-loop |
| **Chronicity** | Acute vs. Subacute vs. Chronic |

🔵 **Complete vs. Partial** — the distinction matters for management:
- **Partial (subacute):** Some gas/fluid passes distally; gas visible in colon on X-ray; more amenable to nonoperative management
- **Complete:** No passage of gas or stool; no distal bowel gas on imaging; higher strangulation risk; more likely to require surgery

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management (All Patients)

🟢 **The "drip and suck" protocol remains the cornerstone:**
1. **NPO** — absolute nil per os
2. **Nasogastric tube** — decompression (reduces distension, aspiration risk)
3. **IV fluid resuscitation** — aggressive crystalloid; correct electrolytes; monitor urine output (target >0.5 mL/kg/hr)
4. **Foley catheter** — accurate fluid balance
5. **Analgesia** — IV opioids or NSAIDs (opioids do NOT mask peritonitis in modern practice)
6. **VTE prophylaxis** — immobilized, dehydrated patients are at high DVT risk
7. **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** — if strangulation suspected, septic, or proceeding to surgery

### A. Adhesive Small Bowel Obstruction

🟢 **Adhesions cause 60–75% of SBO** in developed countries. Risk factors: prior surgery (especially colorectal, gynecological), peritonitis, radiation.

🟢 **Nonoperative management (NOM) succeeds in 65–80% of adhesive SBO:**
- Criteria: partial obstruction, no signs of strangulation, no closed-loop on CT
- Duration: **48–72 hours** of NGT decompression and IV fluids with serial abdominal examination every 4–6 hours
- Monitor for clinical deterioration: increasing pain, fever, tachycardia, rising WBC/lactate

🔵 **WSES 2017 Bologna Guidelines — key recommendations:**
1. CT with IV contrast is recommended for all patients with SBO (strong recommendation)
2. Water-soluble contrast (Gastrografin) at 24–48 hours if no resolution — both diagnostic and therapeutic
3. NOM should not exceed **72 hours** without resolution
4. Laparoscopic adhesiolysis is feasible and preferred in selected cases (first episode, single band suspected, no dense matting)
5. Open surgery for: multiple prior operations, anticipated dense adhesions, hostile abdomen

🔵 **Laparoscopic vs. open adhesiolysis:**

| Factor | Laparoscopic | Open |
|--------|-------------|------|
| **Indication** | First episode, predicted single band | Recurrent, multiple prior surgeries |
| **Conversion rate** | 15–30% | — |
| **Complications** | Inadvertent enterotomy 6–10% (higher if dense) | Higher wound complications |
| **LOS** | Shorter (2–4 days) | Longer (5–7 days) |
| **Recurrence** | Likely lower (fewer new adhesions) | Higher |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **Bologna Guidelines scoring system** stratifies adhesive SBO patients into low, medium, and high risk for surgical intervention using CT features (complete obstruction, small bowel feces sign, free fluid, mesenteric edema, reduced wall enhancement). This guides timing and approach.

### B. Large Bowel Obstruction

🟢 **Colorectal carcinoma** accounts for ~60% of LBO, most commonly in the **sigmoid colon** and **splenic flexure** (watershed areas).

🔵 **Emergency surgery vs. stent as bridge to surgery (SBTS):**

The concept of SBTS: Place a self-expanding metallic stent (SEMS) endoscopically to relieve obstruction, allow bowel preparation, optimize the patient, then perform elective single-stage resection with primary anastomosis.

🔵 **Key trial evidence:**

| Trial | Findings |
|-------|---------|
| **ESCO (2017)** | SBTS achieved higher primary anastomosis rate; no difference in overall morbidity |
| **CReST (2022)** | No significant difference in 30-day mortality; higher stent-related perforation (7%); similar stoma rates at 6 months |
| **European meta-analyses** | Concerns about tumor seeding from stent-related perforation reducing long-term oncological outcomes |

🔵 **Current consensus:** SBTS is reasonable in patients with **left-sided malignant LBO** who are high surgical risk (ASA ≥ III) or as a bridge in those requiring optimization. In fit patients with curable disease, **emergency surgery** (resection ± primary anastomosis or Hartmann's procedure) remains preferred.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** ESGE 2020 guidelines recommend **against** routine SBTS as bridge to elective surgery in curable left-sided colon cancer, citing concerns about perforation (up to 7%) and potential adverse oncological outcomes. SBTS is recommended primarily for **palliation** in incurable/metastatic disease.

### C. Sigmoid Volvulus

🟢 **Epidemiology:** Most common form of colonic volvulus (60–75% of all volvulus). Classic patient: elderly, institutionalized, psychiatric patients, chronic constipation, high-fiber diet (endemic in "volvulus belt" — Africa, Middle East, South America).

🟢 **Management algorithm:**
1. **No peritonitis → Endoscopic decompression** (rigid or flexible sigmoidoscopy): success rate 70–90%
2. **Semi-elective sigmoidectomy with primary anastomosis** — during the same admission after bowel preparation
3. **Recurrence without definitive surgery: 40–60%** — this drives the recommendation for semi-elective resection

🔵 **If gangrenous bowel is found** at endoscopy (dark/necrotic mucosa, bloody effluent) → abandon endoscopic decompression → **emergency laparotomy** → Hartmann's procedure (resection with end colostomy).

### D. Cecal Volvulus

🔵 **Key difference from sigmoid volvulus:** Endoscopic decompression is **ineffective** (success rate <5%) and not recommended.

🟢 **Treatment: Right hemicolectomy** — the definitive procedure. Primary ileocolic anastomosis is usually safe even in the emergency setting (proximal bowel is not dilated).

🔵 **Cecopexy** (fixation without resection) has a recurrence rate of 10–40% and is reserved only for patients too unfit for resection.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Cecal volvulus has three subtypes — axial torsion (most common), cecal bascule (anterior folding without torsion), and combined. Bascule historically was considered less dangerous, but recent data shows similar complication rates, and all require operative management.

## 8. Complications

🟢 **Complications of untreated or delayed obstruction:**

| Complication | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Bowel necrosis/perforation** | Strangulation → ischemia → transmural necrosis |
| **Peritonitis** | Perforation or bacterial translocation |
| **Sepsis and multiorgan failure** | Bacteremia, endotoxemia |
| **Aspiration pneumonia** | Vomiting with impaired consciousness |
| **Hypovolemic shock** | Third-spacing, vomiting, poor intake |
| **Electrolyte derangements** | Massive GI losses |
| **Short bowel syndrome** | After extensive resection for necrosis |
| **Abdominal compartment syndrome** | Massive bowel dilation |

🔵 **Complications of surgery for obstruction:**
- Anastomotic leak (higher in emergency setting, especially with ischemic bowel margins)
- Inadvertent enterotomy (6–10% in adhesiolysis, up to 20% in hostile abdomen)
- Wound infection (contaminated/dirty field)
- Recurrent obstruction (adhesions: 12–17% over 10 years)
- Stoma-related complications (parastomal hernia, retraction, prolapse)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **second-look laparotomy** is an important concept in borderline viability cases. When bowel viability is uncertain at initial surgery, the abdomen can be temporarily closed and re-explored at 24–48 hours. Adjuncts for assessing viability intraoperatively include: IV fluorescein with Wood's lamp, ICG fluorescence angiography (increasingly favored), and Doppler ultrasonography.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.49; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.25*

## 9. Special Types

### Gallstone Ileus

🔵 **A rare cause of SBO (1–4%)** but accounts for up to 25% of SBO in patients >65 years without prior surgery.

🟢 **Pathophysiology:** A large gallstone (>2.5 cm) erodes through an inflamed gallbladder into the adjacent duodenum (cholecystoduodenal fistula, most common) → migrates distally → impacts at the narrowest point of the small bowel, typically the **terminal ileum**.

🟢 **Rigler's triad** (pathognomonic on imaging):
1. **Pneumobilia** (air in the biliary tree)
2. **SBO**
3. **Ectopic gallstone** (often calcified, visible on CT)

> *Cross-reference: Chapter 2 — Cholelithiasis & Its Complications*

🔵 **Treatment — ongoing debate:**
- **Enterolithotomy alone** (stone extraction via enterotomy) — safest in acute setting; addresses the emergency
- **One-stage procedure** (enterolithotomy + cholecystectomy + fistula repair) — definitive but higher morbidity; reserved for fit patients
- **Cholecystectomy can be deferred** — only 10% develop recurrent gallstone ileus; the fistula often closes spontaneously

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Always examine the entire bowel — **16% have a second impacted stone** that can cause recurrent obstruction if missed. Bouveret syndrome is the rare variant where the gallstone impacts in the duodenum, causing gastric outlet obstruction.

### Intussusception in Adults

🔵 Adult intussusception differs fundamentally from pediatric:
- **90% have a pathological lead point** (polyp, tumor, Meckel's diverticulum)
- **25% are malignant** (higher in colocolonic intussusception — up to 40%)
- **Air/hydrostatic reduction is NOT appropriate** — oncologic resection is standard
- **Small bowel:** resection with primary anastomosis (lead point usually benign)
- **Colonic:** formal oncological resection (lead point often malignant)

### Ogilvie's Syndrome (Acute Colonic Pseudo-Obstruction)

🔵 **Definition:** Massive colonic dilation without mechanical obstruction. Caused by autonomic imbalance (excess sympathetic / deficient parasympathetic tone).

🟢 **Typical patient:** Elderly, hospitalized, post-surgical (especially orthopedic, cardiac), critically ill, on opioids/anticholinergics, with electrolyte abnormalities.

🔵 **Management — stepwise:**
1. **Correct reversible factors:** Stop offending medications (opioids, anticholinergics), correct electrolytes (K⁺, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺), mobilize patient
2. **Conservative (24–48 hrs):** NGT, rectal tube, ambulation, knee-chest positioning
3. **Neostigmine** (2 mg IV over 3–5 minutes): Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor; response rate **80–90%** within minutes. Monitor for **bradycardia** — atropine at bedside. Contraindicated in mechanical obstruction, active bronchospasm, recent MI.
4. **Colonoscopic decompression:** If neostigmine fails or is contraindicated; success 60–90% but recurrence common (30–40%)
5. **Surgery (cecostomy or colectomy):** Only for perforation, peritonitis, or failure of all above

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The cecal diameter threshold for intervention is debated but generally **>12 cm** or **rapid distension over 24–48 hours** warrants escalation. The PONEC trial (Loftus 1999) established neostigmine as effective for Ogilvie's — response was dramatic, with a median time to decompression of **4 minutes** after infusion.

### Paralytic Ileus

🟢 **The most common functional obstruction** — expected after any abdominal surgery. Normal resolution: stomach 24 hours, small bowel 24–48 hours, colon 48–72 hours.

🟢 **Prolonged ileus** (>3–5 days postoperatively) — look for: intra-abdominal abscess, electrolyte imbalance (especially hypokalemia), opioid use, retroperitoneal hematoma.

🔵 **Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols** reduce ileus duration through: early feeding, minimized opioids (multimodal analgesia), epidural anesthesia, gum chewing, alvimopan (peripheral mu-opioid receptor antagonist — reduces postoperative ileus by ~12 hours), and early mobilization.

## 10. Other Important Topics

### Radiation Enteritis
🔵 Chronic radiation injury (typically after pelvic radiation for cervical, rectal, or prostate cancer) causes fibrosis, strictures, and adhesions — may present with SBO years after treatment. Management is challenging: adhesiolysis is hazardous in irradiated tissue; strictureplasty or bypass may be preferred over resection.

### Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA) Syndrome
🔵 Compression of the third part of the duodenum between the SMA and aorta, due to loss of the intervening mesenteric fat pad. Typically in young patients after rapid weight loss, scoliosis surgery, or body casting. Aortomesenteric angle reduced to <22° (normal 38–56°). Initial management: nutritional rehabilitation. Surgery (duodenojejunostomy or Strong's procedure) if conservative measures fail.

### Abdominal Cocoon (Sclerosing Encapsulating Peritonitis)
🔴 Rare cause of recurrent SBO where the small bowel is encased in a fibrous membrane. Idiopathic (tropical) or secondary (peritoneal dialysis, practolol, tuberculosis). CT shows a "cauliflower" or "cocoon" sign. Treatment: careful surgical excision of the membrane with adhesiolysis.

### Prevention of Adhesive Obstruction
🔵 **Adhesion barriers:** Hyaluronic acid-carboxymethylcellulose membrane (Seprafilm) reduces adhesion formation but evidence for reducing clinically significant SBO is limited. Meticulous surgical technique (minimize peritoneal trauma, avoid foreign bodies, ensure hemostasis) remains the best prevention.

## 11. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Intestinal obstruction is classified as mechanical or functional**, and further as small bowel or large bowel — each with different causes, presentation, and management.
2. **Adhesions are the #1 cause of SBO in developed countries (60–75%)**; hernias are #1 worldwide. Colorectal cancer is the #1 cause of LBO.
3. **Cardinal features:** Colicky pain, vomiting (early in proximal SBO, late in distal/LBO), distension, and obstipation.
4. **A change from colicky to constant pain signals strangulation** — the most critical symptom evolution.
5. **CT with IV contrast is the gold standard** for diagnosis, identifying the transition point, cause, and complications (sensitivity 92–95%).
6. **Initial management for all: "Drip and suck"** — NGT decompression, IV fluid resuscitation, electrolyte correction, serial examination.
7. **Always examine hernia orifices.** A missed incarcerated hernia is an avoidable disaster.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

8. **Closed-loop obstruction is a surgical emergency** — NGT decompression is futile; CT shows C/U-shaped loop with converging mesentery.
9. **Gastrografin challenge** is both diagnostic and therapeutic in adhesive SBO: contrast reaching the colon at 6–8 hours predicts resolution (Sn 96%, Sp 98%).
10. **WSES guidelines: NOM for adhesive SBO should not exceed 72 hours.** Operate if no resolution or clinical deterioration.
11. **Sigmoid volvulus: endoscopic decompression → semi-elective sigmoidectomy.** Cecal volvulus: right hemicolectomy (endoscopic decompression is ineffective).
12. **Stent as bridge to surgery in malignant LBO** is reserved for high-risk patients and palliation; not recommended routinely in curable disease (ESGE 2020).
13. **Ogilvie's syndrome:** Correct reversible factors → neostigmine (80–90% response) → colonoscopic decompression → surgery only as last resort.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

14. **No clinical or lab parameter reliably distinguishes simple from strangulated obstruction.** CT findings (reduced wall enhancement, free fluid, pneumatosis) are the best indicators but still miss ~17%.
15. **Gallstone ileus — Rigler's triad** (pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic stone); always check for a second stone (16%); enterolithotomy alone is adequate in the acute setting.
16. **The small bowel feces sign on CT** indicates prolonged distal SBO with bacterial overgrowth — correlates with higher need for surgery.
17. **ICG fluorescence angiography** is increasingly used intraoperatively to assess bowel viability, reducing unnecessary resection and missed ischemia.
18. **Cecal diameter >12 cm is the classical threshold for perforation risk** in LBO/Ogilvie's — but rate of distension matters more than absolute diameter (Law of Laplace: tension = pressure × radius).
