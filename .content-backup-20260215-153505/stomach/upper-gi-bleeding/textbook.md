## 1. History — The Ancient Hemorrhage

Upper gastrointestinal bleeding (UGIB) has been recognized since antiquity. Ancient Egyptian papyri describe "vomiting of blood," and Hippocrates noted that hematemesis was an ominous sign in febrile illness. The modern understanding, however, evolved through three pivotal eras.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1823 | **Jean Cruveilhier** — First systematic description of gastric ulcers and their complications including hemorrhage |
| 1881 | **Mikulicz** — First successful gastrorrhaphy for bleeding gastric ulcer |
| 1897 | **Karl Landsteiner** — Discovery of blood groups, enabling transfusion for hemorrhagic shock |
| 1958 | **Basil Hirschowitz** — First flexible fiberoptic gastroscope; revolutionized diagnosis of UGIB |
| 1970s | **Protell, Papp & colleagues** — Endoscopic hemostasis techniques (electrocoagulation, laser) |
| 1989 | **Lau et al.** — Landmark trial establishing therapeutic endoscopy as definitive management |
| 2004 | **Barkun et al.** — First international consensus on nonvariceal UGIB management |

The history of UGIB mirrors the evolution of gastroenterology itself — from blind clinical diagnosis to direct visualization and intervention. Before flexible endoscopy, UGIB carried mortality rates exceeding 30%. Today, mortality for nonvariceal UGIB is 2–10%, and for variceal bleeding 15–25% — a testament to endoscopic and pharmacological advances.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19–20; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46*

## 2. Anatomy — The Bleeding Landscape

Understanding UGIB requires knowledge of the vascular anatomy of the upper GI tract — from the esophagus to the ligament of Treitz.

🟢 **Definition:** UGIB originates proximal to the **ligament of Treitz** (duodenojejunal flexion). This includes the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum.

### Esophageal Blood Supply

🟢 The esophagus receives segmental blood supply:
- **Cervical:** Inferior thyroid artery
- **Thoracic:** Bronchial arteries, direct aortic branches
- **Abdominal:** Left gastric artery and inferior phrenic artery

🔵 **Submucosal venous plexus** drains into the azygos system (systemic) and left gastric/short gastric veins (portal). This **portosystemic anastomosis** at the gastroesophageal junction is the anatomical basis for esophageal varices in portal hypertension.

### Gastric Blood Supply

🟢 The stomach has the **richest blood supply** of any abdominal organ — supplied by branches of the celiac trunk:
- **Lesser curvature:** Left gastric artery (most common source of bleeding in PUD) + right gastric artery
- **Greater curvature:** Left gastroepiploic (from splenic) + right gastroepiploic (from gastroduodenal)
- **Fundus:** Short gastric arteries (from splenic artery)

🔵 The extensive anastomotic network means that ligation of a single artery rarely devascularizes the stomach — but also means that bleeding ulcers often have dual arterial supply, complicating hemostasis.

### Duodenal Blood Supply

🟢 The **gastroduodenal artery (GDA)** runs posterior to the first part of the duodenum. A posterior duodenal ulcer eroding into the GDA is the classic cause of **massive UGIB** — this is the single most important anatomical relationship in UGIB surgery.

🔵 The pancreaticoduodenal arcades (superior and inferior) connect the celiac and SMA territories. The duodenum therefore has dual blood supply, and duodenal ulcer bleeding often involves these arcade vessels.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **left gastric artery** gives rise to the **posterior gastric artery** in ~70% of individuals — this vessel runs behind the stomach and can be a source of occult bleeding in posterior gastric ulcers that is missed on standard angiography if only anterior projections are obtained.

## 3. Etiology — Why the Gut Bleeds

🟢 **Causes of UGIB by frequency:**

| Cause | Frequency | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Peptic ulcer disease** | 30–50% | Duodenal > Gastric; *H. pylori* and NSAIDs are the two dominant risk factors |
| **Esophageal/gastric varices** | 10–20% | In patients with portal hypertension; high mortality |
| **Mallory-Weiss tear** | 5–10% | Mucosal tear at GEJ from retching/vomiting |
| **Erosive gastritis/duodenitis** | 5–15% | NSAIDs, alcohol, stress, critical illness |
| **Esophagitis** | 5–10% | Severe reflux, infectious (CMV, HSV, Candida in immunocompromised) |
| **Malignancy** | 2–5% | Gastric cancer, GISTs, esophageal cancer |
| **Dieulafoy's lesion** | 1–2% | Caliber-persistent submucosal artery; classically in proximal stomach |
| **Angiodysplasia** | 1–2% | More common in elderly; often multiple |
| **Aortoenteric fistula** | <1% | **Must exclude in any patient with prior aortic graft surgery** |
| **Hemobilia / hemosuccus pancreaticus** | Rare | Post-procedural or pseudoaneurysm-related |

🔵 **Important clinical distinction:**

- **Variceal** vs. **Nonvariceal** UGIB — this is the **first branch point** in management, as resuscitation strategy, pharmacotherapy, endoscopic technique, and prognosis differ fundamentally.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Dieulafoy's lesion:** A submucosal arteriole (1–3 mm diameter, 10× normal caliber) that fails to branch into capillaries before reaching the mucosa. Located in the **proximal stomach** (lesser curvature, within 6 cm of GEJ) in 70–80% of cases. The overlying mucosa is normal — there is no ulcer. Bleeding is arterial and intermittent, making diagnosis notoriously difficult. The vessel must be seen at endoscopy during active bleeding or with a visible vessel/adherent clot to confirm diagnosis. Endoscopic band ligation or hemoclip application is first-line treatment.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19–20; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.20*

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Modes of Presentation

🟢 **Classic presentations — know these patterns:**

| Presentation | Description | Suggests |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Hematemesis** | Vomiting of fresh red blood or "coffee grounds" | Active or recent UGIB; red blood = arterial or brisk venous |
| **Melena** | Black, tarry, foul-smelling stool | UGIB (>90%); requires as little as 50–100 mL of blood in upper GI tract |
| **Hematochezia** | Fresh red blood per rectum | Usually LGIB, but occurs in **massive UGIB** (>1000 mL) with rapid transit |
| **Syncope / shock** | Postural hypotension, tachycardia, altered sensorium | Significant hemorrhage (>30% blood volume loss) |
| **Occult bleeding** | Iron-deficiency anemia without overt bleeding | Chronic low-grade UGIB (Cameron lesions, GAVE, angiodysplasia) |

🟢 **Key pearl:** Hematemesis is always upper GI in origin. Melena is upper GI in >90% of cases. **Hematochezia with hemodynamic instability should prompt consideration of a massive UGIB** before assuming lower GI source.

🔵 **Coffee-ground emesis** results from the conversion of hemoglobin to hematin by gastric acid — it indicates that blood has been in contact with gastric acid for some time (slower or stopped bleeding).

### History and Physical Examination

🟢 **Critical history elements:**

- **Medications:** NSAIDs (including OTC), aspirin, anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs), antiplatelet agents (clopidogrel), SSRIs, corticosteroids
- **Alcohol use:** Cirrhosis risk (variceal bleeding), alcoholic gastropathy
- **Prior bleeding episodes, PUD history, *H. pylori* status**
- **Liver disease stigmata:** Jaundice, ascites, spider angiomata, palmar erythema → suspect variceal bleeding
- **Prior aortic surgery:** Aortoenteric fistula (herald bleed → massive exsanguination)
- **Retching/vomiting before hematemesis:** Mallory-Weiss tear
- **Weight loss, early satiety:** Malignancy

🟢 **Physical exam priorities:**
1. **Hemodynamic assessment** (pulse, BP, postural changes) — the most important initial step
2. **Signs of chronic liver disease** (spider nevi, caput medusae, ascites, splenomegaly)
3. **Abdominal exam** (tenderness, peritonitis, masses)
4. **Digital rectal exam** (melena, fresh blood, stool color)
5. **Nasogastric aspirate** — controversial but a bloody or coffee-ground aspirate confirms UGIB (a clear aspirate does NOT exclude it — 15% of duodenal bleeding shows clear NG aspirate due to competent pylorus)

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19*

## 5. Risk Stratification — Who Dies and Who Goes Home?

### Pre-Endoscopy Scoring

🟢 **Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS):** The most important pre-endoscopy scoring system.

| Variable | Points |
|----------|--------|
| BUN ≥18.2 mg/dL (6.5 mmol/L) | 2 |
| BUN ≥22.4 (8.0) | 3 |
| BUN ≥28 (10.0) | 4 |
| BUN ≥70 (25.0) | 6 |
| Hemoglobin (men) 12–13, (women) 10–12 g/dL | 1 |
| Hemoglobin 10–12 (men), <10 (women) | 3 |
| Hemoglobin <10 (men) | 6 |
| Systolic BP 100–109 | 1 |
| SBP 90–99 | 2 |
| SBP <90 | 3 |
| Heart rate ≥100 | 1 |
| Melena | 1 |
| Syncope | 2 |
| Liver disease | 2 |
| Heart failure | 2 |

🟢 **GBS = 0** → Very low risk; can be managed as **outpatient** without endoscopy. This is the primary clinical utility: identifying patients safe for discharge.

🔵 **GBS ≥1** → Hospital admission recommended; higher scores predict need for intervention, transfusion, surgery, or death.

### Post-Endoscopy Scoring

🟢 **Rockall Score** — Uses both pre-endoscopy (clinical) and post-endoscopy variables:

| Variable | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|
| Age | <60 | 60–79 | ≥80 | — |
| Shock | None | Pulse >100 | SBP <100 | — |
| Comorbidity | None | — | CCF, IHD, major | Renal failure, liver failure, metastatic cancer |
| Diagnosis | Mallory-Weiss, no lesion | All other | GI malignancy | — |
| Stigmata of recent hemorrhage | None or dark spot | — | Blood, adherent clot, visible vessel, spurting | — |

🟢 **Rockall ≤2** = Low risk (rebleed <5%, mortality <0.2%). **Rockall ≥8** = High risk (mortality >40%).

🔵 **AIMS65 Score:** Albumin <3, INR >1.5, Mental status altered, SBP ≤90, age ≥65. Each variable = 1 point. Better predictor of **mortality** than GBS or Rockall. Score ≥2 → ICU admission recommended.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The **ABC score** (age, blood tests including albumin, comorbidities) is an emerging validated tool that outperforms Rockall and AIMS65 for mortality prediction. Machine learning models (e.g., PERSEVERE) integrating EHR data in real-time are being developed but not yet in clinical use.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.20*

## 6. Initial Resuscitation and Management

### The First 60 Minutes

🟢 **ABCDE approach — UGIB is a resuscitation emergency before it is a diagnostic problem:**

1. **Airway:** Protect the airway. Intubation for massive hematemesis, altered sensorium, or high aspiration risk (especially before endoscopy in unstable patients with active hematemesis)
2. **Breathing:** Supplemental O₂
3. **Circulation:**
   - Two large-bore IV cannulae (≥16G) or central access
   - Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation (balanced solutions preferred)
   - **Crossmatch and prepare blood products**
   - **Restrictive transfusion strategy** (target Hb 7–8 g/dL) in hemodynamically stable patients

🟢 **Restrictive vs. liberal transfusion — a critical concept:**

The landmark **Villanueva et al. (2013)** RCT demonstrated:
- Restrictive (transfuse at Hb <7) vs. liberal (transfuse at Hb <9) strategy
- Restrictive group had **lower mortality** (5% vs. 9%), lower rebleeding (10% vs. 16%), and fewer complications
- Especially beneficial in **Child A and B cirrhotics** — over-transfusion raises portal pressure and worsens variceal bleeding
- **Exception:** Patients with acute coronary syndromes, massive ongoing hemorrhage, or hemodynamic instability — transfuse to Hb ≥8–9

🔵 **Blood product management:**
- **Packed RBCs:** As above, restrictive threshold
- **Platelets:** Transfuse if <50,000/μL with active bleeding
- **FFP:** INR >1.5 with active bleeding (though evidence is limited; prioritize endoscopy over complete INR correction)
- **Massive transfusion protocol:** Activate if estimated blood loss >30% volume, ongoing hemodynamic instability despite 2L crystalloid, or transfusion of ≥4 units pRBC in 1 hour

### Pharmacological Therapy

🟢 **IV Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI):**
- **High-dose bolus:** Omeprazole/pantoprazole 80 mg IV bolus → 8 mg/hr infusion for 72 hours
- **Rationale:** Clot stability requires pH >6; gastric acid dissolves clots and impairs platelet aggregation
- 🔵 **Timing controversy:** Pre-endoscopy PPI may downgrade stigmata of recent hemorrhage (SRH) and reduce need for endoscopic therapy, but **does NOT reduce rebleeding, surgery, or mortality** when given before endoscopy. Nevertheless, widely administered pre-endoscopy in practice.
- 🟢 Post-endoscopy high-dose PPI **does reduce rebleeding and mortality** in patients with high-risk stigmata (active bleeding, visible vessel, adherent clot)

🟢 **For suspected variceal bleeding — start IMMEDIATELY, do not wait for endoscopy:**
- **IV Octreotide:** 50 μg bolus → 50 μg/hr infusion for 3–5 days (reduces splanchnic blood flow, lowers portal pressure)
- **OR Terlipressin:** 2 mg IV q4h for 48 hours → then 1 mg q4h (vasopressin analogue; the only vasoactive agent shown to reduce mortality in variceal bleeding)
- **Prophylactic antibiotics:** Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily for 7 days (reduces infection, rebleeding, and **mortality** in cirrhotic patients with UGIB — NNT = 10)

🔵 **Prokinetics:** Erythromycin 250 mg IV 30–90 minutes before endoscopy improves gastric visualization (clears blood/clots), reduces need for second-look endoscopy, and shortens procedure time. Recommended in guidelines (ESGE, ACG) for patients with suspected large-volume hematemesis.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Tranexamic Acid:** The **HALT-IT** trial (2020, 12,009 patients) definitively showed that tranexamic acid does NOT reduce mortality in UGIB and may increase venous thromboembolic events. It should **not** be used routinely despite its efficacy in trauma hemorrhage.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19–20; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.20*

## 7. Endoscopic Management — The Definitive Step

### Timing of Endoscopy

🟢 **Current recommendations:**

| Scenario | Timing | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| **Hemodynamically stable, nonvariceal** | Within **24 hours** of presentation | Standard of care; earlier does not improve outcomes |
| **High-risk features** (GBS ≥12, hemodynamic instability after resuscitation) | Within **12 hours** | May improve outcomes in very high-risk patients (ESGE 2021) |
| **Suspected variceal bleeding** | Within **12 hours** | Earlier control reduces mortality |
| **Massive ongoing hemorrhage, hemodynamic instability** | **Emergent** (after resuscitation) | Endoscopy is both diagnostic and therapeutic |

🔵 **Ultra-early endoscopy (<6 hours)** has NOT been shown to improve outcomes in nonvariceal UGIB (the 2020 Lau et al. RCT confirmed this). Focus on adequate resuscitation before endoscopy.

### Forrest Classification (Nonvariceal Bleeding)

🟢 **Essential classification — dictates endoscopic treatment and prognosis:**

| Forrest Class | Description | Rebleed Risk (no Rx) | Endoscopic Rx? |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Ia** | Spurting hemorrhage | 90% | ✅ Yes |
| **Ib** | Oozing hemorrhage | 55% | ✅ Yes |
| **IIa** | Non-bleeding visible vessel | 43% | ✅ Yes |
| **IIb** | Adherent clot | 22% | ⚠️ Consider (remove clot, treat underlying lesion) |
| **IIc** | Flat pigmented spot | 7% | ❌ No |
| **III** | Clean-base ulcer | 2% | ❌ No (early discharge) |

🟢 **Rule:** Endoscopic therapy is indicated for **Forrest Ia, Ib, and IIa** lesions. Forrest IIb is debated — current guidelines (ESGE 2021) recommend clot removal (with irrigation, not mechanical avulsion) and treatment of the underlying lesion if high-risk stigmata are found.

### Endoscopic Techniques

🟢 **Modalities:**

| Technique | Mechanism | Best For |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Injection** (epinephrine 1:10,000) | Tamponade + vasoconstriction | **Never use as monotherapy** — always combine with a second modality |
| **Thermal** (bipolar electrocoagulation, heater probe) | Coaptive coagulation of vessel | Ulcer bleeding (Forrest Ia/Ib/IIa) |
| **Mechanical** (hemoclips, over-the-scope clip [OTSC]) | Physical compression of vessel | Visible vessels, Dieulafoy's, Mallory-Weiss; OTSC for larger/fibrotic vessels |
| **Topical** (hemostatic powder — TC-325/Hemospray) | Contact-activated hemostatic barrier | Diffuse oozing, tumoral bleeding, bridge to definitive therapy |
| **Band ligation** | Mechanical strangulation | Esophageal varices (gold standard), Dieulafoy's |

🟢 **Combination therapy** (injection + thermal OR injection + mechanical) is superior to monotherapy for high-risk lesions. Standard of care for Forrest Ia/Ib/IIa.

🔵 **Over-the-scope clip (OTSC):** A bear-claw-like clip that captures a larger tissue volume than standard hemoclips. The **STING trial** (2019) showed OTSC is superior to standard therapy for **rebleeding** after initial endoscopic failure. Current guidelines recommend OTSC as first-line for endoscopic retreatment of rebleeding peptic ulcers.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Doppler endoscopic probe (DEP):** Allows real-time assessment of blood flow beneath an ulcer base. Studies show that treating until Doppler signal disappears reduces rebleeding rates to <5%, compared to 10–15% with visual assessment alone. Not widely available but represents the future of personalized endoscopic hemostasis.

### Variceal Bleeding — Endoscopic Specifics

🟢 **Esophageal varices:** Endoscopic **band ligation (EVL)** is the gold standard.
- Superior to sclerotherapy (fewer complications, lower rebleed rate)
- Apply bands from GEJ upward in a spiral pattern
- Repeat every 2–4 weeks until eradication

🟢 **Gastric varices — Sarin classification:**

| Type | Location | Treatment |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **GOV1** | Extension of esophageal varices along lesser curvature | EVL (treat like esophageal varices) |
| **GOV2** | Extension toward fundus | **Cyanoacrylate glue injection** or TIPS |
| **IGV1** | Isolated fundal varices | Cyanoacrylate glue or TIPS (BRTO if available) |
| **IGV2** | Isolated varices elsewhere in stomach | Rare; treat based on size and bleeding |

🔵 **Cyanoacrylate (tissue glue) injection** is the endoscopic treatment of choice for **fundal varices** (GOV2, IGV1). Hemostasis rate >90%. Risk of systemic embolization (pulmonary, cerebral) is 2–5%.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Balloon-occluded retrograde transvenous obliteration (BRTO):** An interventional radiological technique that obliterates gastric varices via the gastrorenal shunt. Particularly effective for IGV1 with a gastrorenal shunt. Preserves hepatic portal perfusion (unlike TIPS), which may improve hepatic encephalopathy. Increasingly used in specialized centers.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19–20; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.20–21*

## 8. Management of Refractory/Recurrent Bleeding

### When Endoscopy Fails

🟢 **The sequence for refractory UGIB:**

```
First endoscopy fails or rebleeding occurs
    │
    ▼
REPEAT ENDOSCOPY (with OTSC or dual therapy)
    │ Fails
    ▼
INTERVENTIONAL RADIOLOGY — Transarterial Embolization (TAE)
    │ Fails or unavailable
    ▼
SURGERY
```

### Interventional Radiology

🔵 **Transarterial embolization (TAE):**
- Selective catheterization of the bleeding vessel (usually GDA, left gastric, or splenic artery branches)
- Embolic agents: coils, gelfoam, polyvinyl alcohol particles, glue
- Technical success: 70–90%; clinical success: 50–80%
- Rebleeding rate: 10–30%
- Risk of ischemia is low in the stomach (rich collateral network) but higher in duodenum

🔵 **TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt):**
- Indicated for **variceal bleeding refractory to endoscopy + pharmacotherapy**
- Creates a portosystemic shunt, reducing portal pressure
- 🟢 **Rescue TIPS** for uncontrolled variceal bleeding → reduces mortality from ~80% to ~30%
- 🔵 **Early/preemptive TIPS** (within 72 hours) for high-risk variceal bleeding (Child-Pugh B with active bleeding at endoscopy, or Child-Pugh C 10–13): reduces rebleeding and **mortality** (Garcia-Pagán et al. 2010, confirmed by multiple subsequent studies)
- Complications: hepatic encephalopathy (30–40%), stent dysfunction

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Covered TIPS stents (PTFE-covered) have dramatically improved long-term patency (>80% at 2 years vs. ~50% for bare metal). All current TIPS should use covered stents.

### Surgical Management

🟢 **Indications for surgery in UGIB:**
1. **Massive hemorrhage** not controlled by endoscopy or IR
2. **Hemodynamic instability** despite resuscitation and two failed endoscopic attempts
3. Transfusion requirement >6 units in 24 hours (some use >3 units per 8 hours)
4. **Perforation** coexisting with hemorrhage

🟢 **Surgical options for bleeding peptic ulcer:**

| Location | Procedure |
|----------|-----------|
| **Duodenal ulcer (posterior, GDA bleed)** | Duodenotomy → **three-stitch ligation** (above, below, medial to GDA) → pyloroplasty. Vagotomy added if *H. pylori*-negative |
| **Gastric ulcer** | Ulcer excision/wedge resection (sends for histology — must exclude malignancy) ± distal gastrectomy |
| **Giant gastric ulcer (>2 cm)** | Distal gastrectomy with ulcer inclusion |

🔵 **The three-point suture technique** for posterior duodenal ulcer bleeding is the single most important emergency surgical maneuver in UGIB: a U-stitch placed above and below the GDA to control the transverse pancreatic branch and the GDA proper, plus a horizontal mattress suture through the medial wall of the duodenum to control the transverse duodenal artery.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** In the era of PPIs and *H. pylori* eradication, definitive acid-reducing surgery (vagotomy + drainage or highly selective vagotomy) is rarely added to emergency ulcer surgery. The focus is hemostasis and closure. Acid suppression is achieved medically postoperatively.

> 📚 *See Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.14; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.20; Fischer 7th Ed, Ch.56*

## 9. Special Scenarios

### Aortoenteric Fistula

🔵 **The lethal diagnosis you must never miss:**
- Occurs in 0.4–1.6% of patients with prior aortic graft surgery (secondary AEF)
- **Primary AEF** (no prior surgery) is extremely rare
- **Classic triad:** GI bleeding + abdominal/back pain + pulsatile abdominal mass — but the full triad is present in <25%
- **Herald bleed:** A self-limited UGIB episode that precedes a catastrophic exsanguination by hours to weeks
- **Diagnosis:** CT angiography (air around graft, loss of fat plane between graft and duodenum, contrast extravasation). Endoscopy shows graft material protruding through the duodenal wall (third/fourth part of duodenum).
- **Treatment:** Emergency surgery — graft excision + extra-anatomic bypass (axillobifemoral) OR in situ graft replacement with rifampin-soaked prosthetic or cryopreserved allograft

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Any UGIB in a patient with a history of aortic surgery is an aortoenteric fistula until proven otherwise. Even if initial endoscopy shows another "obvious" source, maintain a high index of suspicion — dual pathology occurs.

### Mallory-Weiss Tear

🟢 Most (80–90%) stop bleeding spontaneously. Endoscopic therapy only for active bleeding. Tears are typically at the **gastric side of the GEJ** on the lesser curvature.

### Gastric Antral Vascular Ectasia (GAVE / "Watermelon Stomach")

🔵 Longitudinal rows of flat, red angiodysplastic lesions radiating from the pylorus, resembling watermelon stripes. Associated with cirrhosis, autoimmune conditions (scleroderma), and chronic renal failure. Causes chronic occult bleeding and iron-deficiency anemia. Treat with **argon plasma coagulation (APC)** or **radiofrequency ablation (RFA)**. Does NOT respond to TIPS (unlike portal hypertensive gastropathy).

### Cameron Lesions

🔵 Linear erosions on the gastric folds at the diaphragmatic hiatus in patients with large **hiatal hernias**. Cause chronic occult blood loss and iron-deficiency anemia. Diagnosed on forward-viewing endoscopy by careful inspection of the hiatal impression. Medical management (PPI + iron) first; hiatal hernia repair for refractory cases.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19–20; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.20*

## 10. Prevention of Rebleeding

### Peptic Ulcer Disease

🟢 **The three pillars of recurrence prevention:**
1. **Test and treat *H. pylori*** — ALL patients with PUD-related UGIB must be tested. False-negative rates are high during acute bleeding (up to 50% for rapid urease test). If negative during acute admission, **retest after 4 weeks** with urea breath test or stool antigen.
2. **PPI therapy** — High-dose IV for 72 hours post-endoscopy for high-risk stigmata → then oral PPI for 8–12 weeks.
3. **NSAID/aspirin review** — Discontinue NSAIDs if possible. If NSAID necessary: use COX-2 selective + PPI co-therapy. Aspirin for cardiovascular prophylaxis: resume within 3–7 days (the **HEAT trial** and prior RCTs show delayed resumption increases cardiovascular mortality without significantly reducing rebleeding).

🔵 **Anticoagulant management:**
- Warfarin: Do NOT delay endoscopy for full INR correction. Vitamin K + FFP/PCC only if INR >2.5 with significant bleeding
- **DOACs:** Short half-life (12–24 hours). Specific reversal agents: idarucizumab (dabigatran), andexanet alfa (anti-Xa agents). Resume anticoagulation within 7–14 days based on thromboembolic risk
- **Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT):** For recent coronary stents — multidisciplinary discussion (GI + cardiology). Aspirin should be continued; P2Y12 inhibitor can be held for 3–5 days and restarted promptly

### Variceal Bleeding — Secondary Prophylaxis

🟢 **After surviving a variceal bleed:**
- **EVL** repeated every 2–4 weeks until variceal eradication + **non-selective beta-blocker (NSBB)** (propranolol or carvedilol)
- Combination (EVL + NSBB) is the standard of care for secondary prophylaxis
- Target: resting heart rate 55–60 bpm, SBP ≥90 mmHg

🔵 **Primary prophylaxis** (before first bleed): NSBB or EVL for medium/large varices. **Carvedilol** (6.25–12.5 mg daily) may be superior to propranolol (additional anti-α1 activity → greater portal pressure reduction).

🔴 **SS Pearl — Baveno VII (2022) updates:**
- Compensated cirrhosis with clinically significant portal hypertension (CSPH) defined by **liver stiffness ≥25 kPa** on transient elastography — can start NSBB without endoscopy
- NSBB may prevent decompensation itself, not just variceal bleeding (**PREDESCI trial**)
- In decompensated cirrhosis (refractory ascites), use NSBB cautiously — some evidence of harm at higher doses

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.46; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.19; Sleisenger 11th Ed, Ch.21*

## 12. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **UGIB = bleeding proximal to the ligament of Treitz.** PUD (30–50%) and varices (10–20%) are the two most common causes.
2. **Hematemesis is always upper GI.** Hematochezia with hemodynamic instability → consider massive UGIB.
3. **Resuscitate before endoscopy.** Restrictive transfusion (Hb target 7–8 g/dL) improves outcomes — Villanueva 2013.
4. **Glasgow-Blatchford Score = 0 → safe for outpatient management.** This is the single most useful pre-endoscopy decision.
5. **EGD within 24 hours** for nonvariceal UGIB; **within 12 hours** for variceal.
6. **Forrest classification** dictates endoscopic treatment: Ia, Ib, IIa → dual therapy. III → early discharge.
7. **Variceal bleeding:** Octreotide/terlipressin + prophylactic antibiotics BEFORE endoscopy. EVL is gold standard.
8. **Always test for *H. pylori*** in PUD-related bleeding. Retest if negative during acute episode.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. **Epinephrine injection is never monotherapy** — always combine with thermal or mechanical.
10. **OTSC** (over-the-scope clip) is first-line for endoscopic retreatment of rebleeding ulcers (STING trial).
11. **Early/preemptive TIPS** (within 72 hours) reduces mortality in high-risk variceal bleeding (Child B with active bleeding, Child C 10–13).
12. **Erythromycin** pre-endoscopy improves visualization in suspected large-volume hematemesis.
13. **Tranexamic acid does NOT work in UGIB** (HALT-IT trial) — do not use.
14. **Three-stitch ligation** of the GDA is the critical surgical technique for posterior duodenal ulcer hemorrhage.
15. **Aspirin for cardiovascular prophylaxis** should be resumed within 3–7 days of UGIB.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

16. **Dieulafoy's lesion:** Caliber-persistent artery in proximal stomach; no overlying ulcer; intermittent arterial bleeding. Band ligation or hemoclip.
17. **Aortoenteric fistula:** Any UGIB with prior aortic surgery = AEF until proven otherwise. "Herald bleed" precedes exsanguination.
18. **Baveno VII:** Liver stiffness ≥25 kPa can initiate NSBB without endoscopic variceal screening. NSBB may prevent decompensation itself (PREDESCI).
19. **BRTO** is an alternative to TIPS for fundal varices with gastrorenal shunt — preserves hepatic perfusion.
20. **Doppler endoscopic probe** reduces rebleeding by guiding therapy to complete arterial flow cessation — the future of personalized hemostasis.
