## 1. History — From Folk Remedies to Burn Centers

Burns are among the oldest recorded injuries in human history. The Edwin Smith Papyrus (~1500 BCE) describes treatments using resin and honey. For millennia, burns were treated with bizarre concoctions — animal dung, egg whites, and boiled oil — often causing more harm than the injury itself.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1607 | **Guilhelmus Fabricius Hildanus** — First classification of burns by depth (three degrees) |
| 1832 | **Guillaume Dupuytren** — Refined burn depth classification to six degrees |
| 1897 | **Robert Wallace** — Introduced skin grafting techniques for burns |
| 1942 | **Cocoanut Grove fire (Boston)** — 492 deaths; revolutionized burn care, fluid resuscitation, and airway management |
| 1952 | **Evans formula** — First systematic fluid resuscitation formula for burns |
| 1968 | **Baxter & Shires** — Developed the **Parkland formula**, still the cornerstone of burn resuscitation |
| 1981 | **Burke & Yannas** — First artificial skin (collagen-glycosaminoglycan matrix) |
| 2000s | **Integra, Matriderm, cultured epithelial autografts** — Era of skin substitutes and regenerative burn surgery |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.19; Schwartz 11th Ed, Ch.8*

The Cocoanut Grove disaster remains the single most transformative event in burn surgery — it spurred the development of dedicated burn units, inhalation injury protocols, and modern fluid resuscitation.

## 2. Epidemiology

🟢 **Global burden:**
- Burns are the **4th most common injury worldwide** after road traffic injuries, falls, and interpersonal violence
- ~180,000 deaths/year globally (WHO); vast majority in low- and middle-income countries
- In India: ~7 million burn injuries annually, ~140,000 deaths
- **Bimodal age distribution:** Young children (scalds) and working-age adults (flame/occupational)
- Males > females globally, except in South Asia (dowry burns, cooking-related injuries)

🟢 **Common etiologies by age:**

| Age Group | Most Common Mechanism |
|-----------|----------------------|
| <5 years | **Scalds** (hot water, tea, cooking liquids) |
| 5–14 years | Flame burns (firecrackers, kerosene) |
| 15–64 years | Flame, occupational (chemical, electrical) |
| >65 years | Scalds, contact burns (impaired sensation/mobility) |

🔵 **Risk factors for burn mortality:** Extremes of age, >40% TBSA, inhalation injury. These three form the basis of the **Baux score** (Age + %TBSA; LA50 ≈ 140 without inhalation injury, ≈110 with).

## 3. Skin Anatomy — Relevant to Burns

🟢 **Why skin anatomy matters:** Burn depth is defined by which layer of skin is destroyed, and management decisions hinge entirely on this.

- **Epidermis** (~0.05–0.1 mm): Keratinocytes in stratified layers; regenerates from basal layer and adnexal structures
- **Dermis** (1–4 mm): Two layers:
  - **Papillary dermis** (superficial) — thin, loose collagen, rich capillary network
  - **Reticular dermis** (deep) — dense collagen, hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands
- **Hypodermis/Subcutis:** Fat, larger vessels, nerves

🟢 **Critical concept:** Skin can regenerate from **epidermal appendages** (hair follicles, sweat glands) embedded deep in the dermis. A burn that destroys all adnexal structures cannot heal by re-epithelialization and **requires surgical intervention** (grafting).

🔵 **Regional skin thickness variation:**
- Thinnest: eyelids (~0.5 mm), medial arm, perineum
- Thickest: back, palms, soles (~4 mm)
- Same thermal insult produces different burn depths at different sites — a scald that causes superficial partial thickness on the back may cause full thickness on the eyelid

## 4. Pathophysiology — Jackson's Burn Wound Model

### Local Response

🟢 **Jackson's three zones (1953):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           BURN WOUND ZONES                   │
│                                              │
│  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐        │
│  │     ZONE OF COAGULATION         │        │
│  │  (Irreversible tissue death)    │        │
│  │  Maximum damage, central area   │        │
│  └─────────────┬───────────────────┘        │
│  ┌─────────────▼───────────────────┐        │
│  │     ZONE OF STASIS              │        │
│  │  (Decreased perfusion,          │        │
│  │   potentially salvageable)      │        │
│  │  KEY THERAPEUTIC TARGET         │        │
│  └─────────────┬───────────────────┘        │
│  ┌─────────────▼───────────────────┐        │
│  │     ZONE OF HYPEREMIA           │        │
│  │  (Vasodilation, will recover)   │        │
│  └─────────────────────────────────┘        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

🟢 **Zone of coagulation:** Central area of maximum thermal damage — irreversible protein denaturation and cell death. No salvage possible.

🟢 **Zone of stasis:** Surrounds the coagulation zone. Decreased perfusion but cells are viable. **This is the therapeutic target** — inadequate resuscitation, infection, edema, or desiccation converts this zone to coagulation (burn wound deepening/conversion). Goal of all early burn care is to prevent this conversion.

🟢 **Zone of hyperemia:** Outermost zone with vasodilation and increased blood flow (inflammatory response). Will recover unless severe sepsis or prolonged hypoperfusion.

🔵 **Burn wound conversion** typically occurs at **48–72 hours** — deepening of the zone of stasis into full-thickness necrosis. Contributing factors: edema, hypotension, infection, topical agents that impair perfusion, hypothermia.

### Systemic Response

🟢 **Burns >20–30% TBSA produce a systemic inflammatory response (burn shock):**

```
Thermal injury → Massive cytokine release
    (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, prostaglandins, histamine)
    → Systemic capillary leak (not just at burn site)
    → Intravascular volume depletion
    → Myocardial depression (burn-specific cardiomyopathy)
    → Increased SVR → Decreased cardiac output
    → End-organ hypoperfusion
```

🟢 **Key features of burn shock:**
- **Capillary leak** is maximal in the first **8–12 hours**, begins resolving by **18–24 hours**
- Both burned and unburned tissue develop edema (systemic leak)
- Hypovolemic + distributive components (unlike pure hemorrhagic shock)
- **Myocardial depression** is independent of volume status — mediated by circulating factors (TNF-α, oxygen free radicals)

🔵 **Metabolic response:** Burns produce the **most profound hypermetabolic state** of any injury:
- Metabolic rate increases 40–100% above baseline (proportional to TBSA)
- Peaks at 5–7 days, can persist for **2 years** after major burns
- Catecholamine-driven: tachycardia, proteolysis, lipolysis, insulin resistance
- Core temperature "reset point" rises to ~38.5°C — patient is not febrile, they are **hypermetabolic**

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The hypermetabolic response after major burns is now understood to persist far longer than initially believed. Jeschke et al. demonstrated elevated resting energy expenditure and catabolic markers for up to **3 years** post-burn. This has implications for long-term nutritional support, anabolic therapies (oxandrolone, propranolol), and rehabilitation protocols.

## 5. Classification — Burn Depth

🟢 **Modern burn depth classification:**

| Classification | Old Term | Depth | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|---------------|----------|-------|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Superficial** | 1st degree | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | 3–7 days; no scar |
| **Superficial partial thickness** | Superficial 2nd degree | Epidermis + papillary dermis | Pink, moist, blisters, brisk capillary refill | Very painful | 7–14 days; minimal scar |
| **Deep partial thickness** | Deep 2nd degree | Epidermis + reticular dermis | Pale/mottled, sluggish capillary refill, less moist | Decreased | 14–21+ days; scarring likely; often needs surgery |
| **Full thickness** | 3rd degree | Entire dermis destroyed | White/waxy/charred, leathery, no blanching | Insensate | Will NOT heal; requires grafting |
| **4th degree** | — | Extends to muscle, bone, tendon | Charred, exposed deep structures | Insensate | Requires flap/amputation |

🟢 **Clinical pearl:** The most difficult distinction is between **superficial partial thickness** and **deep partial thickness** — this determines whether a burn will heal spontaneously or require surgery. Examination at 48–72 hours is more reliable than at presentation.

🔵 **Adjuncts for burn depth assessment:**
- **Laser Doppler imaging (LDI):** Measures dermal perfusion; >95% accuracy for predicting healing by day 3–5. Gold standard in specialized centers.
- **Indocyanine green (ICG) angiography:** Emerging; real-time perfusion mapping
- Clinical assessment alone has only 60–75% accuracy

🔴 **SS Pearl:** LDI has been shown to reduce time to decision for surgery by 2–3 days and decrease unnecessary operations by 20–40%. NICE (UK) recommends LDI for all indeterminate-depth burns not healed by day 14.

## 6. Assessment — TBSA Estimation

🟢 **Three methods for TBSA estimation:**

### A. Rule of Nines (Wallace)

```
Adult Rule of Nines:
┌──────────────────────────┐
│  Head & Neck      =  9%  │
│  Each Upper Limb  =  9%  │
│  Anterior Trunk   = 18%  │
│  Posterior Trunk   = 18%  │
│  Each Lower Limb  = 18%  │
│  Perineum         =  1%  │
│  TOTAL            = 100% │
└──────────────────────────┘
```

🟢 **Pediatric modification (Lund & Browder is preferred):** In children, the head is proportionally larger and limbs smaller:
- Infant head = 18% (vs. 9% adult); each leg = 14% (vs. 18%)
- Changes ~1% per year of age

### B. Lund & Browder Chart

🟢 **Most accurate method.** Age-adjusted chart accounting for changing body proportions. Should be used for all significant burns, especially in children. Available as standardized forms in all burn units.

### C. Palmar Method

🟢 The patient's **palm (including fingers)** ≈ **1% TBSA**. Useful for small or scattered burns.

🔵 **Common errors in TBSA estimation:**
- Including superficial (1st degree) burns — these should be **excluded** from TBSA calculations for resuscitation
- Overestimation in obese patients
- Initial overestimation is the most common error (leads to over-resuscitation → fluid creep)

## 7. Initial Management — The First 24 Hours

### A. Primary Survey (ATLS Approach)

🟢 **Burns are trauma — apply ATLS principles first:**

**Airway:**
- Highest priority — airway edema progresses rapidly in the first 12–24 hours
- **Signs of inhalation injury:** Burns in enclosed space, singed nasal hairs, facial burns, carbonaceous sputum, hoarseness, stridor
- 🟢 **Intubate early if ANY suspicion** — once edema peaks, intubation becomes impossible
- Fiber-optic bronchoscopy confirms inhalation injury (erythema, soot, mucosal sloughing)

**Breathing:**
- Circumferential chest burns → escharotomy if ventilation impaired
- Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning: headache, confusion, cherry-red skin (unreliable). Pulse oximetry is falsely normal. **ABG with co-oximetry** is required. Treat with 100% O₂.
- 🔵 Cyanide poisoning: suspect in industrial fires (plastics, synthetics). Treat with hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit).

**Circulation:**
- 2 large-bore IV lines — through burned skin if necessary
- Begin fluid resuscitation immediately for burns >20% TBSA (adults) or >10% TBSA (children)

🟢 **Stop the burning process:**
- Remove clothing and jewelry
- Cool with running tap water (15–20°C) for **20 minutes** within 3 hours of injury
- **Do NOT use ice** (causes vasoconstriction and deepens injury)
- Cover with clean, dry sheets; prevent hypothermia

### B. Fluid Resuscitation

🟢 **The Parkland Formula (Baxter):**

```
Total fluid in first 24 hours = 4 mL × body weight (kg) × %TBSA burned

• First half in the first 8 hours (from time of burn, NOT from presentation)
• Second half over the next 16 hours
• Fluid: Lactated Ringer's solution
• Endpoint: Urine output 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr (adults); 1–1.5 mL/kg/hr (children)
```

🟢 **Critical points:**
- The Parkland formula is a **starting estimate** — titrate to urine output
- Time zero is the **time of burn**, not arrival at hospital
- Children <20 kg: add **maintenance fluids with dextrose** (burn patients mobilize glycogen poorly)
- Colloids generally added after 8–24 hours when capillary leak decreases

🔵 **"Fluid creep" — the modern problem:**
- Over-resuscitation is now more common and dangerous than under-resuscitation
- Causes: abdominal compartment syndrome, extremity compartment syndrome, pulmonary edema, orbital compartment syndrome
- Actual volumes given often exceed Parkland estimates by 50–100%
- Contributing factors: opioid use (increases ADH), anxiety-driven boluses, inhalation injury
- **Target UO = 0.5 mL/kg/hr** — resist the urge to chase higher numbers

🔴 **SS Pearl — Adjuncts to reduce fluid volumes:**
- **Albumin (5%)** at 12–24 hours (ISBI guidelines) — reduces total crystalloid by 20–30%
- **Vitamin C (high-dose, 66 mg/kg/hr for 24 hrs):** Antioxidant that reduces capillary leak. RCTs show 30–40% reduction in fluid requirements. Not yet standard of care but gaining evidence.
- **Plasma/FFP resuscitation** (Scandinavian approach): Used in some European centers for massive burns
- **Colloid timing:** The 2023 ISBI guidelines now recommend starting colloids earlier (at 8 hours) in patients trending toward fluid creep

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.19; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.22*

## 8. Inhalation Injury

🟢 **Three mechanisms of inhalation injury:**

| Type | Mechanism | Timing | Management |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| **Upper airway thermal injury** | Direct heat to supraglottic structures | Immediate (0–24 hrs) | Early intubation |
| **Lower airway chemical injury** | Toxic combustion products (aldehydes, HCl, phosgene) damage bronchial mucosa | 24–72 hrs | Supportive; bronchoscopy-guided toileting |
| **Systemic toxicity** | CO and cyanide | Immediate | 100% O₂ (CO); hydroxocobalamin (CN) |

🟢 **CO poisoning:**
- COHb >10%: symptoms begin (headache, nausea)
- COHb >30%: confusion, syncope
- COHb >60%: coma, death
- Half-life of COHb: 4 hours on room air → **40 minutes on 100% O₂** → 20 minutes with hyperbaric O₂
- **100% FiO₂ via non-rebreather** for all suspected cases until COHb <10%

🔵 **Inhalation injury increases mortality independently:**
- A 20% TBSA burn WITHOUT inhalation: mortality ~5%
- A 20% TBSA burn WITH inhalation: mortality ~20%
- Adds ~17% to mortality at any given TBSA (equivalent to adding 17% TBSA)
- Increases fluid requirements by 30–50% above Parkland calculations

🔵 **Bronchoscopic grading (Endorf & Gamelli):**
- Grade 0: No injury
- Grade 1: Minor erythema, soot
- Grade 2: Moderate edema, bronchorrhea
- Grade 3: Severe edema, mucosal sloughing, obliteration
- Grade 4: Mucosal loss, necrosis, luminal obliteration

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Nebulized heparin + N-acetylcysteine (NAC) protocol for inhalation injury reduces reintubation rates and atelectasis. Mechanism: heparin dissolves fibrin casts; NAC breaks down mucus. Evidence from UTMB Galveston and military burn centers supports this as adjunctive therapy, though large RCTs are lacking.

## 9. Surgical Management

### A. Escharotomy

🟢 **Indication:** Circumferential full-thickness burns causing:
- **Chest:** Restrictive ventilatory compromise (rising airway pressures, hypoxia)
- **Limbs:** Vascular compromise (absent pulses, compartment syndrome)

🟢 **Technique:**
- Performed at **bedside** — no anesthesia needed (full-thickness burns are insensate)
- Incision through eschar to subcutaneous fat (NOT into muscle — that is fasciotomy)
- **Limbs:** Mid-lateral and mid-medial lines; extend across joints
- **Chest:** Bilateral anterior axillary lines connected by transverse subcostal incision
- **Hands:** Along ulnar border of digits; dorsal interosseous incisions
- Must be done within **6 hours** of circulatory compromise to prevent irreversible ischemia

🔵 **Fasciotomy** is indicated when escharotomy is insufficient (especially electrical burns — deep tissue damage exceeds surface injury). Technique follows standard compartment syndrome fasciotomy principles.

### B. Excision and Grafting

🟢 **Principle:** Early excision of devitalized tissue and wound coverage reduces sepsis, hospital stay, and mortality. This is the **single most important advance** in modern burn surgery.

🟢 **Early tangential excision (Janzekovic, 1970):**
- Sequential shaving of thin layers of burned tissue until viable, punctate bleeding dermis is reached
- Performed within **72 hours** of injury for deep partial and full-thickness burns
- Followed immediately by skin grafting

🟢 **Types of skin grafts:**

| Graft Type | Thickness | Donor Site Healing | Best Use |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| **Split-thickness (STSG)** | 0.008–0.018 inch | Heals in 10–14 days | Standard burn coverage |
| **Full-thickness (FTSG)** | Includes full dermis | Requires closure/graft | Face, hands, joints (cosmetic/functional areas) |

🟢 **Meshing:** STSGs can be meshed (1:1.5, 1:3, up to 1:6) to increase coverage area and allow fluid drainage. Higher mesh ratios = more scarring. **Sheet grafts** (unmeshed) used on face and hands for cosmetic results.

🔵 **Operative limits:**
- Maximum excision per session: ~20% TBSA (limited by blood loss — expect 100–150 mL per 1% TBSA excised)
- Staged excision for massive burns (>40% TBSA)
- Tourniquet use on limbs reduces blood loss
- Tumescent technique (subcutaneous epinephrine/saline) reduces bleeding

🔵 **Temporary wound coverage (when autograft is insufficient):**

| Material | Type | Duration | Notes |
|----------|------|----------|-------|
| **Allograft (cadaveric)** | Biological | 2–3 weeks | Gold standard temporary cover; eventually rejected |
| **Xenograft (porcine)** | Biological | 5–7 days | Readily available; good pain control |
| **Biobrane** | Biosynthetic | Until healed/grafted | Good for superficial partial thickness |
| **Integra** | Dermal regeneration template | 2–3 weeks (then STSG over neo-dermis) | Creates neo-dermis; excellent for deep burns |
| **Negative pressure wound therapy** | Mechanical | Variable | Improves graft take over irregular surfaces |

🔴 **SS Pearl — Cultured Epithelial Autografts (CEA):** A small skin biopsy (2×2 cm) can be expanded into sheets covering the entire body surface within 3 weeks. Limitations: fragile, expensive, poor take rate (30–70%), no dermis (poor long-term quality). Best used with dermal templates (Integra + CEA = "sandwich" technique). ReCell® spray-on skin cells is a newer alternative with faster application.

## 10. Special Burns

### A. Electrical Burns

🟢 **Classification:**
- **Low voltage** (<1000V): Household current; localized contact burns
- **High voltage** (>1000V): Devastating — current follows path of least resistance through tissues
- **Flash/arc burns:** Thermal injury from arc; no current flow through body
- **Lightning:** Massive instantaneous discharge; unique injury pattern

🟢 **Key features of high-voltage electrical burns:**
- **Iceberg injury:** Surface burns vastly underestimate deep tissue destruction
- Current travels along neurovascular bundles → deep muscle necrosis, vascular thrombosis
- **Entry and exit wounds** — exit wound often larger/more destructive
- Rhabdomyolysis → **myoglobinuria → acute kidney injury** (dark/port-wine urine)
- Cardiac arrhythmias (ventricular fibrillation with AC; asystole with DC)
- Neurological: LOC, spinal cord injury, peripheral neuropathy (immediate or delayed)
- Cataracts (may develop months to years later)

🟢 **Management priorities unique to electrical burns:**
- **ECG monitoring** for all significant electrical injuries (12–24 hrs minimum)
- **Aggressive fluid resuscitation** — Parkland formula underestimates needs; target UO **1–1.5 mL/kg/hr** (to clear myoglobin)
- **Alkalinize urine** (sodium bicarbonate) if myoglobinuria present
- **Fasciotomy** (not escharotomy) — compartment syndrome is the rule, not the exception
- Debridement may be needed in stages as injury demarcates over days

🔵 **Cardiac monitoring guidelines:**
- Abnormal ECG at presentation → 24-hour monitoring
- Loss of consciousness, high voltage, or cardiac arrest → ICU monitoring
- Normal ECG + low voltage + no LOC → may discharge after 4–6 hours observation

### B. Chemical Burns

🟢 **Principles:**
- **Remove the agent** — remove all clothing; brush off dry chemicals before irrigating
- **Copious water irrigation** for minimum **30 minutes** (some agents require hours)
- **Do NOT attempt neutralization** — exothermic reaction worsens injury
- Alkali burns are generally **worse** than acid burns (liquefactive necrosis penetrates deeper vs. coagulative necrosis from acids)

🔵 **Specific agents:**
- **Hydrofluoric acid (HF):** Life-threatening even with small TBSA. Fluoride binds calcium → severe hypocalcemia, cardiac arrest. Treat with **topical/injectable calcium gluconate**. Monitor ECG and serum calcium.
- **Cement (calcium hydroxide):** Alkali — prolonged contact causes deep burns; irrigate extensively
- **Phosphorus (white):** Ignites on contact with air; debride particles under water; use copper sulfate (1%) to identify fragments (turns black)

### C. Radiation Burns

🔵 Uncommon outside industrial accidents and radiotherapy. Injury is delayed (days to weeks). Progressive obliterative endarteritis causes chronic non-healing wounds. Management: excision and well-vascularized flap coverage (grafts fail on irradiated beds).

### D. Non-Accidental Burns (Child Abuse)

🟢 **Red flags:**
- **Stocking/glove distribution** — forced immersion scald
- **Symmetrical burns** on both extremities
- **Sharply demarcated** burn margins (no splash pattern)
- **Cigarette burns** — circular, uniform, 7–8 mm diameter
- Burns inconsistent with stated mechanism
- Delay in seeking medical care
- Prior injuries or previous presentations

🟢 **Mandatory:** Report suspected non-accidental injury. Document burn pattern meticulously with photographs.

## 11. Burn Wound Infection and Sepsis

🟢 **Burns are the most immunosuppressive non-iatrogenic injury.** Loss of skin barrier + systemic immune dysfunction = extreme infection susceptibility.

🟢 **Timeline of colonization:**
- Day 0–3: Gram-positive (Staph, Strep)
- Day 3–21: Gram-negative (Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, Klebsiella)
- Day 7+: Fungal (Candida, Aspergillus) — especially with prolonged antibiotics

🟢 **Burn wound infection diagnosis:**
- >10⁵ organisms per gram of tissue on **quantitative wound biopsy** (gold standard)
- Clinical signs: focal discoloration, eschar separation, erythema/cellulitis, rapid eschar conversion
- **Burn wound sepsis** (ABA criteria): change in wound appearance + systemic signs (hemodynamic instability, rising WBC or leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, glucose intolerance, decreased UO)

🔵 **Topical antimicrobials:**

| Agent | Spectrum | Pros | Cons |
|-------|----------|------|------|
| **Silver sulfadiazine (SSD)** | Broad (Gram +/-) | Painless, easy application | Pseudoeschar formation; leukopenia; delays epithelialization; sulfa allergy |
| **Mafenide acetate (Sulfamylon)** | Excellent Gram-negative, penetrates eschar | Best for ear cartilage (prevents chondritis); penetrates eschar | Painful; carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (metabolic acidosis) |
| **Silver nitrate (0.5%)** | Broad | Painless | Stains everything; electrolyte depletion (hyponatremia, hypochloremia) |
| **Nanocrystalline silver (Acticoat)** | Broad | Sustained release; fewer dressing changes | Expensive |
| **Mupirocin** | MRSA | Targeted anti-MRSA | Limited spectrum |

🔵 **Systemic antibiotics:** NOT used prophylactically in burns (promotes resistance). Used only for confirmed infection — culture-directed therapy.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Pseudomonas aeruginosa remains the leading cause of burn wound sepsis globally. Its virulence factors (exotoxin A, elastase, biofilm formation) make it uniquely devastating. Early excision has reduced Pseudomonas burn wound sepsis from >50% to <10% in modern burn centers — the strongest argument for aggressive surgical debridement.

## 12. Nutrition in Burns

🟢 **Burns produce the most severe catabolic state in clinical medicine.**

🟢 **Nutritional requirements:**
- **Calories:** Curreri formula: 25 kcal/kg/day + 40 kcal/%TBSA/day (adults). Toronto formula is more accurate (accounts for day post-burn, temperature, caloric intake).
- **Protein:** 1.5–2 g/kg/day (up to 25% of total calories as protein)
- **Route:** Enteral preferred — start within 6 hours of admission (reduces bacterial translocation, maintains gut barrier)
- **Tube feed placement:** Nasogastric or post-pyloric; continue through surgeries

🔵 **Pharmacological modulation of hypermetabolism:**
- **Propranolol:** β-blocker; reduces resting energy expenditure by 15–20%, decreases muscle catabolism. Dose: titrate to reduce HR by 15–20%. Strongest evidence of any anti-catabolic agent in burns.
- **Oxandrolone:** Anabolic steroid; 10 mg BID; increases lean body mass, improves wound healing. FDA-approved for burn catabolism.
- **Insulin:** Tight glucose control (target 100–140 mg/dL); anabolic effects
- **Recombinant human growth hormone (rhGH):** Accelerates wound healing but associated with increased mortality in critically ill adults (limited to pediatric burns)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The combination of propranolol + oxandrolone has shown additive anti-catabolic effects in pediatric burns. Herndon's group at UTMB Galveston demonstrated that multi-modal anti-catabolic therapy (exercise + propranolol + oxandrolone) can restore lean body mass to 95% of predicted within 12 months, compared to 75% with standard care.

## 13. Complications

🟢 **Acute complications:**

| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| **Curling's ulcer** | Stress ulceration (gastric/duodenal) | PPI/H2 blocker prophylaxis; enteral feeding |
| **Compartment syndrome** | Circumferential burns + fluid resuscitation | Escharotomy/fasciotomy |
| **Abdominal compartment syndrome** | Fluid creep; circumferential trunk burns | Escharotomy; paracentesis; decompressive laparotomy |
| **DVT/PE** | Hypercoagulable state, immobility | Prophylactic anticoagulation |
| **Hypothermia** | Loss of skin barrier | Warm environment (28–33°C); warm fluids |

🔵 **Late complications:**
- **Hypertrophic scarring:** Most common long-term complication; risk factors: deep partial/full thickness, delayed wound closure, darker skin, young age, genetic predisposition
- **Contractures:** Across joints — prevented by splinting in position of function, early mobilization, pressure garments
- **Marjolin's ulcer:** Squamous cell carcinoma arising in chronic burn scar (latency 10–25+ years). Aggressive — 20–40% metastasis rate. Wide excision required.
- **Heterotopic ossification:** Particularly around elbows in burns crossing joints

🔴 **SS Pearl — Marjolin's ulcer:** Any non-healing wound or nodular change in a burn scar must be biopsied. These SCCs are far more aggressive than UV-induced SCCs, with higher rates of lymph node metastasis. Treatment is wide local excision with 2 cm margins + sentinel lymph node biopsy consideration. Mohs surgery is generally not recommended due to the aggressive biology.

## 14. Criteria for Burn Center Referral

🟢 **ABA (American Burn Association) referral criteria:**

1. Partial thickness burns >10% TBSA
2. Full thickness burns (any size)
3. Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
4. Electrical burns (including lightning)
5. Chemical burns
6. Inhalation injury
7. Burns in patients with significant comorbidities
8. Burns with associated trauma (if burn is the greater risk)
9. Burns in children (facilities without pediatric expertise)
10. Patients requiring special social, emotional, or rehabilitative support

## 15. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Jackson's three zones:** Coagulation (dead), stasis (salvageable — therapeutic target), hyperemia (will recover).
2. **Burn depth:** Superficial partial = heals spontaneously; deep partial/full thickness = needs surgery. The critical distinction.
3. **TBSA estimation:** Rule of Nines for adults; Lund & Browder for accuracy. Exclude superficial (1st degree) burns.
4. **Parkland formula:** 4 mL × kg × %TBSA in 24 hours (half in first 8 hours from time of burn). Titrate to UO 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr.
5. **Airway first.** Intubate early if inhalation injury suspected — don't wait for obstruction.
6. **CO poisoning:** Pulse oximetry is unreliable. ABG with co-oximetry. 100% O₂.
7. **Early excision and grafting** is the single most important advance in burn surgery — reduces sepsis and mortality.
8. **Silver sulfadiazine** is the most commonly used topical agent; mafenide acetate penetrates eschar.
9. **Electrical burns:** Iceberg injury — surface underestimates damage. Monitor ECG. Target UO 1–1.5 mL/kg/hr. Fasciotomy often needed.
10. **Chemical burns:** Irrigate copiously. Do NOT neutralize. Alkali worse than acid.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

11. Fluid creep is the modern problem — over-resuscitation causes compartment syndrome, pulmonary edema. Resist chasing high UO.
12. Inhalation injury independently adds ~17% TBSA-equivalent mortality and increases fluid needs 30–50%.
13. Propranolol is the best-studied anti-catabolic agent in burns — reduces REE by 15–20%.
14. Burn wound conversion at 48–72 hours — all early care aims to prevent zone of stasis → coagulation.
15. LDI is the most accurate method for burn depth assessment (>95% accuracy by day 3–5).
16. Quantitative wound biopsy (>10⁵ organisms/g) is the gold standard for burn wound infection diagnosis.
17. No prophylactic systemic antibiotics in burns.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

18. Hypermetabolic response persists up to 3 years — implications for long-term anabolic therapy and rehabilitation.
19. High-dose vitamin C (66 mg/kg/hr) reduces crystalloid requirements by 30–40% — emerging adjunct.
20. Nebulized heparin + NAC for inhalation injury — reduces reintubation and atelectasis.
21. CEA + dermal template "sandwich technique" for massive burns when donor sites are exhausted.
22. Marjolin's ulcer: aggressive SCC in burn scars; 20–40% metastasis rate; wide excision, not Mohs.
23. Two-disease concept of hypermetabolic response — propranolol + oxandrolone + exercise shows additive benefit.
