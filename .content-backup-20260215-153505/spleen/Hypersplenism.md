# Hypersplenism: Diagnosis & Management

> **Source**: Maingot's Part VII, Fischer's Mastery of Surgery
> **Topic System**: [[Spleen Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Hypersplenism is a clinical syndrome of cytopenias (one or more cell lines) due to excessive splenic sequestration and destruction, associated with splenomegaly, and correctable by splenectomy.

### Key Points

1. **Definition**: Splenomegaly + cytopenia(s) + hyperplastic bone marrow + corrected by splenectomy
2. **Mechanism**: Increased pooling + accelerated destruction of blood cells
3. **Causes**: Portal hypertension (most common), hematologic disorders, infections, storage diseases
4. **Clinical features**: Pancytopenia or selective cytopenia, bleeding, infections
5. **Treatment**: Treat underlying cause; splenectomy if symptomatic and cause untreatable

---

## Detailed Notes

### Diagnostic Criteria (Classic)

All four criteria should be met:
1. **Splenomegaly** (palpable or on imaging)
2. **Cytopenia(s)** (anemia, leukopenia, and/or thrombocytopenia)
3. **Hyperplastic bone marrow** (marrow trying to compensate)
4. **Correction after splenectomy** (proves splenic cause)

### Pathophysiology

```
NORMAL SPLEEN:
- Pools ~30% of platelets, few RBCs, WBCs
- Filters old/damaged cells

HYPERSPLENISM (enlarged spleen):
- Pools up to 90% of platelets
- Increased RBC/WBC destruction
- Accelerated passage through splenic cords
- Bone marrow cannot compensate

Result: CYTOPENIAS despite hyperplastic marrow
```

### Causes of Hypersplenism

#### Congestive (Portal Hypertension)
- **Cirrhosis** (most common overall cause)
- Portal vein thrombosis
- Splenic vein thrombosis
- Budd-Chiari syndrome
- Congestive heart failure (rare)

#### Hematologic Disorders
- Hemolytic anemias (spherocytosis, sickle cell)
- Thalassemia
- Lymphoproliferative disorders
- Myeloproliferative disorders
- Hairy cell leukemia

#### Infectious
- Malaria (tropical splenomegaly)
- Leishmaniasis (kala-azar)
- Schistosomiasis
- Infectious mononucleosis
- HIV

#### Infiltrative/Storage
- Gaucher disease
- Niemann-Pick disease
- Amyloidosis
- Sarcoidosis

#### Other
- Felty syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia)
- SLE

### Clinical Presentation

| Finding | Cause |
|---------|-------|
| **Splenomegaly** | Palpable LUQ mass, early satiety |
| **Anemia** | Fatigue, pallor, dyspnea |
| **Thrombocytopenia** | Easy bruising, petechiae, bleeding |
| **Leukopenia** | Recurrent infections |
| **LUQ discomfort** | Splenic enlargement |

### Evaluation

**Laboratory:**
- CBC with differential and reticulocyte count
- Peripheral smear (rule out other causes)
- LFTs (portal hypertension)
- Hemolysis workup if indicated

**Imaging:**
- Ultrasound: Spleen size, portal vein, liver
- CT: Better anatomic detail
- Doppler: Portal/splenic vein patency

**Bone Marrow:**
- Hyperplastic (compensating for peripheral destruction)
- Rules out primary marrow pathology

### Treatment Options

#### Address Underlying Cause
- Portal hypertension: Beta-blockers, TIPS, liver transplant evaluation
- Infections: Antimicrobials
- Hematologic: Disease-specific therapy

#### Splenectomy

**Indications:**
- Symptomatic cytopenias (bleeding, infections, transfusion-dependent anemia)
- Failed medical management
- Underlying cause cannot be treated

**Considerations in Cirrhosis:**
- Higher surgical risk (coagulopathy, varices)
- May be done with shunt surgery
- Consider partial splenic embolization instead

**Outcomes:**
- Usually improves cytopenias
- Platelet count rises rapidly (often dramatically)
- May unmask thrombotic tendency

#### Partial Splenic Embolization

**Indications:**
- High surgical risk
- Portal hypertension with varices
- Alternative when splenectomy contraindicated

**Technique:**
- Embolize 50-70% of spleen
- Preserves some splenic function (reduces OPSI risk)

**Complications:**
- Post-embolization syndrome (pain, fever)
- Splenic abscess
- May need repeat procedure

### Hypersplenism vs Primary Marrow Disease

| Feature | Hypersplenism | Primary Marrow |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Spleen** | Enlarged | Usually normal |
| **Bone marrow** | Hyperplastic | Abnormal/infiltrated |
| **Reticulocytes** | Increased | Normal/decreased |
| **Response to splenectomy** | Cytopenias improve | No improvement |

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What are the four diagnostic criteria for hypersplenism?
   A: (1) Splenomegaly, (2) Cytopenia(s), (3) Hyperplastic bone marrow, (4) Correction after splenectomy

2. Q: What is the most common cause of hypersplenism in developed countries?
   A: Portal hypertension secondary to cirrhosis

3. Q: What is the role of partial splenic embolization?
   A: Alternative to splenectomy in high-surgical-risk patients; preserves some splenic function while reducing sequestration
