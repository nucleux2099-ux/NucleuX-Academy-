# Splenic Trauma: Grading & Management

> **Source**: Maingot's Part VII, AAST Guidelines, EAST Guidelines
> **Topic System**: [[Spleen Index]]
> **Phase**: Shoot

---

## Core Concepts (Shoot Phase)

### Definition & Overview

> [!summary] One-liner
> Splenic injury is the most common solid organ injury in blunt abdominal trauma, managed by Non-Operative Management (NOM) in hemodynamically stable patients with success rates >90% for low-grade injuries.

### Key Points

1. **Epidemiology**: Most common solid organ injured in blunt trauma; 40-55% of abdominal injuries
2. **Mechanism**: Blunt (MVA, falls, sports) >> penetrating
3. **Grading**: AAST scale (I-V) guides management
4. **Management paradigm shift**: NOM is standard for stable patients; ~80% managed non-operatively
5. **Goal**: Splenic preservation to prevent OPSI (especially in children)

### Clinical Pearl
> [!tip] Kehr's Sign
> Left shoulder pain (referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation by blood) - present in ~50% of splenic injuries.

## Detailed Notes

### Anatomy Review

**Splenic Ligaments:**
- Gastrosplenic ligament (contains short gastric vessels)
- Splenorenal ligament (contains splenic vessels)
- Phrenosplenic ligament
- Splenocolic ligament

**Blood Supply:**
- Splenic artery (from celiac trunk) → branches at hilum
- Segmental supply allows partial splenectomy

### AAST Splenic Injury Grading Scale

| Grade | Injury Description |
|-------|-------------------|
| **I** | Subcapsular hematoma <10% surface area; Laceration <1 cm depth |
| **II** | Subcapsular hematoma 10-50%; Intraparenchymal <5 cm; Laceration 1-3 cm depth |
| **III** | Subcapsular hematoma >50% or expanding; Ruptured subcapsular/parenchymal; Laceration >3 cm or involving trabecular vessels |
| **IV** | Laceration involving segmental or hilar vessels with >25% devascularization |
| **V** | Shattered spleen; Hilar vascular injury with complete devascularization |

### Non-Operative Management (NOM)

**Criteria for NOM:**
- Hemodynamically stable (or responds to resuscitation)
- No other indications for laparotomy
- No peritonitis
- Ability to monitor (ICU if high grade)

**NOM Protocol:**
| Grade | Bed rest | Serial exams | Repeat imaging | Activity restriction |
|-------|----------|--------------|----------------|---------------------|
| I-II | 24-48h | Q4-6h | Rarely needed | 4-6 weeks |
| III | 2-3 days | Q4h | If deteriorates | 6-8 weeks |
| IV-V | 3-5 days | Q4h, ICU | Consider routine | 8-12 weeks |

**NOM Success Rates:**
- Grade I-II: >95%
- Grade III: 90%
- Grade IV-V: 75-80% (higher with angioembolization)

### Angioembolization

**Indications:**
- Contrast blush on CT (active bleeding)
- Pseudoaneurysm
- High-grade injury (IV-V) in stable patient
- Adjunct to increase NOM success

**Technique:**
- Selective (distal) embolization preferred (preserves splenic function)
- Proximal (main splenic artery) if diffuse injury

**Complications:**
- Splenic infarction/abscess
- Coil migration
- Re-bleeding (5-10%)

### Operative Management

**Indications:**
- Hemodynamic instability not responding to resuscitation
- Peritonitis/other laparotomy indications
- Failed NOM
- Grade V with exsanguination

**Surgical Options:**

| Technique | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Splenectomy** | Severe injury, instability, multiple injuries |
| **Splenorrhaphy** | Minor lacerations in stable patient |
| **Partial splenectomy** | Localized injury, preserve function |
| **Mesh wrap** | Diffuse oozing, capsular tears |

### Damage Control Splenectomy

In critically ill patients:
1. Rapid splenectomy
2. Pack and close abdomen
3. ICU resuscitation
4. Return for definitive closure

### Post-Splenectomy Care

**Immediate:**
- Vaccinations (ideally 14 days post-op): Pneumococcal, H. influenzae, Meningococcal
- Educate about OPSI risk
- Medic alert bracelet

### Special Situations

#### Delayed Splenic Rupture
- Occurs 48h to weeks after injury
- Often subcapsular hematoma that ruptures
- Presents with sudden hemodynamic deterioration
- May occur after discharge - patient education important

#### Pediatric Considerations
- Higher NOM success rates
- Splenic preservation even more important (higher OPSI risk)
- More conservative with surgery

## Retrieval Practice

### Quick Recall Questions

1. Q: What is the most common solid organ injured in blunt abdominal trauma?
   A: Spleen (40-55% of blunt abdominal injuries)

2. Q: What is Kehr's sign?
   A: Left shoulder pain due to referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation by blood from splenic injury

3. Q: What CT finding suggests need for angioembolization?
   A: Contrast blush (active extravasation) or pseudoaneurysm
