## 1. History — From Goiter Remedies to Precision Oncology

The thyroid gland has been recognized since antiquity. Chinese medical texts from 2700 BCE described goiters and recommended seaweed for treatment — an empirical iodine therapy millennia before the element was discovered. Galen named the gland "thyreoeides" (shield-shaped) in the 2nd century CE, and Vesalius illustrated it in *De Humani Corporis Fabrica* (1543), though he believed it simply lubricated the larynx.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1170 | **Roger of Salerno** — First recorded surgical treatment of goiter |
| 1791 | **Pierre-Joseph Desault** — First partial thyroidectomy (Paris) |
| 1850 | **Chatin** — Linked iodine deficiency to endemic goiter |
| 1873 | **Theodor Billroth** — Pioneered thyroidectomy in Vienna; early mortality ~40% from hemorrhage and infection |
| 1880s | **Theodor Kocher** — Perfected the capsular dissection technique; reduced mortality to <1%; Nobel Prize 1909 |
| 1907 | **Kocher** — Described "cachexia strumipriva" (postoperative hypothyroidism), establishing the gland's endocrine role |
| 1936 | **Saul Hertz** — Proposed radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy for thyroid disease |
| 1946 | **Seidlin** — First successful RAI treatment of thyroid cancer metastases |
| 1996 | **Gagner** — First endoscopic thyroidectomy |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.12*

Kocher's contribution cannot be overstated. By meticulous attention to hemostasis, capsular dissection, and identification of the recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN), he transformed thyroid surgery from near-certain catastrophe to one of the safest operations in surgery. His Nobel Prize — the first awarded to a surgeon — was for "his works on the physiology, pathology, and surgery of the thyroid gland."

## 2. Anatomy & Embryology

### Embryology

🟢 The thyroid is the **first endocrine gland to develop**, arising from the **foramen cecum** at the base of the tongue during the **3rd–4th week** of gestation. The thyroid anlage descends along the **thyroglossal duct** to reach its final pretracheal position by the **7th week**. The thyroglossal duct normally obliterates; persistence leads to thyroglossal duct cysts (the most common midline neck mass in children).

🔵 **The ultimobranchial bodies** (4th–5th pharyngeal pouches) contribute **parafollicular C cells** — neural crest-derived cells that produce calcitonin. These cells are the origin of **medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC)**.

🟢 **Key embryological anomalies:**
- **Thyroglossal duct cyst** — Midline mass, elevates with swallowing and tongue protrusion; Sistrunk procedure (excise cyst + central hyoid bone + tract to foramen cecum)
- **Lingual thyroid** — Failure of descent; may be the only functioning thyroid tissue
- **Pyramidal lobe** — Present in ~50%; remnant of the thyroglossal duct; extends superiorly from the isthmus

### Surgical Anatomy

🟢 **Key anatomical facts:**

- **Location:** Anterior to tracheal rings 2–4, wrapped around the anterolateral trachea
- **Lobes:** Two lateral lobes connected by an isthmus; each lobe ~4 × 2 × 2 cm
- **Weight:** Normal gland 15–25 g
- **Capsule:** True (adherent) capsule and a false (surgical) capsule derived from pretracheal fascia — the plane of dissection lies between these two layers

🟢 **Blood supply:**

| Artery | Origin | Supplies |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Superior thyroid artery** | First branch of external carotid | Upper pole |
| **Inferior thyroid artery** | Thyrocervical trunk (subclavian) | Lower pole, parathyroids |
| **Thyroidea ima** | Aortic arch or brachiocephalic (present in ~3%) | Isthmus; beware during tracheostomy |

🟢 **Venous drainage:** Superior and middle thyroid veins → IJV; inferior thyroid veins → brachiocephalic veins.

🟢 **The two nerves that define thyroid surgery:**

| Nerve | Course | Injury Consequence |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** | Ascends in the tracheoesophageal groove; right RLN loops under subclavian, left loops under aortic arch | **Unilateral:** Hoarseness (vocal cord paralysis); **Bilateral:** Airway obstruction → emergency |
| **External branch of superior laryngeal nerve (EBSLN)** | Runs with superior thyroid artery to cricothyroid muscle | Loss of high-pitched phonation ("high C"); critical for singers/speakers |

🔵 **RLN anatomical pearls:**
- The nerve is **nonrecurrent** in ~0.5–1% on the right side (associated with aberrant right subclavian artery — arteria lusoria); almost never nonrecurrent on the left
- The nerve passes **posterior** to the inferior thyroid artery in 60%, anterior in 20%, and between branches in 20%
- **Zuckerkandl's tubercle** (posterolateral thyroid tissue extension) is a reliable landmark — the RLN lies just deep and medial to it
- **Berry's ligament** (posterior suspensory ligament) — the danger zone; the RLN is most vulnerable here as it enters the larynx

🔵 **Parathyroid glands:** Usually four; superior pair derived from the 4th pharyngeal pouch (more constant position — posterolateral, at the cricothyroid junction); inferior pair from the 3rd pouch (more variable — may descend into the thymus). Preservation of parathyroid blood supply (from inferior thyroid artery branches) is essential during thyroidectomy.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Intraoperative nerve monitoring (IONM) does not reduce the rate of RLN injury in experienced hands but changes surgical strategy — particularly in staged bilateral procedures. If signal loss occurs on the first side, the surgeon can abort the contralateral dissection to avoid bilateral injury.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.12; Fischer 7th Ed*

## 3. Pathophysiology

### Thyroid Nodule Formation

🟢 Thyroid nodules are exceedingly common — **palpable in 4–7% of adults** but detected in **up to 67%** on high-resolution ultrasound. The vast majority (90–95%) are **benign**.

🟢 **Common etiologies of thyroid nodules:**
- Colloid nodules (most common)
- Follicular adenomas
- Thyroiditis (Hashimoto's, subacute)
- Cysts (simple, complex, hemorrhagic)
- Malignancy (~5–10%)

🔵 **Nodulogenesis** involves a complex interplay of:
- **TSH stimulation** — Chronic TSH elevation drives follicular cell proliferation
- **Iodine deficiency** — The strongest environmental risk factor for nodular goiter
- **Genetic mutations** — Somatic mutations in TSHR, GNAS, RAS, BRAF drive clonal expansion
- **Growth factors** — IGF-1, EGF, and local paracrine factors
- **Radiation exposure** — Particularly childhood exposure; increases both benign and malignant nodules

### Thyroid Carcinogenesis

🟢 **The BRAF–RAS paradigm (simplified):**

```
DIFFERENTIATED THYROID CANCER (DTC)

Two major molecular pathways:

1. BRAF V600E mutation
   → Constitutive MAPK activation
   → Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC)
   → Classical and tall-cell variants
   → More aggressive behavior

2. RAS mutations (NRAS, HRAS, KRAS)
   → MAPK + PI3K pathway activation
   → Follicular pattern tumors
   → Follicular thyroid carcinoma (FTC)
   → Follicular variant of PTC
   → Less aggressive generally

3. RET/PTC rearrangements
   → PTC (especially radiation-induced)
   → Common in children/young adults
```

🔵 **Progression model:**

```
Normal follicular cell
    → Follicular adenoma (RAS mutation)
    → FTC (PAX8-PPARγ or additional hits)

Normal follicular cell
    → PTC (BRAF V600E or RET/PTC)
    → Dedifferentiation (TERT promoter, TP53)
    → Poorly differentiated → Anaplastic (ATC)
```

🔴 **SS Pearl — Molecular markers in clinical practice:**
- **BRAF V600E** in PTC: associated with extrathyroidal extension, lymph node metastasis, radioiodine refractoriness, and higher recurrence
- **TERT promoter mutations** (C228T, C250T): strongest molecular predictor of aggressive behavior; synergistic with BRAF
- **Molecular classifier panels** (Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3) reduce unnecessary surgery for indeterminate cytology (Bethesda III/IV) by identifying low-risk nodules

🔵 **Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC):**
- Arises from parafollicular C cells (neural crest origin)
- 75% sporadic, 25% hereditary (MEN2A, MEN2B, familial MTC)
- **RET proto-oncogene** germline mutations drive hereditary MTC
- Does NOT concentrate iodine → RAI is useless
- Tumor markers: **calcitonin** and **CEA**

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; Harrison's 22nd Ed; Robbins 10th Ed, Ch.20*

## 4. Clinical Features

### Thyroid Nodules

🟢 **Most thyroid nodules are asymptomatic** — discovered incidentally on exam, imaging (CT, carotid US, PET), or by the patient.

🟢 **Symptoms suggestive of benign disease:**
- Sudden pain (hemorrhage into a cyst)
- Tenderness (thyroiditis)
- Bilateral diffuse enlargement (Graves', Hashimoto's)

🟢 **Red flags for malignancy:**
- Firm, fixed, nontender nodule
- Rapid growth
- Cervical lymphadenopathy
- Hoarseness (RLN involvement)
- Dysphagia, dyspnea (invasion)
- History of childhood head/neck radiation
- Family history of thyroid cancer or MEN syndrome
- Age <20 or >60 years
- Male sex (higher malignancy rate per nodule)

🔵 **MEN syndromes and thyroid cancer:**

| Syndrome | Gene | Thyroid Component | Other Features |
|----------|------|-------------------|----------------|
| **MEN2A** | RET | MTC (>95% penetrance) | Pheochromocytoma (50%), hyperparathyroidism (20%) |
| **MEN2B** | RET | MTC (earliest onset, most aggressive) | Pheochromocytoma, mucosal neuromas, marfanoid habitus |
| **Familial MTC** | RET | MTC only | — |

🔴 **SS Pearl:** In MEN2B, MTC can develop before age 1 — prophylactic thyroidectomy is recommended within the first 6 months of life. In MEN2A, timing of prophylactic thyroidectomy is guided by the specific RET codon mutation (ATA risk stratification: highest risk → thyroidectomy by age 2; high risk → by age 5).

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; Harrison's 22nd Ed*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Initial Evaluation

🟢 **Every patient with a thyroid nodule needs:**
1. **History and physical examination** (including voice assessment)
2. **TSH level** — First-line blood test
   - Low TSH → obtain thyroid scintigraphy (hot nodule = autonomous → almost never malignant)
   - Normal/high TSH → proceed to ultrasound
3. **Thyroid ultrasound** — Characterize the nodule

### Ultrasound Assessment

🟢 **ACR TI-RADS (Thyroid Imaging Reporting and Data System):**

| Feature | Points | Suspicious Findings |
|---------|--------|-------------------|
| **Composition** | 0–2 | Solid > mixed > cystic |
| **Echogenicity** | 0–3 | Very hypoechoic most suspicious |
| **Shape** | 0–3 | Taller-than-wide (AP > transverse) |
| **Margin** | 0–3 | Irregular, extrathyroidal extension |
| **Echogenic foci** | 0–3 | Punctate echogenic foci (microcalcifications) |

| TI-RADS Category | Points | FNA Threshold |
|-------------------|--------|--------------|
| TR1 (Benign) | 0 | No FNA |
| TR2 (Not Suspicious) | 2 | No FNA |
| TR3 (Mildly Suspicious) | 3 | ≥2.5 cm |
| TR4 (Moderately Suspicious) | 4–6 | ≥1.5 cm |
| TR5 (Highly Suspicious) | ≥7 | ≥1.0 cm |

🔵 **Ultrasound features with highest specificity for malignancy:**
- Microcalcifications (psammoma bodies in PTC)
- Taller-than-wide shape
- Irregular/spiculated margins
- Marked hypoechogenicity
- Suspicious cervical lymphadenopathy (loss of fatty hilum, cystic change, microcalcifications, peripheral vascularity)

### Fine-Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC)

🟢 **FNAC is the most important diagnostic test** — determines management of thyroid nodules. Should be performed under ultrasound guidance.

🟢 **Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology (2023 Update):**

| Category | Diagnosis | Malignancy Risk | Recommended Management |
|----------|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **I** | Non-diagnostic / Unsatisfactory | 5–10% | Repeat FNA in 4–6 weeks |
| **II** | Benign | 0–3% | Clinical/US follow-up |
| **III** | Atypia of Undetermined Significance (AUS) / Follicular Lesion of Undetermined Significance (FLUS) | 6–18% | Repeat FNA, molecular testing, or lobectomy |
| **IV** | Follicular Neoplasm / Suspicious for Follicular Neoplasm | 10–40% | Molecular testing or diagnostic lobectomy |
| **V** | Suspicious for Malignancy | 45–75% | Lobectomy or total thyroidectomy |
| **VI** | Malignant | 94–99% | Surgery (extent based on tumor type/size) |

🔵 **The "indeterminate" dilemma (Bethesda III and IV):**
- These categories account for ~25% of FNAs
- Historically required diagnostic surgery → 70–80% proved benign on final pathology
- **Molecular testing** now reduces unnecessary surgery:
  - **Afirma GSC:** "Rule-out" test (high sensitivity, high NPV) — benign result avoids surgery
  - **ThyroSeq v3:** "Rule-in" and "rule-out" — identifies specific mutations
  - Both achieve ~50% reduction in diagnostic surgeries for indeterminate nodules

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The combination of Bethesda III cytology + BRAF V600E positivity on molecular testing essentially confirms PTC — proceed directly to total thyroidectomy without diagnostic lobectomy.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.12; ATA Guidelines 2015*

## 6. Classification & Staging

### Histological Classification

🟢 **WHO Classification of Thyroid Tumors (5th Edition, 2022):**

| Category | Type | Frequency | Behavior |
|----------|------|-----------|----------|
| **Differentiated** | **Papillary (PTC)** | ~80% | Excellent prognosis; lymphatic spread |
| | **Follicular (FTC)** | ~10% | Hematogenous spread (bone, lung) |
| | **Hürthle cell (oncocytic)** | 3–4% | Now separate entity; more aggressive than FTC |
| **Poorly Differentiated** | PDTC (Turin criteria) | 2–3% | Intermediate between DTC and ATC |
| **Anaplastic** | ATC | 1–2% | One of most lethal human cancers; median survival 3–6 months |
| **Medullary** | MTC | 3–5% | C-cell origin; calcitonin marker |
| **Other** | Lymphoma, metastases | <1% | Thyroid lymphoma: usually DLBCL arising in Hashimoto's |

🔵 **PTC variants of clinical importance:**

| Variant | Features |
|---------|----------|
| Classical | Orphan Annie nuclei, psammoma bodies, nuclear grooves/inclusions |
| Follicular variant (infiltrative) | RAS-driven; reclassified as "invasive encapsulated FVPTC" |
| **NIFTP** | Non-invasive follicular thyroid neoplasm with papillary-like nuclear features — reclassified as **non-malignant** (2016); reduces "cancer" diagnoses |
| Tall-cell | Cells 3× taller than wide; BRAF-driven; more aggressive |
| Diffuse sclerosing | Young females; bilateral; extensive lymphatic permeation; good prognosis despite dramatic presentation |
| Hobnail/columnar | Aggressive variants; consider total thyroidectomy + RAI |

### TNM Staging (AJCC 8th Edition)

🟢 **Age is the most powerful prognostic factor in DTC.** The AJCC system uses an age cutoff of **55 years**.

**Patients <55 years:**

| Stage | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **I** | Any T, any N, M0 |
| **II** | Any T, any N, M1 |

**Patients ≥55 years:**

| Stage | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **I** | T1–T2, N0, M0 |
| **II** | T1–T2 N1 M0 or T3 any N M0 |
| **III** | T4a any N M0 |
| **IVA** | T4b any N M0 |
| **IVB** | Any T, any N, M1 |

🔵 **Key point:** A 30-year-old with a 4 cm PTC and bilateral cervical lymph node metastases is Stage I. A 60-year-old with the same tumor is Stage II–III. This reflects the dramatically different biology of DTC in young versus older patients.

🔴 **Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma** is always **Stage IV** regardless of extent (IVA = intrathyroidal; IVB = extrathyroidal; IVC = distant metastases).

### ATA Risk Stratification (Recurrence Risk)

🔵 **More clinically useful than AJCC staging for guiding treatment intensity:**

| Risk | Definition | Recurrence Rate |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Low** | Intrathyroidal DTC <4 cm, N0, M0, no vascular invasion, no aggressive histology; complete resection | 1–3% |
| **Intermediate** | Aggressive histology, minor ETE, vascular invasion, N1 (≤5 small nodes), RAI-avid disease in neck | 8–22% |
| **High** | Gross ETE, incomplete resection, distant metastases, N1 (>5 nodes or any node >3 cm), BRAF+TERT | 30–70% |

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.36; ATA Guidelines 2015; AJCC 8th Ed*

## 7. Treatment

### Initial Management

🟢 **All patients with newly diagnosed thyroid cancer require:**
- Complete thyroid ultrasound (both lobes + central and lateral neck compartments)
- Laryngoscopy (assess vocal cord function preoperatively)
- Cross-sectional imaging (CT without iodinated contrast or MRI) for locally advanced disease or suspected lateral neck nodes

### A. Differentiated Thyroid Cancer (PTC and FTC)

#### Surgery

🟢 **Lobectomy vs. Total Thyroidectomy:**

The 2015 ATA guidelines shifted the paradigm — lobectomy is now acceptable for many patients who previously would have undergone total thyroidectomy.

| Factor | Lobectomy | Total Thyroidectomy |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Tumor size | ≤4 cm | >4 cm |
| Multifocality | Unifocal | Bilateral |
| Extrathyroidal extension | No | Yes |
| Node metastases | N0 | N1 |
| Distant metastases | M0 | M1 |
| Radiation history | No | Yes |
| Histology | Low-risk variants | Aggressive variants |

🔵 **Completion thyroidectomy:** Required if lobectomy pathology reveals unexpected high-risk features (ETE, vascular invasion, aggressive variant, positive margins). Ideally performed within 6 months or deferred with RAI remnant ablation.

🔵 **Lymph node surgery:**
- **Central neck dissection (level VI):** Therapeutic for clinically/radiographically involved nodes. Prophylactic CND is controversial for PTC — may upstage but does not clearly improve survival; does increase hypoparathyroidism risk
- **Lateral neck dissection (levels II–V):** Only therapeutic (biopsy-proven N1b). Always compartment-oriented — no "berry-picking"
- **For MTC:** Total thyroidectomy + bilateral CND is standard, even if clinically N0

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Transoral endoscopic thyroidectomy vestibular approach (TOETVA) and robotic thyroidectomy (bilateral axillo-breast approach, retroauricular, transaxillary) offer scar-free neck outcomes. Oncologic outcomes appear equivalent for select low-risk tumors, but should be performed only in high-volume centers.

#### Radioactive Iodine (RAI) Therapy

🔵 **Indications (post-total thyroidectomy):**

| ATA Risk | RAI Recommendation |
|----------|-------------------|
| Low | Generally NOT recommended (remnant ablation may be considered) |
| Intermediate | Recommended (30–100 mCi) — adjuvant treatment |
| High | Strongly recommended (100–200 mCi) — therapeutic intent |

🔵 **Preparation for RAI:**
- **TSH stimulation** (>30 mU/L) via: thyroid hormone withdrawal (4 weeks LT4, or 2 weeks LT3 then 2 weeks off) OR recombinant human TSH (rhTSH/Thyrogen) injections
- **Low-iodine diet** for 1–2 weeks preceding RAI
- Avoid iodinated contrast for ≥3 months pre-RAI

🔵 **Post-therapy whole-body scan (WBS):** Performed 5–7 days after RAI; may reveal previously undetected metastases in 10–15% of patients.

🔴 **SS Pearl — RAI-refractory disease:** Defined as loss of RAI uptake in metastatic lesions, progression despite RAI, or cumulative dose >600 mCi without benefit. These patients are candidates for **tyrosine kinase inhibitors** (lenvatinib, sorafenib) — both showed significant PFS benefit in the SELECT and DECISION trials.

#### TSH Suppression & Surveillance

🟢 **All DTC patients require levothyroxine** — for replacement (post-total thyroidectomy) and TSH suppression (to reduce TSH-driven tumor growth).

🔵 **TSH targets:**

| Risk / Response | TSH Goal (mU/L) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| High risk / incomplete response | <0.1 |
| Intermediate risk | 0.1–0.5 |
| Low risk / excellent response | 0.5–2.0 |
| Biochemically complete response (any initial risk) | 0.5–2.0 |

🔵 **Surveillance (dynamic risk stratification):**
- **Thyroglobulin (Tg):** Tumor marker for DTC; only reliable after total thyroidectomy + RAI remnant ablation. Trend matters more than single value.
- **Anti-Tg antibodies:** Present in ~25% of DTC patients; interfere with Tg assay. Rising levels may indicate recurrence.
- **Neck ultrasound:** At 6–12 months, then periodically based on risk
- **Stimulated Tg** (after TSH stimulation): Most sensitive test for recurrence in low-risk patients

🟢 **Excellent response (most common):**
- Stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL (or suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL)
- Negative anti-Tg antibodies
- No structural disease on imaging
- Recurrence risk: 1–4% → can de-escalate surveillance and TSH suppression

### B. Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)

🔵 **Key management principles:**

1. **All MTC patients need RET testing** — sporadic or hereditary
2. **Preoperative pheochromocytoma screen** (urinary/plasma metanephrines) — MUST rule out before thyroidectomy (risk of catecholamine crisis)
3. **Surgery:** Total thyroidectomy + bilateral central neck dissection (minimum); lateral neck dissection if calcitonin >200 pg/mL or N1b on imaging
4. **No role for RAI** (C cells do not concentrate iodine)
5. **Postoperative markers:** Calcitonin and CEA; doubling times are the most important prognostic factor
6. **Persistent/recurrent MTC with calcitonin >150 pg/mL:** Imaging workup (CT neck/chest/abdomen, bone scan, liver MRI, PET-CT)
7. **Systemic therapy for advanced MTC:** Vandetanib or cabozantinib (RET-targeted TKIs); selpercatinib and pralsetinib (selective RET inhibitors — superior efficacy, fewer side effects)

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Selpercatinib (LOXO-292) achieved a 73% response rate in RET-mutant MTC (LIBRETTO-001 trial) — a paradigm shift from nonselective TKIs. Now first-line for advanced RET-mutant MTC.

### C. Anaplastic Thyroid Carcinoma (ATC)

🟢 **The most lethal thyroid cancer.** Median survival: 3–6 months. Always Stage IV at diagnosis.

🔵 **Management:**
- **Resectable (IVA):** Surgery → adjuvant chemoradiation (weekly doxorubicin or paclitaxel as radiosensitizer)
- **Unresectable (IVB/IVC):** Chemoradiation; clinical trials; palliative care
- **BRAF-mutant ATC (~40%):** Dabrafenib + trametinib — FDA-approved (2018) based on dramatic responses; median OS extended to >12 months in responders
- **PD-L1-positive ATC:** Immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, spartalizumab) show activity
- **Airway management is critical** — may need tracheostomy or stenting

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The BRAF-targeted revolution: ATC with BRAF V600E mutation treated with neoadjuvant dabrafenib/trametinib can convert unresectable tumors to resectable — an approach that was unthinkable a decade ago. Check BRAF status urgently in all ATC diagnoses.

## 8. Complications of Thyroid Surgery

🟢 **Major complications and rates:**

| Complication | Temporary | Permanent | Prevention |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **RLN injury** | 5–7% | 1–2% | Visual nerve identification (gold standard); IONM |
| **Hypoparathyroidism** | 15–30% | 1–4% | Meticulous capsular dissection; parathyroid autotransplantation if devascularized |
| **Hemorrhage / hematoma** | 0.5–2% | — | Hemostasis; close monitoring for 6–24 hours; drain controversial |
| **Wound infection** | <1% | — | Clean surgical technique |
| **Chyle leak** | <1% | — | Especially with lateral neck dissection; low-fat / MCT diet; surgical repair if persistent |

🟢 **Post-thyroidectomy hemorrhage** is a surgical emergency:
- Presents with neck swelling, dyspnea, stridor within 6–24 hours
- **Do NOT wait for imaging** — open the wound at bedside if airway compromise
- Evacuate hematoma → return to OR for exploration and hemostasis

🔵 **Hypoparathyroidism management:**
- **Temporary:** Oral calcium (2–4 g/day) ± calcitriol (0.5–1 μg/day); wean over weeks
- **Permanent:** Lifelong calcium + calcitriol; monitor serum calcium, phosphorus, 24-hour urine calcium
- **Hungry bone syndrome:** Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia in patients with preoperative hyperparathyroidism or thyrotoxicosis; may be profound (Ca <7 mg/dL); requires aggressive IV calcium

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Autotransplantation of devascularized parathyroids: dice into 1 mm fragments, implant into sternocleidomastoid muscle pocket; mark with a suture or clip. Function returns in 4–6 weeks. If all four glands are devascularized, implant into the nondominant forearm (allows future monitoring via ipsilateral/contralateral arm PTH gradient).

## 9. Special Topics

### Thyroid Incidentalomas

🔵 **The tsunami of incidental findings:**
- PET-avid thyroid nodules: malignancy risk 30–35% → FNA all focal PET-avid nodules
- CT incidentalomas: follow up with dedicated US if >1 cm or suspicious features
- Carotid US incidentalomas: most common scenario; apply TI-RADS criteria

### Thyroid Cancer in Pregnancy

🔵 DTC diagnosed during pregnancy:
- **If diagnosed in first trimester/early second trimester:** Can observe with US monitoring; surgery in second trimester if significant growth (>50% volume or >20% in two dimensions)
- **If stable:** Defer surgery to postpartum
- **TSH suppression** with levothyroxine is safe and recommended
- **Avoid RAI** in pregnancy (absolute contraindication)

### Active Surveillance for Papillary Microcarcinoma

🔴 **SS Pearl — A paradigm shift from Japan:**
- Kuma Hospital and Cancer Institute Hospital (Tokyo) demonstrated that observation of **papillary microcarcinomas (≤1 cm, no high-risk features)** is safe
- After 10 years: only 8% grew >3 mm; <4% developed nodal metastasis
- Active surveillance now endorsed by ATA (2015) as an **alternative to immediate surgery** for select low-risk papillary microcarcinomas
- Selection criteria: no extrathyroidal extension on US, no N1, not adjacent to trachea or RLN, no aggressive cytology, reliable patient follow-up

> 📚 *See ATA Guidelines 2015; Ito et al., Thyroid 2014*

## 10. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Thyroid nodules are common (50–67% on US)** but malignancy risk is only 5–10%.
2. **TSH is the first blood test.** Low TSH → scintigraphy; normal/high → ultrasound → FNA.
3. **FNAC is the most important diagnostic test.** Report using the Bethesda System (I–VI).
4. **Papillary thyroid carcinoma** is the most common thyroid cancer (~80%); excellent prognosis.
5. **Key US features of malignancy:** Microcalcifications, hypoechoic, taller-than-wide, irregular margins.
6. **Total thyroidectomy** for tumors >4 cm, bilateral disease, ETE, N1, M1, or aggressive histology. **Lobectomy** is acceptable for ≤4 cm unifocal low-risk DTC.
7. **The two nerves:** RLN (voice) and EBSLN (pitch) — identify and preserve in every thyroidectomy.
8. **Post-thyroidectomy hemorrhage is a surgical emergency** — open the wound at bedside if airway threatened.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

9. **Bethesda III/IV (indeterminate cytology):** Molecular testing (Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3) reduces unnecessary surgery by ~50%.
10. **ATA risk stratification** (Low/Intermediate/High) guides RAI and TSH suppression intensity — more clinically useful than AJCC staging.
11. **Age <55 in DTC** = maximum Stage II even with distant metastases (AJCC 8th edition).
12. **MTC:** Always check RET, always rule out pheochromocytoma before surgery, bilateral CND mandatory. Calcitonin doubling time is the key prognostic factor.
13. **RAI preparation:** TSH >30 mU/L (withdrawal or rhTSH) + low-iodine diet. Post-therapy WBS may upstage 10–15%.
14. **Thyroglobulin** is the tumor marker for DTC after total thyroidectomy; anti-Tg antibodies interfere with the assay — trend both.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

15. **BRAF V600E + TERT promoter co-mutation** = strongest molecular predictor of aggressive DTC behavior.
16. **Active surveillance** for papillary microcarcinoma (≤1 cm, low-risk) is a validated alternative to surgery (Japanese data, ATA-endorsed).
17. **Selpercatinib** — selective RET inhibitor achieving 73% response rate in RET-mutant MTC; first-line for advanced disease.
18. **Dabrafenib + trametinib** for BRAF-mutant ATC — can convert unresectable to resectable; FDA-approved 2018.
19. **RAI-refractory DTC** → lenvatinib or sorafenib (TKIs); lenvatinib showed 18.3-month PFS benefit (SELECT trial).
20. **NIFTP reclassification (2016)** removed the "carcinoma" label from non-invasive encapsulated FVPTC — reducing overdiagnosis and overtreatment.
