## 1. History — Humanity's Oldest Anorectal Affliction

Hemorrhoids have plagued humanity since antiquity. The Edwin Smith Papyrus (~1700 BCE) and Ebers Papyrus (~1550 BCE) describe anorectal remedies involving acacia leaves and ground minerals applied to the "opening of the back." Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) was the first to describe treatment in detail — he advocated cauterization with a hot iron and also described a ligation technique using woolen thread that is recognizably similar to modern rubber band ligation.

The word "hemorrhoid" derives from the Greek *haimorrhoides* (αἱμορροΐδες) — "liable to bleed" (*haima* = blood, *rhoos* = flowing), coined by Hippocrates himself.

🟢 **Key historical milestones:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| ~1550 BCE | **Ebers Papyrus** — Earliest written record of anorectal disease and topical treatments |
| ~400 BCE | **Hippocrates** — Described cautery and ligation; first use of the term *haimorrhoides* |
| 1st c. CE | **Celsus** — Described excisional hemorrhoidectomy with ligation |
| 1882 | **Whitehead** — Circumferential hemorrhoidectomy (now largely abandoned due to stricture) |
| 1937 | **Milligan & Morgan** — Open (excisional) hemorrhoidectomy; remains the global gold standard |
| 1959 | **Blaisdell/Barron** — Rubber band ligation popularized as office procedure |
| 1993 | **Ferguson** — Closed hemorrhoidectomy widely adopted in North America |
| 1998 | **Longo** — Stapled hemorrhoidopexy (PPH) introduced |
| 2005 | **Morinaga** — Doppler-guided hemorrhoidal artery ligation (DG-HAL) |

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.148; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.136; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.47*

The story of hemorrhoids is one of progressive de-escalation — from radical excision toward less invasive, anatomy-preserving approaches. This mirrors our evolving understanding that hemorrhoids are not pathological structures to be destroyed but normal cushions whose displacement and degeneration cause symptoms.

## 2. Anatomy — The Anal Cushion Complex

### Gross Anatomy of the Anal Canal

🟢 The anal canal extends from the anorectal ring (palpable junction of puborectalis and rectal wall) to the anal verge. Its **surgical length** is approximately 4 cm.

🟢 **Key landmarks:**

- **Dentate (pectinate) line** — Located ~2 cm from the anal verge; marks the embryological junction of endoderm (above) and ectoderm (below). This is the single most important anatomical landmark in hemorrhoid surgery.
- **Above the dentate line:** Columnar epithelium, visceral innervation (autonomic — painless), superior rectal arterial supply, portal venous drainage
- **Below the dentate line:** Squamous epithelium, somatic innervation (inferior rectal nerve — exquisitely sensitive), inferior rectal arterial supply, systemic venous drainage

### The Anal Cushion Theory

🟢 **Hemorrhoids are normal anatomical structures.** Thomson (1975) demonstrated that the anal cushions — submucosal vascular plexuses of arterioles, venules, and smooth muscle (Treitz's muscle) — are present in all individuals, including fetuses. They contribute approximately **15–20% of resting anal canal pressure** and provide fine continence by sealing the anal canal.

🟢 **Three primary cushion positions:** Left lateral, right anterolateral, and right posterolateral (corresponding to the classic **3, 7, and 11 o'clock** positions in lithotomy). These positions correspond to the terminal branches of the superior rectal artery.

🔵 **Treitz's muscle (muscularis submucosae ani):** This smooth muscle network anchors the cushions to the internal sphincter and conjoint longitudinal muscle. Degeneration of Treitz's muscle with age and straining is central to cushion prolapse — the connective tissue framework literally gives way.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The Parks ligament (mucosal suspensory ligament) is a distinct fibromuscular structure that helps tether the cushions to the internal sphincter. Histological studies show progressive fragmentation of this ligament in Grade III–IV hemorrhoids, explaining irreversible prolapse. Stapled hemorrhoidopexy and mucopexy techniques specifically target restoration of this anatomical relationship.

### Vascular Supply

🔵 **Arterial:**
- **Superior rectal artery** (terminal branch of IMA) — primary supply to internal hemorrhoids; branches at 3, 7, 11 o'clock
- **Middle rectal artery** (from internal iliac) — variable contribution
- **Inferior rectal artery** (from internal pudendal) — supplies external hemorrhoids and perianal skin

🔵 **Venous:** The hemorrhoidal plexus is a network of sinusoidal vessels, not true veins. The internal hemorrhoidal plexus communicates with the external plexus (portosystemic anastomosis) — hence the old association with portal hypertension, though hemorrhoids are NOT more common in cirrhotics (a persistent myth).

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Doppler studies (Schuurman et al.) have shown that the number and position of arterial branches feeding the cushions is highly variable — up to 6 branches, not always at the classic 3-7-11 positions. This explains why Doppler-guided ligation (DG-HAL) targets arteries individually rather than relying on classical anatomy.

## 3. Pathophysiology — Sliding Anal Cushions, Not "Varicose Veins"

### The Modern Understanding

🟢 **Hemorrhoidal disease is NOT varicose veins of the anal canal.** This outdated concept has been replaced by the **"sliding anal cushion" theory** (Thomson, 1975):

```
Normal anal cushions (present in everyone)
    → Chronic straining / constipation / aging / pregnancy
    → Degeneration of Treitz's muscle & Parks ligament
    → Loss of fibromuscular support
    → Cushion engorgement + downward displacement
    → Mucosal prolapse, erosion, bleeding
    → Symptomatic hemorrhoidal disease
```

🟢 **Contributing factors:**
- **Straining at stool** — The single most important modifiable risk factor
- **Constipation / diarrhea** — Both increase shear stress on cushions
- **Low-fiber diet** — Strong epidemiological association
- **Prolonged toilet sitting** — Unsupported pelvic floor → venous engorgement
- **Aging** — Connective tissue degeneration
- **Pregnancy** — Hormonal effects on connective tissue + increased pelvic venous pressure + direct pressure from gravid uterus
- **Heavy lifting / increased intra-abdominal pressure**
- **Genetic predisposition** — Familial clustering documented

🔵 **Hemodynamic component:** Arteriolar shunting within the cushions leads to sinusoidal engorgement and arterial-type bleeding (bright red, pulsatile). This explains why hemorrhoidal bleeding is characteristically bright red despite the location above the dentate line — it is arterial blood, not venous.

🔵 **Mucosal ischemia-reperfusion:** Repeated prolapse and reduction subjects the cushion mucosa to cycles of ischemia (during prolapse/strangulation) and reperfusion, leading to chronic inflammation, ulceration, and erosion — perpetuating the bleeding cycle.

🔴 **SS Pearl — Portal hypertension and hemorrhoids:** Despite the portosystemic connection, population studies consistently show that hemorrhoids are NOT more common in cirrhotics than the general population. Rectal varices (distinct from hemorrhoids) do occur in portal hypertension and arise from the deeper submucosal rectal venous plexus. Endoscopic ultrasound can distinguish the two. Treating hemorrhoids by banding in cirrhotics carries a higher bleeding risk but is not contraindicated.

## 4. Clinical Features

### Classification

🟢 **Internal hemorrhoids** — Arise above the dentate line (covered by columnar/transitional epithelium; painless unless complicated)

🟢 **External hemorrhoids** — Arise below the dentate line (covered by squamous epithelium; somatic innervation → painful)

🟢 **Mixed hemorrhoids** — Components both above and below the dentate line (most common in advanced disease)

### Goligher Classification (Internal Hemorrhoids)

🟢 **The universally used grading system:**

| Grade | Description | Key Feature |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **I** | Bleed but do not prolapse | Visible only on anoscopy |
| **II** | Prolapse with straining, reduce spontaneously | Return to canal after defecation |
| **III** | Prolapse requiring manual reduction | Patient pushes them back |
| **IV** | Permanently prolapsed, cannot be reduced | Irreducible; may strangulate |

### Presenting Symptoms

🟢 **Bleeding** — The most common symptom
- Characteristically **bright red**, painless, occurring during or after defecation
- Drips into the toilet, streaks on the paper, or coats the stool (NOT mixed with stool)
- Can be profuse enough to cause iron-deficiency anemia (especially in elderly patients with Grade I–II hemorrhoids who don't seek help)

🟢 **Prolapse** — The second most common complaint
- Sensation of a mass at the anus during defecation
- Grades II–IV; associated with mucous discharge and pruritus

🟢 **Other symptoms:**
- **Pruritus ani** — From mucous discharge and chronic moisture
- **Mucous discharge** — From exposed columnar mucosa
- **Soiling/incontinence** — Incomplete seal from displaced cushions
- **Pain** — Internal hemorrhoids are typically PAINLESS. Pain suggests: thrombosis, strangulation, fissure, or an alternative diagnosis

🔵 **Thrombosed external hemorrhoid:**
- Acute, exquisitely painful perianal lump
- Appears as a tense, bluish, subcutaneous nodule at the anal verge
- Peaks in pain at 48–72 hours, then gradually resolves over 7–10 days
- NOT the same as prolapsed thrombosed internal hemorrhoids (a distinct, more serious entity)

🔵 **Acute hemorrhoidal crisis (strangulated/incarcerated hemorrhoids):**
- Prolapsed Grade IV internal hemorrhoids with thrombosis and edema
- Severe pain, inability to reduce, circumferential swelling
- Risk of mucosal necrosis and secondary infection
- Constitutes a surgical emergency

### Special Populations

🔵 **Pregnancy:** Up to 35% of pregnant women develop hemorrhoidal symptoms. Most resolve postpartum. Conservative management preferred; procedural intervention reserved for refractory postpartum disease.

🔵 **Immunocompromised patients:** Higher risk of secondary infection. Band ligation may cause sepsis — use with extreme caution in neutropenic or HIV patients with low CD4 counts.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** Perianal Crohn's disease can mimic hemorrhoids. Hypertrophic anal skin tags in Crohn's are often misdiagnosed as external hemorrhoids. Hemorrhoid surgery in Crohn's carries a 30–40% risk of nonhealing wounds — avoid unless absolutely necessary and after multidisciplinary discussion.

> 📚 *See Shackelford 9th Ed, Ch.148; Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.136*

## 5. Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment

🟢 **History:** Directed inquiry about bleeding pattern (bright red, on paper/in bowl, relation to defecation), prolapse (spontaneous reduction vs. manual), pain, bowel habits, diet, and family history of colorectal cancer.

🟢 **Examination:** Performed in **left lateral (Sims') position** or prone jackknife:
1. **Inspection** — Perianal skin tags, external hemorrhoids, prolapse (ask patient to strain), fissures, fistula openings, dermatological conditions
2. **Digital rectal exam** — Internal hemorrhoids are usually NOT palpable (they are soft and compress). DRE evaluates sphincter tone, masses, tenderness, prostate (if applicable)
3. **Anoscopy** — The key diagnostic tool. Visualizes internal hemorrhoids at 3, 7, 11 o'clock, assesses size and grading. Performed with a slotted (Fansler) anoscope for optimal visualization

🔵 **Proctoscopy/rigid sigmoidoscopy** — Excludes rectal pathology (proctitis, rectal polyps, rectal cancer) up to 25 cm.

### When to Investigate Further

🟢 **Red flags requiring colonoscopy:**
- Age ≥45 (or ≥40 with risk factors) with new rectal bleeding
- Change in bowel habit
- Family history of colorectal cancer
- Iron-deficiency anemia
- Weight loss
- Bleeding atypical for hemorrhoids (dark blood, mixed with stool)
- Failure to improve with appropriate hemorrhoid treatment

🔵 **NICE/BSG/AGA guidelines** all emphasize that hemorrhoids are a **diagnosis of exclusion** in patients with risk factors for colorectal malignancy. Never attribute rectal bleeding solely to hemorrhoids without appropriate investigation.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** In patients with known hemorrhoids AND colorectal cancer risk factors, both conditions can coexist. Up to 3% of patients initially diagnosed with "hemorrhoids" are subsequently found to have colorectal neoplasia. The hemorrhoid does not protect against cancer.

> 📚 *See Sabiston 22nd Ed, Ch.136; Maingot 12th Ed, Ch.47*

## 6. Treatment

### General Principles

🟢 **Step-up approach:** Conservative → office-based → operative. The least invasive effective treatment is always preferred.

### A. Conservative Management (All Grades, First-Line)

🟢 **Dietary and lifestyle modification — The foundation of ALL hemorrhoid management:**
- **High-fiber diet** (25–30 g/day) or fiber supplements (psyllium, methylcellulose)
- **Adequate hydration** (1.5–2 L/day)
- **Avoid straining** — time-limited toilet sits (<5 minutes)
- **Avoid prolonged sitting**
- **Regular exercise**

🟢 **Evidence for fiber:** A Cochrane meta-analysis (7 RCTs, n=378) showed fiber supplementation **reduces bleeding by 50%** and overall symptom improvement by 47%. Fiber alone resolves symptoms in the majority of Grade I and many Grade II hemorrhoids.

🟢 **Topical therapy:**
- **Sitz baths** (warm water, 10–15 min, 2–3 times daily) — soothe symptoms, no curative evidence
- **Topical preparations** (e.g., hydrocortisone, lidocaine, phenylephrine) — symptomatic relief only; limit steroid use to <2 weeks (risk of skin atrophy)
- **Flavonoids** (micronized purified flavonoid fraction — Daflon/MPFF): 🔵 Meta-analysis of 14 RCTs shows reduction in bleeding, pain, and recurrence. Mechanism: improved venous tone and reduced capillary permeability. Widely used in Europe/Asia; limited adoption in North America.

### B. Office-Based Procedures (Grade I–III)

#### Rubber Band Ligation (RBL)

🟢 **The most effective office-based treatment** — considered the gold standard for Grade I–III internal hemorrhoids.

**Technique:**
- Band placed at base of hemorrhoid cushion **above the dentate line** (insensate zone)
- Causes ischemic necrosis → tissue sloughs in 5–7 days → fibrosis and fixation
- Usually one cushion per session; can band multiple simultaneously (with higher complication risk)
- Repeat at 4–6 week intervals if needed

**Results:**
- Success rate: 60–80% for Grade I–II; 50–70% for Grade III
- Recurrence: 30–50% over 5 years (but easily repeatable)

🔵 **Complications:**
- Pain (if band placed too close to dentate line) — most common
- Delayed bleeding (7–14 days, when eschar separates) — 1–2%
- Urinary retention — 0.5%
- **Pelvic sepsis** — Rare (1:50,000) but potentially fatal; presents with fever, pelvic pain, urinary retention within 3–7 days. **Necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum (Fournier's gangrene) has been reported.** Requires emergency debridement + IV antibiotics.

🔴 **SS Pearl:** RBL is contraindicated in patients on dual antiplatelet therapy and should be used cautiously in patients on anticoagulants. A recent ASCRS position statement suggests holding anticoagulation is not mandatory for single-band ligation, but multi-banding sessions should be avoided.

#### Other Office Procedures

🔵 **Infrared coagulation (IRC):** Coagulates hemorrhoidal tissue via infrared light. Less effective than RBL but less painful. Best for Grade I–II. Requires more sessions.

🔵 **Sclerotherapy:** Injection of sclerosant (5% phenol in almond oil, or sodium tetradecyl sulfate) into the submucosa above the hemorrhoid. Creates fibrosis and fixation. Simple and safe but least effective of office procedures. Risk of prostatic abscess in males (avoid anterior injections).

🔵 **Comparative evidence (RBL vs. IRC vs. sclerotherapy):** MacRae and McLeod meta-analysis — RBL is superior to all other office procedures for Grade I–III hemorrhoids with lower retreatment rates, despite slightly more pain.

### C. Operative Management (Grade III–IV, Failed Office Therapy)

#### Excisional Hemorrhoidectomy

🟢 **Milligan-Morgan (open) hemorrhoidectomy:**
- Three-column excision leaving the wounds open to heal by secondary intention
- Mucosal bridges preserved between excision sites (critical to prevent stricture)
- Most widely performed globally; gold standard for Grade III–IV

🟢 **Ferguson (closed) hemorrhoidectomy:**
- Same excision but wounds closed with running absorbable suture
- Preferred in North America
- Faster healing but no difference in long-term outcomes vs. Milligan-Morgan

🔵 **Technique essentials (both):**
- Position: prone jackknife (preferred) or lithotomy
- Identify and preserve the internal sphincter (visible as white fibers beneath the hemorrhoid)
- Begin excision at perianal skin, extend to pedicle above the dentate line
- Secure pedicle with suture ligation (not diathermy alone for the pedicle)
- Leave adequate mucosal bridges (minimum 1 cm between excision sites)
- Hemostasis meticulously achieved

🔵 **Energy devices:** LigaSure, Harmonic scalpel, and bipolar diathermy all reduce operative time and intraoperative bleeding compared to cold scalpel. **LigaSure hemorrhoidectomy** has the most evidence — meta-analyses show less pain and shorter operative time vs. conventional, with equivalent recurrence.

🟢 **Postoperative pain** is the major limitation of excisional hemorrhoidectomy. Multimodal analgesia is essential:
- Metronidazole (reduces bacterial colonization of wounds → less pain; NNT = 3)
- Local anesthetic infiltration/pudendal nerve block
- Oral analgesics (NSAIDs + paracetamol ± short-course weak opioid)
- Topical GTN 0.2% or diltiazem 2% (reduces sphincter spasm → less pain)
- Stool softeners (lactulose/docusate) — essential from day 1

#### Stapled Hemorrhoidopexy (Procedure for Prolapse and Hemorrhoids — PPH)

🔵 **Concept:** Does NOT excise hemorrhoids. A circular stapler excises a circumferential ring of rectal mucosa above the hemorrhoidal cushions, thereby:
1. Lifting the prolapsed cushions back to their anatomical position
2. Interrupting arterial inflow to the cushions
3. Reducing cushion size through devascularization

**Advantages:** Less pain, faster recovery, earlier return to work
**Disadvantages:** Higher recurrence rate, unique complications

🔵 **eTHoS trial (2016, UK, n=777):** The largest RCT comparing stapled vs. conventional hemorrhoidectomy. At 24 months:
- Stapled: higher recurrence (18% vs. 10%)
- Stapled: higher residual prolapse
- Stapled: more reinterventions
- No significant difference in pain at 24 months
- **Conclusion: Conventional hemorrhoidectomy is more effective long-term**

🔴 **Unique complications of stapled hemorrhoidopexy:**
- **Staple-line bleeding** (5–8%)
- **Urgency/tenesmus** (from low staple line)
- **Rectovaginal fistula** — Rare but devastating (failure to check for vaginal tenting before firing)
- **Rectal pocket syndrome** (staple line creates a mucosal pocket trapping stool)
- **Chronic proctalgia** (if staple line incorporates muscle)
- **Rectal perforation/pelvic sepsis** — Rare; life-threatening

🔴 **SS Pearl:** The NICE (UK) guidelines now recommend conventional hemorrhoidectomy over stapled hemorrhoidopexy based on eTHoS data. Stapled hemorrhoidopexy remains used selectively for circumferential Grade III hemorrhoids where mucosal bridges would be insufficient for conventional excision.

#### Doppler-Guided Hemorrhoidal Artery Ligation (DG-HAL) ± Recto-Anal Repair (RAR)

🔵 **Concept:** A Doppler-equipped proctoscope identifies and ligates the terminal branches of the superior rectal artery feeding the hemorrhoidal cushions. RAR adds a mucopexy (plicating prolapsed mucosa) to address prolapse.

**HubBLe trial (2016, UK, n=370):** DG-HAL vs. RBL for Grade II–III:
- Similar 12-month recurrence
- DG-HAL: more pain, higher cost, no superiority
- **Conclusion: RBL should be first-line; DG-HAL offers no advantage**

🔴 **Current position:** DG-HAL ± RAR has largely fallen out of favor as a standalone technique. It may have a niche role in Grade II–III patients who prefer an operative approach but wish to avoid excisional surgery.

## 7. Complications of Hemorrhoid Surgery

🔵 **Common to all procedures:**

| Complication | Incidence | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| **Pain** | Universal (excisional) | Multimodal analgesia; sphincter spasm is the primary driver |
| **Bleeding** | 1–6% | Primary (<24h) or secondary (7–14 days); usually self-limiting |
| **Urinary retention** | 2–15% | More common in males, spinal anesthesia, excessive IV fluids; limit periop fluids to <500 mL |
| **Anal stenosis** | 1–3% | From excessive tissue excision or failure to preserve mucosal bridges; treat with dilation |
| **Fecal incontinence** | 2–12% (minor) | Usually flatus incontinence from internal sphincter damage; mostly temporary |
| **Recurrence** | 5–15% (excisional) | Higher with non-excisional procedures |

🔴 **SS Pearl — Continence after hemorrhoidectomy:** The anal cushions contribute to fine continence. Even successful hemorrhoidectomy may reduce resting anal pressure by 20–30% (loss of cushion bulk + internal sphincter injury). Preoperative anal manometry should be considered in patients with pre-existing continence concerns, prior anorectal surgery, or obstetric injury. In these patients, non-excisional approaches (DG-HAL/RAR, stapled) may be preferred.

🔴 **Post-hemorrhoidectomy pelvic sepsis:** Rare but catastrophic. Fever, pelvic pain, urinary retention 3–10 days post-procedure. Requires urgent CT, IV antibiotics, and often surgical drainage. Mortality up to 50% if untreated. Maintain a high index of suspicion for any patient with fever after anorectal procedures.

## 8. Special Topics

### Thrombosed External Hemorrhoid

🟢 **Management:**
- **Presentation within 72 hours + severe pain:** Excision (NOT incision and drainage — incision leaves the clot capsule and leads to recurrence, skin tags, and re-thrombosis)
- **After 72 hours or mild symptoms:** Conservative (sitz baths, analgesics, stool softeners; resolves in 7–14 days, may leave a residual skin tag)

🔵 **Excision technique:** Local anesthesia (1% lidocaine with epinephrine), elliptical incision encompassing the thrombosed vein and overlying skin, remove entire thrombosed complex, achieve hemostasis, leave wound open.

### Hemorrhoids in Pregnancy

🔵 Management is primarily conservative (fiber, sitz baths, topical treatments). Most hemorrhoids developing during pregnancy resolve within 6 weeks postpartum. Operative intervention is reserved for rare cases of strangulation or intractable symptoms unresponsive to conservative measures. RBL and hemorrhoidectomy are deferred to the postpartum period.

### Hemorrhoids in Inflammatory Bowel Disease

🔴 **Key rule: Exercise extreme caution.** In Crohn's disease, any anorectal surgery risks non-healing wounds (30–40%). In ulcerative colitis, hemorrhoidal surgery is generally safe. Always confirm the diagnosis — what appears to be hemorrhoids may be hypertrophic Crohn's tags, fissures, or abscesses.

### Hemorrhoids and Anticoagulation

🔵 **Medical management preferred.** If intervention is required, single-session RBL is generally safe without stopping anticoagulation (ASCRS). Excisional hemorrhoidectomy requires anticoagulation management per institutional protocols.

## 9. Key Points / Summary

### 🟢 UG Essentials — The Non-Negotiables

1. **Hemorrhoids are normal anal cushions** at 3, 7, and 11 o'clock positions (lithotomy). Disease occurs when they become symptomatic through displacement and degeneration.
2. **Internal hemorrhoids** arise above the dentate line (painless, bleed); **external hemorrhoids** arise below (painful, thrombose).
3. **Goligher classification:** Grade I (bleed only) → Grade IV (irreducible prolapse). This determines treatment.
4. **Bright red painless rectal bleeding** during defecation is the hallmark symptom. Pain suggests thrombosis, fissure, or alternative diagnosis.
5. **Dietary fiber supplementation reduces bleeding by 50%** — first-line for all grades.
6. **Rubber band ligation** is the most effective office procedure for Grade I–III.
7. **Excisional hemorrhoidectomy** (Milligan-Morgan or Ferguson) is the gold standard for Grade III–IV and failed office therapy.
8. **Thrombosed external hemorrhoid <72h with severe pain → excise; >72h → conservative.**
9. **Always exclude colorectal malignancy** in patients ≥45 or with red flag symptoms.

### 🔵 PG Depth — Beyond the Basics

10. Thomson's sliding anal cushion theory (1975) replaced the "varicose vein" concept. Degeneration of Treitz's muscle and Parks ligament causes cushion displacement.
11. Hemorrhoidal bleeding is arterial (bright red) due to arteriolar-sinusoidal shunting within the cushions.
12. Hemorrhoids are NOT more common in portal hypertension — distinguish from rectal varices.
13. eTHoS trial: conventional hemorrhoidectomy superior to stapled hemorrhoidopexy at 24 months (lower recurrence, fewer reinterventions).
14. HubBLe trial: DG-HAL offers no advantage over RBL for Grade II–III.
15. Urinary retention is the most common complication after hemorrhoidectomy — limit IV fluids, consider local/regional anesthesia.
16. Metronidazole reduces post-hemorrhoidectomy pain (NNT = 3).
17. Topical GTN/diltiazem reduces post-operative sphincter spasm and pain.

### 🔴 SS Pearls — The Expert Layer

18. Anal cushions contribute 15–20% of resting anal pressure; hemorrhoidectomy may permanently reduce continence — consider manometry in at-risk patients.
19. Rectovaginal fistula is a devastating complication of stapled hemorrhoidopexy — always check for vaginal tenting before firing.
20. Pelvic sepsis after anorectal procedures (including banding) can be fatal — maintain high suspicion for fever + pelvic pain post-procedure.
21. Crohn's disease: 30–40% non-healing wound rate after hemorrhoid surgery — avoid if possible.
22. Doppler studies show variable arterial anatomy (not always 3-7-11) — DG-HAL targets individual branches.
23. Flavonoids (MPFF) have Level 1 evidence for reducing hemorrhoidal bleeding and symptoms.
